\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial psychiatric consultation transcript requires comparing several possible disorders under incomplete evidence and selecting one primary diagnosis. Key information about illness course, onset, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity may not be fully elicited in a single consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, but fluent language understanding does not ensure reliable differential diagnosis.

Endpoint accuracy shows whether the final answer is correct, but not whether the error occurred because the system failed to generate the reference diagnosis, did not retain it after criterion verification, or generated and verified it but selected another disorder as primary. To study and localize these failures, we propose HiED, a hybrid multi-agent framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts. HiED records candidate generation, ICD-10 criterion-by-criterion verification, and primary-diagnosis selection separately and produces an inspectable audit trace for each prediction.

On an internal test set of 1,000 cases, Top-3 coverage of the reference diagnosis was 0.802, whereas Top-1 primary-diagnosis accuracy was 0.518. Among 915 cases with a corresponding criterion checker, the reference diagnosis remained in the logic-confirmed set in 857 cases (0.937). These results show that the main gap is not only whether the system can generate a plausible candidate diagnosis, but also whether it can choose the most appropriate primary diagnosis from several candidates supported by the criteria. We call this the primary-selection bottleneck.

This thesis makes two contributions: (1) an auditable framework that separates candidate generation, criterion verification, and primary selection; and (2) reproducible experimental evidence that primary selection is an important source of error in the synthetic Chinese transcripts used in this study. HiED has not been validated using real-patient data from Taiwan and should be viewed as a prototype for research and clinician review, not an autonomous diagnostic tool. The Taiwanese context refers to Traditional Chinese interaction, local inference, and clinician-facing audit design; real-patient clinical validation remains future work.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric outpatient care in Taiwan is widely accessible, but this access also creates a heavy clinical workload. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit different levels of medical facilities without referral, and large medical centers may receive more than ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in one day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A first psychiatric visit must cover history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and an initial diagnostic judgment within limited time. The resulting note or transcript often contains incomplete information about onset, duration, course, medical or substance-related causes, and previous treatment.

The evidence may also be indirect. In Chinese-speaking settings, patients often describe distress through insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, or gastrointestinal symptoms before using explicit mood or anxiety terms~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. Taiwanese consultations may also mix Mandarin, Taiwanese expressions, and local clinical wording. The same condition can therefore be described in several ways within one visit.

Differential diagnosis remains difficult because common psychiatric disorders share many symptoms. Depression, anxiety, stress-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, somatic symptom disorders, and sleep disorders may all involve insomnia, fatigue, poor concentration, and impaired daily function. ICD-10 provides requirements for symptoms, duration, impairment, course, and exclusions~\citep{who2019icd10}, but several disorders may still fit the same transcript. Evidence for both depression and anxiety, for example, may reflect comorbidity or overlapping symptoms from one main condition~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}. Even structured diagnostic studies report limited agreement for some common disorders~\citep{regier2013dsm5}.

The main difficulty is therefore comparison. Clinicians must keep several diagnoses in mind, note which criteria are supported or still unknown, choose the best primary diagnosis, and decide whether other diagnoses are independent comorbidities. Under time pressure, this process is vulnerable to anchoring and premature closure~\citep{croskerry2003cognitive}. When key facts were not asked, the clinician must also decide whether to make a provisional diagnosis, collect more information, or delay the decision.\ct{I don't quite understand this part.}\ct{I don't fully understand this paragraph. \section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

These conditions motivate decision support rather than autonomous diagnosis. Large language models can read free-form dialogue, summarize symptoms, generate differential diagnoses, and return structured outputs~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. However, plausible text is not the same as reliable disorder-level selection. Performance often falls when models move from broad mental-health judgments to distinctions among similar disorders~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. This thesis focuses on one question: when the reference diagnosis is not selected, was it missing from the leading candidates, rejected by criterion checking, or present but not chosen as primary?\ct{Does our model deal with all these problems?}

We study this question with three measures. \emph{Candidate detection} asks whether a gold parent diagnosis appears in the ranked Top-3. \emph{Criterion verification} asks whether a gold parent appears in the logic-confirmed set for cases covered by the checker bank. \emph{Primary selection} asks whether the final primary prediction belongs to the gold parent set. These measures are used to locate errors; they do not describe a fixed chronological process for every policy.

We use the term \emph{selection bottleneck} when candidate coverage or verification retention is much higher than Top-1 accuracy. The pattern means that a gold diagnosis is often available in the system output but is not selected as primary. It does not mean that every error passes both earlier checks, or that every transcript contains enough information for a unique diagnosis.\ct{what problem?}

This distinction matters because different errors need different solutions. Missing candidates call for better generation or retrieval. A candidate that fails criterion checking points to the checker, the rules, or missing evidence. A candidate that remains available but is not selected points to the final comparison among plausible diagnoses. The detailed metric definitions are given in \Cref{sec:gold-conventions}.

\section{System Overview}

To study these errors, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid multi-agent system for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis from initial-consultation transcripts. The intended context is Taiwanese outpatient care, but all quantitative results in this thesis use synthetic Chinese transcript datasets; no Taiwanese patient results are reported.

HiED has five main parts. It formats the dialogue while preserving speaker turns, retrieves class-balanced examples, asks an LLM diagnostician for a ranked differential and a primary diagnosis, checks each active diagnosis against operationalized ICD-10 criteria, and applies deterministic rules to form a logic-confirmed set. Each criterion is marked as \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}.

The selected Direct-Answer policy keeps the diagnostician's primary diagnosis and uses the checker and logic outputs as an audit. The alternative Nominate-then-Select policy uses the same upstream outputs but allows the logic-confirmed set to change the final primary. Their comparison tests whether criterion compatibility is more useful as an audit or as a selector. Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} describes the architecture in detail.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions.

First, it presents an auditable hybrid architecture that combines LLM diagnosis, explicit ICD-10 criterion checking, and deterministic rules. The system records criterion states and supporting evidence instead of relying only on a free-form rationale. The trace can be inspected and replayed, but it does not prove that the LLM's hidden reasoning is faithful or that the checker judgments are clinically correct~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}.

Second, it presents a stage-aware evaluation framework for separating candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection. This framework makes the selection bottleneck measurable and supports controlled tests of logic-based re-selection and other repair methods. The negative repair results narrow several simple explanations under the tested settings, but they do not show that better selectors or additional clinical information can never help.

\section{Thesis Organization}

Chapter~\ref{ch:related} reviews related psychiatric LLM, multi-agent, and auditable diagnosis research. Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} describes HiED. Chapter~\ref{ch:data} defines the datasets and metrics, and Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental} explains the experiments. Chapters~\ref{ch:results} and~\ref{ch:external} report the internal and external synthetic results. Chapter~\ref{ch:error} examines error patterns. Chapters~\ref{ch:discussion}--\ref{ch:conclusion} discuss the findings, limitations, conclusions, and future work.


\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

Psychiatric LLM studies cover several different tasks, including examination questions, short vignettes, diagnostic reasoning, and comparison with clinicians~\citep{qiu2023largeai,li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. These studies show that LLMs can produce plausible clinical answers, but performance depends on the input, label space, and reference standard. Detecting depression from text or audio is also different from choosing one primary diagnosis among several overlapping disorders~\citep{li2025addreview}.

Chinese psychiatric datasets make disorder-level evaluation possible. LingxiDiagBench includes static diagnosis and dynamic consultation over the synthetic LingxiDiag-16K corpus and reports a clear drop from broad depression--anxiety classification to fine-grained diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. MDD-5k provides a second synthetic Chinese diagnostic-dialogue resource based on anonymized cases~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. These datasets support reproducible experiments, but they do not replace real-patient validation.

Existing systems also differ in whether they can ask questions. MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, and DSM5AgentFlow study inquiry or interactive diagnostic workflows~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels focus more on diagnosis from information already available in records or transcripts~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. Interactive systems can obtain missing evidence, while fixed-input systems isolate how well a model uses the same evidence. This thesis studies the fixed-input setting.

Most psychiatric studies report final diagnostic accuracy, consultation quality, or explanation quality. They do not usually report, on the same cases, whether the correct diagnosis entered the leading candidates, passed explicit criterion checking, and was finally selected as primary.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

Medical multi-agent systems assign different roles to LLMs and combine their outputs through consultation, debate, or adaptive collaboration~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. AMIE and related work also show the value of broad differential diagnosis, realistic consultation, and expert review~\citep{mcduff2025amie,tu2025conversational}. These systems demonstrate that structured interaction can help, but adding agents does not guarantee a better final answer. Agents may repeat the same model bias, and free-form discussion can make it difficult to identify which component changed the decision.

For this thesis, the important issue is controlled attribution. If ranking, criterion checking, debate, and finalization all change at once, an accuracy difference cannot show which change helped. HiED therefore compares two policies that reuse the same ranked candidates and checker outputs: Direct-Answer keeps the diagnostician's decision, whereas Nominate-then-Select uses criterion compatibility to choose the primary.

\section{Auditability and Criterion Grounding}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

A fluent explanation is not proof of faithful reasoning. Model-generated chains can sound coherent while not reflecting the factors that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. In healthcare, explanation quality, interpretability, and clinical validity therefore need separate evaluation~\citep{rudin2019stop,huang2024xiai}.

Diagnostic-chain and symptom-based methods make outputs easier to inspect~\citep{chen2025cod,lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. However, a generated reasoning chain is still another model output. This thesis uses the narrower term \emph{auditability}: an external record that links transcript evidence, criterion states, rule results, and the final output. The record can be checked and replayed, but it does not reveal the model's hidden reasoning or turn checker outputs into clinical ground truth.

Constraint-logic systems are the closest related approach because they translate diagnostic criteria into explicit rules~\citep{kim2025clp}. HiED keeps the learned diagnostician separate from the criterion and rule layer. Under Direct-Answer, the structured layer audits the decision; under Nominate-then-Select, it is tested as a selector. Prior work has not used this separation to compare verification retention with primary selection on Chinese psychiatric transcripts.

\section{Structured Knowledge and Selection}

Knowledge-based diagnosis methods represent relations among symptoms, disorders, and missing information. MedKGI supports information-guided inquiry, and DKEC uses structured domain knowledge for label prediction~\citep{wang2025medkgi,ge2024dkec}. Such relations may help when disorders share symptoms but differ in onset, duration, exclusions, or causal direction.

HiED does not implement a full knowledge-graph selector. Instead, this literature motivates future work if simple criterion counts and local re-ranking are not enough. A stronger selector may need to compare which diagnosis best explains the full presentation, not only how many criteria each diagnosis satisfies.

\section{Research Gap and Research Questions}

Prior work shows that LLMs can diagnose psychiatric cases, ask follow-up questions, use diagnostic criteria, and work in multi-agent systems. Three gaps remain. First, fixed-evidence diagnosis and active information gathering are often mixed in end-to-end evaluations. Second, final accuracy does not show whether the correct diagnosis was missing, rejected, or simply not selected. Third, criterion grounding is rarely tested separately as an audit and as a final selector.

This thesis addresses these gaps with four questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] Where is the reference diagnosis lost: before Top-3 coverage, during criterion verification, or at Top-1 selection?
\item[RQ2] Does criterion-based re-selection improve over the diagnostician's Direct-Answer decision?
\item[RQ3] Can the tested repair methods reduce the remaining selection gap?
\item[RQ4] How stable are the main pattern and repair results across model size, retrieval, external synthetic data, label-space expansion, and major error groups?
\end{description}

RQ1 and RQ2 are answered by the internal evaluation, RQ3 by the repair experiments, and RQ4 by the robustness and external analyses. Clinical effectiveness, human transcript-only performance, and criterion-level clinical validity remain future work.


\chapter{HiED System Architecture}
\label{ch:architecture}

\section{Input, Diagnostic Scope, and Pipeline}

HiED processes one Chinese psychiatric consultation transcript at a time. Each case is stored as an ordered sequence of clinician and patient turns. Speaker labels and turn order are preserved when the prompt is built. If the transcript is too long for the model context, the system keeps complete turns from the beginning and end and inserts an omission marker; it does not summarize or invent the missing middle text. The task is limited to initial outpatient diagnosis, so the system can use only information stated in the current dialogue.\ct{Is this about input and transcript format?}

The diagnostic scope is fixed before each run. The canonical evaluation uses fourteen active ICD-10 entries, including subtype entries within F41 and F43. These collapse to eleven parent classes for evaluation; the separate \emph{Others} class is defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}. The same active entries are used for every patient unless a scope-expansion experiment changes the configuration.

The diagnostician prompt contains the task instructions, the active diagnoses, the speaker-labeled transcript\ct{?}, retrieved examples, and the required JSON output format. Retrieval is completed before generation; the model cannot request additional tools or cases during the response.\ct{?}

\section{Class-Balanced Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

Retrieval-augmented generation adds relevant examples to the prompt~\citep{lewis2020rag}. HiED encodes the current transcript and training cases with BGE-M3 and searches the vectors with FAISS~\citep{chen2024bgem3,douze2024faiss}. Instead of taking only the globally nearest cases, it attempts to retrieve at most one case for each active parent class. This reduces domination by frequent diagnoses.

The classes searched are fixed by the diagnostic profile, not chosen separately for each patient\ct{Are the candidate classes pre-determined, or patient-specific?}. The returned example within each class depends on the current transcript. Subtypes that share a parent code, such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2, also share a retrieval class. Retrieved cases are shown only to the diagnostician; they do not enter the checker prompts or directly select the primary diagnosis.

\section{Diagnostician}

The canonical diagnostician uses Qwen3-32B-AWQ served through vLLM. Its prompt requests exactly five ranked active entries with short reasons, one primary entry chosen from the first three, and at most one optional comorbid entry\ct{?}. The primary field and the first ranked item are separate. A gold diagnosis may therefore appear in the Top-3 even when the committed primary is wrong.

The Direct-Answer prompt does not request a calibrated probability or an abstention decision. Confidence-based methods are separate repair experiments, not removals of a component from the reference architecture.\ct{Is this an ablation study? If so, why in Appendix?}

\section{Criterion Checkers and Logic Engine}

The checker bank uses study-specific operationalizations of ICD-10 criteria\ct{Is this ICD 10?}~\citep{who2019icd10}. The five ranked diagnoses are checked first, followed by the remaining active entries, so the audit covers the full profile.

A checker uses three evidence states.\par\ct{What 3 states?}\par \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, and \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} are defined as follows. \texttt{met} means that the transcript supports the criterion. \texttt{not\_met} means that the transcript provides evidence against it. \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} means that the transcript does not support either conclusion. Each result may include a short evidence span and a checker confidence value.

The deterministic logic engine then applies the stored rule for each diagnosis. Rules may require all criteria, a minimum number of core and total criteria, a symptom count, or specific groups of criteria. The same checker trace always produces the same confirmed set. However, confirmation means that a diagnosis is compatible with the operational rules; it does not show that the diagnosis should be primary. Several diagnoses may be confirmed in the same case.

Checker states are model-generated and have not been validated as clinician ground truth. Their purpose is to create a clear record that can be inspected and replayed, not to prove that the criterion judgments are correct.

\section{Finalization Policies}

Under Direct-Answer (DA), the diagnostician's primary field becomes the final primary diagnosis; the first ranked diagnosis is used only when that field is missing. An optional comorbidity is retained when it differs from the primary. Checker and logic outputs do not replace the DA primary.

Nominate-then-Select (NtS) uses criterion compatibility to choose the primary. It selects the first confirmed diagnosis in the five-entry ranking. If none is confirmed, it selects another confirmed active entry with the highest met ratio. If no diagnosis is confirmed, it falls back to the first ranked diagnosis. NtS does not emit a comorbid diagnosis.

DA and NtS reuse the same ranked candidates and checker traces and differ only in the finalization rule. This provides a direct test of whether criterion-based re-selection improves the primary diagnosis. Pairwise comparison and debate are optional repair methods discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}.

\section{Audit Output}

The final record contains the predicted diagnosis set, ranked differential, criterion states, evidence spans, confirmed diagnoses, finalization source, prompt hash, failures, retrieval events, and stage timings. A reviewer can therefore challenge the candidate ranking, an individual criterion judgment, a rule, or the final selection separately.

This record follows the definition of auditability in Section~\ref{sec:auditability-definition}. It makes the system easier to inspect, but it does not expose the LLM's hidden reasoning, prove clinical correctness, or replace real-patient validation.

\section{Diagnostic Scope and Gold Labels}
\label{sec:gold-conventions}

The selected \texttt{paper\_canonical} profile allows the system to emit 14 ICD-10 entries\ct{Should you introduce ICD-10 earlier}:
\[
\{\mathrm{F20},\mathrm{F31},\mathrm{F32},\mathrm{F39},
\mathrm{F41.0},\mathrm{F41.1},\mathrm{F41.2},\mathrm{F42},
\mathrm{F43.1},\mathrm{F43.2},\mathrm{F45},\mathrm{F51},
\mathrm{F98},\mathrm{Z71}\}.
\]
F41 and F43 include subtype entries. For scoring, decimal suffixes are removed, so the 14 entries become 11 parent labels. Adding \emph{Others} gives the twelve-class paper label space:
\[
\mathcal{C}=\{\mathrm{F20},\mathrm{F31},\mathrm{F32},\mathrm{F39},\mathrm{F41},
\mathrm{F42},\mathrm{F43},\mathrm{F45},\mathrm{F51},\mathrm{F98},
\mathrm{Z71},\mathrm{Others}\}.
\]
For example, F41.1 maps to F41. A raw parent outside this set maps to \emph{Others}. Scope-expanded experiments activate another profile rather than changing this mapping silently.

For case $x$, $G(x)$ is the deduplicated set of projected gold parents. LingxiDiag cases may have more than one gold parent. The paper therefore uses an \emph{any-gold} rule for its main ranking metrics: a prediction is correct when it matches any member of $G(x)$. The first-listed gold parent is used only in analyses explicitly labeled as single-label, such as the confusion matrix.

\section{Prediction Views and Metrics}

The evaluator keeps three outputs separate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item The \emph{primary view}, $p_1(x)$, is the final primary diagnosis.
\item The \emph{ranked view}, $R_3(x)$, contains the primary followed by the remaining deduplicated diagnostician ranking, limited to three parent labels.
\item The \emph{multilabel view}, $M(x)$, contains the primary and any emitted comorbid parent.
\end{enumerate}

Top-1 measures final-primary agreement:
\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}1}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)].
\]
Top-3 measures short-list coverage:
\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}3}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing].
\]
Exact Match requires equality between the predicted and gold parent sets:
\[
\mathrm{Exact}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[M(x)=G(x)].
\]
Macro-F1 gives each of the twelve classes equal weight. Weighted-F1 weights classes by gold support. A system without a real ranked output is not assigned an artificial Top-3 from the fixed candidate list.

The repository also stores an older first-listed-gold Top-1 field. It is used for some single-label analyses but not for the paper headline. This is why the canonical run can have first-listed accuracy 0.509 and paper Top-1 0.518 without contradiction.

\section{Stage-Wise Analysis}

For each case, the thesis records three indicators:
\[
D_3(x)=\mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
R(x)=\mathbb{1}[L(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
S(x)=\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
where $L(x)$ is the logic-confirmed parent set. $D_3$ and $S$ are averaged over all cases. $R$ is averaged only over cases whose non-\emph{Others} gold parent has an active checker, and its denominator is always reported.

These measures show whether a gold diagnosis appears in the short list, is supported by the criterion audit, and is selected as primary. They are analytical views of one recorded output, not a claim that every policy executes the stages in this order. In Direct-Answer, for example, the primary is emitted before the checker audit is created.

\section{Statistical Tests and Run Checks}

Single proportions are reported with 95\% Wilson intervals where useful. Paired system comparisons use the exact McNemar test. Holm--Bonferroni correction is applied within stated comparison families. A non-significant result is not treated as proof of equivalence.

Each run is checked for case count, case order, configuration and prompt identity, output schema, ranked-output availability, parse failures, and candidate validity. The canonical internal run uses Qwen3-32B-AWQ through vLLM, greedy decoding, an 8,192-token context window, and a 1,536-token output limit. The repository records the model, code version, split fingerprint, configuration, retrieval-index hashes, predictions, and metrics.

\section{Failure Localization}

RQ1 uses the $D_3$--$R$--$S$ measures defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}. They ask whether the reference diagnosis appears in the Top-3, appears in the confirmed set when it is checkable, and is selected as primary. Confirmed-set size and criterion-state counts show whether the audit rejects the reference diagnosis or keeps many diagnoses at the same time.

A separate close-cluster analysis estimates how many wrong DA cases might be recoverable if a better rule could choose among criterion-supported alternatives. Because this analysis uses the gold label to identify recoverable cases, it is an upper bound, not a deployable method or an accuracy estimate.

\section{Finalization and Checker Sensitivity}

RQ2 is tested mainly by DA versus NtS. Checker sensitivity has a narrower role. Under DA, checker and logic outputs are created after the primary commitment and cannot overwrite it. Changing checker granularity or verification scope under DA therefore tests the audit output, not a new primary selector. Equal Top-1 values across those variants should not be read as proof that checker design is unimportant under every finalization policy.

Top-1 and Exact Match are reported separately. A policy may improve the predicted label set while selecting a worse primary, or it may raise Exact Match by suppressing secondary labels. Neither pattern is described as an overall improvement without examining the output contract.

\section{Selection-Repair Tests}

RQ3 tests four repair families. Each targets a different explanation for the gap between a useful candidate list and the final primary.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Selection-repair families.}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}p{2.8cm}p{4.0cm}X@{}}
\toprule
Method & Hypothesis & Test\\
\midrule
Pairwise comparison & The error is local confusion between close candidates & Compare nearby candidates using their recorded evidence; use frozen-output replay to isolate the final comparison step\\

\end{tabularx}

Pairwise results are reported in two forms. An independent run shows the complete pairwise-enabled system, but it may also include generation variation. A replay on frozen DA outputs changes only the downstream comparison and therefore gives the cleaner causal test.

The debate protocol uses two information views rather than one advocate per diagnosis. Advocate-D receives the diagnostician ranking, rationale, and retrieved-case summary. Advocate-C receives the criterion-evidence table. If their leading candidates differ, a judge resolves the case after the fixed number of rounds.

The deterministic fusion sweep is performed only on development data. The selected E3 rule is frozen before internal testing. This prevents test-set rule selection. Self-consistency and CISC use stochastic diagnostician samples, whereas the reference run is greedy. Because the checker, logic engine, and finalization are not rerun, these experiments test the stability of diagnostician commitment rather than uncertainty in the whole pipeline.

For all repairs, parent-projected any-gold Top-1 is the primary outcome. Top-3, Exact Match, macro-F1, and weighted-F1 are secondary. Flip counts are reported for replay methods so that offsetting correct-to-wrong and wrong-to-correct changes remain visible.

\section{Exploratory and Robustness Analyses}

Exploratory probes test other possible explanations but do not redefine the selected system. A pipeline-feature ranker uses rank and checker features. BGE-M3 similarity methods compare cases or diagnosis descriptions. A TF--IDF logistic-regression model learns directly from transcript words. These methods use different inputs and are not treated as one common learned-selector experiment.

Other probes examine explicit evidence extraction, a second pass guided by unresolved criteria, checker fine-tuning, and confidence calibration. A development-only gain or a gain that fails under external transfer is reported as exploratory evidence, not as a repaired selection bottleneck. Detailed implementations and artifacts are retained in the repository.

RQ4 uses several distinct tests:

\begin{itemize}
\item Qwen3 models at 4B, 8B, 14B, and 32B test size sensitivity within one model family.
\item Retrieval and similarity studies separate prompt-side retrieval from post-hoc reranking.
\item MDD-5k tests transfer to another synthetic corpus.
\item The broader diagnostic profile tests whether adding output classes improves scope coverage.
\item Confusion pairs and worked cases describe where errors are concentrated.
\end{itemize}

These analyses answer different questions. Model-size stability does not prove cross-family robustness. Scope expansion shows whether a diagnosis can be represented, not whether the model has learned when to select it. Error analysis explains observed failures but is not an independent robustness test.

\section{Reporting and Freeze Discipline}
\label{sec:freeze-discipline}

Paired comparisons use exact McNemar tests under a common correctness definition. Wilson intervals describe individual proportions. Multiplicity correction is used only within declared comparison families. Non-significant differences are not called equivalent.

The internal test case IDs are excluded from the retrieval index. Model and rule selection use development data. Any confirmatory rule or configuration is frozen before it is scored on the internal split. MDD-5k labels are not used to tune the in-domain system. Post-freeze work is labeled as exploratory or robustness evidence.

Under this design, RQ1 is answered by the stage-wise analysis, RQ2 by DA versus NtS and the checker audit, RQ3 by the frozen repair tests, and RQ4 by model-size, retrieval, external-transfer, scope, and error-boundary analyses. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} reports the internal results under this hierarchy.

\section{Main Error Patterns}

The largest confusion is F41$\rightarrow$F32 (178 cases), compared with only 24 in the reverse direction. Several other errors also end at F32, including F39, \emph{Others}, F51, and F98 cases. This suggests a frequent depression-label attractor when symptoms overlap or the transcript does not clearly establish duration, onset, autonomic symptoms, or the main source of impairment. The counts describe model--reference disagreement in a synthetic corpus; they do not adjudicate which label is clinically correct in every case.

Missing information is also common in the audit trace. An \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} state may be appropriate when a transcript never establishes prior episodes, substance use, medical causes, or whether a second syndrome is independent. Changing thresholds can retain or reject more candidates, but it cannot create a missing fact. This supports testing targeted evidence acquisition rather than relying only on new weights or rules.

The 284 candidate-present, wrong-commitment cases are the clearest selection errors: a gold parent is already in Top-3, but another diagnosis is chosen. Criterion verification offers related evidence, but not on the same denominator: it retains a gold parent in 857 of 915 eligible cases and confirms a median of six entries. The audit therefore often preserves the answer without resolving primacy.

Two further patterns concern the output contract. NtS improves Exact Match by emitting one label, but it lowers Top-1 and cannot detect comorbidity. Likewise, broader scope makes more diagnoses legal to emit, yet the retargeting sample recovers none of the 58 newly available gold parents. Output conservatism, scope, and primary selection must therefore be evaluated separately.

\section{Two Uses of the Audit Trace}

In offline research, reference labels allow the analyst to ask whether the diagnosis was in scope, in Top-3, retained by verification, and finally selected. The same trace also shows how many alternatives were confirmed and which facts remained unresolved. This supports failure localization, but it uses the reference label and is unavailable during live inference.

For a new case without a reference label, a clinician can instead review the evidence for the final primary, the leading alternatives, unresolved discriminative criteria, useful follow-up questions, and the source of finalization. This can expose ambiguity and unsupported claims, but it cannot determine the correct diagnosis automatically.

\section{Summary}

The main errors arise from four distinct sources: missing candidates, wrong commitment among available candidates, diagnoses outside the active scope, and missing distinguishing information. The audit trace separates these cases, but clinical adjudication still requires evidence beyond the model output.


\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Main Interpretation}

The main finding is that one accuracy score hides three different questions: whether the reference diagnosis appears in the ranked shortlist, whether the criterion layer finds it compatible with the transcript, and whether the system selects it as the primary diagnosis. On the internal test set, HiED--DA reaches 0.802 Top-3 and 0.518 Top-1. The reference diagnosis is also logic-confirmed in 857 of 915 checker-eligible cases, but the median confirmed set contains six active entries. These values use different denominators and do not form a strict sequence. Together, they show that finding a plausible diagnosis is easier than choosing one final diagnosis.

The external MDD-5k results support the same point more narrowly. In the matched 925-case run, Top-3 is 0.8508 and Top-1 is 0.5708. This shows that the ranking-to-selection gap remains under a different synthetic data distribution. However, the external analysis does not include the same verification measure and therefore does not reproduce the full internal analysis.

\section{Compatibility Is Not the Same as Primary Diagnosis}

The criterion checker evaluates each diagnosis separately. It asks whether the transcript supports that diagnosis, not whether it is better than the other supported diagnoses. This distinction matters because common symptoms such as poor sleep, fatigue, low mood, worry, avoidance, and reduced concentration can support several disorders. Choosing a primary diagnosis often depends on comparative facts such as onset, duration, previous episodes, exclusionary causes, and whether one disorder explains the other symptoms.

This explains why broad criterion confirmation does not lead directly to a reliable final choice. It also explains the Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select result. Replacing the diagnostician's choice with the current logic-based selector lowers Top-1 from 0.518 to 0.490. Nominate-then-Select has higher Exact Match, but it outputs no secondary diagnosis and therefore benefits from the large number of single-label cases. The current criterion track is useful as a high-recall audit, but it is not a better primary selector.

The stage-wise analysis still changes how errors are understood. Missing the reference diagnosis in the shortlist points to candidate generation or retrieval. Finding it in the shortlist but not in the confirmed set points to evidence interpretation or missing information. Finding it in both views but selecting another diagnosis points more directly to comparative selection. These cases require different remedies and cannot be separated by endpoint accuracy alone.

\section{What the Repair Studies Show}

The tested repair methods cover several simple explanations for the gap. Pairwise reasoning and debate add more comparison. Deterministic fusion applies fixed rules to existing signals. Self-consistency tests whether greedy decoding hides a stable majority choice. Learned and similarity-based selectors test whether a fitted ranking rule can reorder the candidates.

None of these methods gives a clear and reproducible improvement over Direct-Answer under the tested protocols. Pairwise reasoning does not change correctness on MDD-5k, the frozen fusion rule reduces performance, the self-consistency gains are small and not statistically significant, and the context-extended debate run performs worse. The experiments use different procedures, so they are not one matched component ablation. Their shared result is more limited: the remaining gap is not fixed by a simple threshold, majority vote, local override, or extra reasoning round.

This is not evidence that the broader method families cannot work. It shows only that the tested versions do not combine the available candidate and criterion signals well enough. Future selectors may need to compare candidates through explicit temporal relations, exclusion conflicts, and evidence that favors one diagnosis over another.

\section{Model Error or Missing Information}

The present experiments cannot fully separate two causes of error. First, the transcript may contain enough information, but the model may use it poorly. Second, the transcript may omit the information needed for a unique primary diagnosis. The 284 cases in which the reference diagnosis is in the Top-3 but is not selected show that useful candidate generation has already occurred in many errors. Broad confirmed sets and frequent \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} states also suggest that some cases lack clear discriminating facts, but they do not prove that missing information caused each error.

A transcript-only human study is needed to separate these causes. Psychiatrists should receive the same transcript and label space as the model. If clinicians also disagree or judge the evidence insufficient, the task or reference label may be the main limit. If clinicians consistently resolve cases that HiED misses, the main problem is more likely the method.

\section{Implications for System Design and Use}

HiED's audit trace is useful because it records the ranked candidates, criterion states, supporting text, confirmed diagnoses, and finalization source. This makes disagreements easier to inspect. It does not reveal the model's hidden reasoning or prove that the checker is clinically correct. The audit supports review; it does not create trust by itself.

The results also suggest that some cases require new evidence rather than another ranking rule. When several diagnoses remain plausible because duration, course, substance use, exclusion, or episode history is missing, the system should identify the missing fact, ask a focused follow-up question, or abstain. Comparative selection is useful when the evidence is sufficient; evidence collection is needed when it is not.

Finally, the Taiwanese setting is the intended use context, not the population validated here. All quantitative evidence comes from synthetic Chinese corpora. The thesis does not test real Taiwanese patients, local language use, hospital workflow, clinician behavior, or patient outcomes. At this stage, HiED is best viewed as a research system and a prototype for clinician review, not an autonomous diagnostic tool.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

\section{Construct and Measurement Limits}

The evaluation target is agreement with corpus-assigned ICD-10 labels after parent-level projection. It is not an independent judgment of the patient's true diagnosis. Parent projection makes two corpora easier to compare but removes subtype detail. The any-gold Top-1 rule is also less strict than matching only the first listed diagnosis. Top-1, Top-3, Exact Match, macro-F1, and weighted-F1 therefore measure different parts of benchmark performance; none alone measures clinical correctness or safety.

The selection bottleneck is an observed difference among output views, not a proven causal process inside the model. Under Direct-Answer, the primary diagnosis is chosen before the checker creates the audit trace. Top-1 and Top-3 use all 1,000 internal cases, whereas Gold-in-confirmed uses 915 checker-eligible cases. These measures help locate errors but should not be read as one nested funnel. The gold-assisted recovery analysis is likewise an upper bound, not a deployable selector.

Criterion states are produced by an LLM checker rather than clinicians. The checker may miss negation, duration, or context, and the deterministic logic engine will carry those errors forward. The audit trace is inspectable and reproducible, but it is not a faithful record of hidden reasoning and has not been clinically validated at the criterion level.

\section{Internal and Statistical Limits}

The official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable, so the in-domain headline uses a pre-registered 1,000-case carve from the original training source. A history scan of earlier result files and retrieval exclusion reduce direct exposure, but the split still comes from the same source distribution as the development data. Foundation-model exposure to the public corpus also cannot be ruled out.

Not every comparison isolates one component. Single versus Single+RAG mainly tests retrieval. Single+RAG versus HiED--DA changes several parts of the system and measures a package effect. Direct-Answer versus Nominate-then-Select is more tightly matched, while pairwise, debate, fusion, self-consistency, and learned selectors use different execution procedures. Their results support conclusions about the tested implementations, not a general ranking of method families.

Several analyses are exploratory or use small samples. Rare classes have wide uncertainty, and a non-significant difference does not prove that two methods are equivalent. Multiple repair attempts also increase the chance of isolated favorable results. The thesis reduces this risk through frozen protocols, paired tests, and reporting negative results, but exploratory findings remain exploratory.

The canonical system also depends on one model family, prompt set, quantized checkpoint, serving stack, context window, and decoding setup. The Qwen3 size analysis does not establish that the same findings hold for unrelated models or runtimes.

\section{External and Clinical Limits}

Both quantitative corpora are synthetic. They support controlled and reproducible experiments, but may contain generator-specific language, simplified ambiguity, limited comorbidity, and less noise than real practice. MDD-5k provides a useful distribution shift, but it is still synthetic and does not provide the same verification analysis as the internal set.

The intended setting is Taiwanese outpatient psychiatry, yet the study does not evaluate real Taiwanese patients, Traditional-Chinese clinical speech, mixed Mandarin--Taiwanese language, local coding practice, or hospital workflow. The fixed diagnostic scope also maps unsupported diagnoses to \texttt{Others}. Expanding the list of legal outputs does not by itself teach the model when to use the new labels, as shown by the retargeting experiment.

No result in this thesis establishes clinical effectiveness, clinician equivalence, treatment benefit, or safe autonomous use. There is no tested clinical abstention policy, calibrated confidence, subgroup fairness analysis, workflow study, or measurement of automation bias. Real-patient validation must therefore precede any deployment claim.

\section{Reproducibility and Governance Limits}

The repository records split fingerprints, configurations, metric definitions, run health, and frozen result artifacts. Exact replay still requires the external datasets, the same model weights and serving environment, and reconstruction of the retrieval index. Reproducing a number is also not the same as validating the clinical meaning of the metric.

Future work with patient data will require institutional approval, privacy controls, access logging, monitoring, incident procedures, and periodic revalidation. Until these requirements and the clinical studies above are completed, HiED should remain a research and review prototype.

\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Conclusions}

This thesis introduced HiED, an evidence-grounded multi-agent system that separates ranked candidate generation, criterion-based audit, and final primary selection. The main result is that these views can disagree. On the internal test set, the reference diagnosis appears in the Top-3 far more often than it is selected as primary, and the criterion layer retains many diagnoses at once. The central problem is therefore not only whether the system can name the correct diagnosis, but whether it can choose it over other plausible diagnoses.

The tested logic-based selector does not improve Top-1 over Direct-Answer, and the repair methods do not provide a clear and reproducible gain. External synthetic evaluation shows that the ranking-to-selection gap remains under distribution shift, but it does not establish the full internal verification pattern or clinical validity. HiED's strongest contribution is an auditable system and an evaluation method that makes these differences visible.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] The largest directly measured gap is between candidate coverage and final selection. In 284 of 1,000 internal cases, a reference diagnosis appears in the Top-3 but is not selected as primary. The broad confirmed sets show that criterion compatibility alone does not identify one best diagnosis.

\item[RQ2] Nominate-then-Select does not improve primary selection. Its Top-1 is lower than Direct-Answer, while its higher Exact Match partly comes from outputting only one label. The current criterion layer is better used as an audit than as the final selector.

\item[RQ3] Pairwise reasoning, debate, fixed fusion, self-consistency, confidence weighting, and learned or similarity-based selection do not give a clear and repeatable improvement under the tested protocols. This conclusion applies to these implementations, not to every possible method in those families.

\item[RQ4] The ranking-to-selection gap remains visible across the tested Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k. Performance also changes with corpus, label scope, and the number of diagnosis classes. These results do not establish performance on real Taiwanese clinical transcripts or on unrelated model families.
\end{description}

\section{Priority Future Work}

Three steps should come before deployment.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Validate the target and the audit.} Psychiatrists should review the same transcripts and label space as the model, record acceptable alternatives and missing facts, and judge whether one primary diagnosis is supported. A separate sample should be annotated for criterion status and evidence spans. These studies will show whether errors mainly come from the model, the transcript, the reference label, or the checker.

    \item \textbf{Improve selection and evidence collection.} Future selectors should compare diagnoses directly using onset, duration, exclusions, episode history, and symptoms left unexplained by the leading diagnosis. When the transcript lacks a decisive fact, the system should ask a focused question or abstain rather than force a precise answer. Conversational diagnostic systems show that LLMs can collect history~\citep{tu2025conversational}; the next step is to ask the question that best separates the remaining candidates.

    \item \textbf{Run pre-registered Taiwanese clinical studies.} A frozen system should first be tested on the full eligible cohort of de-identified outpatient transcripts, with the recorded diagnosis, transcript-only clinician review, and fuller clinical adjudication reported separately. Only after target validity, audit accuracy, and patient-data performance are established should studies examine workflow value, fairness, automation bias, privacy, monitoring, and broader diagnostic scope.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Final Conclusion}

A psychiatric LLM can include the reference diagnosis among its leading candidates and still choose another diagnosis as primary. Criterion checking makes this problem easier to inspect, but broad compatibility does not solve the final comparison. HiED provides an auditable architecture and an evaluation framework that separates these stages. It is not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic system.
