初診精神科晤談逐字稿中的鑑別診斷，本質上是一種在證據不完整條件下進行的比較式決策。多個疾病診斷可能同時與同一份逐字稿相容，但最終通常只能有一個被判定為主診斷。然而，用以區分病程、發病時序、排除病因與共病關係等關鍵鑑別資訊，未必會在單次初診晤談中被完整引出。大型語言模型雖然能流暢地閱讀並理解這類逐字稿，近期也已有大量研究嘗試將其應用於精神科診斷任務，但語言理解的流暢性並不等同於可靠的鑑別診斷能力。

既有評測呈現出一致的落差：模型在粗粒度分類任務上往往表現良好，但一旦進入細粒度的疾病層級鑑別診斷，準確率便會大幅下降。這個落差促使本研究提出一套可稽核的系統架構，用以定位基準標籤分歧出現於哪一類系統輸出。模型可能生成一個合理且受到診斷準則支持的候選疾病，卻仍未將其選為與基準標籤一致的主診斷。候選診斷生成、依準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇三者之間的差異，通常會被單一準確率指標所掩蓋，使研究者難以判斷分歧記錄於哪個環節。

為了使這些不同的分歧位置能夠被分別觀察，並檢驗主診斷選擇是否構成主要瓶頸，本研究提出 HiED——一套面向中文精神科初診對話逐字稿的鑑別診斷框架。HiED 將候選診斷偵測、ICD-10 逐準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇保留為可分別分析的輸出，而非將整個流程簡化為單一分類事件。同時，系統會在每次預測時輸出逐準則的稽核軌跡，使研究者能夠檢視診斷依據並定位分歧位置。HiED 並非已完成臨床驗證的自主診斷工具，而是一項用於分析逐字稿式精神科診斷分歧的研究工具。

因此，本論文有兩項主要貢獻：其一，提出一套可稽核的分歧定位框架，使候選診斷生成、準則驗證與主診斷選擇三個診斷環節得以分別觀察與分析；其二，提供可重現的證據，指出在本研究評估的 HiED 設定與合成逐字稿基準評測中，主診斷選擇構成主要的已記錄瓶頸。換言之，在本研究之合成逐字稿設定下，系統的主要限制不僅在於能否生成合理的候選診斷，而更在於能否在多個皆具一定準則支持的候選之間，進行有效比較並判定最適合作為主診斷者。最後需說明，本文題目所稱之臺灣脈絡，指涉系統之設計動機與預期部署場域（繁體中文互動、本地端推論、面向臨床人員之稽核），而非已完成之臺灣真實病人臨床驗證；本文現階段的量化結論均來自合成中文逐字稿語料。臺灣真實病人資料之臨床驗證目前於長庚紀念醫院進行中，其結果不納入本論文現階段的量化結論。

\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial-consultation psychiatric transcript is a comparative decision under incomplete evidence. Multiple disorders may be compatible with the same transcript, yet only one can typically be judged the primary diagnosis. Key discriminating information---illness course, onset timing, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity relationships---may not be fully elicited within a single initial consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, and recent research has applied them to psychiatric diagnostic tasks, but linguistic fluency does not equate to reliable differential diagnostic ability.

Existing evaluations show a consistent gap: models perform well on coarse-grained classification, but accuracy drops sharply on fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis. This gap motivates an auditable architecture that locates where benchmark disagreement appears among the recorded system outputs. A model may identify a benchmark gold diagnosis as a plausible, criterion-compatible candidate but still select a different primary diagnosis. The distinction among candidate-diagnosis generation, criterion-based verification, and primary-diagnosis selection is usually obscured by a single accuracy metric.

To make these disagreement locations separately observable and to examine whether primary-diagnosis selection is a major bottleneck, we propose HiED, a differential-diagnosis framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts. HiED records candidate detection, ICD-10 criterion verification, primary selection, and criterion-level evidence as separately analyzable views rather than reducing diagnosis to one classification event. It is a research instrument for analyzing transcript-based diagnostic disagreement, not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic tool. The title's Taiwanese context denotes the intended deployment setting; ongoing real-patient validation at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital (CGMH) is outside the present quantitative conclusions.

This thesis makes two main contributions: an auditable architecture with recorded diagnostic artifacts, and reproducible evidence of a primary-selection bottleneck in the evaluated HiED configurations under synthetic transcript-only benchmarks. In these settings, the observed limitation lies not only in generating plausible candidates but also in comparing criterion-compatible alternatives and selecting one as the committed primary diagnosis.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric outpatient care in Taiwan is widely accessible, but this access also creates a heavy clinical workload. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit different levels of medical facilities without referral, and large medical centers may receive more than ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in one day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A first psychiatric visit must cover history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and an initial diagnostic judgment within limited time. The resulting note or transcript often contains incomplete information about onset, duration, course, medical or substance-related causes, and previous treatment.

The evidence may also be indirect. In Chinese-speaking settings, patients often describe distress through insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, or gastrointestinal symptoms before using explicit mood or anxiety terms~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. Taiwanese consultations may also mix Mandarin, Taiwanese expressions, and local clinical wording. The same condition can therefore be described in several ways within one visit.

Differential diagnosis remains difficult because common psychiatric disorders share many symptoms. Depression, anxiety, stress-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, somatic symptom disorders, and sleep disorders may all involve insomnia, fatigue, poor concentration, and impaired daily function. In diagnostic assessment, these symptoms are interpreted through disorder-specific criteria. These criteria do not treat every symptom independently or equally: depending on the disorder, a diagnosis may require particular core symptoms, a minimum number of additional symptoms, a specified duration, functional impairment, and the absence of exclusionary causes~\citep{who2019icd10}. Diagnostic assessment therefore involves matching the available evidence to a disorder-specific combination of requirements, rather than assigning a diagnosis from the presence of one symptom alone. Even when diagnostic criteria are applied, several disorders may remain compatible with the same transcript. Evidence for both depression and anxiety, for example, may reflect comorbidity or overlapping symptoms from one primary condition~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}. Even structured diagnostic studies report limited agreement for some common disorders~\citep{regier2013dsm5}.

These features make first-visit psychiatric diagnosis a problem of comparative bookkeeping under incomplete evidence. The clinician must document a best-supported working diagnosis, keep plausible alternatives visible, and identify which missing information should be collected at follow-up. Time pressure can increase the risk of anchoring or premature closure when several disorders share the same symptoms~\citep{croskerry2003cognitive}.
\section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

Large language models offer capabilities that are well aligned with psychiatric transcript analysis: they can process free-form dialogue, organize symptom evidence, retrieve relevant examples, and produce structured outputs. Medical multi-agent systems can further divide the diagnostic task among specialized roles and combine their outputs~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. However, these capabilities do not by themselves ensure reliable differential diagnosis from a fixed transcript.

Benchmark disagreement can arise at different points in the diagnostic process. A benchmark gold diagnosis may never enter the candidate set, may be absent from the criterion-compatible set, or may remain available but not be selected as the committed primary diagnosis. These disagreement patterns call for different system improvements, yet they are often summarized by a single final-label accuracy measure.

This creates the central technical problem studied in this thesis: existing evaluation often does not reveal whether disagreement arises during candidate generation, criterion-based verification, or primary-diagnosis selection. Without separately observable outputs for these stages, it is difficult to determine whether better retrieval, evidence checking, or comparative selection is needed. The next section introduces HiED as a framework designed to make these distinctions observable.

\section{Overview of HiED and Study Scope}

To make disagreements in candidate generation, criterion verification, and primary selection separately observable, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid multi-agent framework for differential diagnosis using Chinese-language transcripts of initial psychiatric consultations. The system is motivated by the Taiwanese outpatient setting. However, the completed quantitative analyses reported in this thesis use synthetic Chinese dialogue datasets. The LingxiDiag psychiatrist pilot and CGMH real-world validation are ongoing complementary studies and are not included in the present quantitative conclusions.

HiED combines two complementary diagnostic processes. An LLM-based Diagnostician produces a ranked differential diagnosis and an emitted primary diagnosis, while diagnosis-specific Criterion Checkers evaluate the same transcript against operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria. The deterministic Compatibility Auditor aggregates the criterion states produced by the Criterion Checkers into a criterion-compatible set, and a finalization policy converts the recorded outputs into a committed diagnosis. The ranked candidates, criterion states, compatibility decisions, and committed diagnosis are stored separately.

This separation supports stage-wise analysis of candidate generation, criterion verification, and primary selection. It allows the study to distinguish a diagnosis that was not proposed, one that was not included in the criterion-compatible set, and one that remained available but was not selected as the committed primary diagnosis.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions.

First, it presents an auditable hybrid architecture that combines LLM-based diagnosis, structured criterion-level checking against operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria, and deterministic compatibility rules. The system records criterion states, checker-generated supporting text, and compatibility-rule outcomes instead of relying only on a free-form rationale. These artifacts support inspection and deterministic replay without being treated as hidden reasoning or clinical ground truth~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}.

Second, it presents a recorded-output evaluation framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection, empirically identifies the primary-selection bottleneck, and tests whether criterion-based finalization and several primary-selection methods close the observed gap under declared comparison contracts.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

Psychiatric LLM studies do not evaluate one uniform diagnostic task. The literature includes psychiatric knowledge or licensing-examination questions, diagnosis from clinical vignettes, focused assessment of particular disorders, automated depression detection, fixed-record diagnosis, and interactive consultation~\citep{qiu2023largeai,li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,li2025addreview,mcduff2025amie}. Results across these settings are not directly interchangeable because the available evidence, diagnostic scope, required output, and reference standard differ.

This distinction is especially important when moving from screening or broad category classification to fine-grained differential diagnosis. Detecting prominent depression or anxiety signals does not require the same comparison as selecting one primary diagnosis among disorders with overlapping symptoms. Disorder-level diagnosis may additionally depend on duration, temporal sequence, illness course, exclusion conditions, and comorbidity. LingxiDiagBench makes this coarse-to-fine gap explicit: performance decreases substantially from binary depression--anxiety classification to comorbidity recognition and twelve-way diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. MDD-5k provides a second synthetic Chinese diagnostic-dialogue resource generated from anonymized patient cases~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. Both resources support reproducible disorder-level experiments, but neither substitutes for validation on naturally occurring clinical conversations.

The evidence setting creates a further distinction. MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, DSM5AgentFlow, and conversational diagnostic AI study inquiry or interactive workflows that can seek additional information during assessment~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow,tu2025conversational}. MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels instead emphasize diagnosis from information already available in records or transcripts~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. Interactive systems combine evidence acquisition with diagnostic reasoning, whereas fixed-input systems must interpret the same potentially incomplete evidence. A fixed-input design therefore isolates how a model uses the available transcript without attributing gains to additional questioning.

Existing evaluations provide limited visibility into whether benchmark disagreement arises at candidate generation, criterion compatibility, or final commitment, motivating evaluations that distinguish these decisions rather than relying only on final-label accuracy.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning}

Retrieval augmentation can serve two different roles in diagnostic systems. Knowledge retrieval supplies external documents, guidelines, or other factual resources to supplement information encoded in model parameters~\citep{lewis2020rag}. Demonstration retrieval instead selects labeled examples or similar cases as prompt context for in-context learning~\citep{liu2022incontext}. In this thesis, retrieval refers to labeled training examples used as prompt-side demonstrations, not to diagnostic guidelines or external evidence about the current patient.

Semantically similar demonstrations can expose corpus-specific expressions, the expected input--output format, examples of the diagnostic label space, and recurring symptom--diagnosis associations. In-context-learning performance, however, depends strongly on which examples are selected~\citep{liu2022incontext}. Retrieved cases describe other patients or synthetic cases; they are demonstrations rather than evidence about the current patient and should not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

The benefit of similarity retrieval is therefore not automatic. The retrieved prompt reflects the label distribution and wording patterns of the indexed corpus. Common labels may appear repeatedly, lexical overlap may encourage corpus-specific shortcuts, and a misleading or weakly related neighbor may interfere with interpretation of the current transcript. Retrieval should consequently be treated as an empirical configuration rather than an inherently beneficial component.

Label-aware balancing may increase the diagnostic diversity of the demonstrations and reduce repeated exposure to frequent classes. It does not, however, change label balance alone. A balanced policy may also alter the number, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, total token length, and context density of the examples. Comparisons with ordinary global similarity retrieval therefore estimate differences between complete retrieval configurations rather than the isolated causal effect of balancing.

Prior work establishes that demonstration choice can affect in-context learning, but it remains unclear how retrieval policies behave in fine-grained psychiatric diagnosis. These findings indicate that retrieval policies should be evaluated under matched conditions, particularly when label imbalance and prompt composition may affect the retrieved demonstrations.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

Medical multi-agent systems use several forms of orchestration. Role-specialized decomposition assigns distinct functions such as diagnosis, evidence extraction, critique, or final decision-making. Deliberative systems combine proposals through consultation, debate, voting, or consensus, while verification-oriented systems separate an initial answer from later checking or revision. MedAgents uses multi-round collaboration among role-based medical experts, and MDAgents adapts the collaboration structure to estimated task complexity~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. Multiagent debate provides a related general mechanism for exchanging and revising competing answers~\citep{du2024debate}.

These arrangements offer several possible advantages. Task decomposition can focus each role on a narrower subproblem, redundant judgments may expose omissions, cross-checking can compare a proposed diagnosis with supporting evidence or criteria, and structured aggregation can preserve intermediate outputs before a final decision. These are design opportunities rather than guarantees: an increase in the number of agents or interaction rounds does not by itself establish better diagnostic accuracy.

Multi-agent systems also introduce characteristic failure modes. Agents that share a backbone model, similar prompts, and the same evidence may produce correlated rather than independent errors. An incorrect upstream candidate or evidence statement can propagate into later critique and selection. Free-form interaction may change several parts of the reasoning process at once, and a final aggregator may still choose an incorrect answer even when individual agents have surfaced useful alternatives.

These dependencies create an attribution problem. When candidate generation, evidence representation, inter-agent discussion, and aggregation all differ between systems, a change in final accuracy is a package-level effect rather than evidence that one component caused the difference. Comparisons with a Single model may also differ in prompt structure, context, inference calls, and compute. Final-answer evaluation alone therefore provides limited information about where a multi-agent configuration helped or failed.

These limitations call for controlled evaluations in which candidate generation, verification, and final aggregation are recorded separately, allowing performance changes to be attributed to specific functions rather than to the multi-agent system as a whole.

\section{Auditability and Criterion Grounding}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

A fluent explanation is not proof of faithful reasoning. Model-generated chains can sound coherent while not reflecting the factors that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. In this thesis, \emph{explainability} refers to a human-facing account of a decision, \emph{interpretability} to how directly a person can understand the decision mechanism, \emph{faithfulness} to whether a stated account reflects the factors that produced the output, and \emph{auditability} to the availability of an external record that can be inspected and replayed. These properties require separate evaluation in high-stakes healthcare settings~\citep{rudin2019stop,huang2024xiai}.

Diagnostic-chain and symptom-based methods can expose intermediate hypotheses, confidence values, or clinically motivated features~\citep{chen2025cod,lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. Psychiatric systems may further ground outputs in diagnostic guidelines, structured interviews, symptom-to-criterion mappings, and supporting or contradictory evidence~\citep{li2026mind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. Constraint-logic approaches make the rule layer more explicit by translating diagnostic criteria into inspectable programs~\citep{kim2025clp}. Together, these approaches make parts of the diagnostic process more accessible to external review.

Criterion grounding nevertheless depends on the quality of the operationalized criteria and the mapping from transcript text to criterion states. A generated evidence statement may be unsupported, a criterion may be represented incorrectly, and an inspectable rule may still implement an inappropriate clinical assumption. The need for expert inspection of model-generated diagnostic rules illustrates this limitation~\citep{kim2025clp}. Criteria-grounded outputs are therefore reviewable model artifacts rather than clinician-validated evidence or clinical ground truth.

An auditable diagnostic system should retain an external record linking source evidence, criterion states, rule results, intermediate diagnostic outputs, and the final decision. Such a record does not reveal hidden model reasoning. A structured criterion layer may be used either to audit a model-generated diagnosis or to participate in final diagnosis selection, and these roles should be evaluated separately because successful evidence recording or compatibility assessment does not imply successful primary selection.

The remaining gap concerns the joint evaluation of these recorded outputs rather than their mere availability.

\section{Primary-Diagnosis Selection}

Criterion compatibility and primary-diagnosis selection answer different questions. Criterion compatibility asks whether each diagnosis is individually consistent with the available evidence under a defined rule. Because psychiatric disorders can share symptoms and more than one diagnosis may remain compatible, this process does not by itself determine whether a diagnosis is primary, comorbid, or a differential alternative. Selecting one committed primary therefore requires comparison among the remaining diagnoses rather than independent criterion satisfaction alone.

Structured diagnostic knowledge offers mechanisms for organizing the evidence used in this comparison. MedKGI combines a medical knowledge graph, information-guided inquiry, and a structured dialogue state to obtain discriminative information during iterative diagnosis~\citep{wang2025medkgi}. DKEC constructs heterogeneous knowledge graphs from external medical sources and incorporates their relations into multi-label diagnosis prediction~\citep{ge2024dkec}. These systems show how structured representations can support evidence acquisition or label prediction, but their evaluated objectives differ from selecting one primary diagnosis among several criterion-compatible psychiatric alternatives in a fixed transcript.

A comparative selector may need to represent symptom--diagnosis relations together with temporal order, duration, illness course, exclusionary causes, syndrome dominance, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity. The reviewed structured methods may organize evidence or identify plausible labels, but they do not directly evaluate whether a criterion-compatible diagnosis remains available yet is not selected when one primary diagnosis is committed. This gap motivates evaluation that separates Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion in the criterion-compatible set, and committed-primary agreement.

However, none of the reviewed evaluations jointly measures Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion in a criterion-compatible set, and committed-primary agreement on the same fixed cases. These differences motivate the stage-wise evaluation questions defined below.

Taken together, the reviewed literature leaves five unresolved questions that are directly relevant to this thesis. First, heterogeneous task definitions and complete-system differences complicate comparisons among conventional classifiers, single LLMs, and multi-stage systems under one split, label mapping, and primary evaluation metric. Second, final-label accuracy does not show whether a benchmark gold label was absent from the ranked candidates, was absent from the criterion-compatible set, or remained available but was not selected as the committed primary. Third, the effect of demonstration retrieval depends on example selection, requiring matched comparison of global similarity and label-aware balanced policies across Single and multi-stage architectures. Fourth, multi-agent and criterion-based interventions often change several stages or inference conditions at once, limiting attribution of final-performance differences to primary selection. Fifth, the persistence of the main findings across model sizes, a second synthetic corpus, changes in diagnostic scope, and cross-corpus transfer, together with the remaining error patterns, requires separate boundary analysis. HiED examines these questions by recording candidate ranking, criterion verification, compatibility, and final selection as separately observable outputs under declared comparison contracts.

This thesis therefore uses five research questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=1.5cm,labelwidth=1.1cm,itemsep=0pt,parsep=0pt,topsep=0pt,partopsep=0pt]
\item[RQ1] How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation metric?
\item[RQ2] How often does a benchmark gold label appear among the ranked candidates or in the criterion-compatible set without being selected as the committed primary diagnosis?
\item[RQ3] How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?
\item[RQ4] Under the evaluated settings, do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve committed Top-1 agreement over Direct-Answer?
\item[RQ5] To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and which error patterns characterize the remaining disagreements?
\end{description}
These RQs define computational analyses on synthetic benchmark data. RQ3 first compares retrieval configurations on the public-validation split and freezes the selected settings, whereas RQ1 evaluates the selected configurations on the fixed internal held-out set. RQ5 comprises complementary model-scale, external synthetic, diagnostic-scope, cross-corpus transfer, and error-pattern analyses rather than a single statistical hypothesis.

\section{Input and Diagnostic Scope}
\label{sec:input-diagnostic-scope}

HiED processes one Chinese psychiatric consultation transcript at a time. The input is an ordered sequence of turns, each marked as \texttt{Clinician} or \texttt{Patient}; speaker labels and turn order are preserved in the prompt. Transcripts longer than the available prompt budget are deterministically truncated by retaining complete turns from the beginning and end, with an explicit marker for the omitted middle. The system does not summarize or invent missing text. This is a fixed-input study: the system cannot ask the patient a follow-up question during inference.

ICD-10 is the diagnostic coding system used to define and label the diagnostic categories in this study~\citep{who2019icd10}. HiED does not consider the full ICD-10 diagnostic space. Instead, it operates within a predefined diagnostic profile that remains fixed for all cases in each run. The main diagnostic profile contains fourteen configured diagnostic categories. F41 and F43 include subtype-level categories, so the fourteen categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels for scoring.

The following terms define the diagnostic scope and criterion-checking process used in HiED. A \emph{configured diagnostic category} is an ICD-10 code that the Diagnostician may rank and for which the system provides a corresponding Criterion Checker. The fourteen category-specific checkers together form the \emph{criterion-checker set}.

For each criterion, the checker assigns one of three model-generated evidence states: \texttt{met} when the transcript provides supporting evidence, \texttt{not\_met} when it provides contradictory evidence, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the available information does not support either conclusion. The checker definitions and compatibility rules are study-specific operationalizations informed by ICD-10 descriptions.

\section{Candidate Generation and Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

This section describes the two candidate-generation components shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}: the Similar-Case Retriever and the Diagnostician.

\paragraph{Similar-Case Retriever.}

The Similar-Case Retriever optionally selects training-set cases that are similar to the current transcript and supplies them to the Diagnostician as prompt-side demonstrations, following the retrieval-augmented generation pattern~\citep{lewis2020rag}. The study evaluates three conditions: no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Global Top-5 returns the five most similar eligible cases. Parent-balanced retrieval selects at most one example for each of the eleven configured scoring parents and can therefore return up to eleven examples.

The parent groups used for retrieval are fixed by the configured diagnostic profile rather than generated separately for each patient. Only the selected example within each group depends on the current transcript. Subtypes that share a scoring parent, such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2, also share one retrieval group. Parent-balanced retrieval may reduce repeated examples from frequent labels, but it also changes the number, label composition, ordering, and total token length of the demonstrations.

Retrieved cases are provided only to the Diagnostician. They are not included in the Criterion Checker prompts, do not constitute evidence about the current patient, and do not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

\paragraph{Diagnostician.}

The Diagnostician receives the speaker-labeled transcript, the configured diagnostic profile, and any retrieved examples. It produces three separately recorded outputs: a ranked list of five candidate diagnoses, one emitted primary diagnosis selected from the three highest-ranked candidates, and at most one optional comorbid diagnosis. The ranked list represents the model's differential diagnosis, whereas the primary field records the diagnosis that becomes committed under the Direct-Answer policy.

The ranked candidates, emitted primary diagnosis, and optional comorbidity are stored separately. A diagnosis may therefore remain present among the leading candidates even when another diagnosis is emitted as primary. This distinction allows candidate coverage and primary selection to be evaluated independently.

\section{Criterion Verification and Compatibility Aggregation}

This section describes the two criterion-processing components shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}: the Criterion Checker and the Compatibility Auditor.

\paragraph{Criterion Checker.}

Each of the fourteen configured diagnostic categories has a corresponding Criterion Checker based on the study's operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria~\citep{who2019icd10}. Every checker receives the same speaker-labeled transcript together with the criteria defined for one diagnostic category. Retrieved examples are not included in checker prompts, and the checkers do not communicate with one another.

For each criterion, the checker assigns one of the three criterion states defined in Section~\ref{sec:input-diagnostic-scope}: \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}. It also returns a short supporting-text field, which may be \texttt{null} when no explicit supporting text is identified. Missing information is not negative evidence: an unresolved criterion remains \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} rather than \texttt{not\_met}. All fourteen configured categories are checked for every case, so criterion verification is not limited to diagnoses in the ranked Top-5.

\paragraph{Compatibility Auditor.}

The Compatibility Auditor is a deterministic module that applies predefined rules to the criterion states produced by the Criterion Checkers and determines whether each configured diagnostic category is included in the \emph{criterion-compatible set}. It does not independently interpret the transcript or revise the checker outputs. Depending on the diagnostic category, the rule may require particular core criteria, a minimum number of total criteria, specified criterion groups, or all listed requirements.

The auditor records the applied rule, the relevant criterion counts, and the resulting inclusion status in the compatibility-rule trace. Because the aggregation is deterministic, the same set of checker-generated criterion states always produces the same criterion-compatible set. The complete category-specific rules are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:configured-profile}.

\paragraph{Interpretation boundary.}

Criterion states and supporting text are model-generated and have not been validated as clinician ground truth. The supporting text is retained for inspection but is not guaranteed to be a verbatim or span-aligned extract or a clinically validated evidence annotation. Criterion compatibility is an operational property under the study configuration; it does not establish that a diagnosis is clinically correct, uniquely supported, or the most appropriate primary diagnosis. Several diagnoses may therefore be included in the criterion-compatible set for the same transcript.

\section{Primary Selection and Finalization}

This section describes the finalization policies and the Committed Diagnosis output shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}. Both policies are evaluated from the same recorded Diagnostician and criterion-processing outputs, but they use these outputs differently. Finalization does not introduce an additional LLM call.

\paragraph{Direct-Answer.}

Direct-Answer (DA) preserves the Diagnostician's emitted decision. The emitted primary field becomes the committed primary diagnosis; if this field is missing, the first-ranked candidate is used as a fallback. DA also retains at most one optional comorbid diagnosis when it differs from the primary. Criterion Checker states and Compatibility Auditor outputs remain available as audit artifacts but do not overwrite the DA committed diagnosis.

\paragraph{Nominate-then-Select.}

Nominate-then-Select (NtS) is a deterministic alternative that uses the recorded candidate ranking and criterion-compatible set to choose one committed primary diagnosis. For configured diagnostic category $d$, the criterion-support ratio is
\[
\operatorname{met\_ratio}(d)=\min\!\left(1,\frac{\text{number of \texttt{met} criteria for }d}{\text{required criterion count for }d}\right).
\]
The denominator is the minimum satisfying count defined by the configured operational rule rather than the total number of listed criteria. NtS first selects the highest-ranked criterion-compatible diagnosis within the ranked Top-5. If none of the ranked candidates is criterion-compatible, it selects the remaining criterion-compatible diagnosis with the highest $\operatorname{met\_ratio}$. Ties are broken using the fixed category order listed in Table~\ref{tab:configured-profile-summary}; this order is used only to ensure deterministic output and does not represent clinical priority. If the criterion-compatible set is empty, NtS falls back to the first-ranked diagnosis.

\paragraph{Output contracts.}

DA produces one committed primary diagnosis and may retain one optional comorbid diagnosis. NtS produces one re-selected committed primary diagnosis and does not emit a comorbid diagnosis. Thus, the two policies differ in both their primary-selection rule and final output cardinality.

\section{Configured Output Space and Gold-Label Projection}
\label{sec:gold-label-conventions}

Throughout this thesis, the dataset-provided diagnoses used for scoring are referred to as \emph{benchmark gold labels}. The term \emph{gold} denotes the official benchmark reference and does not imply a uniquely correct clinical diagnosis.

The main system uses fourteen configured ICD-10 diagnostic categories, listed in Table~\ref{tab:configured-profile-summary}. For evaluation, subtype-level categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels. Let $G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x)$ be the raw benchmark gold codes for case $x$, and let $\pi$ map subtype-level codes to their scoring parents, mapping F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2 to F41 and F43.1 and F43.2 to F43. Codes already defined at the scoring-parent level are left unchanged. Let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of eleven configured scoring parents. The in-scope gold-label set is
\[
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\{\pi(c):c\in G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x),\ \pi(c)\in\mathcal{C}\}.
\]
The scored gold-label set is then
\[
G(x)=
\begin{cases}
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x), & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)\neq\varnothing,\\
\{\mathrm{Others}\}, & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\varnothing.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus, when a case contains both an out-of-scope code and F41.1, its scored gold-label set is $\{\mathrm{F41}\}$ rather than $\{\mathrm{Others},\mathrm{F41}\}$. A case is in scope when $G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)$ is nonempty and out of scope otherwise. Because the main system has neither an \emph{Others} output nor a trained unknown detector, it cannot correctly represent an out-of-scope case.

For case $x$, $G(x)$ is the deduplicated gold-label set after parent-level projection; we refer to the individual labels in $G(x)$ as \emph{benchmark gold parents}. LingxiDiag cases may contain more than one gold parent label, and neither dataset provides a psychiatrist-reviewed unique clinical primary diagnosis. The first-listed gold parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses such as the confusion matrix.

\section{Datasets, Case Construction, and Split Roles}

The study uses two synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogue datasets. LingxiDiag-16K, the dataset underlying LingxiDiagBench, is used for system development and internal held-out evaluation, whereas MDD-5k is used for external cross-dataset evaluation.

\subsection{LingxiDiag-16K}

LingxiDiag-16K is the synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogue dataset underlying LingxiDiagBench and was generated for static diagnostic classification and interactive consultation tasks~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. It provides a fixed diagnostic label space and sufficient cases for training, configuration selection, and internal held-out evaluation. The published LingxiDiagBench results also show that fine-grained disorder-level diagnosis is substantially more difficult than broad depression--anxiety classification.

The accessible release used in this study contained 14,000 training-source cases and 1,000 public-validation cases. The official test file was not available in the accessible release used when the experiments were designed.

The 1,000-case internal held-out set was sampled from the 14,000-case training source using the first-listed projected parent label to approximately match the public-validation class distribution. A fixed random seed was used for reproducibility. The remaining 13,000 cases were used to train conventional baselines and build the retrieval index. The first-listed label served only as a stratification variable and was not treated as a clinically validated primary diagnosis. Case-ID checks confirmed that held-out cases were excluded from baseline training, the retrieval index, and all configuration-selection procedures. No additional semantic near-duplicate screening was performed.

The evaluated release contains 925 patient-level cases. \section{Prediction Views and Metrics}
\label{sec:prediction-metrics}

The evaluator keeps four recorded output views separate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item The \emph{primary view}, $p_1(x)$, is the committed primary diagnosis.
\item The \emph{ranked view}, $R_3(x)$, contains the first three distinct parent labels in the method's genuine ranked output. For HiED, it is taken from the recorded Diagnostician ranking and is independent of later DA or NtS finalization.
\item The \emph{compatibility view}, $L(x)$, is the set of scoring parent labels classified as criterion-compatible by the Compatibility Auditor under the configured operational rules.
\item The \emph{multilabel view}, $M(x)$, contains the committed primary diagnosis and any emitted comorbid parent label.
\end{enumerate}

TF--IDF + logistic regression obtains its genuine Top-3 from ranked class scores, whereas HiED obtains it from the Diagnostician ranking. Single does not produce a ranked differential diagnosis and therefore has no genuine ranked Top-3. For compatibility with the LingxiDiagBench aggregate reporting convention, a separate benchmark-defined Top-3 hit rate can nevertheless be computed from Single's available emitted-label list, ordered with the primary diagnosis first and truncated to three labels. This emitted-label measure may include comorbid diagnoses and is not interpreted as ranked candidate coverage or used to define $D_3$.

Because a case may contain more than one gold label, Top-$k$ Accuracy counts a case as correct when at least one gold label appears among the first $k$ predictions. Exact Match, also called subset accuracy, requires the complete predicted label set to equal the complete gold-label set. These are benchmark-agreement measures, not independent measures of the patient's true clinical diagnosis.

For example, suppose that the projected gold-label set contains F41, while the system ranks F32 first and F41.1 second. Because F41.1 is projected to its scoring parent F41, the case is incorrect under committed Top-1 evaluation but correct under Top-3 evaluation.

DA may emit a committed primary diagnosis and one optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits one committed primary diagnosis. Their Top-1 comparison therefore evaluates the committed primary directly, but differences in Exact Match, Macro-F1, or Weighted-F1 also reflect output cardinality and cannot be interpreted as isolated effects of primary re-selection.

\section{Case-Level Indicators for Disagreement Localization}
\label{sec:case-level-indicators}

The measures defined in the preceding section summarize performance across evaluation cases. For case-level disagreement localization, this thesis uses three corresponding binary indicators: Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion in the criterion-compatible set, and committed-primary agreement.

For case $x$,
\[
\begin{aligned}
D_3(x) &= \mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],\\
I(x) &= \mathbb{1}[L(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],\\
S(x) &= \mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)].
\end{aligned}
\]

$D_3(x)$ is the case-level indicator underlying Top-3 Accuracy: it equals one when at least one benchmark gold label appears in the genuine ranked Top-3. $I(x)$ equals one when the criterion-compatible set contains at least one benchmark gold label. $S(x)$ is the case-level indicator underlying committed Top-1 Accuracy: it equals one when the committed primary diagnosis matches at least one benchmark gold label.

Accordingly, Top-3 Accuracy is the mean of $D_3(x)$ across all evaluation cases, and committed Top-1 Accuracy is the mean of $S(x)$. The gold-label inclusion rate is the mean of $I(x)$ over checker-eligible cases.

$D_3$ and $S$ are available for all 1,000 internal evaluation cases, whereas $I$ is defined only for the 915 checker-eligible cases whose benchmark gold-label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} parent represented by a corresponding Criterion Checker. The three aggregate rates therefore use different denominators and must not be interpreted as consecutive stages of one numerical funnel.

These indicators describe complementary recorded output views for the same case. They do not imply that every finalization policy executes candidate ranking, criterion checking, and primary selection in this chronological order.

\section{Statistical Analysis}

The case is the unit of analysis, and all statistical tests are two-sided with a significance level of $\alpha=0.05$. When confidence intervals are reported for a single binary proportion, 95\% Wilson intervals are used. Paired comparisons require identical case IDs and evaluation-metric definitions. For binary correctness outcomes, the effect estimate is the paired difference in correctness proportions, and the exact McNemar test is computed from the two discordant counts: cases correct only under the first configuration and cases correct only under the second. Main paired comparisons report the effect estimate and its confidence interval; detailed discordant counts and $p$-values are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

Confidence intervals for paired accuracy differences and finalization difference-in-differences contrasts are estimated by resampling case IDs with replacement using 10,000 paired bootstrap replicates and seed 42. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles define the reported 95\% confidence interval. Reported $p$-values are interpreted together with effect direction, confidence intervals, and discordant counts rather than as standalone evidence.

Holm correction is applied when multiple prespecified tests belong to the same comparison family defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}; unrelated or isolated comparisons are not pooled into an artificial family. A non-significant result indicates insufficient evidence of a difference and is not interpreted as evidence of equivalence.

\section{Retrieval-Configuration Selection and Benchmark Positioning}
\label{sec:benchmark-retrieval-design}

RQ3 selects one retrieval configuration separately for Single and HiED--DA on the public-validation split, after which RQ1 evaluates the frozen configurations on the internal held-out set. Both analyses use the parent-label projection and evaluation measures defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}.

\subsection{Retrieval-Configuration Selection (RQ3)}
Retrieval is evaluated separately within the Single and HiED--DA architectures under three retrieval configurations: no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Within each architecture, the backbone model, prompt family, configured diagnostic scope, decoding settings, and output contract remain fixed. The same public-validation cases are evaluated under all three configurations.

For each architecture, retrieval selection followed a prespecified majority-based promotion rule over five validation metrics: Top-1 Accuracy, Top-3 Accuracy (genuine ranked Top-3 for HiED--DA and benchmark-defined emitted-label Top-3 for Single), Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1. A candidate configuration replaced the currently retained configuration only when it achieved higher values on more than half of these five metrics; otherwise, the currently retained configuration was kept. Once selected, each configuration was frozen before evaluation on the internal held-out set. Validation results were used only for configuration selection and were not treated as independent test estimates. Held-out results were not used to revise the selected configurations.

Global Top-5 and parent-balanced retrieval differ in demonstration count, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, label balance, and total prompt length. Their comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than the isolated effect of parent balancing.

\subsection{Same-Split Benchmark Positioning (RQ1)}
After retrieval-configuration selection, the validation-selected Single and HiED--DA configurations are evaluated together with the Majority baseline and TF--IDF with logistic regression on the same fixed 1,000-case internal held-out set. All methods use the same parent-label projection and scoring implementation.

Committed Top-1 Accuracy is the primary comparison metric because every evaluated configuration produces one committed primary diagnosis. Genuine Top-3 and multilabel measures are reported only when the corresponding output view is available. Single does not produce a genuine ranked differential diagnosis; its benchmark-defined Top-3, when reported for compatibility with LingxiDiagBench, is computed from the available emitted-label list and is not interpreted as ranked candidate coverage.

The Majority baseline predicts the most frequent scoring parent in the training source. TF--IDF with logistic regression follows the prespecified character-level, one-vs-rest implementation described in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

These comparisons are not component-controlled ablations. TF--IDF with logistic regression, Single, and HiED differ in training procedure, retrieval exposure, number of model calls, intermediate outputs, and output contract. Their performance differences therefore reflect the complete system configurations and cannot be attributed solely to multi-agent orchestration.

\paragraph{Supplementary oracle analysis.}
\label{sec:oracle-design}
To estimate optimistic primary-selection headroom within the recorded outputs, a gold-informed oracle searches a prespecified set of ranked and criterion-compatible alternatives and selects a benchmark gold diagnosis whenever the eligibility rules are satisfied. Cases whose committed primary diagnosis already matches a benchmark gold label are left unchanged. Because the procedure directly uses benchmark labels and assumes that oracle-eligible disagreements can be corrected without introducing new errors, it is non-deployable and is reported only as an assumption-dependent supplementary analysis. The candidate pool, eligibility thresholds, and sensitivity analyses are detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Primary-Selection Intervention Design}
\label{sec:primary-selection-intervention-design}

RQ4 evaluates whether alternative finalization and primary-selection procedures improve committed-primary benchmark agreement relative to Direct-Answer (DA).

\paragraph{Primary matched finalization comparison.}
The prespecified primary comparison evaluates DA against Nominate-then-Select (NtS). Within each retrieval condition, both policies reuse the same Diagnostician ranking, criterion states, and criterion-compatible set. DA retains the Diagnostician's emitted primary diagnosis, whereas NtS applies the deterministic finalization rule defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}.

DA and NtS are compared on the same 1,000 internal held-out cases under no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. The primary comparison measure is the paired difference in case-level committed Top-1 correctness. Paired-bootstrap confidence intervals and exact McNemar tests are computed as defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}.

Because DA and NtS share the same upstream artifacts and NtS introduces no additional LLM inference, this is the most controlled RQ4 comparison of the two finalization policies.

\paragraph{Complementary primary-selection methods.}
Three additional method families provide complementary comparisons. Deterministic fusion applies one prespecified replacement rule to the recorded candidate ranking, compatibility status, and criterion-support summaries. Pairwise comparison and debate introduce additional LLM inference to compare plausible diagnostic alternatives. Self-consistency and confidence-informed self-consistency aggregate repeated Diagnostician outputs using unweighted and confidence-weighted primary votes, respectively. Deterministic fusion therefore evaluates one specific recorded-output rule, whereas the remaining methods are evaluated as complete inference configurations. Complete replacement rules, candidate-selection procedures, inference settings, and tie-breaking rules are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\paragraph{Comparison and reporting boundaries.}
Unless otherwise stated, additional internal methods are compared with the validation-selected parent-balanced HiED--DA configuration. Paired differences and confidence intervals are reported only when both methods cover the same cases and retain complete case-level outputs. Methods that alter prompts, input contexts, inference budgets, or output-generation procedures are interpreted as complete method configurations rather than as controlled tests of an isolated selection component.

Committed Top-1 Accuracy is the common comparison metric because each method produces exactly one committed primary diagnosis. Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 are not used for the primary RQ4 comparisons because DA may additionally emit one comorbid diagnosis, whereas several alternatives emit only a single diagnosis. Detailed case-eligibility rules, inference budgets, seed schedules, and method configurations are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Sensitivity and Transfer Analyses}
\label{sec:sensitivity-transfer-design}

RQ5 examines model-scale sensitivity, external cross-dataset transfer, diagnostic-scope expansion, cross-corpus lexical transfer, and the patterns that characterize the remaining benchmark disagreements.

\paragraph{Within-family model-scale sensitivity.}
Qwen3-4B, 8B, 14B, and 32B are evaluated on the same fixed 1,000-case internal held-out set while holding the prompt family, configured diagnostic scope, retrieval policy, decoding contract, and evaluation metrics constant. Committed Top-1 Accuracy, genuine Top-3 Accuracy, structured-output failures, and abstention events are recorded to assess whether candidate availability and committed-primary agreement vary substantially with model size within the evaluated model family and serving setup.

\paragraph{External synthetic evaluation.}
MDD-5k provides external cross-dataset evaluation under a different Chinese synthetic dialogue-generation process and differs from LingxiDiag-16K in transcript characteristics, label frequencies, and diagnostic coverage. The primary multi-class analyses use the complete 925-case evaluation set and an 878-case in-scope subset in which at least one benchmark gold diagnosis is represented in HiED's main configured output space. All 878 in-scope cases are also checker-eligible under this profile and therefore support the external $D_3$--$I$--$S$ analysis.

A separate 490-case F32-versus-F41 subset is evaluated as a secondary binary analysis and is not treated as the main external performance estimate. The external trace records the Diagnostician ranking, criterion states, criterion-compatible set, and committed primary diagnosis for every case. It therefore supports committed Top-1 Accuracy, genuine Top-3 Accuracy, gold-label inclusion, and primary-selection comparisons on the same recorded trace. The $D_3$, $I$, and $S$ indicators use the definitions established in Section~\ref{sec:case-level-indicators}.

\paragraph{Configuration-only diagnostic-scope expansion.}
A configuration-level experiment expands the diagnostic profile from 14 to 33 configured categories by adding category-specific checker definitions without updating model weights. The expanded configuration is evaluated separately from the primary external analysis to test whether newly representable benchmark gold diagnoses appear in the ranked candidates or become the committed primary diagnosis.

\paragraph{Cross-corpus lexical transfer.}
The same TF--IDF classifier family is trained separately on each synthetic corpus and evaluated both within corpus and on the other corpus. This analysis examines whether lexical associations learned under one synthetic dialogue-generation process transfer to the other.

\paragraph{Descriptive disagreement patterns.}
Remaining benchmark disagreements are characterized using a first-listed-label confusion matrix, frequent pairings between the first-listed projected benchmark parent and the predicted primary diagnosis, and the recorded-output profiles defined by $D_3$, $I$, and $S$. Because the confusion matrix assigns one first-listed projected benchmark parent to each case, it is treated as a descriptive visualization rather than as the primary multilabel evaluation.

The model-scale and MDD-5k analyses provide the main evidence for RQ5. Diagnostic-scope expansion, cross-corpus lexical transfer, and descriptive disagreement-pattern analyses provide supporting evidence about the evaluated boundary conditions.

\section{Complementary Clinical Evaluation Plan}
\label{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan}

Two ongoing studies provide complementary clinical evaluation outside the completed computational RQs: a blinded psychiatrist review of twenty selected LingxiDiag disagreement cases and a CGMH real-world validation. Because they use different populations and reference conditions, the two studies are analyzed separately.

\subsection{LingxiDiag Pilot Psychiatrist Review}
\label{sec:lingxidiag-pilot-review}

The LingxiDiag pilot evaluates the feasibility of the psychiatrist-review procedure and provides psychiatrist characterization of a selected synthetic disagreement population.

Twenty cases are sampled without replacement from the 272-case primary-selection disagreement group defined in Section~\ref{sec:stage-wise-analysis-design}, satisfying $D_3=1$, $I=1$, and $S=0$. The random seed and selected case identifiers are fixed before psychiatrist review.

Two psychiatrists independently review all twenty cases in separately randomized orders. They remain blinded to the benchmark labels, HiED outputs, computational disagreement profile, case-selection rationale, and the other reviewer's responses.

The review records both provisional and final diagnostic judgments. Reviewers first read the transcript and record whether the available information supports a unique primary diagnosis, their provisional primary diagnosis, possible comorbid diagnoses, and up to three ranked differential diagnoses. They then label the prespecified canonical evidence items and diagnosis-specific qualifiers as \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}. After completing the evidence annotation, reviewers may revise their diagnostic judgments. Both the provisional and final responses are retained.

Shared symptom and impairment concepts are annotated once, whereas diagnosis-specific duration, frequency, temporal, relational, threshold, and exclusion conditions remain separate. The mapping between the physician-facing evidence items and the original Criterion Checker items is defined and frozen before annotation.

After both reviewers complete their final independent assessments, the research team compares their provisional and final judgments with the LingxiDiag benchmark gold labels, HiED ranked and committed diagnoses, criterion states, and system-derived compatibility decisions.

Pre-consensus inter-rater agreement is calculated from the independent responses. Any subsequent consensus or adjudication is conducted using the transcript and independent annotations while the benchmark labels and HiED outputs remain concealed. Planned analyses include agreement on the final transcript-only primary diagnosis, ranked-differential coverage, criterion-state agreement, pre-consensus inter-rater agreement, provisional-to-final diagnostic changes, and agreement between psychiatrist-evidence-derived and system-derived compatibility decisions. Because the pilot includes only twenty synthetic cases selected from one disagreement population, its findings are interpreted as feasibility and exploratory agreement evidence rather than as an estimate of population-level clinical accuracy.

\subsection{Ongoing CGMH Real-World Validation}
\label{sec:cgmh-validation}

The CGMH study is an ongoing real-world validation using psychiatric consultations included under prespecified clinical eligibility criteria. Cases are not selected according to whether HiED agrees with the recorded diagnosis. The model checkpoint, prompts, retrieval configuration, diagnostic scope, criterion definitions, compatibility rules, transcript-preprocessing procedure, and evaluation implementation are fixed before outcome analysis.

The CGMH evaluation contains three separate analyses.

First, HiED predictions are compared with diagnoses recorded during routine clinical care. This analysis measures agreement with the routine clinical record. The recorded diagnosis may incorporate information unavailable in the transcript provided to HiED, including prior history, longitudinal follow-up, collateral information, laboratory findings, and other clinical observations. Disagreement is therefore not automatically interpreted as a system error.

Second, two psychiatrists independently review the same transcript version and diagnostic scope provided to HiED. They remain blinded to the recorded clinical diagnosis, HiED outputs, and each other's responses. As in the LingxiDiag pilot, provisional diagnostic judgments are recorded before the evidence annotation, and reviewers may revise them afterward. Both provisional and final judgments are retained.

After independent review, disagreements are resolved through a prespecified consensus or adjudication procedure while the recorded clinical diagnosis and HiED outputs remain concealed. This process produces a psychiatrist transcript-only reference under an information condition more closely matched to the system input.

HiED is compared separately with each independent reviewer and with the transcript-only reference. Planned outcomes include committed-primary agreement, ranked-differential coverage, comorbidity agreement, inter-rater agreement, provisional-to-final diagnostic changes, criterion-state agreement, diagnostic-scope coverage, and agreement between system-derived and psychiatrist-evidence-derived compatibility decisions.

Third, an exploratory audit-assisted review examines how psychiatrists assess the HiED ranked differential, criterion states, supporting text, compatibility decisions, and committed diagnosis after completing the blinded transcript-only assessment and consensus or adjudication. The recorded clinical diagnosis remains concealed during this review. Reviewers assess the consistency of the trace with the transcript, identify criterion-level errors when present, and record whether exposure to HiED changes the diagnosis established during the blinded review.

Post-disclosure judgments are analyzed separately as exploratory human--AI review and usability evidence. They are not treated as independent reference diagnoses or as evidence that the audit trace improves diagnostic accuracy.

Results from the three information conditions are reported separately rather than combined into a single overall agreement estimate.

The strong lexical baseline indicates that transcript surface patterns contain substantial information about the benchmark labels, potentially including regularities specific to synthetic generation. It also shows that the remaining HiED disagreements cannot be explained only by an absence of predictive information in the transcripts.

HiED's additional empirical value lies instead in the diagnostic artifacts unavailable from the direct baselines: an explicit ranked differential, criterion-level outputs, compatibility decisions, and a traceable committed diagnosis. The following sections compare candidate detection, gold-label inclusion, and committed-primary agreement.

The tested selectors therefore do not recover the external gap identified in Section~\ref{sec:mdd-multiway}.

Detailed discordant counts, exact McNemar tests, and paired-bootstrap results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}, Table~\ref{tab:external-paired-tests}.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

The results support a stage-wise interpretation of HiED rather than an accuracy-leading claim. Its ranked, criterion, compatibility, and finalization outputs reveal where benchmark disagreement remains after a plausible diagnosis has entered the differential. The external synthetic evaluation supports the same $D_3$--$I$--$S$ views and identifies a corresponding ranked-and-compatible nonselection group under a second synthetic generation process.

This distinction matters because the same benchmark error can arise under different recorded conditions. A missing candidate calls for better candidate generation, absence from the criterion-compatible set calls for scrutiny of the evidence representation or compatibility rule, and a diagnosis present in both the Top-3 and criterion-compatible set but not selected calls for comparative selection. The contribution of the trace is to separate these questions without treating the recorded output views as a causal model of the language model's internal reasoning.

\section{Compatibility and Primary Selection in the Evaluated Trace}
\label{sec:discussion-primary-selection}

Candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection answer different questions. Candidate detection asks whether a plausible diagnosis appears among the leading alternatives. Criterion verification asks whether each configured diagnosis is compatible with the available transcript under its study-specific operational rule. Neither stage directly compares the plausible diagnoses to determine which one should be committed as primary.

This distinction is important because psychiatric disorders frequently share symptoms. Sleep disturbance, fatigue, impaired concentration, low mood, worry, avoidance, and functional impairment may support several diagnostic categories at the same time. A transcript may therefore be compatible with more than one diagnosis. Satisfying an individual compatibility rule does not establish whether a diagnosis is primary, secondary, comorbid, or less explanatory than another compatible alternative.

The recorded-output analysis in Chapter~\ref{ch:error} makes this limitation visible. Cases in which a benchmark gold parent is ranked and criterion-compatible but not selected often place that diagnosis near the top of the differential. Compatible-set size and aggregate criterion-state proportions provided little descriptive separation between the compared groups and were insufficient to identify the committed diagnosis in the evaluated trace.

Reliable primary selection requires comparative relations that independent criterion counts do not encode. These relations may include temporal order, illness course, episode structure, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity. For example, the system must distinguish whether anxiety symptoms form the main syndrome, occur within a depressive episode, represent an independent comorbidity, or remain insufficiently characterized by the available transcript.

The concentration of disagreements in particular diagnostic directions, especially F41-to-F32, indicates that the observed pattern is not uniformly distributed. Possible explanations include corpus-specific wording, learned class preferences, overlapping symptom descriptions, and missing temporal or exclusionary evidence. The present experiments do not isolate these causes, and independent psychiatrist review is required to determine their clinical interpretation.

Primary selection is therefore the largest remaining \emph{recorded} bottleneck under the evaluated HiED configuration and synthetic benchmark contract.

\section{Why the Evaluated Selection Strategies Did Not Reliably Close the Gap}
\label{sec:discussion-selection-strategies}

The gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement indicates that a benchmark-consistent diagnosis is often already available among the leading candidates. However, candidate availability does not imply that the recorded outputs contain a simple and reliable rule for replacing the committed primary diagnosis.

The recorded-output methods provide the clearest test of this possibility because they reuse the existing candidate ranking, criterion states, and compatibility decisions. Nominate-then-Select and deterministic fusion nevertheless did not improve Direct-Answer. These methods rely on rank position, compatibility status, and criterion-support summaries, but none of these signals directly represents why one compatible diagnosis should be preferred over another. A rule that overturns an incorrect commitment may therefore also replace an initially correct decision.

Pairwise comparison and debate attempt to make the comparison among candidates more explicit. However, these methods introduce new prompts, inference calls, context arrangements, and decision procedures and must be interpreted as complete configurations rather than isolated selection mechanisms. Under the evaluated settings, additional comparison did not reliably improve the committed diagnosis.

One important limitation is that all methods reason over the same fixed transcript. Additional inference may reorganize or reinterpret the available evidence, but it cannot acquire missing information about symptom onset, illness course, episode history, exclusionary causes, or causal relationships. When these distinctions are absent or ambiguous, another reasoning call may change the model's preference without resolving the underlying diagnostic uncertainty.

Sampling-based methods address preference stability rather than comparative validity. A diagnosis selected consistently across samples may reflect a stable clinical interpretation, but it may also reflect a stable corpus prior or model bias. Majority voting cannot correct a preference that is consistently misaligned with the benchmark, and confidence weighting is useful only when the model's stated confidence is associated with comparative correctness.

The observed gap should therefore be interpreted as analytical decision headroom rather than demonstrated automatic recoverability. The results do not show that criterion-based selection, pairwise comparison, debate, or sampling are generally ineffective. They show that the specific prompts, models, thresholds, inference budgets, and aggregation rules evaluated in this thesis did not reliably convert candidate availability into improved committed-primary agreement.

More effective selection may require explicit comparative representations and, when necessary, additional evidence beyond the fixed transcript.

\section{Boundaries Beyond Primary Selection}
\label{sec:discussion-set-scope-transfer}

Primary selection is not the only decision involved in producing a diagnostic output. The results also identify separate limitations in diagnostic-set construction, diagnostic scope, and transfer across data distributions. These limitations occur at different points and should not be interpreted as additional instances of the primary-selection bottleneck.

Diagnostic-set construction determines which diagnoses should accompany the committed primary diagnosis. Chapter~\ref{ch:error} shows that a benchmark-consistent primary can coexist with an incorrect complete diagnosis set. Primary diagnoses, supported comorbidities, and differential alternatives therefore require distinct output roles. Set-level measures such as Exact Match and label-level F1 also reflect output cardinality and comorbidity construction rather than primary selection alone.

Diagnostic scope defines which diagnoses the system is able to rank, verify, or emit. A diagnosis that is absent from the configured profile cannot be recovered through improved candidate ranking or finalization. However, the scope-expansion experiment in Chapter~\ref{ch:external} also shows that making a diagnosis representable does not automatically cause the system to rank or select it. Broader coverage may require corresponding examples, retrieval support, prompt adaptation, or training that changes system behavior rather than only its available labels.

External transfer presents a further boundary. MDD-5k reproduces the $D_3$--$I$--$S$ distinction under a second synthetic data-generation process, including 225 checker-eligible cases in which a benchmark gold parent is ranked and criterion-compatible but not selected. The strong degradation of the lexical classifier across corpora also indicates that predictive signals learned within one synthetic corpus may depend strongly on corpus-specific wording, generation procedures, and label distributions.

These findings place complementary limits on the main conclusion. Primary selection is the largest remaining recorded bottleneck within the evaluated internal HiED trace, but it does not account for errors in comorbidity construction, unavailable diagnostic categories, or distribution-specific prediction behavior. The external evidence supports partial persistence across synthetic corpora, not transfer to real patients or clinical workflows.

\section{Implications for Auditable Diagnostic Systems}
\label{sec:discussion-clinical-implications}

HiED's main auditability contribution is to preserve distinct diagnostic artifacts rather than only a final label. The ranked differential, criterion states, supporting text, compatibility decisions, and finalization source allow a reviewer to determine whether a benchmark gold diagnosis was unavailable, omitted from the leading differential, absent from the criterion-compatible set, or present in that set but not committed as primary.

This inspectability should not be confused with correctness. The retained artifacts do not reveal the language model's hidden reasoning, validate the Criterion Checker, or establish that the committed diagnosis is clinically appropriate. A coherent audit trace may still contain unsupported evidence, an incorrect criterion state, or a plausible but clinically suboptimal diagnosis. Auditability is therefore an analytical and review capability rather than evidence of clinical reliability.

The results also suggest that future diagnostic systems should support evidence acquisition rather than relying only on repeated inference over a fixed transcript. When the leading diagnoses differ in duration, temporal sequence, episode history, exclusionary causes, or the relationship among mood, anxiety, trauma, and sleep symptoms, the system could identify the unresolved distinction and request a targeted follow-up question.

When additional information cannot be obtained, insufficient-evidence, abstention, or referral outputs should be evaluated as alternatives to forcing a unique primary diagnosis. These behaviors were not evaluated in the present experiments and remain design directions for future clinical studies.

Overall, the findings support a distinction between making diagnostic alternatives inspectable and establishing that a final decision is clinically reliable. HiED contributes the former under the evaluated synthetic benchmark conditions; the latter remains dependent on independent clinical validation, workflow evaluation, and safety assessment.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

This chapter defines five sets of boundaries on the reported claims: benchmark and measurement validity, dataset generalizability, experimental-comparison validity, statistical and reproducibility limits, and clinical deployment. Together, these boundaries specify how far the reported benchmark findings can be interpreted.

\section{Benchmark and Measurement Limitations}

The primary computational evaluation measures agreement with the dataset-provided benchmark gold diagnoses after the parent-level projection defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}, rather than agreement with independently adjudicated clinical diagnoses. Some cases contain more than one projected benchmark gold parent, and Committed Top-1 Accuracy counts a case as correct when the committed primary diagnosis matches any of them. The projection provides a common scoring space but removes subtype-level distinctions. The first-listed projected benchmark gold parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses, such as the confusion matrix, and does not replace the complete benchmark gold-label set used for multilabel evaluation.

The metrics defined in Section~\ref{sec:prediction-metrics} evaluate different output views and are not interchangeable. In particular, set-level metrics reflect diagnostic-set construction and output cardinality in addition to committed-primary agreement. The $D_3$, $I$, and $S$ indicators defined in Section~\ref{sec:case-level-indicators} describe distinct recorded-output views and use different eligible populations where applicable; their aggregate rates should therefore not be interpreted as a causal or chronological funnel. The gold-informed oracle defined in Section~\ref{sec:oracle-design} directly uses benchmark labels and is consequently a nondeployable, assumption-dependent estimate of optimistic selection headroom.

Accordingly, the measurement framework can identify where benchmark disagreement is recorded across the available output views, but not why it occurred. It cannot reveal the model's internal causal reasoning, identify a uniquely correct clinical primary diagnosis, or establish that a localized disagreement can be automatically corrected.

\section{Dataset and Generalizability Limitations}

LingxiDiag-16K and MDD-5k consist of synthetically generated Chinese psychiatric dialogues. They provide controlled and reproducible evaluation settings but may exhibit regular generation patterns, simplified ambiguity, limited comorbidity patterns, clearer symptom cues, and less conversational variability and longitudinal complexity than real psychiatric consultations. Similar findings across the two datasets may therefore reflect properties shared by synthetic dialogue generation rather than general properties of clinical diagnosis.

Because the official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable, the main internal evaluation uses a fixed 1,000-case held-out subset sampled from the LingxiDiag-16K training source. Case identifiers were excluded from training and retrieval, but the evaluation cases still share the source generation process, language style, label distribution, and annotation procedure with the development data. A complete semantic near-duplicate audit was not performed. The internal results should therefore be interpreted as same-source held-out evaluation rather than independent external validation.

The public-validation split was used repeatedly for prompt development, compatibility-rule refinement, sensitivity analysis, and retrieval selection. It therefore represents development evidence rather than an independent estimate of generalization. The reversal between the validation-selected retrieval ordering and the held-out ordering further illustrates the uncertainty associated with configuration selection from a limited development split.

MDD-5k provides a second synthetic distribution with different transcript characteristics, label frequencies, and diagnostic coverage. Although the same $D_3$--$I$--$S$ definitions can be applied, the observed cross-dataset differences remain entangled with changes in label distribution, diagnostic scope, transcript characteristics, and possible effects of transcript truncation that were not separately quantified.

The cross-corpus lexical results further indicate that strong within-corpus prediction may rely on corpus-specific wording, generation patterns, and label priors. Overall, the completed evidence establishes same-source held-out performance and provides partial cross-dataset evidence under a second synthetic distribution. It does not establish generalization to naturally occurring psychiatric consultations with real patients, independent clinical sites, or Taiwanese psychiatric practice.

\section{Model and Experimental-Comparison Limitations}
\label{sec:model-comparison-limitations}

Most comparisons in this thesis alter more than one system component. Unless otherwise stated, they are therefore interpreted at the declared configuration level rather than as estimates of an isolated component effect.

The main same-split comparison evaluates TF--IDF with logistic regression, Single, and HiED using a common case set, label projection, and scoring implementation. However, the configurations differ in training procedure, retrieval exposure, prompts, number of inference calls, intermediate outputs, and output contract. Their performance differences therefore cannot be attributed solely to multi-agent orchestration. Published LingxiDiagBench results differ further in inference and scoring procedures and are included only for descriptive benchmark positioning.

The retrieval analysis is more controlled because the backbone model, prompt family, diagnostic scope, decoding settings, and output contract remain fixed within each architecture. However, global Top-5 and parent-balanced retrieval also differ in demonstration count, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, label balance, and total retrieved-example token length. In addition, the primary retrieval configurations for Single and HiED were selected independently on the validation split, so the main system comparison is not retrieval matched.

The DA--NtS comparison is the most controlled finalization comparison because both policies reuse the same ranked candidates, criterion states, and criterion-compatible set. Nevertheless, it evaluates only one deterministic NtS rule and should not be generalized to all criterion-based selection strategies. Set-level comparisons are also affected by the fact that DA may additionally emit one comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits only the committed primary diagnosis.

Deterministic fusion evaluates one prespecified rule over the recorded outputs. Pairwise comparison, debate, self-consistency, and confidence-informed self-consistency additionally alter prompts, inference calls, decoding procedures, context organization, aggregation rules, or computational budgets. Diagnostic-scope expansion changes the available categories, prompts, checker definitions, and compatibility rules without additional model training. These analyses characterize the evaluated method configurations but do not identify the contribution of any single reasoning mechanism.

Finally, the model-scale analysis is restricted to the evaluated Qwen3 model family and serving setup. Similar findings across these model sizes do not establish robustness across unrelated architectures, training procedures, quantization schemes, or deployment environments.

\section{Statistical and Reproducibility Limitations}
\label{sec:statistical-reproducibility-limitations}

The reported confidence intervals and statistical tests quantify uncertainty over the observed benchmark cases under fixed system outputs and evaluation configurations. They do not capture uncertainty arising from benchmark validity, prompt design, checkpoint selection, synthetic data generation, or performance in a new population. The reported statistical uncertainty therefore applies to the evaluated sample and should not be generalized to all models, datasets, or clinical settings.

Several analyses involve small or imbalanced subgroups. Rare diagnostic categories and the 12-case ranked-but-not-included group with $D_3=1$, $I=0$, and $S=0$ do not support stable class-specific or group-level conclusions. A confidence interval containing zero indicates insufficient evidence of a difference rather than evidence of equivalence. Conversely, an interval excluding zero provides evidence of a difference between the evaluated configurations, but it does not identify which component caused that difference when multiple components vary simultaneously.

The analyses also differ in prespecification and record completeness. The same-split benchmark comparison, validation-based retrieval selection, internal $D_3$--$I$--$S$ analysis, and paired DA--NtS comparison constitute the main quantitative evidence. The gold-informed oracle, diagnostic-scope expansion, lexical transfer, and additional selection interventions provide supporting evidence. Some supporting analyses lack complete paired case-level records, which limits uncertainty estimation and prevents reconstruction of which cases changed from benchmark disagreement to agreement or vice versa.

Reproduction requires preservation of the complete evaluation contract, including the model and serving environment; prompts, retrieval settings, and diagnostic configuration; data splits and recorded outputs; label projections and output-view definitions; and statistical procedures. A numerically similar result obtained using a different Top-3 definition, denominator, eligible population, or compatibility rule does not reproduce the same evaluation measure.

\section{Clinical Validity and Deployment Boundaries}
\label{sec:clinical-deployment-boundaries}

The computational analyses localize disagreement across diagnostic scope, candidate ranking, criterion compatibility, and primary commitment. They cannot determine whether an individual disagreement arises from the system, incomplete transcript evidence, or limitations of the benchmark gold labels themselves. The benchmark gold diagnoses have not been independently adjudicated as unique clinical primaries, and the recorded criterion states and supporting text remain model-generated rather than clinician-validated evidence. The recorded-output groups must therefore be interpreted as computational benchmark-disagreement profiles rather than psychiatrist-confirmed diagnostic errors.

The blinded LingxiDiag pilot and CGMH real-world validation described in Section~\ref{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan} remain ongoing and contribute no completed clinical evidence to this thesis. The LingxiDiag pilot concerns a selected synthetic disagreement sample rather than a representative clinical population, and post-disclosure CGMH judgments are analyzed separately from independent transcript-only reference assessments.

HiED does not implement a validated mechanism for identifying unknown or out-of-scope presentations. The \texttt{Others} label is assigned only during evaluation when none of the benchmark gold diagnoses is represented in the scoring space; it is not available as a system output. The current confidence fields are not calibrated to diagnostic correctness or clinical risk, and no clinically validated abstention, referral, or targeted follow-up policy has been evaluated.

HiED is motivated by the Taiwanese psychiatric context, but its clinical effectiveness has not been demonstrated in Taiwan or elsewhere. The completed evidence does not establish performance for naturally occurring psychiatric consultations, transcripts of spontaneous or code-switched Mandarin--Taiwanese interactions, local coding practices, integration with hospital information systems, clinician workload, subgroup fairness, treatment decisions, patient outcomes, or safety.

Auditability and reproducibility do not establish clinical validity. An inspectable and repeatable trace may still contain an unsupported evidence claim, an incorrect criterion state, or an inappropriate committed diagnosis. HiED should therefore remain an offline research and review framework until independent clinical adjudication, prospective workflow and safety evaluation, appropriate privacy and governance controls, and plans for post-deployment monitoring and revalidation are in place.

\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis introduced HiED, an auditable multi-stage framework for differential diagnosis using Chinese-language transcripts of initial psychiatric consultations. Rather than reducing diagnosis to a single final label, HiED preserves the ranked differential diagnosis, criterion states, criterion-compatible set, compatibility-rule trace, and committed primary diagnosis as separately inspectable artifacts. Its main contribution is therefore a system and evaluation framework for analyzing benchmark disagreement across recorded outputs rather than an overall predictive advantage.

On the fixed internal held-out set, HiED did not outperform the strongest same-split predictor, but its recorded artifacts exposed a persistent gap between candidate availability, gold-label inclusion, and committed-primary agreement. The largest disagreement profile involved cases in which a benchmark gold parent appeared in both the genuine Top-3 and the criterion-compatible set but was not committed as primary. Detailed analyses further showed that these disagreements were concentrated in particular ranks and diagnostic directions, while compatible-set size and aggregate criterion-state composition provided limited separation from cases with committed-primary agreement. Separate diagnostic-set disagreements also remained in some cases despite a benchmark-consistent committed primary.

No evaluated primary-selection intervention reliably closed this gap, and the gap remained observable across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k. Under the evaluated HiED configuration and synthetic benchmark contract, primary selection therefore emerged as the dominant recorded bottleneck. HiED's completed contribution is to make candidate generation, criterion verification, compatibility, and commitment separately observable and auditable; clinical validity remains to be established through the ongoing psychiatrist review and real-world validation.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}

The following answers are restricted to the evaluated synthetic benchmarks, declared output contracts, and recorded system artifacts.

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=1.5cm,labelwidth=1.1cm,itemsep=0.2em,parsep=0pt]

\item[RQ1] \textbf{How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation metric?}

HiED did not outperform the strongest same-split predictor. TF--IDF with logistic regression achieved the highest Committed Top-1 Accuracy, while the validation-selected Single and HiED--DA configurations achieved nearly identical committed-primary benchmark agreement. HiED's distinct empirical value lies in the ranked differential diagnosis, criterion states, criterion-compatible set, compatibility-rule trace, and finalization record that enable recorded-output analysis rather than in an overall accuracy advantage.

\item[RQ2] \textbf{How often does a benchmark gold label appear among the ranked candidates or in the criterion-compatible set without being selected as the committed primary diagnosis?}

Among the 915 checker-eligible cases, 272 satisfied $D_3=1$, $I=1$, and $S=0$: a benchmark gold parent appeared in both the genuine Top-3 and the criterion-compatible set, but another diagnosis was committed as primary. This was the largest committed-primary-disagreement group and supports primary selection as the dominant recorded bottleneck under the evaluated HiED configuration and benchmark contract.

\item[RQ3] \textbf{How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?}

Retrieval benefited Single more consistently than HiED. Global Top-5 achieved the strongest values across the prespecified validation metrics for Single and was therefore selected. For HiED--DA, global Top-5 won only two of the five prespecified metrics, so parent-balanced retrieval was retained under the majority rule. Global Top-5 nevertheless produced higher held-out point estimates across the reported HiED metrics, showing that the validation-selected ordering did not persist on the internal held-out set.

\item[RQ4] \textbf{Under the evaluated settings, do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve committed Top-1 agreement over Direct-Answer?}

No evaluated intervention produced a clear improvement over Direct-Answer. NtS provided the most controlled finalization comparison because it reused the same ranked candidates, criterion states, and criterion-compatible set, but it reduced committed Top-1 agreement under all three retrieval conditions. Deterministic fusion, pairwise comparison, debate, self-consistency, and confidence-informed self-consistency likewise produced either lower point estimates or statistically uncertain differences under the evaluated configurations.

\item[RQ5] \textbf{To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and which error patterns characterize the remaining disagreements?}

The gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement remained observable across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k. Among 878 checker-eligible MDD-5k cases, 225 contained a benchmark gold parent in both the genuine Top-3 and the criterion-compatible set but committed another diagnosis as primary. Diagnostic-scope expansion increased output-space representability without producing committed-primary recovery, while cross-corpus TF--IDF performance degraded substantially.

In the internal 272-case disagreement group, the highest-ranked benchmark gold parent was usually second, and the disagreements were concentrated in the F41-to-F32 direction. Compatible-set size and aggregate criterion-state composition did not clearly distinguish this group from cases with committed-primary agreement.

\end{description}

\section{Future Work}

\paragraph{Completion and reporting of the planned clinical evaluations.}

The blinded LingxiDiag pilot and CGMH real-world validation should be completed under the frozen protocols defined in Section~\ref{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan}. The LingxiDiag pilot should be reported as feasibility and exploratory agreement evidence for a selected synthetic disagreement population. The CGMH study should separately report agreement with routine clinical diagnoses, the blinded transcript-only psychiatrist reference, and post-disclosure audit-assisted judgments because these comparisons use different information conditions.

\paragraph{Comparative primary selection and diagnostic-set construction.}

Future primary-selection methods should explicitly compare plausible diagnoses rather than rely only on independent criterion satisfaction, compatible-set membership, ranking position, or criterion counts. Relevant comparative relations include temporal order, illness course, episode structure, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, explanatory dependence, and the distinction among a primary diagnosis, a comorbid diagnosis, and a differential alternative. Primary selection and diagnostic-set construction should be modeled and evaluated as separate decisions, with explicit output roles for each type of diagnosis.

Where possible, new finalization methods should be evaluated using fixed upstream artifacts so that changes in commitment can be separated from changes in candidate generation, prompting, or inference budget. Complete paired case-level records should be preserved to support gain--loss analysis, uncertainty estimation, and reproducible error review.

\paragraph{Targeted evidence acquisition, abstention, and broader validation.}

When the available transcript does not contain the information required to distinguish the leading diagnoses, future systems should identify the unresolved comparison and request targeted follow-up evidence. Relevant questions may address symptom duration, temporal sequence, previous episodes, substance exposure, medical explanations, functional impairment, or the relationships among mood, anxiety, trauma, and sleep symptoms.

When additional evidence cannot be obtained, insufficient-evidence, abstention, and referral outputs should be evaluated as alternatives to forcing a unique primary diagnosis. This work requires calibrated uncertainty and validated detection of unknown or out-of-scope presentations.

Broader technical evaluation should include unrelated model families, independently collected corpora, naturally occurring clinical transcripts, complete ranked and compatibility artifacts, and explicit evaluation of diagnostic-set construction. Clinical development should assess clinician interaction, automation bias, review burden, subgroup performance, privacy, governance, patient safety, and prospective clinical outcomes before any deployment claim is made.

HiED shows that candidate availability and criterion compatibility do not by themselves guarantee committed-primary benchmark agreement. Its current contribution is to make this gap observable and auditable; improving comparative selection and establishing clinical validity and safe use remain central directions for future work.

\paragraph{Deterministic fusion.}
The rule keeps the DA primary unless that diagnosis is absent from the criterion-compatible set. It considers criterion-compatible alternatives in the Diagnostician's Top-3 and replaces the DA primary only when an alternative's criterion met ratio exceeds the DA ratio by at least 0.20. The eligible diagnosis with the highest met ratio is selected; ties are resolved by Diagnostician rank, then the fixed category order in the diagnostic profile, and finally the diagnostic code.

\paragraph{Pairwise configurations.}
Pairwise comparison begins with diagnoses whose criterion met ratio is at least 0.50. Candidates enter the close comparison cluster when the gap between adjacent met-ratio values is below 0.10. The pairwise Differential agent compares the eligible cluster, and a valid returned diagnosis is promoted according to the independent or forced-override contract. These runs use deterministic decoding (temperature 0, \texttt{top\_k}=1) and a 1,536-token output budget. Because the full HiED path is re-executed, the resulting ranking may differ from the recorded DA trace.

\paragraph{Two-view debate.}
The debate candidate pool is the union of the Diagnostician's ranked Top-5 and the criterion-compatible diagnoses. A diagnostic advocate receives the ranking, reasoning summary, and retrieval gist, while a criterion advocate receives the criterion evidence. The advocates run for at most two rounds. Agreement determines the primary diagnosis; otherwise a judge resolves the disagreement. A case therefore uses at most four advocate calls and one judge call, excluding malformed-output reasks. Decoding uses temperature 0, \texttt{top\_k}=1, and a 1,536-token output budget. The internal and external context windows are 8,192 and 24,576 tokens, respectively.

\paragraph{Repeated-generation configurations.}
Self-consistency and confidence-informed self-consistency use $K=3$ and $K=5$ Diagnostician samples, temperature 0.7, \texttt{top\_p}=1.0, a 768-token output budget, and fixed seeds 424200--424204. Self-consistency uses unweighted primary-vote counts. Confidence-informed self-consistency weights each vote by the reported confidence after clipping it to $[0,1]$; a missing confidence receives weight 1. Primary ties are resolved by weighted score, raw vote count, earliest sample, and diagnostic code. Ranked-list aggregation uses weighted Borda-style points over the first five positions, with ties resolved by earliest occurrence and then diagnostic code.
