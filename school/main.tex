初診精神科晤談逐字稿中的鑑別診斷，本質上是一種在證據不完整條件下進行的比較式決策。多個疾病診斷可能同時與同一份逐字稿相容，但最終通常只能有一個被判定為主診斷。然而，用以區分病程、發病時序、排除病因與共病關係等關鍵鑑別資訊，未必會在單次初診晤談中被完整引出。大型語言模型雖然能流暢地閱讀並理解這類逐字稿，近期也已有大量研究嘗試將其應用於精神科診斷任務，但語言理解的流暢性並不等同於可靠的鑑別診斷能力。

既有評測呈現出一致的落差：模型在粗粒度分類任務上往往表現良好，但一旦進入細粒度的疾病層級鑑別診斷，準確率便會大幅下降。這個落差促使本研究提出一套可稽核的系統架構，用以明確定位錯誤發生於診斷流程中的哪一個階段。模型可能生成一個合理且受到診斷準則支持的候選疾病，卻仍未能將其正確選為主診斷。候選診斷生成、依準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇三者之間的差異，通常會被單一準確率指標所掩蓋，使研究者難以判斷系統究竟在哪個環節失敗。

為了使這些不同的失敗階段能夠被分別觀察，並檢驗主診斷選擇是否構成主要瓶頸，本研究提出 HiED——一套面向中文精神科初診對話逐字稿的鑑別診斷框架。HiED 將候選診斷偵測、ICD-10 逐準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇保留為可分別分析的階段，而非將整個流程簡化為單一分類事件。同時，系統會在每次預測時輸出逐準則的稽核軌跡，使研究者能夠檢視診斷依據並定位系統失敗的位置。HiED 並非已完成臨床驗證的自主診斷工具，而是一項用於分析與檢驗逐字稿式精神科診斷失敗模式的研究工具。

因此，本論文有兩項主要貢獻：其一，提出一套可稽核的失敗定位框架，使上述各診斷階段得以分別觀察；其二，提供可重現的證據，指出在本研究評估的 HiED 設定與合成逐字稿基準評測中，主診斷選擇構成主要的失敗瓶頸。換言之，在本研究之合成逐字稿設定下，系統的主要限制不僅在於能否生成合理的候選診斷，而更在於能否在多個皆具一定準則支持的候選之間，進行有效比較並判定最適合作為主診斷者。最後需說明，本文題目所稱之臺灣脈絡，指涉系統之設計動機與預期部署場域（繁體中文互動、本地端推論、面向臨床人員之稽核），而非已完成之臺灣真實病人臨床驗證；本文現階段的量化結論均來自合成中文逐字稿語料。臺灣真實病人資料之臨床驗證目前於長庚紀念醫院進行中，其結果不納入本論文現階段的量化結論。

\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial-consultation psychiatric transcript is a comparative decision under incomplete evidence. Multiple disorders may be compatible with the same transcript, yet only one can typically be judged the primary diagnosis. Key discriminating information---illness course, onset timing, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity relationships---may not be fully elicited within a single initial consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, and recent research has applied them to psychiatric diagnostic tasks, but linguistic fluency does not equate to reliable differential diagnostic ability.

Existing evaluations show a consistent gap: models perform well on coarse-grained classification, but accuracy drops sharply on fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis. This gap motivates an auditable architecture that locates the stage at which a diagnostic error occurs. A model may generate a plausible disorder supported by diagnostic criteria, yet fail to select it as the primary diagnosis. The distinction among candidate-diagnosis generation, criterion-based verification, and primary-diagnosis selection is usually obscured by a single accuracy metric.

To make these disagreement locations separately observable and to examine whether primary-diagnosis selection is a major bottleneck, we propose HiED, a differential-diagnosis framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts. HiED records outputs for candidate detection, ICD-10 criterion verification, and primary selection as separately analyzable views rather than reducing diagnosis to one classification event. A criterion-level audit trace records evidence and disagreement locations for each prediction. HiED is a research instrument for analyzing transcript-based diagnostic disagreement, not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic tool. The Taiwanese context in the title denotes the intended deployment setting. Real-patient validation in Taiwan is ongoing at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital (CGMH) and is not included in the present quantitative conclusions.

This thesis makes two main contributions: an auditable failure-localization framework and reproducible evidence of a primary-selection bottleneck in the evaluated HiED configurations under synthetic transcript-only benchmarks. In these settings, the observed limitation lies not only in generating plausible candidates, but in comparing criterion-supported alternatives and selecting the most appropriate primary diagnosis.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric outpatient care in Taiwan is widely accessible, but this access also creates a heavy clinical workload. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit different levels of medical facilities without referral, and large medical centers may receive more than ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in one day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A first psychiatric visit must cover history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and an initial diagnostic judgment within limited time. The resulting note or transcript often contains incomplete information about onset, duration, course, medical or substance-related causes, and previous treatment.

The evidence may also be indirect. In Chinese-speaking settings, patients often describe distress through insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, or gastrointestinal symptoms before using explicit mood or anxiety terms~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. Taiwanese consultations may also mix Mandarin, Taiwanese expressions, and local clinical wording. The same condition can therefore be described in several ways within one visit.

Differential diagnosis remains difficult because common psychiatric disorders share many symptoms. Depression, anxiety, stress-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, somatic symptom disorders, and sleep disorders may all involve insomnia, fatigue, poor concentration, and impaired daily function. In diagnostic assessment, these symptoms are interpreted through disorder-specific criteria. These criteria do not treat every symptom independently or equally: depending on the disorder, a diagnosis may require particular core symptoms, a minimum number of additional symptoms, a specified duration, functional impairment, and the absence of exclusionary causes~\citep{who2019icd10}. Diagnostic assessment therefore involves matching the available evidence to a disorder-specific combination of requirements, rather than assigning a diagnosis from the presence of one symptom alone. Even when diagnostic criteria are applied, several disorders may remain compatible with the same transcript. Evidence for both depression and anxiety, for example, may reflect comorbidity or overlapping symptoms from one primary condition~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}. Even structured diagnostic studies report limited agreement for some common disorders~\citep{regier2013dsm5}.

These features make first-visit psychiatric diagnosis a problem of comparative bookkeeping under incomplete evidence. The clinician must document a best-supported working diagnosis, keep plausible alternatives visible, and identify which missing information should be collected at follow-up. Time pressure can increase the risk of anchoring or premature closure when several disorders share the same symptoms~\citep{croskerry2003cognitive}.
\section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

Large language models offer capabilities that are well aligned with psychiatric transcript analysis: they can process free-form dialogue, organize symptom evidence, retrieve relevant examples, and produce structured outputs. Medical multi-agent systems can further divide the diagnostic task among specialized roles and combine their outputs~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. However, these capabilities do not by themselves ensure reliable differential diagnosis from a fixed transcript.

A final diagnostic error may arise for different reasons. A clinically relevant diagnosis may never be proposed as a candidate, the available transcript may not provide sufficient criterion-level support for that diagnosis, or the diagnosis may remain plausible but lose the final comparison against competing alternatives. These failure modes require different remedies, but they are usually collapsed into a single final-accuracy score.

This creates the central technical problem studied in this thesis: existing evaluation often does not reveal whether disagreement arises during candidate generation, criterion-based verification, or primary-diagnosis selection. Without separately observable outputs for these stages, it is difficult to determine whether better retrieval, evidence checking, or comparative selection is needed. The next section introduces HiED as a framework designed to make these distinctions observable.

\section{Overview of HiED and Study Scope}

To make the different sources of diagnostic disagreement observable, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid multi-agent framework for differential diagnosis from Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts. The system is motivated by the Taiwanese outpatient setting. However, the completed quantitative analyses reported in this thesis use synthetic Chinese dialogue datasets. The LingxiDiag psychiatrist pilot and CGMH real-world validation are ongoing complementary studies and are not included in the present quantitative conclusions.

HiED combines two complementary diagnostic processes. An LLM-based Diagnostician produces a ranked differential diagnosis and an emitted primary diagnosis, while diagnosis-specific Criterion Checkers evaluate the same transcript against operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria. A deterministic compatibility module aggregates the criterion judgments, and a finalization policy converts the recorded outputs into a committed diagnosis. The ranked candidates, criterion judgments, compatibility decisions, and committed diagnosis are stored separately.

This separation supports stage-wise analysis of candidate generation, criterion verification, and primary selection. It allows the study to distinguish a diagnosis that was not proposed, one that was not included in the criterion-compatible set, and one that remained available but was not selected as the committed primary diagnosis. 

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions.

First, it presents an auditable hybrid architecture that combines LLM-based diagnosis, structured criterion-level checking against operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria, and deterministic compatibility rules. The system records criterion states, checker-generated supporting text, and compatibility-rule outcomes instead of relying only on a free-form rationale. The trace can be inspected and replayed, but it does not prove that the LLM's hidden reasoning is faithful or that the checker judgments are clinically correct~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}.

Second, it presents a stage-wise disagreement-localization and empirical evaluation framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection, quantifies the primary-selection bottleneck, and tests whether criterion-based finalization and several primary-selection methods close the observed gap. The absence of improvement under the evaluated selection interventions narrows several simple explanations under the tested settings, but it does not show that better selectors or additional clinical information can never help.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

Psychiatric LLM studies do not evaluate one uniform diagnostic task. The literature includes psychiatric knowledge or licensing-examination questions, diagnosis from clinical vignettes, focused assessment of particular disorders, automated depression detection, fixed-record diagnosis, and interactive consultation~\citep{qiu2023largeai,li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,li2025addreview,mcduff2025amie}. Results across these settings are not directly interchangeable because the available evidence, diagnostic scope, required output, and reference standard differ.

This distinction is especially important when moving from screening or broad category classification to fine-grained differential diagnosis. Detecting prominent depression or anxiety signals does not require the same comparison as selecting one primary diagnosis among disorders with overlapping symptoms. Disorder-level diagnosis may additionally depend on duration, temporal sequence, illness course, exclusion conditions, and comorbidity. LingxiDiagBench makes this coarse-to-fine gap explicit: performance decreases substantially from binary depression--anxiety classification to comorbidity recognition and twelve-way diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. MDD-5k provides a second synthetic Chinese diagnostic-dialogue resource generated from anonymized patient cases~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. Both resources support reproducible disorder-level experiments, but neither substitutes for validation on naturally occurring clinical conversations.

The evidence setting creates a further distinction. MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, DSM5AgentFlow, and conversational diagnostic AI study inquiry or interactive workflows that can seek additional information during assessment~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow,tu2025conversational}. MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels instead emphasize diagnosis from information already available in records or transcripts~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. Interactive systems combine evidence acquisition with diagnostic reasoning, whereas fixed-input systems must interpret the same potentially incomplete evidence. A fixed-input design therefore isolates how a model uses the available transcript without attributing gains to additional questioning.

Existing evaluations also differ in what they make observable. Final-label accuracy can show whether the emitted diagnosis agrees with the reference, but it cannot show whether a reference diagnosis was never considered, appeared among the candidates but was absent from a criterion-compatible set, or remained plausible but was not selected as primary. Some psychiatric systems expose diagnostic hypotheses, criteria-grounded rationales, or intermediate dialogue states, yet the published evaluations do not generally measure Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion in a criterion-compatible set, and committed-primary agreement together on the same fixed cases.

These limitations motivate evaluations that distinguish candidate generation, criterion support, and final diagnostic selection rather than relying only on final-label accuracy.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning}

Retrieval augmentation can serve two different roles in diagnostic systems. Knowledge retrieval supplies external documents, guidelines, or other factual resources to supplement information encoded in model parameters~\citep{lewis2020rag}. Demonstration retrieval instead selects labeled examples or similar cases as prompt context for in-context learning~\citep{liu2022incontext}. In this thesis, retrieval refers to labeled training examples used as prompt-side demonstrations, not to diagnostic guidelines or external evidence about the current patient.

Semantically similar demonstrations can expose corpus-specific expressions, the expected input--output format, examples of the diagnostic label space, and recurring symptom--diagnosis associations. In-context-learning performance, however, depends strongly on which examples are selected~\citep{liu2022incontext}. Retrieved cases describe other patients or synthetic cases; they are demonstrations rather than evidence about the current patient and should not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

The benefit of similarity retrieval is therefore not automatic. The retrieved prompt reflects the label distribution and wording patterns of the indexed corpus. Common labels may appear repeatedly, lexical overlap may encourage corpus-specific shortcuts, and a misleading or weakly related neighbor may interfere with interpretation of the current transcript. Retrieval should consequently be treated as an empirical configuration rather than an inherently beneficial component.

Label-aware balancing may increase the diagnostic diversity of the demonstrations and reduce repeated exposure to frequent classes. It does not, however, change label balance alone. A balanced policy may also alter the number, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, total token length, and context density of the examples. Comparisons with ordinary global similarity retrieval therefore estimate differences between complete retrieval configurations rather than the isolated causal effect of balancing.

Prior work establishes that demonstration choice can affect in-context learning, but it remains unclear how retrieval policies behave in fine-grained psychiatric diagnosis. These findings indicate that retrieval policies should be evaluated under matched conditions, particularly when label imbalance and prompt composition may affect the retrieved demonstrations.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

Medical multi-agent systems use several forms of orchestration. Role-specialized decomposition assigns distinct functions such as diagnosis, evidence extraction, critique, or final decision-making. Deliberative systems combine proposals through consultation, debate, voting, or consensus, while verification-oriented systems separate an initial answer from later checking or revision. MedAgents uses multi-round collaboration among role-based medical experts, and MDAgents adapts the collaboration structure to estimated task complexity~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. Multiagent debate provides a related general mechanism for exchanging and revising competing answers~\citep{du2024debate}.

These arrangements offer several possible advantages. Task decomposition can focus each role on a narrower subproblem, redundant judgments may expose omissions, cross-checking can compare a proposed diagnosis with supporting evidence or criteria, and structured aggregation can preserve intermediate outputs before a final decision. These are design opportunities rather than guarantees: an increase in the number of agents or interaction rounds does not by itself establish better diagnostic accuracy.

Multi-agent systems also introduce characteristic failure modes. Agents that share a backbone model, similar prompts, and the same evidence may produce correlated rather than independent errors. An incorrect upstream candidate or evidence statement can propagate into later critique and selection. Free-form interaction may change several parts of the reasoning process at once, and a final aggregator may still choose an incorrect answer even when individual agents have surfaced useful alternatives.

These dependencies create an attribution problem. When candidate generation, evidence representation, inter-agent discussion, and aggregation all differ between systems, a change in final accuracy is a package-level effect rather than evidence that one component caused the difference. Comparisons with a Single model may also differ in prompt structure, context, inference calls, and compute. Final-answer evaluation alone therefore provides limited information about where a multi-agent configuration helped or failed.

These limitations call for controlled evaluations in which candidate generation, verification, and final aggregation are recorded separately, allowing performance changes to be attributed to specific functions rather than to the multi-agent system as a whole.

\section{Auditability and Criterion Grounding}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

A fluent explanation is not proof of faithful reasoning. Model-generated chains can sound coherent while not reflecting the factors that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. In this thesis, \emph{explainability} refers to a human-facing account of a decision, \emph{interpretability} to how directly a person can understand the decision mechanism, \emph{faithfulness} to whether a stated account reflects the factors that produced the output, and \emph{auditability} to the availability of an external record that can be inspected and replayed. These properties require separate evaluation in high-stakes healthcare settings~\citep{rudin2019stop,huang2024xiai}.

Diagnostic-chain and symptom-based methods can expose intermediate hypotheses, confidence values, or clinically motivated features~\citep{chen2025cod,lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. Psychiatric systems may further ground outputs in diagnostic guidelines, structured interviews, symptom-to-criterion mappings, and supporting or contradictory evidence~\citep{li2026mind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. Constraint-logic approaches make the rule layer more explicit by translating diagnostic criteria into inspectable programs~\citep{kim2025clp}. Together, these approaches make parts of the diagnostic process more accessible to external review.

Criterion grounding nevertheless depends on the quality of the operationalized criteria and the mapping from transcript text to criterion states. A generated evidence statement may be unsupported, a criterion may be represented incorrectly, and an inspectable rule may still implement an inappropriate clinical assumption. The need for expert inspection of model-generated diagnostic rules illustrates this limitation~\citep{kim2025clp}. Criteria-grounded outputs are therefore reviewable model artifacts rather than clinician-validated evidence or clinical ground truth.

An auditable diagnostic system should retain an external record linking source evidence, criterion states, rule results, intermediate diagnostic outputs, and the final decision. Such a record does not reveal hidden model reasoning. A structured criterion layer may be used either to audit a model-generated diagnosis or to participate in final diagnosis selection, and these roles should be evaluated separately because successful evidence recording or compatibility assessment does not imply successful primary selection.

Among the studies reviewed in this thesis, we did not identify an evaluation that separately measures Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion in a criterion-compatible set, and committed-primary agreement on the same fixed Chinese psychiatric transcripts. This evaluation gap motivates a stage-wise design that separates evidence auditing from diagnosis selection; it is not a claim that prior systems lack diagnostic criteria or intermediate reasoning outputs.

\section{Primary-Diagnosis Selection}

Criterion compatibility and primary-diagnosis selection answer different questions. Criterion compatibility asks whether each diagnosis is individually consistent with the available evidence under a defined rule. Because psychiatric disorders can share symptoms and more than one diagnosis may remain compatible, this process does not by itself determine whether a diagnosis is primary, comorbid, or a differential alternative. Selecting one committed primary therefore requires comparison among the remaining diagnoses rather than independent criterion satisfaction alone.

Structured diagnostic knowledge offers mechanisms for organizing the evidence used in this comparison. MedKGI combines a medical knowledge graph, information-guided inquiry, and a structured dialogue state to obtain discriminative information during iterative diagnosis~\citep{wang2025medkgi}. DKEC constructs heterogeneous knowledge graphs from external medical sources and incorporates their relations into multi-label diagnosis prediction~\citep{ge2024dkec}. These systems show how structured representations can support evidence acquisition or label prediction, but their evaluated objectives differ from selecting one primary diagnosis among several criterion-compatible psychiatric alternatives in a fixed transcript.

A comparative selector may need to represent symptom--diagnosis relations together with temporal order, duration, illness course, exclusionary causes, syndrome dominance, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity. The reviewed structured methods may organize evidence or identify plausible labels, but they do not directly evaluate whether a criterion-supported diagnosis remains available yet is not selected when one primary diagnosis is committed. This gap motivates evaluation that separates Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion in the criterion-compatible set, and committed-primary agreement.

\section{Research Gap and Research Questions}

Taken together, the reviewed literature shows that LLM diagnosis, retrieved demonstrations, multi-agent orchestration, and criterion grounding can each support psychiatric diagnosis. However, the studies use different case sets, label mappings, endpoints, and observable intermediate outputs. They therefore do not by themselves establish how Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion in a criterion-compatible set, and committed-primary agreement relate on the same cases.

The reviewed literature consequently leaves five unresolved questions that are directly relevant to this thesis. First, heterogeneous task definitions and complete-system differences complicate comparisons among conventional classifiers, single LLMs, and multi-stage systems under one split, label mapping, and primary endpoint. Second, final-label accuracy does not show whether a benchmark diagnosis was absent from the ranked candidates, was absent from the criterion-compatible set, or remained available but was not selected as the committed primary. Third, the effect of demonstration retrieval depends on example selection, requiring matched comparison of global similarity and label-aware balanced policies across Single and multi-stage architectures. Fourth, multi-agent and criterion-based interventions often change several stages or inference conditions at once, limiting attribution of final-performance differences to primary selection. Fifth, the persistence of the main findings across model sizes, a second synthetic corpus, changes in diagnostic scope, and cross-corpus transfer, together with the remaining error patterns, requires separate boundary analysis. HiED examines these questions by recording candidate ranking, criterion verification, compatibility, and final selection as separately observable outputs under declared comparison contracts.

This thesis therefore uses five research questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?
\item[RQ2] How often is a benchmark diagnosis present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?
\item[RQ3] How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?
\item[RQ4] Under the evaluated settings, do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve committed Top-1 agreement over Direct-Answer?
\item[RQ5] To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and which error patterns characterize the remaining disagreements?
\end{description}
These RQs define computational analyses on synthetic benchmark data. RQ1 and RQ3 use matched comparisons on the same fixed internal test set, with common label projection and evaluation code. RQ5 combines complementary sensitivity, external synthetic, scope, transfer, and error-pattern analyses rather than one statistical hypothesis. Clinical effectiveness and criterion-level clinical validity are not answered by the completed computational RQs; the LingxiDiag pilot review and CGMH real-world validation are separate ongoing clinical studies.

\section{Input and Diagnostic Scope}
\label{sec:input-diagnostic-scope}

HiED processes one Chinese psychiatric consultation transcript at a time. The input is an ordered sequence of turns, each marked as \texttt{Clinician} or \texttt{Patient}; speaker labels and turn order are preserved in the prompt. Transcripts longer than the available prompt budget are deterministically truncated by retaining complete turns from the beginning and end, with an explicit marker for the omitted middle. The system does not summarize or invent missing text. This is a fixed-input study: the system cannot ask the patient a follow-up question during inference.

ICD-10 is the diagnostic coding system used to name the disorders in this study~\citep{who2019icd10}. The system does not search all ICD-10 diagnoses. Before a run begins, it loads one fixed \emph{configured diagnostic profile} that is used for every patient. The main diagnostic profile contains fourteen configured diagnostic categories. F41 and F43 include subtype-level categories, so the fourteen categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels for scoring.

The following terms define the diagnostic scope and criterion-checking process used in HiED. A \emph{configured diagnostic category} is an ICD-10 code that the Diagnostician may rank and for which the system provides a corresponding Criterion Checker. The fourteen category-specific checkers together form the \emph{criterion-checker set}.

For each criterion, the checker assigns one of three model-generated evidence states: \texttt{met} when the transcript provides supporting evidence, \texttt{not\_met} when it provides contradictory evidence, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the available information does not support either conclusion. The checker definitions and compatibility rules are study-specific operationalizations informed by ICD-10 descriptions. They do not reproduce the full official ICD-10 manual and have not been independently validated as clinical ground truth.

\section{Candidate Generation and Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

This section describes the two candidate-generation components shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}: the Similar-Case Retriever and the Diagnostician.

\paragraph{Similar-Case Retriever.}

The Similar-Case Retriever optionally selects training-set cases that are similar to the current transcript and supplies them to the Diagnostician as prompt-side demonstrations, following the retrieval-augmented generation pattern~\citep{lewis2020rag}. The study evaluates three conditions: no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Global Top-5 returns the five most similar eligible cases. Parent-balanced retrieval selects at most one example for each of the eleven configured scoring parents and can therefore return up to eleven examples.

The parent groups used for retrieval are fixed by the configured diagnostic profile rather than generated separately for each patient. Only the selected example within each group depends on the current transcript. Subtypes that share a scoring parent, such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2, also share one retrieval group. Parent-balanced retrieval may reduce repeated examples from frequent labels, but it also changes the number, label composition, ordering, and total token length of the demonstrations. The retrieval comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating balancing as a single causal factor.

Retrieved cases are provided only to the Diagnostician. They are not included in the Criterion Checker prompts, do not constitute evidence about the current patient, and do not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

\paragraph{Diagnostician.}

The Diagnostician receives the speaker-labeled transcript, the configured diagnostic profile, and any retrieved examples. It produces three separately recorded outputs: a ranked list of five candidate diagnoses, one emitted primary diagnosis selected from the three highest-ranked candidates, and at most one optional comorbid diagnosis. The ranked list represents the model's differential diagnosis, whereas the primary field records the diagnosis that becomes committed under the Direct-Answer policy.

The ranked candidates, emitted primary diagnosis, and optional comorbidity are stored separately. A diagnosis may therefore remain present among the leading candidates even when another diagnosis is emitted as primary. This distinction allows candidate coverage and primary selection to be evaluated independently.

\section{Criterion Verification and Compatibility Aggregation}

This section describes the two criterion-processing components shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}: the Criterion Checker and the Compatibility Auditor.

\paragraph{Criterion Checker.}

Each of the fourteen configured diagnostic categories has a corresponding Criterion Checker based on the study's operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria~\citep{who2019icd10}. Every checker receives the same speaker-labeled transcript together with the criteria defined for one diagnostic category. Retrieved examples are not included in checker prompts, and the checkers do not communicate with one another.

For each criterion, the checker assigns one of the three criterion states defined in Section~\ref{sec:input-diagnostic-scope}: \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}. It also returns a short supporting-text field, which may be \texttt{null} when no explicit supporting text is identified. Missing information is not negative evidence: an unresolved criterion remains \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} rather than \texttt{not\_met}. All fourteen configured categories are checked for every case, so criterion verification is not limited to diagnoses in the ranked Top-5.

\paragraph{Compatibility Auditor.}

The Compatibility Auditor is a deterministic module that converts the criterion-level states produced by the diagnosis-specific Criterion Checkers into diagnosis-level compatibility decisions. It does not independently interpret the transcript or revise the checker judgments. For each configured diagnostic category, the auditor applies the predefined operational rule associated with that category. Depending on the diagnosis, a rule may require particular core criteria, a minimum number of total criteria, specified criterion groups, or all listed requirements. A diagnosis is added to the \emph{criterion-compatible set} only when its configured rule is satisfied.

The auditor records the applied rule, the relevant criterion counts, and the resulting compatibility decision in the compatibility-rule trace. Because the aggregation is deterministic, the same set of checker-generated criterion states always produces the same criterion-compatible set. The complete category-specific rules are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:configured-profile}.

\paragraph{Interpretation boundary.}

Criterion states and supporting text are model-generated and have not been validated as clinician ground truth. The supporting text is retained for inspection but is not guaranteed to be a verbatim or span-aligned extract or a clinically validated evidence annotation. Criterion compatibility is an operational property under the study configuration; it does not establish that a diagnosis is clinically correct, uniquely supported, or the most appropriate primary diagnosis. Several diagnoses may therefore be included in the criterion-compatible set for the same transcript.

\section{Primary Selection and Finalization}

This section describes the finalization policies and the Committed Diagnosis output shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}. Both policies are evaluated from the same recorded Diagnostician and criterion-processing outputs, but they use these outputs differently. Finalization does not introduce an additional LLM call.

\paragraph{Direct-Answer.}

Direct-Answer (DA) preserves the Diagnostician's emitted decision. The emitted primary field becomes the committed primary diagnosis; if this field is missing, the first-ranked candidate is used as a fallback. DA also retains at most one optional comorbid diagnosis when it differs from the primary. Criterion Checker states and Compatibility Auditor outputs remain available as audit artifacts but do not overwrite the DA committed diagnosis.

\paragraph{Nominate-then-Select.}

Nominate-then-Select (NtS) is a deterministic alternative that uses the recorded candidate ranking and criterion-compatible set to choose one committed primary diagnosis. For configured diagnostic category $d$, the criterion-support ratio is
\[
\operatorname{met\_ratio}(d)=\min\!\left(1,\frac{\text{number of \texttt{met} criteria for }d}{\text{required criterion count for }d}\right).
\]
The denominator is the minimum satisfying count defined by the configured operational rule rather than the total number of listed criteria. NtS first selects the highest-ranked criterion-compatible diagnosis within the ranked Top-5. If none of the ranked candidates is criterion-compatible, it selects from the remaining criterion-compatible diagnoses by descending $\operatorname{met\_ratio}$, with ties resolved by the predefined configured-category order. If the criterion-compatible set is empty, NtS falls back to the first-ranked diagnosis.

\paragraph{Output contracts.}

DA produces one committed primary diagnosis and may retain one optional comorbid diagnosis. NtS produces one re-selected committed primary diagnosis and does not emit a comorbid diagnosis. Thus, the two policies differ in both their primary-selection rule and final output cardinality.

\section{Configured Output Space and Gold-Label Projection}
\label{sec:gold-label-conventions}

Throughout this thesis, the dataset-provided diagnoses used for scoring are referred to as \emph{benchmark gold labels}. The term \emph{gold} denotes the official benchmark reference and does not imply a uniquely correct clinical diagnosis.

The main system uses fourteen configured ICD-10 diagnostic categories, listed in Table~\ref{tab:configured-profile-summary}. For evaluation, subtype-level categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels. Let $G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x)$ be the raw benchmark gold codes for case $x$, let $\pi$ map subtype codes such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2 to their scoring parent, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of eleven configured scoring parents. The in-scope gold-label set is
\[
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\{\pi(c):c\in G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x),\ \pi(c)\in\mathcal{C}\}.
\]
The scored gold-label set is then
\[
G(x)=
\begin{cases}
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x), & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)\neq\varnothing,\\
\{\mathrm{Others}\}, & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\varnothing.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus, a mixed case such as F90 with F41.1 is scored as $\{\mathrm{F41}\}$ rather than $\{\mathrm{Others},\mathrm{F41}\}$. A case is in scope when $G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)$ is nonempty and out of scope otherwise. Because the main system has neither an \emph{Others} output nor a trained unknown detector, it cannot correctly represent an out-of-scope case.

For case $x$, $G(x)$ is the deduplicated gold-label set after parent-level projection. LingxiDiag cases may contain more than one gold parent label, and neither dataset provides a psychiatrist-reviewed unique clinical primary diagnosis. The first-listed gold parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses such as the confusion matrix.

\section{Datasets, Case Construction, and Split Roles}

The study uses two synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogue datasets. LingxiDiag-16K, the dataset underlying LingxiDiagBench, provides the source data for development and internal held-out evaluation, whereas MDD-5k is used for external cross-dataset evaluation.

\subsection{LingxiDiag-16K}

LingxiDiag-16K is the synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogue dataset underlying LingxiDiagBench and was generated for static diagnostic classification and interactive consultation tasks~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. It provides a fixed diagnostic label space and sufficient cases for training, configuration selection, and internal held-out evaluation. The published LingxiDiagBench results also show that fine-grained disorder-level diagnosis is substantially more difficult than broad depression--anxiety classification.

The accessible release used in this study contained 14,000 training-source cases and 1,000 public-validation cases. The official test file was not available in the accessible release used when the experiments were designed. To obtain a held-out internal evaluation set, 1,000 cases were separated from the 14,000-case training source before the main experiments. The remaining 13,000 cases were used to train conventional baselines and build the retrieval index.

The internal held-out set was stratified to approximate the public-validation distribution using the first-listed projected parent label and a fixed random seed. The first-listed label was used only as a sampling variable and was not treated as a psychiatrist-validated clinical primary diagnosis. Exact case-ID checks confirmed that held-out cases were excluded from the training and retrieval pools. No separate semantic near-duplicate screen was performed. The held-out cases were excluded from all reported configuration-selection procedures.

\subsection{MDD-5k}

MDD-5k is a synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogue dataset generated from anonymized psychiatric case information through a neuro-symbolic multi-agent process~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. Some patient files contain multiple dialogue rounds. The study adapter concatenates these rounds in their recorded order while preserving doctor and patient turns, and each patient file is treated as one evaluation case.

The evaluated release contains 925 patient-level cases. Under HiED's main configured output space defined in Section~\ref{sec:gold-label-conventions}, a case is defined as \emph{in scope} when at least one projected benchmark gold diagnosis can be emitted by the system and as \emph{out of scope} when none of its projected gold diagnoses is represented in that output space. Among the 925 external cases, 878 are in scope and the remaining 47 are out of scope. Because MDD-5k transcripts are generally longer than those in LingxiDiag-16K, the same prompt-budget rule defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} is applied to the external evaluation. MDD-5k is used as an external synthetic benchmark and does not constitute validation on real patient transcripts.

\section{Prediction Views and Metrics}

The evaluator keeps four recorded output views separate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item The \emph{primary view}, $p_1(x)$, is the committed primary diagnosis.
\item The \emph{ranked view}, $R_3(x)$, contains the first three distinct parent labels in the method's genuine ranked output. For HiED, it is taken from the recorded Diagnostician ranking and is independent of later DA or NtS finalization.
\item The \emph{compatibility view}, $L(x)$, is the set of scoring parent labels classified as criterion-compatible by the Compatibility Auditor under the configured operational rules.
\item The \emph{multilabel view}, $M(x)$, contains the committed primary diagnosis and any emitted comorbid parent label.
\end{enumerate}

TF--IDF + logistic regression obtains its Top-3 from ranked class scores. HiED obtains it from the Diagnostician ranking. A Single system without a genuine ranked output is not assigned an artificial Top-3.

Because a case may contain more than one gold label, Top-$k$ Accuracy counts a case as correct when at least one gold label appears among the first $k$ predictions. Exact Match, also called subset accuracy, requires the complete predicted label set to equal the complete gold-label set. These are benchmark-agreement measures, not independent measures of the patient's true clinical diagnosis.

For example, suppose that the projected gold-label set contains F41, while the system ranks F32 first and F41.1 second. Because F41.1 is projected to its scoring parent F41, the case is incorrect under committed Top-1 evaluation but correct under Top-3 evaluation.

DA may emit a committed primary diagnosis and one optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits one committed primary diagnosis. Their Top-1 comparison therefore evaluates the committed primary directly, but differences in Exact Match, Macro-F1, or Weighted-F1 also reflect output cardinality and cannot be interpreted as isolated effects of primary re-selection.

Table~\ref{tab:metric-contract} summarizes which recorded output view is evaluated by each measure. The subsequent equations provide the formal case-level definitions used by the scoring implementation.

\section{Disagreement-Localization Indicators}

The thesis defines three disagreement-localization indicators. Top-3 candidate detection and committed-primary agreement are defined for all evaluation cases, whereas gold-label inclusion is defined only for checker-eligible cases.

For case $x$,
\[
D_3(x)=\mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
R(x)=\mathbb{1}[L(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
S(x)=\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
where $L(x)$ is the set of scoring parent labels classified as criterion-compatible by the Compatibility Auditor. $D_3(x)$ indicates whether at least one benchmark gold parent appears in the genuine ranked Top-3, $R(x)$ indicates whether the criterion-compatible set contains at least one benchmark gold parent, and $S(x)$ indicates whether the committed primary diagnosis matches at least one benchmark gold parent.

$D_3$ and $S$ are computed over all 1,000 internal evaluation cases. $R$ is computed only for the 915 checker-eligible cases whose gold-label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} parent represented by a corresponding Criterion Checker. The resulting rates therefore use different denominators and should not be interpreted as consecutive stages of a single case-level funnel.

These indicators provide complementary views of the recorded outputs for the same case: Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion in the criterion-compatible set, and committed-primary agreement. They do not imply that every finalization policy executes these operations in that chronological order.

\section{Statistical Analysis}

The case is the unit of analysis, and all statistical tests are two-sided with a significance level of $\alpha=0.05$. When confidence intervals are reported for a single binary proportion, 95\% Wilson intervals are used. Paired comparisons require identical case IDs and endpoint definitions. For binary correctness outcomes, the effect estimate is the paired difference in correctness proportions, and the exact McNemar test is computed from the two discordant counts: cases correct only under the first configuration and cases correct only under the second. Main paired comparisons report the effect estimate and its confidence interval; detailed discordant counts and $p$-values are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

Confidence intervals for paired accuracy differences and finalization difference-in-differences contrasts are estimated by resampling case IDs with replacement using 10,000 paired bootstrap replicates and seed 42. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles define the reported 95\% confidence interval. Reported $p$-values are interpreted together with effect direction, confidence intervals, and discordant counts rather than as standalone evidence.

Holm correction is applied when multiple prespecified tests belong to the same comparison family defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}; unrelated or isolated comparisons are not pooled into an artificial family. A non-significant result indicates insufficient evidence of a difference and is not interpreted as evidence of equivalence. When compared configurations also differ in context length, number of model calls, output contract, or other execution settings, the paired result is interpreted as a comparison of complete configurations rather than as the isolated effect of one component.

\section{Retrieval-Configuration Selection and Benchmark Positioning}
\label{sec:benchmark-retrieval-design}

This section corresponds to the first two computational blocks in Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-sequence}. Retrieval configurations are first compared and selected separately for the Single and HiED--DA architectures using the public-validation split (RQ3). The selected configurations are then frozen and evaluated on the fixed internal held-out set for same-split benchmark positioning (RQ1). Both analyses use the parent-label projection and evaluation measures defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}.

\subsection{Retrieval-Configuration Selection (RQ3)}
Retrieval is evaluated separately within the Single and HiED--DA architectures under three conditions: no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Within each architecture, the backbone model, prompt family, configured diagnostic scope, decoding settings, and output contract remain fixed. The same public-validation cases are evaluated under all three retrieval conditions.

The public-validation split is used to select one retrieval configuration separately for Single and HiED--DA according to the predefined promotion rule. Once selected, each configuration is frozen before evaluation on the internal held-out set. Validation results are therefore used for configuration selection and are not interpreted as independent test estimates. Held-out results are not used to revise the selected configurations.

Global Top-5 and parent-balanced retrieval differ not only in label balance but also in the number, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, and total token length of the retrieved demonstrations. Their comparison is therefore interpreted as a comparison of complete retrieval configurations, rather than as the isolated causal effect of parent balancing.

\subsection{Same-Split Benchmark Positioning (RQ1)}
After retrieval-configuration selection, the validation-selected Single and HiED--DA configurations are evaluated together with the Majority baseline and TF--IDF with logistic regression on the same fixed 1,000-case internal held-out set. All methods use the same parent-label projection and scoring implementation.

Committed Top-1 Accuracy is the common primary endpoint because every evaluated configuration produces a committed primary diagnosis. Genuine Top-3 and multilabel measures are reported only when the corresponding output view is available. In particular, Single produces a committed primary diagnosis and may emit an optional comorbid diagnosis, but it does not produce a genuine ordered differential. Its outputs are therefore not converted into an artificial Top-3 ranking.

The Majority baseline predicts the most frequent scoring parent in the training source. TF--IDF with logistic regression follows the prespecified character-level, one-vs-rest implementation described in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

This analysis compares complete diagnostic configurations under the same held-out evaluation contract; it is not a component-controlled architecture ablation. The conventional classifier, Single, and HiED differ in training procedure, retrieval exposure, number of model calls, intermediate outputs, and output contract. Performance differences are therefore interpreted at the complete-configuration level rather than as the isolated effect of multi-agent orchestration.

\section{Stage-Wise Disagreement Localization}
\label{sec:stage-wise-analysis-design}

This analysis addresses RQ2 using the case-level trace from the validation-selected HiED--DA configuration with parent-balanced retrieval on the fixed 1,000-case internal held-out set. The trace contains the ranked differential, criterion states, criterion-compatible set, and committed primary diagnosis required to compare the recorded output views. In this section, \emph{disagreement} refers to disagreement with the benchmark gold-label set under the defined scoring contract, rather than an independently confirmed clinical diagnostic error.

The analysis uses Top-3 candidate detection $D_3$, gold-label inclusion $R$, and committed-primary agreement $S$, as defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}. Gold-label inclusion indicates whether the criterion-compatible set contains at least one benchmark gold parent. $D_3$ and $S$ are available for all 1,000 internal cases. $R$ is defined only for the 915 checker-eligible cases whose benchmark gold-label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} scoring parent represented by a corresponding Criterion Checker. Analyses involving all three indicators are therefore restricted to these 915 cases.

For the checker-eligible analysis, cases are partitioned into four mutually exclusive recorded-output groups:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item committed-primary agreement, defined by $S=1$;
\item Top-3 candidate miss, defined by $D_3=0$ and $S=0$;
\item ranked, but absent from the criterion-compatible set, defined by $D_3=1$, $R=0$, and $S=0$; and
\item ranked and criterion-compatible, but not selected, defined by $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$.
\end{enumerate}

Cases with $S=1$ are grouped together regardless of gold-label inclusion because the partition is designed to localize cases with committed-primary disagreement. The fourth group is the main profile for cases in which a benchmark gold parent appears in both the genuine Top-3 and criterion-compatible set but is not committed as primary.

These indicators describe separately recorded output views rather than a chronological funnel through which every case passes. Under Direct-Answer, the committed diagnosis is taken from the Diagnostician, while the Criterion Checkers independently evaluate the complete configured diagnostic scope rather than only the ranked candidates. The group labels therefore describe observable output patterns and do not by themselves identify the clinical or causal source of a disagreement.

\paragraph{Supplementary upper-bound analysis.}
A gold-informed oracle examines a broader set of ranked and criterion-compatible alternatives to estimate optimistic primary-selection headroom. Because the oracle uses benchmark labels to identify favorable candidates and assumes that eligible disagreements can be corrected without harming initially benchmark-consistent predictions, it is treated as a non-deployable upper bound rather than an implemented selector or the primary answer to RQ2. Its candidate filter, thresholds, and sensitivity analyses are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Primary-Selection Intervention Design}
\label{sec:primary-selection-intervention-design}

RQ4 evaluates whether alternative finalization and primary-selection procedures improve committed-primary benchmark agreement relative to Direct-Answer (DA). Committed Top-1 Accuracy is the primary endpoint because all evaluated methods produce one committed primary diagnosis.

\paragraph{Primary matched finalization comparison.}
The prespecified primary comparison evaluates DA against Nominate-then-Select (NtS). Within each retrieval condition, both finalization policies reuse the same Diagnostician ranking, Criterion Checker states, and criterion-compatible set. DA preserves the Diagnostician's emitted primary diagnosis, whereas NtS applies the deterministic finalization rule defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}.

DA and NtS are compared on the same 1,000 internal held-out cases under no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. The primary effect estimate is the paired difference in committed Top-1 correctness. Paired-bootstrap confidence intervals and exact McNemar tests are computed as defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}.

Because DA and NtS share the same upstream artifacts and NtS introduces no additional LLM inference, this comparison provides the clearest evidence about the evaluated deterministic finalization rule. It does not establish the effectiveness of criterion-based primary-selection methods in general.

\paragraph{Supporting recorded-output method.}
Deterministic fusion combines recorded candidate rank, compatibility status, and criterion-support summaries before deciding whether to replace the DA primary diagnosis. It reuses existing system artifacts but evaluates one predefined combination rule rather than a general fusion framework.

\paragraph{Supporting additional-inference methods.}
Pairwise comparison and debate introduce additional LLM inference to compare plausible diagnostic alternatives. These methods may differ from DA in prompt structure, context organization, number of inference calls, decoding behavior, and final decision procedure. Their results are therefore interpreted as complete-configuration comparisons rather than isolated effects of primary selection.

\paragraph{Supporting repeated-generation methods.}
Self-consistency generates repeated Diagnostician outputs and selects the diagnosis receiving the largest number of primary votes. Confidence-informed self-consistency uses the same samples but weights the votes by the confidence recorded in each output. These analyses test whether repeated model preferences produce more stable committed-primary agreement; they do not validate the model's reported confidence or constitute a fixed-artifact finalization comparison.

\paragraph{Comparison and reporting boundaries.}
Additional internal interventions use the validation-selected parent-balanced DA configuration as the reference unless otherwise specified. Paired effects and confidence intervals are reported only when the compared methods use identical case IDs and complete case-level outputs. When prompts, contexts, inference budgets, or output-generation procedures differ, results are interpreted as comparisons of declared complete configurations.

Committed Top-1 Accuracy is the common endpoint across the evaluated primary-selection methods. Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 are not used to isolate primary-selection effects because DA may emit an optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas several alternative methods emit only one committed diagnosis. Detailed eligibility rules, inference budgets, seed schedules, and supporting configurations are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Sensitivity and Transfer Analyses}
\label{sec:sensitivity-transfer-design}

RQ5 examines whether the main internal findings remain observable under changes in model scale, evaluation corpus, and configured diagnostic scope, and whether corpus-specific lexical representations transfer across the two synthetic datasets. These analyses provide sensitivity and boundary evidence under the tested conditions rather than establishing general robustness, clinical generalizability, or transfer across unrelated model families and real-world populations.

\paragraph{Within-family model-scale sensitivity.}
Model-scale sensitivity is evaluated using Qwen3-4B, 8B, 14B, and 32B on the same fixed 1,000-case internal held-out set. The prompt family, configured diagnostic scope, retrieval policy, decoding contract, and evaluation endpoints are held constant across model sizes. Committed Top-1 Accuracy, genuine Top-3 Accuracy, structured-output failures, and abstention events are recorded.

This comparison examines whether candidate availability and committed-primary agreement depend strongly on one Qwen3 model size. Because all evaluated models belong to the same model family and serving setup, the analysis does not establish sensitivity across unrelated architectures, training procedures, or deployment environments.

\paragraph{External synthetic evaluation.}
MDD-5k is used for external cross-dataset evaluation under a second Chinese synthetic dialogue-generation process with different transcript characteristics, label frequencies, and diagnostic coverage. The primary multi-class analyses use the complete 925-case evaluation set and the 878-case in-scope subset for which at least one benchmark gold diagnosis is represented in HiED's main configured output space.

A separate 490-case F32-versus-F41 subset is evaluated as a secondary binary analysis and is not treated as the main external performance estimate. The available external trace supports committed Top-1 Accuracy, genuine Top-3 Accuracy, comparison between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement, and same-trace primary-selection comparisons. It does not contain matched gold-label-inclusion outputs, so the complete internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis cannot be reproduced on MDD-5k.

\paragraph{Configuration-only diagnostic-scope expansion.}
A configuration-level experiment expands the diagnostic profile from fourteen to 33 configured diagnostic categories by adding category-specific checker definitions without updating the model weights. The expanded configuration is evaluated separately and is not used in the primary external analysis.

This experiment distinguishes output-space representability from behavioral recovery. It tests whether making a benchmark diagnosis available within the configured diagnostic profile is sufficient for that diagnosis to appear in the ranked candidates or become the committed primary diagnosis. It does not establish the general effectiveness, clinical validity, or safety of diagnostic-scope expansion.

\paragraph{Cross-corpus lexical transfer.}
Cross-corpus lexical transfer is evaluated using the same TF--IDF classifier family trained on one synthetic corpus and tested both within that corpus and on the other corpus. The analysis examines whether corpus-derived lexical associations with benchmark labels remain predictive under a different synthetic generation process.

This experiment evaluates the portability of a lexical representation. It does not evaluate transfer of the complete HiED architecture or establish the clinical validity of the underlying diagnostic labels.

\paragraph{Descriptive diagnostic error patterns.}
Remaining benchmark disagreements are characterized using first-listed-label confusion patterns, frequent benchmark-to-predicted diagnosis pairs, and the stage-wise output profiles defined by $D_3$, $R$, and $S$. The confusion matrix uses one first-listed projected benchmark parent per case and is treated as a descriptive visualization rather than the primary multilabel endpoint.

Criterion-state, compatibility, and stage-wise output analyses are used to describe where disagreement is recorded in the evaluated system. They do not identify the clinical cause of an individual disagreement or establish that the benchmark diagnosis is clinically preferable.

The model-scale and MDD-5k analyses provide the primary evidence for RQ5. Diagnostic-scope expansion, cross-corpus lexical transfer, and descriptive error-pattern analyses are interpreted as supporting boundary analyses.

\section{Complementary Clinical Evaluation Plan}
\label{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan}

The complementary clinical evaluation comprises two ongoing studies conducted separately from the completed computational RQs. The LingxiDiag pilot uses twenty selected disagreement cases from the internal held-out set for blinded review by two psychiatrists. The CGMH study evaluates real-world psychiatric consultations by comparing HiED outputs with diagnoses recorded during routine clinical care and with a blinded psychiatrist transcript-only reference. The two studies use different populations and reference conditions; their findings will therefore be analyzed separately and are not included in the completed computational results reported in this thesis.

\subsection{LingxiDiag Pilot Psychiatrist Review}
\label{sec:lingxidiag-pilot-review}

The LingxiDiag pilot evaluates the feasibility of the psychiatrist-review procedure and provides independent clinical characterization of a selected synthetic disagreement population. It is not a real-patient clinical validation study and is not designed to estimate overall clinical diagnostic accuracy.

Twenty cases are sampled without replacement from the 272 checker-eligible internal cases satisfying $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$. In these cases, at least one benchmark gold parent appears in the genuine Top-3 and is also present in the criterion-compatible set, but another diagnosis is committed as primary. The random seed and selected case identifiers are fixed before psychiatrist review.

Two psychiatrists independently review all twenty cases in separately randomized orders. They remain blinded to the benchmark labels, HiED outputs, computational disagreement profile, case-selection rationale, and the other reviewer's responses.

The review records both provisional and final diagnostic judgments. Reviewers first read the transcript and record whether the available information supports a unique primary diagnosis, their provisional primary diagnosis, possible comorbid diagnoses, and up to three ranked differential diagnoses. They then label the prespecified canonical evidence items and diagnosis-specific qualifiers as \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}. After completing the evidence annotation, reviewers may revise their diagnostic judgments. Both the provisional and final responses are retained.

Shared symptom and impairment concepts are annotated once, whereas diagnosis-specific duration, frequency, temporal, relational, threshold, and exclusion conditions remain separate. The mapping between the physician-facing evidence items and the original Criterion Checker items is defined and frozen before annotation.

Benchmark labels and HiED outputs are not disclosed during independent review. After both reviewers complete their final independent assessments, the research team compares the provisional and final psychiatrist judgments with the LingxiDiag benchmark labels, HiED ranked and committed diagnoses, Criterion Checker states, and deterministic compatibility decisions.

Pre-consensus inter-rater agreement is retained. Any subsequent consensus or adjudication is conducted using the transcript and the independent annotations while the benchmark labels and HiED outputs remain concealed. The planned pilot analyses include agreement on the final transcript-only primary diagnosis, ranked differential coverage, criterion-state agreement, inter-rater agreement, changes between provisional and final diagnoses, and agreement between psychiatrist-derived and system-derived compatibility decisions. Because the pilot contains only twenty synthetic cases selected from one disagreement population, its findings are interpreted as feasibility and exploratory agreement evidence rather than population-level clinical validation.

\subsection{Ongoing CGMH Real-World Validation}
\label{sec:cgmh-validation}

The CGMH study is an ongoing real-world validation using psychiatric consultations included according to prespecified clinical eligibility and inclusion procedures. Cases are not selected according to whether HiED agrees with the recorded diagnosis. The model checkpoint, prompts, retrieval configuration, diagnostic scope, criterion definitions, compatibility rules, transcript-preprocessing procedure, and evaluation implementation are fixed before outcome analysis.

The CGMH evaluation contains three separate analyses.

First, HiED predictions are compared with diagnoses recorded during routine clinical care. This analysis measures agreement with the clinical record. The recorded diagnosis may incorporate information unavailable in the transcript provided to HiED, including prior history, longitudinal follow-up, collateral information, laboratory findings, and other clinical observations. Disagreement is therefore not automatically interpreted as a system error.

Second, two psychiatrists independently review the same transcript version and diagnostic scope provided to HiED. They remain blinded to the recorded clinical diagnosis, HiED outputs, and each other's responses. As in the LingxiDiag pilot, provisional diagnostic judgments are recorded before the evidence annotation, and reviewers may revise them afterward. Both provisional and final judgments are retained.

After independent review, disagreements are resolved through a prespecified consensus or adjudication procedure while the recorded clinical diagnosis and HiED outputs remain concealed. This process produces a psychiatrist transcript-only reference under an information condition matched more closely to the system input.

HiED is compared separately with each independent reviewer and with the transcript-only reference. Planned outcomes include committed-primary agreement, ranked-differential coverage, comorbidity agreement, inter-rater agreement, changes between provisional and final diagnoses, Criterion Checker state agreement, diagnostic-scope coverage, and agreement between system-derived and psychiatrist-evidence-derived compatibility decisions.

Third, an exploratory audit-assisted review examines how psychiatrists assess the HiED ranked differential, criterion states, supporting text, compatibility decisions, and committed diagnosis after completing the blinded transcript-only assessment and consensus or adjudication. The recorded clinical diagnosis remains concealed during this review. Reviewers assess the consistency of the trace with the transcript, identify criterion-level errors when present, and record whether exposure to HiED changes their final blinded diagnosis.

Judgments made after disclosure of the HiED output are analyzed separately as exploratory human--AI review and usability evidence. They are not treated as independent reference diagnoses, the clinical gold standard, or proof that the audit trace improves diagnostic accuracy.

The CGMH analyses therefore distinguish agreement with the routine clinical record, agreement with a blinded transcript-only psychiatrist reference, and exploratory audit-assisted review. These analyses use different information conditions and are not combined into one overall agreement estimate. As the study is ongoing, no incomplete CGMH results contribute to the quantitative conclusions of this thesis.

\paragraph{Supplementary oracle analysis.}

A supplementary gold-informed oracle estimates an optimistic upper bound on primary-selection headroom. Criterion-supported candidates with a met-ratio of at least 0.5 are ordered by met-ratio. Adjacent candidates whose met-ratio difference is below $T=0.1$ are grouped into a close supported cluster. An initially incorrect case is counted as oracle-eligible when at least one benchmark gold parent appears in any such cluster.

Under this definition, 349 of the 1,000 cases are initially incorrect but contain a benchmark gold parent in a close supported cluster. If all 349 cases were corrected while all 518 initially correct cases remained unchanged, committed Top-1 agreement would reach the optimistic upper bound

\[
\frac{518+349}{1000}=0.867.
\]

This is not the performance of an implemented selector. The oracle uses the benchmark gold label to identify the preferred candidate, assumes that every eligible disagreement can be corrected, and assumes that no initially benchmark-consistent case is harmed. It is therefore a non-deployable, no-harm correction upper bound rather than an estimate of expected recoverable performance. The broader oracle candidate window also differs from the genuine Top-3 used to define the 284-case gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement. Full operational details and threshold sensitivity results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\paragraph{Recorded-output methods.}

Nominate-then-Select and deterministic fusion both reuse the ranked and criterion-derived outputs recorded in the common DA trace. Their differences from DA can therefore be attributed more directly to how the available outputs are converted into a committed primary diagnosis.

Both methods reduce Top-1 agreement, and their confidence intervals exclude zero. The external results therefore reinforce the internal finding that high candidate coverage and criterion support do not by themselves provide a sufficiently selective signal for deterministic primary re-selection. Reusing the criterion-compatible set or combining the recorded signals changes correct decisions as well as incorrect ones.

\paragraph{Additional-inference methods.}

The conservative pairwise gate produces no committed changes under its predefined eligibility conditions and therefore reproduces the DA result. It provides no evidence of improvement, but it also does not test the effect of pairwise comparison on a substantial changed subset.

Two-view debate performs below DA. Because this method introduces additional prompts, model calls, context, and a judging step, the result should be interpreted as the performance of the complete debate configuration rather than as the isolated causal effect of debate.

\paragraph{Sampling-based methods.}

Self-consistency and confidence-informed self-consistency produce small positive point estimates relative to DA at both sampling budgets. However, all corresponding confidence intervals include zero, so the observed differences do not provide clear evidence of an external improvement.

SC and CISC produce identical committed predictions in the retained external records. Under these runs, confidence weighting therefore does not change the decision obtained from the underlying samples. The results should not be interpreted as evidence that confidence weighting and unweighted voting are generally equivalent.

Taken together, none of the evaluated strategies provides a clear and reliable improvement over DA on MDD-5k. Recorded-output re-selection substantially reduces benchmark agreement, debate also performs below the reference configuration, and sampling-based aggregation yields only small and statistically uncertain point estimates. The external gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement identified in Section~\ref{sec:mdd-multiway} therefore represents potential decision headroom, but it does not imply that the tested selectors can recover that headroom.

Detailed discordant counts, exact McNemar tests, and paired-bootstrap results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}, Table~\ref{tab:external-paired-tests}. Set-level metrics are not used to isolate primary-selection effects because DA may emit an optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas several alternative policies emit only one committed diagnosis.

\chapter{Stage-Wise Disagreement Analysis}
\label{ch:error}

This chapter characterizes the benchmark-disagreement locations identified in Chapter~\ref{ch:results} by examining candidate ranks, diagnostic directions, criterion-state patterns, diagnostic-set construction, and selected output traces. The primary analysis uses the validation-selected parent-balanced HiED--DA trace on the fixed internal test set, with the applicable population stated for each analysis. Throughout this chapter, a \emph{disagreement} denotes non-agreement with the projected benchmark gold labels at a recorded system output; it does not establish a psychiatrist-confirmed diagnostic error or identify its clinical cause.

\section{Stage-Wise Disagreement Taxonomy}
\label{sec:ch8-stagewise-taxonomy}

This chapter separates benchmark disagreements according to three recorded output views. The indicators introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:data} are: $D_3$, which indicates whether a benchmark gold parent appears in the Diagnostician's genuine Top-3; $R$, which indicates whether the criterion-compatible set contains a benchmark gold parent; and $S$, which indicates whether the committed primary diagnosis matches a benchmark gold parent.

Top-3 candidate detection and committed-primary agreement are available for all 1,000 internal cases. Gold-label inclusion is defined only for the 915 checker-eligible cases. The joint $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis is therefore restricted to these 915 cases. The indicators describe recorded system outputs and should not be interpreted as a strictly causal runtime sequence, because Direct-Answer commits its diagnosis before the Criterion Checker is executed and the Checker evaluates the full configured diagnostic scope rather than only the ranked Top-3.

For the checker-eligible analysis, cases are divided into four mutually exclusive groups:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*),nosep]

\subsection{Top-3 Candidate Misses}

Across all 1,000 internal cases, 198 cases do not contain a benchmark gold parent in the genuine Top-3. Of these cases, 113 are checker-eligible and also have committed-primary disagreement, placing them in the mutually exclusive Top-3 candidate miss group. The other 85 cases are scored as the evaluation-only \emph{Others} category and do not have a corresponding gold-parent checker.

The criterion-compatible set contains a benchmark gold parent in 79 of these 113 cases, even though that diagnosis is absent from the Diagnostician's Top-3. In the remaining 34 cases, the benchmark gold parent is absent from both output views. This difference reflects the non-nested system design: the Diagnostician produces a limited differential, whereas the Criterion Checker evaluates the complete configured diagnostic scope.

Gold-label inclusion outside the Top-3 does not provide a deployable correction under Direct-Answer, because the committed diagnosis is selected from the Diagnostician's ranked candidates. These cases therefore remain Top-3 candidate misses under the recorded output contract.

\subsection{Ranked but Absent from the Criterion-Compatible Set}

Twenty-four of the 915 checker-eligible cases contain a benchmark gold parent in the genuine Top-3 but not in the criterion-compatible set. Twelve nevertheless commit a benchmark-consistent primary diagnosis under Direct-Answer. The other twelve form the mutually exclusive \emph{ranked, but absent from the criterion-compatible set} group.

The checker traces in these cases contain a high proportion of \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} states for criteria associated with the benchmark diagnosis. This describes how the system recorded the available evidence but does not establish whether the relevant information was absent from the transcript or whether the checker judgments were clinically correct.

The largest remaining disagreement group consists of 272 cases in which a benchmark gold parent is present in both the genuine Top-3 and criterion-compatible set, but another diagnosis is committed as primary. The following section examines this 272-case group's ranking positions, diagnostic directions, and compatibility patterns.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

The results support a stage-wise interpretation of HiED rather than an accuracy-leading claim. Its ranked, criterion, compatibility, and finalization outputs reveal where benchmark disagreement remains after a plausible diagnosis has entered the differential. The external synthetic evaluation reproduces the gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement but lacks matched gold-label-inclusion outputs, so it provides partial rather than complete replication of the internal localization.

This distinction matters because the same benchmark error can arise under different recorded conditions. A missing candidate calls for better candidate generation, absence from the criterion-compatible set calls for scrutiny of the evidence representation or compatibility rule, and a diagnosis present in both the Top-3 and criterion-compatible set but not selected calls for comparative selection. The contribution of the trace is to separate these questions without treating the recorded output views as a causal model of the language model's internal reasoning.

\section{Why Compatibility Does Not Determine Primacy}
\label{sec:discussion-primary-selection}

Candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection answer different questions. Candidate detection asks whether a plausible diagnosis appears among the leading alternatives. Criterion verification asks whether each configured diagnosis is compatible with the available transcript under its study-specific operational rule. Neither stage directly compares the plausible diagnoses to determine which one should be committed as primary.

This distinction is important because psychiatric disorders frequently share symptoms. Sleep disturbance, fatigue, impaired concentration, low mood, worry, avoidance, and functional impairment may support several diagnostic categories at the same time. A transcript may therefore be compatible with more than one diagnosis. Satisfying an individual compatibility rule does not establish whether a diagnosis is primary, secondary, comorbid, or less explanatory than another compatible alternative.

The stage-wise analysis in Chapter~\ref{ch:error} makes this limitation visible. Cases in which a benchmark gold parent is ranked and criterion-compatible but not selected often place that diagnosis near the top of the differential. At the same time, compatible-set size and aggregate proportions of \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} states are similar between these cases and cases with a benchmark-consistent committed primary. Broad checker-derived quantities therefore do not provide a clear rule for choosing among compatible diagnoses.

Reliable primary selection requires comparative relations that independent criterion counts do not encode. These relations may include temporal order, illness course, episode structure, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity. For example, the system must distinguish whether anxiety symptoms form the main syndrome, occur within a depressive episode, represent an independent comorbidity, or remain insufficiently characterized by the available transcript.

The concentration of disagreements in particular diagnostic directions, especially F41-to-F32, indicates that the observed pattern is not uniformly distributed. Possible explanations include corpus-specific wording, learned class preferences, overlapping symptom descriptions, and missing temporal or exclusionary evidence. The present experiments do not isolate these causes, and independent psychiatrist review is required to determine their clinical interpretation.

Primary selection is therefore the largest remaining \emph{recorded} bottleneck under the evaluated HiED configuration and synthetic benchmark contract. This claim is deliberately bounded. The $D_3$, $R$, and $S$ indicators describe related system outputs rather than a causal runtime funnel, and the analysis does not establish that every benchmark disagreement is a clinically incorrect or automatically recoverable decision.

\section{Why the Evaluated Selection Strategies Did Not Reliably Close the Gap}
\label{sec:discussion-selection-strategies}

The gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement indicates that a benchmark-consistent diagnosis is often already available among the leading candidates. However, candidate availability does not imply that the recorded outputs contain a simple and reliable rule for replacing the committed primary diagnosis.

The recorded-output methods provide the clearest test of this possibility because they reuse the existing candidate ranking, criterion states, and compatibility decisions. Nominate-then-Select and deterministic fusion nevertheless did not improve Direct-Answer. These methods rely on rank position, compatibility status, and criterion-support summaries, but none of these signals directly represents why one compatible diagnosis should be preferred over another. A rule that overturns an incorrect commitment may therefore also replace an initially correct decision.

Pairwise comparison and debate attempt to make the comparison among candidates more explicit. However, these methods introduce new prompts, inference calls, context arrangements, and decision procedures and must be interpreted as complete configurations rather than isolated selection mechanisms. Under the evaluated settings, additional comparison did not reliably improve the committed diagnosis.

One important limitation is that all methods reason over the same fixed transcript. Additional inference may reorganize or reinterpret the available evidence, but it cannot acquire missing information about symptom onset, illness course, episode history, exclusionary causes, or causal relationships. When these distinctions are absent or ambiguous, another reasoning call may change the model's preference without resolving the underlying diagnostic uncertainty.

Sampling-based methods address preference stability rather than comparative validity. A diagnosis selected consistently across samples may reflect a stable clinical interpretation, but it may also reflect a stable corpus prior or model bias. Majority voting cannot correct a preference that is consistently misaligned with the benchmark, and confidence weighting is useful only when the model's stated confidence is associated with comparative correctness.

The observed gap should therefore be interpreted as analytical decision headroom rather than demonstrated automatic recoverability. The results do not show that criterion-based selection, pairwise comparison, debate, or sampling are generally ineffective. They show that the specific prompts, models, thresholds, inference budgets, and aggregation rules evaluated in this thesis did not reliably convert candidate availability into improved committed-primary agreement.

A more effective selector may require explicit representations of temporal order, syndrome dominance, explanatory dependence, exclusionary evidence, and comorbidity. When the available transcript does not support a unique primary diagnosis, requesting targeted follow-up information or abstaining may be more appropriate than forcing another selection from the same evidence.

\section{Boundaries Beyond Primary Selection}
\label{sec:discussion-set-scope-transfer}

Primary selection is not the only decision involved in producing a diagnostic output. The results also identify separate limitations in diagnostic-set construction, diagnostic scope, and transfer across data distributions. These limitations occur at different points and should not be interpreted as additional instances of the primary-selection bottleneck.

Diagnostic-set construction determines which diagnoses should accompany the committed primary diagnosis. Chapter~\ref{ch:error} shows that a benchmark-consistent primary can coexist with an incorrect complete diagnosis set. Primary diagnoses, supported comorbidities, and differential alternatives therefore require distinct output roles. Set-level measures such as Exact Match and label-level F1 also reflect output cardinality and comorbidity construction rather than primary selection alone.

Diagnostic scope defines which diagnoses the system is able to rank, verify, or emit. A diagnosis that is absent from the configured profile cannot be recovered through improved candidate ranking or finalization. However, the scope-expansion experiment in Chapter~\ref{ch:external} also shows that making a diagnosis representable does not automatically cause the system to rank or select it. Broader coverage may require corresponding examples, retrieval support, prompt adaptation, or training that changes system behavior rather than only its available labels.

External transfer presents a further boundary. MDD-5k reproduces the gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement under a second synthetic data-generation process, but its retained artifacts do not support the complete internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis. The strong degradation of the lexical classifier across corpora also indicates that predictive signals learned within one synthetic corpus may depend strongly on corpus-specific wording, generation procedures, and label distributions.

These findings place complementary limits on the main conclusion. Primary selection is the largest remaining recorded bottleneck within the evaluated internal HiED trace, but it does not account for errors in comorbidity construction, unavailable diagnostic categories, or distribution-specific prediction behavior. The external evidence supports partial persistence across synthetic corpora, not transfer to real patients or clinical workflows.

\section{Implications for Auditable Diagnostic Systems}
\label{sec:discussion-clinical-implications}

The completed computational analyses cannot determine whether an individual disagreement is primarily attributable to the system, the available transcript, or the benchmark reference. The blinded LingxiDiag pilot and the ongoing CGMH real-world validation provide separate pathways for clinical interpretation, as described in Section~\ref{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan}. Neither study contributes completed results to the present quantitative conclusions.

HiED's main auditability contribution is to preserve distinct diagnostic artifacts rather than only a final label. The ranked differential, criterion states, supporting text, compatibility decisions, and finalization source allow a reviewer to determine whether a benchmark diagnosis was unavailable, omitted from the leading differential, absent from the criterion-compatible set, or present in that set but not committed as primary.

This inspectability should not be confused with correctness. The retained artifacts do not reveal the language model's hidden reasoning, validate the Criterion Checker, or establish that the committed diagnosis is clinically appropriate. A coherent audit trace may still contain unsupported evidence, an incorrect criterion state, or a plausible but clinically suboptimal diagnosis. Auditability is therefore an analytical and review capability rather than evidence of clinical reliability.

The results also suggest that future diagnostic systems should support evidence acquisition rather than relying only on repeated inference over a fixed transcript. When the leading diagnoses differ in duration, temporal sequence, episode history, exclusionary causes, or the relationship among mood, anxiety, trauma, and sleep symptoms, the system could identify the unresolved distinction and request a targeted follow-up question.

When additional information cannot be obtained, the safer output may be to state that the available transcript does not support a unique primary diagnosis. An abstention, uncertainty, or referral mechanism may be more appropriate than forcing a commitment among similarly supported alternatives. These behaviors were not evaluated in the present experiments and remain design directions for future clinical studies.

Overall, the findings support a distinction between making diagnostic alternatives inspectable and establishing that a final decision is clinically reliable. HiED contributes the former under the evaluated synthetic benchmark conditions; the latter remains dependent on independent clinical validation, workflow evaluation, and safety assessment.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

This chapter defines five boundaries on the reported claims: benchmark and measurement validity, dataset generalizability, experimental-comparison validity, statistical and reproducibility limits, and clinical deployment. They do not invalidate the benchmark results, but specify how far those results can be interpreted.

\section{Benchmark and Measurement Limitations}

The primary evaluation target is agreement with the dataset-provided diagnoses after the parent-level projection defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}, rather than agreement with an independently adjudicated clinical diagnosis. Some cases contain more than one projected benchmark parent, and committed Top-1 is counted as correct when the selected diagnosis matches any of them. The projection provides a common scoring space but removes subtype-level distinctions. The first-listed benchmark parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses, such as the confusion matrix, and is not the primary multilabel reference.

The reported metrics evaluate different system outputs. Committed Top-1 evaluates the selected primary diagnosis, genuine Top-3 evaluates the ranked differential, gold-label inclusion evaluates the criterion-compatible set, and Exact Match and label-level F1 evaluate the complete emitted diagnosis set. These measures are not interchangeable. In particular, set-level metrics are influenced by comorbidity construction and output cardinality and should not be interpreted as isolated measures of primary selection.

The $D_3$, $R$, and $S$ indicators are analytical views of recorded system artifacts rather than stages in a strictly causal runtime funnel. Direct-Answer commits its primary diagnosis before criterion verification, while the Criterion Checker evaluates the complete configured diagnostic scope rather than only the ranked Top-3. Analyses based on Top-3 candidate detection, gold-label inclusion, and committed-primary agreement therefore use different operational definitions and, in some cases, different eligible populations.

The gold-informed oracle is likewise an analytical upper bound rather than an implemented selection method. It uses benchmark labels to identify favorable alternatives and assumes that eligible disagreements can be corrected without harming initially correct cases. It therefore estimates optimistic decision headroom but does not represent expected deployable performance.

Overall, the measurement framework can localize where benchmark disagreement appears in the recorded outputs. It cannot establish the language model's internal causal reasoning, identify a uniquely correct clinical primary diagnosis, or show that the localized disagreements can be automatically recovered.

\section{Dataset and Generalizability Limitations}

LingxiDiag-16K and MDD-5k consist of synthetically generated Chinese psychiatric dialogues. They provide controlled and reproducible evaluation settings but may contain regular generation patterns, simplified diagnostic ambiguity, limited comorbidity, clearer symptom cues, and less conversational and longitudinal complexity than real psychiatric consultations. Similar findings across the two datasets may therefore reflect properties shared by synthetic dialogue generation rather than general characteristics of clinical diagnosis.

Because the official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable, the main internal evaluation uses a fixed 1,000-case held-out subset sampled from the LingxiDiag-16K training source. Case identifiers were excluded from training and retrieval, but the evaluation cases still share the source generation process, language style, label distribution, and annotation procedure with the development data. A complete semantic near-duplicate audit was not performed. The internal results should therefore be interpreted as same-source held-out performance rather than independent external validation.

The public-validation split was used repeatedly for prompt development, compatibility-rule refinement, sensitivity analysis, and retrieval selection. It consequently represents development evidence rather than an independent estimate of generalization. The reversal between the validation-selected retrieval ordering and the held-out results further illustrates the uncertainty associated with configuration selection from a limited development split.

MDD-5k introduces a second synthetic generation process with different transcript characteristics, label frequencies, and diagnostic coverage. It reproduces the gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement, but its common trace does not contain the matched gold-label-inclusion outputs required to reconstruct the complete internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis. Its results also combine corpus shift with differences in diagnostic scope and possible effects of transcript truncation that were not separately quantified.

The cross-corpus lexical results further indicate that strong within-corpus prediction may rely on corpus-specific wording, generation patterns, and label priors. Overall, the completed evidence demonstrates same-source behavior and partial persistence under a second synthetic distribution. It does not establish generalization to naturally occurring psychiatric conversations, real patients, independent hospitals, or Taiwanese clinical practice.

\section{Model and Experimental-Comparison Limitations}
\label{sec:model-comparison-limitations}

The main same-split comparison evaluates complete predictive configurations under a common case set, label projection, and scoring implementation. TF--IDF, Single, and HiED differ in their training procedures, retrieval exposure, prompts, number of inference calls, intermediate outputs, and output contracts. Their performance differences therefore represent comparisons among complete systems rather than isolated evidence for or against the multi-agent architecture. Published LingxiDiagBench results differ further in inference and scoring procedures and are included only for descriptive benchmark positioning.

The retrieval analysis is more controlled because the backbone model, prompt family, diagnostic scope, and output contract remain fixed within each architecture. However, global Top-5 and parent-balanced retrieval differ not only in label balance, but also in the number, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, and total token length of the retrieved examples. Their comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than the isolated causal effect of parent balancing. In addition, the primary retrieval settings for Single and HiED were selected independently on the validation split, so the main system comparison is not retrieval matched.

The DA--NtS comparison provides the clearest test of finalization because both policies reuse the same ranked candidates and criterion-checking artifacts. Nevertheless, the result concerns one deterministic NtS rule and should not be generalized to all criterion-based selection strategies. Set-level measures are also affected by the fact that DA may emit an optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits only one committed diagnosis.

The additional selection analyses introduce broader configuration changes. Deterministic fusion, pairwise comparison, debate, and sampling differ from DA in one or more of their prompts, inference calls, decoding procedures, context organization, aggregation rules, or computational budgets. Diagnostic-scope expansion also changes the available categories, prompts, checker definitions, and compatibility rules without additional model training. These results should therefore be interpreted as evaluations of the declared complete configurations rather than causal comparisons of isolated reasoning mechanisms.

Finally, the model-scale analysis is restricted to the evaluated Qwen3 model family and serving setup. Similar behavior across these sizes does not establish robustness across unrelated architectures, training procedures, quantization schemes, or deployment environments.

\section{Statistical and Reproducibility Limitations}
\label{sec:statistical-reproducibility-limitations}

The reported confidence intervals and statistical tests quantify uncertainty for the observed benchmark cases under fixed system outputs and evaluation configurations. They do not account for uncertainty in benchmark validity, prompt design, checkpoint selection, synthetic data generation, or performance in a new population. Statistical uncertainty within the evaluated sample should therefore not be interpreted as uncertainty over all possible models, datasets, or clinical settings.

Several analyses also involve small or imbalanced subgroups. Rare diagnostic categories and the strict 12-case group with $D_3=1$, $R=0$, and $S=0$ do not support stable class-specific or group-level conclusions. An interval containing zero indicates insufficient evidence of a difference rather than equivalence. Conversely, an interval excluding zero establishes a difference between the evaluated configurations but does not identify which component caused that difference when the configurations vary in multiple ways.

The analyses differ in their degree of prespecification and record completeness. The same-split benchmark comparison, validation-based retrieval selection, internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis, and paired DA--NtS comparison form the primary quantitative evidence. The gold-informed oracle, diagnostic-scope expansion, lexical transfer, and additional selection interventions are supporting analyses. Some secondary methods lack complete case-level paired records, limiting uncertainty estimation and preventing reconstruction of which individual cases improved or deteriorated.

Reproduction requires preservation of the complete evaluation contract, including the model and serving environment, prompts and diagnostic configuration, data splits and recorded outputs, label projections and output-view definitions, and statistical procedures. A numerically similar result obtained with a different Top-3 definition, denominator, eligible population, or compatibility rule does not reproduce the same endpoint.

Finally, computational and analytical reproducibility remain distinct from clinical validity. A prediction pipeline and its numerical results may be reproduced exactly while still relying on an ambiguous benchmark diagnosis, an incorrect criterion state, or a clinically inappropriate operational rule.

\section{Clinical Validity and Deployment Boundaries}
\label{sec:clinical-deployment-boundaries}

The computational analyses localize disagreement among diagnostic scope, candidate ranking, criterion compatibility, and primary commitment. They cannot determine whether an individual disagreement is primarily attributable to the system, incomplete transcript evidence, or the benchmark reference. The benchmark diagnoses have not been independently adjudicated as unique clinical primaries, and the Criterion Checker states and supporting text remain model-generated rather than clinician-validated evidence. The reported failure groups must therefore be interpreted as computational benchmark-disagreement profiles rather than psychiatrist-confirmed diagnostic errors.

The blinded LingxiDiag pilot and the CGMH real-world validation described in Section~\ref{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan} are ongoing and contribute no completed results to the present thesis. The LingxiDiag pilot provides exploratory agreement evidence for selected synthetic cases. The CGMH study separately evaluates agreement with recorded clinical diagnoses, agreement with a blinded transcript-only psychiatrist reference, and exploratory audit-assisted review. Decisions recorded after disclosure of the HiED output are not independent reference judgments and cannot by themselves establish improved clinical accuracy.

HiED does not implement a validated mechanism for identifying unknown or out-of-scope presentations. The \texttt{Others} label is assigned only during evaluation when none of the benchmark diagnoses is represented in the scoring space; it is not a diagnosis emitted by the system. The current confidence fields are not calibrated to diagnostic correctness or clinical risk, and no clinically validated abstention, referral, or targeted follow-up policy has been evaluated.

Taiwan is the intended application context rather than a population in which clinical effectiveness has been demonstrated. The completed evidence does not establish performance for naturally occurring psychiatric consultations, spontaneous or mixed Mandarin--Taiwanese speech, local coding practices, hospital-system integration, clinician workload, subgroup fairness, treatment decisions, patient outcomes, or safety. The audit-assisted CGMH component is exploratory and does not constitute a prospective clinical-effectiveness study.

Auditability and reproducibility do not establish clinical validity. An inspectable and repeatable trace may still contain unsupported evidence, an incorrect criterion judgment, or an inappropriate committed diagnosis. HiED should therefore remain an offline research and review framework until independent clinical adjudication, prospective workflow and safety evaluation, appropriate privacy and governance controls, and ongoing monitoring and revalidation have been completed.

\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis introduced HiED, an auditable multi-stage framework for differential diagnosis from Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts. Rather than reducing diagnosis to a single final label, HiED preserves the ranked differential, diagnosis-specific criterion states, deterministic compatibility decisions, and committed primary diagnosis as separately inspectable artifacts. Its main contribution is therefore a system and evaluation framework for locating benchmark disagreement, rather than a state-of-the-art predictive result.

On the fixed internal evaluation set, TF--IDF + logistic regression was the strongest same-split predictor, while the validation-selected Single and HiED--DA configurations achieved similar committed-primary performance. HiED nevertheless exposed a substantial gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement. The largest strict recorded disagreement group consisted of 272 checker-eligible cases in which a benchmark gold parent appeared in the genuine Top-3 and was also present in the criterion-compatible set, but another diagnosis was committed as primary.

The analysis further showed that candidate availability and individual criterion compatibility do not determine comparative primacy. The 272-case group often involved nearby alternatives and concentrated in particular diagnostic directions, while compatible-set size and aggregate criterion-state composition did not clearly distinguish it from benchmark-consistent commitments.

None of the evaluated deterministic, pairwise, debate, fusion, or sampling-based selection strategies provided a clear and attributable improvement over Direct-Answer. The gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement remained observable across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k, although the external trace did not provide the gold-label-inclusion outputs required for a complete replication of the internal stage-wise analysis.

These findings identify primary selection as the largest remaining \emph{recorded} bottleneck under the evaluated HiED configuration and synthetic benchmark contract. They do not establish that every benchmark disagreement is a clinically incorrect or automatically recoverable decision. The completed contribution of HiED is to make candidate generation, criterion verification, compatibility, and commitment separately observable; reliable comparative diagnosis and clinical validity remain to be established through independent psychiatrist review and real-world validation.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}

The following answers are restricted to the evaluated synthetic benchmarks, declared output contracts, and recorded system artifacts. They do not represent completed clinical-validation findings.

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=1.5cm,labelwidth=1.1cm,itemsep=0.2em,parsep=0pt]

\item[RQ1] \textbf{How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?}

HiED did not outperform the strongest same-split predictor. TF--IDF + logistic regression achieved the highest committed Top-1 Accuracy, while the validation-selected Single and HiED--DA configurations produced nearly identical committed-primary performance. HiED's distinct empirical value lies in the ranked differential, criterion states, compatibility decisions, and finalization trace that enable stage-wise analysis rather than in an overall accuracy advantage.

\item[RQ2] \textbf{How often is a benchmark diagnosis present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?}

A benchmark gold parent appeared in the genuine Top-3 substantially more often than it was selected as the committed primary diagnosis. Among the 915 checker-eligible cases, 272 satisfied $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$: a benchmark gold parent was present in both the Top-3 and criterion-compatible set, but another diagnosis was committed as primary. This \emph{ranked and criterion-compatible, but not selected} group was the largest strict recorded disagreement subset. It identifies primary selection as the largest remaining recorded bottleneck under the evaluated contract, but does not establish that all 272 cases are clinically incorrect or automatically recoverable.

\item[RQ3] \textbf{How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?}

Retrieval benefited Single more consistently than HiED. Global Top-5 was selected for Single on validation, and both retrieval configurations improved its held-out Top-1 result relative to no retrieval. HiED showed mixed validation outcomes, and parent-balanced retrieval was retained according to the predefined selection rule; however, global Top-5 produced higher held-out point estimates across the reported HiED endpoints. The results therefore do not identify a stable best retrieval configuration for HiED or isolate a causal benefit of parent balancing.

\item[RQ4] \textbf{Under the evaluated settings, do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve committed Top-1 agreement over Direct-Answer?}

No evaluated intervention produced a clear and attributable improvement over Direct-Answer. NtS provided the cleanest finalization comparison because it reused the same upstream ranking and criterion-checking artifacts, but it reduced committed Top-1 agreement under all three retrieval conditions. Deterministic fusion, pairwise comparison, debate, and sampling-based methods likewise produced negative or statistically uncertain results under the tested configurations. These findings apply to the evaluated rules, prompts, models, and inference budgets rather than to the broader method families in general.

\item[RQ5] \textbf{To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and which error patterns characterize the remaining disagreements?}

The gap between candidate availability and committed-primary agreement remained observable across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k. The external trace did not contain matched gold-label-inclusion outputs, so the complete internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis could not be replicated. Diagnostic-scope expansion increased output-space representability without producing committed-primary recovery, while cross-corpus TF--IDF performance degraded substantially.

Within this 272-case group, the highest-ranked benchmark gold parent was usually second, and disagreements concentrated in the F41-to-F32 direction. Compatible-set size and aggregate criterion-state composition did not clearly distinguish these cases from benchmark-consistent commitments. These findings demonstrate persistence and concentrated error patterns only under the tested synthetic conditions, not robustness across unrelated models or clinical populations.

\end{description}

\section{Future Work}

\paragraph{Completion of the planned clinical evaluations.}

The LingxiDiag pilot and CGMH real-world validation should be completed and reported as separate studies. The LingxiDiag pilot should report the frozen case sample, reviewer characteristics, provisional and final transcript-only diagnoses, changes following evidence annotation, canonical evidence and diagnosis-specific qualifier labels, pre-consensus inter-rater agreement, consensus or adjudication outcomes, and offline agreement with the benchmark and HiED outputs. Because the cases are selected from one synthetic disagreement population, the findings should remain framed as feasibility and exploratory agreement evidence.

The CGMH study should separately report agreement between HiED and the recorded clinical diagnoses, agreement with the blinded transcript-only psychiatrist reference, provisional-to-final diagnostic changes, criterion-state and compatibility agreement, diagnostic-scope coverage, and exploratory audit-assisted review outcomes. The recorded diagnosis, blinded transcript-only reference, and psychiatrist decisions made after viewing HiED should remain separate because they represent different information conditions.

\paragraph{Comparative primary selection and evidence representation.}

Future primary-selection methods should model the comparative relations among plausible diagnoses rather than relying only on independent criterion satisfaction, compatible-set membership, ranking position, or criterion counts. Relevant relations include temporal order, illness course, episode structure, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, explanatory dependence, and the distinction among a primary disorder, a comorbid condition, and a differential alternative.

These methods should be evaluated using fixed upstream artifacts whenever possible so that changes in finalization can be distinguished from changes in candidate generation, prompting, or inference budget. Complete case-level records should also be retained to support paired gain--loss analysis and reproducible error review.

\paragraph{Targeted evidence acquisition, abstention, and broader validation.}

When the available transcript does not contain the information required to distinguish the leading diagnoses, future systems should identify the unresolved distinction and request a targeted follow-up question. Such questions may concern symptom duration, temporal sequence, previous episodes, substance exposure, medical explanations, functional impairment, or the relationship among mood, anxiety, trauma, and sleep symptoms.

When additional evidence cannot be obtained, an explicit insufficient-evidence, abstention, or referral output may be safer than forcing a unique primary diagnosis. These mechanisms require direct evaluation together with calibrated uncertainty and validated detection of unknown or out-of-scope presentations.

Broader technical evaluation should include unrelated model families, independently collected corpora, naturally occurring clinical transcripts, complete ranking and criterion-compatible-set outputs, and direct evaluation of diagnostic-set construction. Clinical development should subsequently examine clinician interaction, automation bias, review burden, subgroup performance, privacy, governance, patient safety, and prospective clinical outcomes before any deployment claim is made.

HiED demonstrates that making plausible diagnoses visible and criterion-compatible is not equivalent to selecting the most appropriate primary diagnosis. Its current contribution is to make this distinction observable and auditable; establishing reliable comparative diagnosis and safe clinical use remains the central direction for future work.

\hline
\endhead
Independent pairwise comparison & Re-executes the HiED pipeline with the pairwise differential stage enabled after criterion checking; eligible candidates require a met ratio of at least 0.50 and enter comparison when an adjacent support gap is below 0.10 & Regenerates upstream outputs and is interpreted as a complete-configuration comparison\\
\hline
Forced pairwise override & Full pairwise-commit pipeline with mandatory promotion of a valid in-cluster preference; genuine ranking may differ from DA & Complete-configuration sensitivity analysis, not an isolated finalizer or deployable fallback policy\\
\hline
Two-view debate & At most two advocate rounds followed by a judge when required; 24,576-token context window & Uses additional calls and a larger context budget than DA\\
\hline
Self-consistency & $K=3$ and $K=5$ Diagnostician samples at temperature 0.7 under fixed seed schedules; primary selected by majority vote & Samples only the Diagnostician and does not reproduce the full HiED pipeline\\
\hline
Confidence-informed self-consistency & Uses the same samples but weights each primary vote by verbalized confidence clipped to $[0,1]$; missing confidence receives weight 1 & Verbalized confidence is not a calibrated probability\\
\hline
Deterministic fusion & Validation-selected rule combining Diagnostician rank, compatibility status, and criterion met ratio; overrides only when the DA primary is not criterion-compatible and a criterion-compatible Top-3 alternative exceeds its ratio by at least 0.20 & Evaluates one selected deterministic rule on held-out cases\\
