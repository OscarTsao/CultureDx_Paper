初診精神科晤談逐字稿中的鑑別診斷，本質上是一種在證據不完整條件下進行的比較式決策。多個疾病診斷可能同時與同一份逐字稿相容，但最終通常只能有一個被判定為主診斷。然而，用以區分病程、發病時序、排除病因與共病關係等關鍵鑑別資訊，未必會在單次初診晤談中被完整引出。大型語言模型雖然能流暢地閱讀並理解這類逐字稿，近期也已有大量研究嘗試將其應用於精神科診斷任務，但語言理解的流暢性並不等同於可靠的鑑別診斷能力。

既有評測呈現出一致的落差：模型在粗粒度分類任務上往往表現良好，但一旦進入細粒度的疾病層級鑑別診斷，準確率便會大幅下降。這個落差促使本研究提出一套可稽核的系統架構，用以明確定位錯誤發生於診斷流程中的哪一個階段。模型可能生成一個合理且受到診斷準則支持的候選疾病，卻仍未能將其正確選為主診斷。候選診斷生成、依準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇三者之間的差異，通常會被單一準確率指標所掩蓋，使研究者難以判斷系統究竟在哪個環節失敗。

為了使這些不同的失敗階段能夠被分別觀察，並檢驗主診斷選擇是否構成主要瓶頸，本研究提出 HiED——一套面向中文精神科初診對話逐字稿的鑑別診斷框架。HiED 將候選診斷偵測、ICD-10 逐準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇保留為可分別分析的階段，而非將整個流程簡化為單一分類事件。同時，系統會在每次預測時輸出逐準則的稽核軌跡，使研究者能夠檢視診斷依據並定位系統失敗的位置。HiED 並非已完成臨床驗證的自主診斷工具，而是一項用於分析與檢驗逐字稿式精神科診斷失敗模式的研究工具。

因此，本論文有兩項主要貢獻：其一，提出一套可稽核的失敗定位框架，使上述各診斷階段得以分別觀察；其二，提供可重現的證據，指出在本研究評估的 HiED 設定與合成逐字稿基準評測中，主診斷選擇構成主要的失敗瓶頸。換言之，在本研究之合成逐字稿設定下，系統的主要限制不僅在於能否生成合理的候選診斷，而更在於能否在多個皆具一定準則支持的候選之間，進行有效比較並判定最適合作為主診斷者。最後需說明，本文題目所稱之臺灣脈絡，指涉系統之設計動機與預期部署場域（繁體中文互動、本地端推論、面向臨床人員之稽核），而非已完成之臺灣真實病人臨床驗證；本文現階段的量化結論均來自合成中文逐字稿語料。臺灣真實病人資料之臨床驗證目前於長庚紀念醫院進行中，其結果不納入本論文現階段的量化結論。

\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial-consultation psychiatric transcript is a comparative decision under incomplete evidence. Multiple disorders may be compatible with the same transcript, yet only one can typically be judged the primary diagnosis. Key discriminating information---illness course, onset timing, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity relationships---may not be fully elicited within a single initial consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, and recent research has applied them to psychiatric diagnostic tasks, but linguistic fluency does not equate to reliable differential diagnostic ability.

Existing evaluations show a consistent gap: models perform well on coarse-grained classification, but accuracy drops sharply on fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis. This gap motivates an auditable architecture that locates the stage at which a diagnostic error occurs. A model may generate a plausible disorder supported by diagnostic criteria, yet fail to select it as the primary diagnosis. The distinction among candidate-diagnosis generation, criterion-based verification, and primary-diagnosis selection is usually obscured by a single accuracy metric.

To make these failure stages separately observable and to examine whether primary-diagnosis selection is a major bottleneck, we propose HiED, a differential-diagnosis framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts. HiED retains candidate detection, ICD-10 criterion verification, and primary selection as separately analyzable stages rather than reducing diagnosis to one classification event. A criterion-level audit trace records evidence and failure locations for each prediction. HiED is a research instrument for analyzing transcript-based diagnostic failures, not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic tool. The Taiwanese context in the title denotes the intended deployment setting. Real-patient validation in Taiwan is ongoing at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital (CGMH) and is not included in the present quantitative conclusions.

This thesis makes two main contributions: an auditable failure-localization framework and reproducible evidence of a primary-selection bottleneck in the evaluated HiED configurations under synthetic transcript-only benchmarks. In these settings, the observed limitation lies not only in generating plausible candidates, but in comparing criterion-supported alternatives and selecting the most appropriate primary diagnosis.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric outpatient care in Taiwan is widely accessible, but this access also creates a heavy clinical workload. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit different levels of medical facilities without referral, and large medical centers may receive more than ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in one day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A first psychiatric visit must cover history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and an initial diagnostic judgment within limited time. The resulting note or transcript often contains incomplete information about onset, duration, course, medical or substance-related causes, and previous treatment.

The evidence may also be indirect. In Chinese-speaking settings, patients often describe distress through insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, or gastrointestinal symptoms before using explicit mood or anxiety terms~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. Taiwanese consultations may also mix Mandarin, Taiwanese expressions, and local clinical wording. The same condition can therefore be described in several ways within one visit.

Differential diagnosis remains difficult because common psychiatric disorders share many symptoms. Depression, anxiety, stress-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, somatic symptom disorders, and sleep disorders may all involve insomnia, fatigue, poor concentration, and impaired daily function. In diagnostic assessment, these symptoms are interpreted through disorder-specific criteria. These criteria do not treat every symptom independently or equally: depending on the disorder, a diagnosis may require particular core symptoms, a minimum number of additional symptoms, a specified duration, functional impairment, and the absence of exclusionary causes~\citep{who2019icd10}. Diagnostic assessment therefore involves matching the available evidence to a disorder-specific combination of requirements, rather than assigning a diagnosis from the presence of one symptom alone. Even when diagnostic criteria are applied, several disorders may remain compatible with the same transcript. Evidence for both depression and anxiety, for example, may reflect comorbidity or overlapping symptoms from one primary condition~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}. Even structured diagnostic studies report limited agreement for some common disorders~\citep{regier2013dsm5}.

These features make first-visit psychiatric diagnosis a problem of comparative bookkeeping under incomplete evidence. The clinician must document a best-supported working diagnosis, keep plausible alternatives visible, and identify which missing information should be collected at follow-up. Time pressure can increase the risk of anchoring or premature closure when several disorders share the same symptoms~\citep{croskerry2003cognitive}.
\section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

Large language models offer capabilities that are well aligned with psychiatric transcript analysis: they can process free-form dialogue, organize symptom evidence, retrieve relevant examples, and produce structured outputs. Medical multi-agent systems can further divide the diagnostic task among specialized roles and combine their outputs~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. However, these capabilities do not by themselves ensure reliable differential diagnosis from a fixed transcript.

A final diagnostic error may arise for different reasons. A clinically relevant diagnosis may never be proposed as a candidate, the available transcript may not provide sufficient criterion-level support for that diagnosis, or the diagnosis may remain plausible but lose the final comparison against competing alternatives. These failure modes require different remedies, but they are usually collapsed into a single final-accuracy score.

This creates the central technical problem studied in this thesis: existing evaluation often does not reveal whether disagreement arises during candidate generation, criterion-based verification, or primary-diagnosis selection. Without separately observable outputs for these stages, it is difficult to determine whether better retrieval, evidence checking, or comparative selection is needed. The next section introduces HiED as a framework designed to make these distinctions observable.

\section{Overview of HiED and Study Scope}

To make these distinctions observable, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid multi-agent system for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis from initial-consultation transcripts. The intended context is Taiwanese outpatient care. The completed quantitative results reported in this thesis are based on synthetic Chinese transcript datasets. Two complementary clinical studies are ongoing: a 20-case pilot psychiatrist review of selected LingxiDiag disagreements and a real-world system--physician agreement study using CGMH data. Their populations, reference standards, and outcomes are analyzed separately, and no incomplete results are included in the present conclusions.

HiED consists of five functional components. First, the \emph{Transcript Input} component preserves speaker labels and turn order while applying the predefined prompt-budget rule. Second, the \emph{Similar-Case Retriever} optionally retrieves relevant examples from the training corpus and supplies them only to the Diagnostician. Third, the \emph{Diagnostician Agent} produces a ranked list of five candidate diagnoses, one primary diagnosis selected from the three highest-ranked candidates, and at most one optional comorbid diagnosis. Fourth, a separate \emph{Criterion Checker Agent} evaluates each configured diagnostic category against its operationalized ICD-10 criteria. For every criterion, the checker records whether the transcript provides supporting evidence (\texttt{met}), contradictory evidence (\texttt{not\_met}), or insufficient evidence to make either judgment (\texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}). Finally, the deterministic \emph{Compatibility Auditor} applies predefined operational rules to the criterion states and constructs a criterion-compatible diagnosis set, containing the diagnoses considered compatible with the available transcript evidence. Because compatibility alone does not determine which diagnosis should be selected as primary, the system uses a finalization policy to produce the committed output. The reference Direct-Answer policy preserves the Diagnostician's emitted primary diagnosis and optional comorbidity. As an evaluated alternative, Nominate-then-Select reuses the same recorded ranking and compatibility results to select a single committed primary diagnosis according to a predefined deterministic rule. Together, these stages preserve the ranked candidates, diagnosis-specific criterion states, compatibility decisions, and final committed diagnosis as separately inspectable artifacts.

These outputs expose three analyzable stages. \emph{Candidate detection} asks whether at least one diagnosis provided by the benchmark dataset appears in the ranked Top-3. \emph{Criterion verification} evaluates each configured diagnosis against its disorder-specific criteria; diagnoses that satisfy the study's operational compatibility rules are retained in the \emph{criterion-compatible set}. \emph{Primary selection} asks which candidate is selected as the primary diagnosis; its observed output is the \emph{committed primary diagnosis}.

Throughout this thesis, \emph{primary selection} denotes the scientific task of choosing one primary diagnosis among plausible candidates. \emph{Finalization} denotes the system-level process that converts the recorded ranking, checker states, and compatibility outputs into the final output; Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select are finalization policies. \emph{Commitment} is reserved for the resulting output state, as in \emph{committed primary diagnosis} or \emph{committed Top-1 agreement}, rather than used as the name of a separate diagnostic stage.

We use the term \emph{primary-selection bottleneck} for the operational pattern in which a benchmark diagnosis is present among leading candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but the system selects a different committed primary diagnosis. The bottleneck is a benchmark-local pattern showing that candidate coverage or criterion compatibility can exceed committed Top-1 agreement; it is not a claim about the true clinical primary diagnosis. It does not show that every error passes both earlier checks, that the corpus identifies a unique clinical primary diagnosis, or that every transcript contains enough evidence for one.

This distinction matters because different errors need different remedies. Missing candidates call for better generation or retrieval. A candidate that fails criterion checking points to the checker, the rules, or missing evidence. A candidate that remains available but is not selected points to the final comparison among plausible diagnoses. The detailed metric definitions are given in \Cref{sec:gold-label-conventions}.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions.

First, it presents an auditable hybrid architecture that combines LLM-based diagnosis, structured criterion-level checking against operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria, and deterministic compatibility rules. The system records criterion states, checker-generated supporting text, and compatibility-rule outcomes instead of relying only on a free-form rationale. The trace can be inspected and replayed, but it does not prove that the LLM's hidden reasoning is faithful or that the checker judgments are clinically correct~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}.

Second, it presents a stage-wise failure-localization and empirical evaluation framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection, quantifies the primary-selection bottleneck, and tests whether criterion-based finalization and several primary-selection methods close the observed gap. The absence of improvement under the evaluated selection interventions narrows several simple explanations under the tested settings, but it does not show that better selectors or additional clinical information can never help.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

Psychiatric LLM studies cover several different tasks, including examination questions, short vignettes, diagnostic reasoning, and comparison with clinicians~\citep{qiu2023largeai,li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. These studies show that LLMs can produce plausible clinical answers, but performance depends on the input, label space, and reference standard. Detecting depression from text or audio is also different from choosing one primary diagnosis among several overlapping disorders~\citep{li2025addreview}.

Chinese psychiatric datasets make disorder-level evaluation possible. LingxiDiagBench includes static diagnosis and dynamic consultation over the synthetic LingxiDiag-16K corpus and reports a clear drop from broad depression--anxiety classification to fine-grained diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. MDD-5k provides a second synthetic Chinese diagnostic-dialogue resource based on anonymized cases~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. These datasets support reproducible experiments, but they do not replace real-patient validation.

Existing systems also differ in whether they can ask questions. MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, and DSM5AgentFlow study inquiry or interactive diagnostic workflows~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels focus more on diagnosis from information already available in records or transcripts~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. Interactive systems can obtain missing evidence, while fixed-input systems isolate how well a model uses the same evidence. This thesis studies the fixed-input setting.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning}

Retrieval-augmented generation conditions a model on external records selected for the current query rather than relying only on information encoded in model parameters~\citep{lewis2020rag}. In-context-learning studies similarly show that semantically related demonstrations can be retrieved to construct a query-specific prompt and that performance depends on which examples are selected~\citep{liu2022incontext}. In transcript-based diagnosis, retrieved cases can therefore provide prompt-side examples of symptom descriptions and diagnostic labels. They are demonstrations rather than patient evidence and should not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

Similarity retrieval can expose useful corpus-local regularities, but it can also reproduce the frequency and wording patterns of the indexed data. Ordinary nearest-neighbor retrieval may repeatedly select examples from common labels, while a parent-balanced policy changes the label composition of the prompt. It may also change the number, ordering, and total token length of the retrieved examples. A comparison between global and parent-balanced retrieval consequently evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating balancing as a single causal factor.

These considerations motivate the retrieval experiments in this thesis. No retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval are compared using the same training-set retrieval index, evaluation cases, label projection, and scoring implementation. The comparison is repeated in both the Single and HiED pipelines so that retrieval behavior is not inferred from only one architecture.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

Medical multi-agent systems assign different roles to LLMs and combine their outputs through consultation, debate, or adaptive collaboration~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. AMIE and related work also show the value of broad differential diagnosis, realistic consultation, and expert review~\citep{mcduff2025amie,tu2025conversational}. These systems demonstrate that structured interaction can help, but adding agents does not guarantee a better final answer. Agents may repeat the same model bias, and free-form discussion can make it difficult to identify which component changed the decision.

For this thesis, the important issue is controlled attribution. If ranking, criterion checking, debate, and finalization all change at once, an accuracy difference cannot show which change helped. HiED therefore compares two policies that reuse the same ranked candidates and checker outputs: Direct-Answer keeps the diagnostician's decision, whereas Nominate-then-Select uses criterion compatibility to choose the primary.

\section{Auditability and Criterion Grounding}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

A fluent explanation is not proof of faithful reasoning. Model-generated chains can sound coherent while not reflecting the factors that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. In healthcare, explanation quality, interpretability, and clinical validity therefore need separate evaluation~\citep{rudin2019stop,huang2024xiai}.

Diagnostic-chain and symptom-based methods make outputs easier to inspect~\citep{chen2025cod,lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. However, a generated reasoning chain is still another model output. This thesis uses the narrower term \emph{auditability}: an external record that links transcript evidence, criterion states, rule results, and the final output. The record can be checked and replayed, but it does not reveal the model's hidden reasoning or turn checker outputs into clinical ground truth.

Constraint-logic systems are the closest related approach because they translate diagnostic criteria into explicit rules~\citep{kim2025clp}. HiED keeps the learned diagnostician separate from the criterion and rule layer. Under Direct-Answer, the structured layer audits the decision; under Nominate-then-Select, it is tested as a selector. Among the reviewed studies, we did not identify an evaluation that uses this separation to compare retention of a benchmark diagnosis with primary selection on the same Chinese psychiatric transcripts.

\section{Structured Diagnostic Knowledge and Primary Selection}

Knowledge-based diagnosis methods represent relations among symptoms, disorders, and missing information. MedKGI supports information-guided inquiry, and DKEC uses structured domain knowledge for label prediction~\citep{wang2025medkgi,ge2024dkec}. Such relations may help when disorders share symptoms but differ in onset, duration, exclusions, or causal direction.

HiED does not implement a full knowledge-graph selector. Instead, this literature motivates future work if simple criterion counts and local re-ranking are not enough. A stronger selector may need to compare which diagnosis best explains the full presentation, not only how many criteria each diagnosis satisfies.

\section{Research Gap and Research Questions}

Prior work shows that LLMs can diagnose psychiatric cases, retrieve examples, use diagnostic criteria, and work in multi-agent systems. Five gaps remain. First, conventional text classifiers, single LLMs, retrieval-augmented LLMs, and structured systems are not always compared under the same split and endpoint. Second, final accuracy does not show whether a benchmark diagnosis was absent from the candidate list or present but not selected as the committed primary. Third, global nearest-neighbor retrieval and parent-balanced retrieval require matched comparison across architectures. Fourth, criterion-based finalization and later primary-selection methods must be evaluated separately from candidate generation and retrieval. Fifth, it remains unclear whether the observed Top-3--Top-1 selection gap persists across model sizes, external corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope. The precise gap addressed here is that HiED retains candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection as separately observable stages evaluated on the same cases.

This thesis therefore uses five research questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?
\item[RQ2] How often is a benchmark diagnosis present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?
\item[RQ3] How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?
\item[RQ4] Do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve over Direct-Answer?
\item[RQ5] To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and what error patterns characterize the remaining failures?
\end{description}
RQ1 and RQ3 use matched comparisons on the same fixed internal test set, with common label projection and evaluation code. Clinical effectiveness and criterion-level clinical validity are not answered by the completed computational RQs; the LingxiDiag pilot review and CGMH real-world validation are separate ongoing clinical studies.

\section{Input and Diagnostic Scope}
\label{sec:input-diagnostic-scope}

HiED processes one Chinese psychiatric consultation transcript at a time. The input is an ordered sequence of turns, each marked as \texttt{Clinician} or \texttt{Patient}; speaker labels and turn order are preserved in the prompt. Transcripts longer than the available prompt budget are deterministically truncated by retaining complete turns from the beginning and end, with an explicit marker for the omitted middle. The system does not summarize or invent missing text. This is a fixed-input study: the system cannot ask the patient a follow-up question during inference.

ICD-10 is the diagnostic coding system used to name the disorders in this study~\citep{who2019icd10}. The system does not search all ICD-10 diagnoses. Before a run begins, it loads one fixed \emph{configured diagnostic profile} that is used for every patient. The main diagnostic profile contains fourteen configured diagnostic categories. F41 and F43 include subtype-level categories, so the fourteen categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels for scoring.

The following terms define the diagnostic scope and criterion-checking process used in HiED. A \emph{configured diagnostic category} is an ICD-10 code that the Diagnostician may rank and for which the system provides a corresponding Criterion Checker. The fourteen category-specific checkers together form the \emph{criterion-checker set}.

For each criterion, the checker assigns one of three model-generated evidence states: \texttt{met} when the transcript provides supporting evidence, \texttt{not\_met} when it provides contradictory evidence, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the available information does not support either conclusion. The checker definitions and compatibility rules are study-specific operationalizations informed by ICD-10 descriptions. They do not reproduce the full official ICD-10 manual and have not been independently validated as clinical ground truth.

In this thesis, an \emph{agent} means an LLM call with a distinct prompt and task. The checker agents run independently; they do not have autonomous memory, planning, or free-form negotiation. HiED is therefore a modular multi-stage LLM orchestration with role-specific agents and deterministic rule aggregation.

\section{Candidate Generation and Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

This section describes the two candidate-generation components shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}: the Similar-Case Retriever and the Diagnostician.

\paragraph{Similar-Case Retriever.}

The Similar-Case Retriever optionally selects training-set cases that are similar to the current transcript and supplies them to the Diagnostician as prompt-side demonstrations, following the retrieval-augmented generation pattern~\citep{lewis2020rag}. The study evaluates three conditions: no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Global Top-5 returns the five most similar eligible cases. Parent-balanced retrieval selects at most one example for each of the eleven configured scoring parents and can therefore return up to eleven examples.

The parent groups used for retrieval are fixed by the configured diagnostic profile rather than generated separately for each patient. Only the selected example within each group depends on the current transcript. Subtypes that share a scoring parent, such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2, also share one retrieval group. Parent-balanced retrieval may reduce repeated examples from frequent labels, but it also changes the number, label composition, ordering, and total token length of the demonstrations. The retrieval comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating balancing as a single causal factor.

Retrieved cases are provided only to the Diagnostician. They are not included in the Criterion Checker prompts, do not constitute evidence about the current patient, and do not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

\paragraph{Diagnostician.}

The Diagnostician receives the speaker-labeled transcript, the configured diagnostic profile, and any retrieved examples. It produces three separately recorded outputs: a ranked list of five candidate diagnoses, one emitted primary diagnosis selected from the three highest-ranked candidates, and at most one optional comorbid diagnosis. The ranked list represents the model's differential diagnosis, whereas the primary field records the diagnosis that becomes committed under the Direct-Answer policy.

The ranked candidates, emitted primary diagnosis, and optional comorbidity are stored separately. A diagnosis may therefore remain present among the leading candidates even when another diagnosis is emitted as primary. This distinction allows candidate coverage and primary selection to be evaluated independently.

\section{Criterion Verification and Compatibility Aggregation}

This section describes the two criterion-processing components shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}: the Criterion Checker and the Compatibility Auditor.

\paragraph{Criterion Checker.}

Each of the fourteen configured diagnostic categories has a corresponding Criterion Checker based on the study's operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria~\citep{who2019icd10}. Every checker receives the same speaker-labeled transcript together with the criteria defined for one diagnostic category. Retrieved examples are not included in checker prompts, and the checkers do not communicate with one another.

For each criterion, the checker assigns one of the three criterion states defined in Section~\ref{sec:input-diagnostic-scope}: \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}. It also returns a short supporting-text field, which may be \texttt{null} when no explicit supporting text is identified. Missing information is not negative evidence: an unresolved criterion remains \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} rather than \texttt{not\_met}. All fourteen configured categories are checked for every case, so criterion verification is not limited to diagnoses in the ranked Top-5.

\paragraph{Compatibility Auditor.}

The Compatibility Auditor is a deterministic module that converts criterion-level states into diagnosis-level compatibility decisions. For each configured diagnostic category, it applies the predefined operational rule associated with that category. Depending on the diagnosis, a rule may require particular core criteria, a minimum number of total criteria, specified criterion groups, or all listed requirements. A diagnosis is added to the \emph{criterion-compatible set} only when its configured rule is satisfied.

The auditor records the applied rule, the relevant criterion counts, and the resulting compatibility decision in the compatibility-rule trace. Because the aggregation is deterministic, the same checker states always produce the same criterion-compatible set. The complete category-specific rules are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:configured-profile}.

\paragraph{Interpretation boundary.}

Criterion states and supporting text are model-generated and have not been validated as clinician ground truth. The supporting text is retained for inspection but is not guaranteed to be a verbatim or span-aligned extract or a clinically validated evidence annotation. Criterion compatibility is an operational property under the study configuration; it does not establish that a diagnosis is clinically correct, uniquely supported, or the most appropriate primary diagnosis. Several diagnoses may therefore be retained in the criterion-compatible set for the same transcript.

\section{Primary Selection and Finalization}

This section describes the finalization policies and the Committed Diagnosis output shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}. Both policies are evaluated from the same recorded Diagnostician and criterion-processing outputs, but they use these outputs differently. Finalization does not introduce an additional LLM call.

\paragraph{Direct-Answer.}

Direct-Answer (DA) preserves the Diagnostician's emitted decision. The emitted primary field becomes the committed primary diagnosis; if this field is missing, the first-ranked candidate is used as a fallback. DA also retains at most one optional comorbid diagnosis when it differs from the primary. Criterion Checker states and Compatibility Auditor outputs remain available as audit artifacts but do not overwrite the DA committed diagnosis.

\paragraph{Nominate-then-Select.}

Nominate-then-Select (NtS) is a deterministic alternative that uses the recorded candidate ranking and criterion-compatible set to choose one committed primary diagnosis. For configured diagnostic category $d$, the criterion-support ratio is
\[
\operatorname{met\_ratio}(d)=\min\!\left(1,\frac{\text{number of \texttt{met} criteria for }d}{\text{required criterion count for }d}\right).
\]
The denominator is the minimum satisfying count defined by the configured operational rule rather than the total number of listed criteria. NtS first selects the highest-ranked criterion-compatible diagnosis within the ranked Top-5. If none of the ranked candidates is criterion-compatible, it selects from the remaining criterion-compatible diagnoses by descending $\operatorname{met\_ratio}$, with ties resolved by the predefined configured-category order. If the criterion-compatible set is empty, NtS falls back to the first-ranked diagnosis.

\paragraph{Output contracts.}

DA produces one committed primary diagnosis and may retain one optional comorbid diagnosis. NtS produces one re-selected committed primary diagnosis and does not emit a comorbid diagnosis. Thus, the two policies differ in both their primary-selection rule and final output cardinality. Additional primary-selection methods are defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental} and do not alter the core architecture described here.

\section{Configured Output Space and Gold-Label Projection}
\label{sec:gold-label-conventions}

Throughout this thesis, the dataset-provided diagnoses used for scoring are referred to as \emph{benchmark gold labels}. The term \emph{gold} denotes the official benchmark reference and does not imply a uniquely correct clinical diagnosis.

The main system uses fourteen configured ICD-10 diagnostic categories, listed in Table~\ref{tab:configured-profile-summary}. For evaluation, subtype-level categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels. Let $G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x)$ be the raw benchmark gold codes for case $x$, let $\pi$ map subtype codes such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2 to their scoring parent, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of eleven configured scoring parents. The in-scope gold-label set is
\[
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\{\pi(c):c\in G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x),\ \pi(c)\in\mathcal{C}\}.
\]
The scored gold-label set is then
\[
G(x)=
\begin{cases}
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x), & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)\neq\varnothing,\\
\{\mathrm{Others}\}, & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\varnothing.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus, a mixed case such as F90 with F41.1 is scored as $\{\mathrm{F41}\}$ rather than $\{\mathrm{Others},\mathrm{F41}\}$. A case is in scope when $G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)$ is nonempty and out of scope otherwise. Because the main system has neither an \emph{Others} output nor a trained unknown detector, it cannot correctly represent an out-of-scope case.

For case $x$, $G(x)$ is the deduplicated gold-label set after parent-level projection. LingxiDiag cases may contain more than one gold parent label, and neither dataset provides a psychiatrist-reviewed unique clinical primary diagnosis. The first-listed gold parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses such as the confusion matrix.

In the running example from Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}, F41.1 is scored as F41. If the gold-label set contains F41 and the system emits F32 while ranking F41.1 second, Top-1 is wrong but Top-3 is correct.

\section{Datasets, Case Construction, and Split Roles}

The study uses two synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogue datasets. LingxiDiagBench is the source corpus for the in-domain experiments. MDD-5k provides an external synthetic test set.

\subsection{LingxiDiagBench}

LingxiDiagBench contains synthetic Chinese psychiatric consultations generated for static diagnostic classification and interactive consultation tasks~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. It is used as the source corpus for the in-domain experiments because it provides a fixed diagnostic label space and sufficient cases for training, configuration selection, and held-out evaluation. Its published results also show that fine-grained disorder-level diagnosis is substantially more difficult than broad depression--anxiety classification.

The accessible release used in this study contained 14,000 training-source cases and 1,000 public-validation cases. The official test file was not available in the accessible release used when the experiments were designed. To obtain a held-out internal evaluation set, 1,000 cases were separated from the 14,000-case training source before the main experiments. The internal held-out set was stratified to approximate the public-validation distribution using the first-listed projected parent label and a fixed random seed. The first-listed label was used only as a sampling variable and was not treated as a psychiatrist-validated clinical primary diagnosis. \section{Prediction Views and Metrics}

The evaluator keeps four recorded output views separate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item The \emph{primary view}, $p_1(x)$, is the committed primary diagnosis.
\item The \emph{ranked view}, $R_3(x)$, contains the first three distinct parent labels in the method's genuine ranked output. For HiED, it is taken from the recorded Diagnostician ranking and is independent of later DA or NtS finalization.
\item The \emph{compatibility view}, $L(x)$, is the set of scoring parent labels retained as criterion-compatible by the Compatibility Auditor under the configured operational rules.
\item The \emph{multilabel view}, $M(x)$, contains the committed primary diagnosis and any emitted comorbid parent label.
\end{enumerate}

TF--IDF + logistic regression obtains its Top-3 from ranked class scores. HiED obtains it from the Diagnostician ranking. A Single system without a genuine ranked output is not assigned an artificial Top-3.

Because a case may contain more than one gold label, Top-$k$ Accuracy counts a case as correct when at least one gold label appears among the first $k$ predictions. Exact Match, also called subset accuracy, requires the complete predicted label set to equal the complete gold-label set. These are benchmark-agreement measures, not independent measures of the patient's true clinical diagnosis.

DA may emit a committed primary diagnosis and one optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits one committed primary diagnosis. Their Top-1 comparison therefore evaluates the committed primary directly, but differences in Exact Match, Macro-F1, or Weighted-F1 also reflect output cardinality and cannot be interpreted as isolated effects of primary re-selection.

Top-3 and committed Top-1 are computed over all 1,000 internal test cases. Gold-label retention in the criterion-compatible set is computed over the 915 cases with at least one non-\emph{Others} gold parent covered by a corresponding Criterion Checker.

\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}1\ Accuracy}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}3\ Accuracy}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
\mathrm{Exact}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[M(x)=G(x)].
\]

Macro-F1 is the unweighted mean of the twelve one-vs-rest label-level F1 scores obtained by binarizing $G(x)$ and $M(x)$ over the fixed scoring classes. Weighted-F1 uses the same label-level F1 scores but weights each class by its gold-label support.

First-listed-label agreement compares the committed diagnosis only with the first-listed projected gold parent. It is used for the confusion matrix but not for the main result. It may differ from any-gold Top-1 Accuracy when a case contains more than one gold parent label.

\section{Failure-Localization Indicators}

The thesis defines three failure-localization indicators. Candidate detection and primary agreement are defined for all evaluation cases, whereas criterion retention is defined only for checker-eligible cases.

For case $x$,
\[
D_3(x)=\mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
R(x)=\mathbb{1}[L(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
S(x)=\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
where $L(x)$ is the set of scoring parent labels retained as criterion-compatible by the Compatibility Auditor. $D_3(x)$ indicates whether at least one benchmark gold parent appears in the genuine ranked Top-3, $R(x)$ indicates whether at least one benchmark gold parent is retained in the criterion-compatible set, and $S(x)$ indicates whether the committed primary diagnosis matches at least one benchmark gold parent.

$D_3$ and $S$ are computed over all 1,000 internal evaluation cases. $R$ is computed only for the 915 checker-eligible cases whose gold-label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} parent represented by a corresponding Criterion Checker. The resulting rates therefore use different denominators and should not be interpreted as consecutive stages of a single case-level funnel.

These indicators provide complementary views of the recorded outputs for the same case. They ask whether a benchmark gold parent was ranked, retained as criterion-compatible, and selected as the committed primary diagnosis; they do not imply that every finalization policy executes these operations in that chronological order.

\section{Statistical Analysis}

The case is the unit of analysis, and all statistical tests are two-sided with a significance level of $\alpha=0.05$. When confidence intervals are reported for a single binary proportion, 95\% Wilson intervals are used. Paired comparisons require identical case IDs and endpoint definitions. For binary correctness outcomes, the effect estimate is the paired difference in correctness proportions, and the exact McNemar test is computed from the two discordant counts: cases correct only under the first configuration and cases correct only under the second. Main paired comparisons report the effect estimate and its confidence interval; detailed discordant counts and $p$-values are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

Confidence intervals for paired accuracy differences and finalization difference-in-differences contrasts are estimated by resampling case IDs with replacement using 10,000 paired bootstrap replicates and seed 42. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles define the reported 95\% confidence interval. Reported $p$-values are interpreted together with effect direction, confidence intervals, and discordant counts rather than as standalone evidence.

Holm correction is applied when multiple prespecified tests belong to the same comparison family defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}; unrelated or isolated comparisons are not pooled into an artificial family. A non-significant result indicates insufficient evidence of a difference and is not interpreted as evidence of equivalence. When compared configurations also differ in context length, number of model calls, output contract, or other execution settings, the paired result is interpreted as a comparison of complete configurations rather than as the isolated effect of one component.

\section{Retrieval-Configuration Selection and Benchmark Positioning}
\label{sec:benchmark-retrieval-design}

This section defines the first evaluation block in Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-sequence}. Retrieval configurations are first compared and selected separately within the Single and HiED--DA architectures using the public-validation split (RQ3). The selected configurations are then fixed and used in the same-split benchmark comparison on the internal held-out set (RQ1). All analyses use the parent-label projection and evaluation measures defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}.

\paragraph{Retrieval-configuration selection (RQ3).}
Retrieval is evaluated separately within the Single and HiED--DA architectures under three conditions: no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Within each architecture, the backbone model, prompt family, configured diagnostic scope, decoding settings, and output contract remain fixed. The comparison is case matched within each architecture.

The public-validation split is used to select the primary retrieval configuration separately for Single and HiED--DA according to the predefined promotion rule. After selection, the chosen configuration is frozen before evaluation on the fixed internal held-out set. Validation results are therefore used for configuration selection and are not interpreted as independent test estimates. Held-out results are not used to revise the selected configurations.

Global Top-5 and parent-balanced retrieval differ not only in label balance, but also in the number, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, and total token length of the retrieved examples. Their comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than the isolated causal effect of parent balancing.

\paragraph{Same-split benchmark positioning (RQ1).}
The main held-out comparison includes the Majority baseline, TF--IDF + logistic regression, the validation-selected Single configuration, and the validation-selected HiED--DA configuration. All methods are evaluated on the same fixed 1,000-case internal set using the same parent-label projection and scoring implementation.

Committed Top-1 Accuracy is the common primary endpoint because every evaluated configuration produces a committed primary diagnosis. Genuine Top-3 and multilabel measures are reported only when the corresponding output view is defined. In particular, Single produces a committed primary diagnosis and optional comorbid diagnoses but does not produce an ordered differential diagnosis; its comorbid outputs are therefore not treated as a genuine Top-3 ranking.

The Majority baseline always predicts the most frequent scoring parent in the training source. TF--IDF + logistic regression uses a prespecified character-level representation and twelve one-vs-rest, class-balanced classifiers trained on the 13,000-case training source. Full implementation settings are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

The comparison is matched at the level of evaluation cases, parent-label projection, and scoring implementation, but it is not a component-controlled architecture ablation. TF--IDF, Single, and HiED differ in training procedure, retrieval exposure, number of model calls, intermediate outputs, and output contract. RQ1 therefore compares complete diagnostic configurations rather than estimating the isolated effect of the multi-agent architecture.

\section{Stage-Wise Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:stage-wise-analysis-design}

The stage-wise analysis addresses RQ2 using the validation-selected parent-balanced HiED--DA trace on the fixed 1,000-case internal held-out set. This trace is used because it contains the complete ranked differential, criterion states, criterion-compatible set, and committed primary diagnosis required for the analysis.

The analysis uses $D_3$, $R$, and $S$ as defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}. Candidate detection and committed-primary agreement, represented by $D_3$ and $S$, are evaluated on all 1,000 internal cases. Criterion retention, represented by $R$, is defined only for the 915 checker-eligible cases whose benchmark label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} scoring parent represented by a corresponding Criterion Checker. Analyses involving all three indicators are therefore restricted to these 915 cases.

For the checker-eligible analysis, cases are partitioned into four mutually exclusive recorded-output groups:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item correct primary selection, defined by $S=1$;
\item candidate-detection failure, defined by $D_3=0$ and $S=0$;
\item criterion-verification failure, defined by $D_3=1$, $R=0$, and $S=0$; and
\item ranked-and-retained nonselection, defined by $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$.
\end{enumerate}

The ranked-and-retained nonselection group is the primary profile used to examine whether benchmark disagreement remains after a benchmark gold parent has already appeared in the genuine Top-3 and been retained as criterion-compatible. The indicators describe related recorded outputs rather than a strictly chronological runtime funnel, because Direct-Answer commits its diagnosis before criterion verification and the Criterion Checker evaluates the complete configured diagnostic scope rather than only the ranked Top-3.

A supplementary gold-informed oracle examines a broader set of ranked and criterion-supported alternatives to estimate optimistic primary-selection headroom. Because the oracle uses benchmark labels to identify favorable candidates and assumes that eligible disagreements can be corrected without harming initially correct predictions, it is treated as a non-deployable upper bound rather than an implemented selector or the primary answer to RQ2. Its candidate filter, thresholds, and sensitivity analyses are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Primary-Selection Intervention Design}
\label{sec:primary-selection-intervention-design}

This section defines the intervention analyses used to answer RQ4. Direct-Answer (DA) is the reference policy, and committed Top-1 Accuracy is the primary endpoint. The analyses test whether alternative procedures for converting the available diagnostic outputs into a committed primary diagnosis change or improve benchmark agreement.

\paragraph{Primary intervention analysis.}
The prespecified primary intervention analysis compares DA with Nominate-then-Select (NtS). Both policies reuse the same Diagnostician ranking, Criterion Checker states, and criterion-compatible set within each retrieval condition. DA preserves the Diagnostician's emitted primary diagnosis, whereas NtS applies the deterministic finalization rule defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} to select from the criterion-compatible candidates.

DA and NtS are compared on the same 1,000 internal cases under no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. The primary effect estimate is the paired difference in committed Top-1 correctness. Paired-bootstrap confidence intervals and exact McNemar tests are used as defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}. Difference-in-differences contrasts examine whether the effect of replacing DA with NtS varies across the three retrieval configurations.

Because DA and NtS share the upstream ranking and criterion-checking artifacts and NtS introduces no additional LLM inference, this comparison provides the clearest evidence about the evaluated deterministic finalization policy. It does not establish the general effectiveness of all criterion-based selection methods.

\paragraph{Supporting recorded-output methods.}
Deterministic fusion provides a supporting analysis that combines recorded candidate rank, compatibility status, and criterion-support summaries before deciding whether to replace the DA primary. It reuses existing system artifacts but evaluates one prespecified combination rule rather than a general fusion framework. Paired effects are reported only when complete case-level records are available.

\paragraph{Supporting additional-inference methods.}
Pairwise comparison and two-view debate introduce new inference to compare plausible alternatives more directly. The pairwise configurations ask the model to choose between closely supported candidates, while debate generates competing diagnostic arguments followed by a judging step. These methods may differ from DA in prompt structure, context organization, number of inference calls, decoding behavior, or final decision procedure. They are therefore interpreted as complete-configuration comparisons rather than isolated finalization ablations.

\paragraph{Supporting sampling-based methods.}
Self-consistency generates repeated Diagnostician outputs and selects the diagnosis receiving the largest number of primary votes. Confidence-informed self-consistency uses the same samples but weights the votes by the confidence recorded in each output. These methods evaluate whether repeated model preferences yield a more stable committed diagnosis; they do not directly validate the model's stated confidence or reproduce the complete HiED pipeline.

\paragraph{Comparison and reporting boundaries.}
Additional internal interventions use the validation-selected parent-balanced DA configuration as the reported reference unless otherwise specified. Paired differences and confidence intervals are reported only when the compared methods use identical case IDs and complete case-level outputs. When prompts, inference budgets, contexts, or output-generation procedures differ, the results are interpreted as comparisons between declared complete configurations.

Committed Top-1 remains the common endpoint across finalization policies. Set-level measures such as Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 are not used to isolate primary-selection effects because DA may emit an optional comorbid diagnosis whereas some alternative policies emit only one committed diagnosis. Exact eligibility rules, thresholds, seed schedules, inference budgets, and supporting statistical details are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Sensitivity and Transfer Analyses}
\label{sec:sensitivity-transfer-design}

This section defines the analyses used to answer RQ5. These analyses examine whether the main in-domain findings remain observable under changes in model scale, a second synthetic corpus, diagnostic scope, and corpus-specific lexical representations. They evaluate sensitivity and partial transfer under the tested conditions rather than establishing general robustness or clinical generalizability.

\paragraph{Within-family model-scale sensitivity.}
Model-scale sensitivity is evaluated using Qwen3-4B, 8B, 14B, and 32B on the same fixed 1,000-case internal set. The prompt family, configured diagnostic scope, retrieval policy, decoding contract, and evaluation endpoints are held constant across sizes. Committed Top-1, genuine Top-3, structured-output failures, and abstention events are recorded.

This comparison tests whether the candidate-coverage and candidate-to-commitment patterns depend strongly on one Qwen3 model size. Because all evaluated models belong to the same model family and serving setup, the analysis does not establish robustness across unrelated architectures, training procedures, or deployment environments.

\paragraph{External synthetic evaluation.}
MDD-5k is used to test whether the main findings remain observable under a second Chinese synthetic data-generation process with different transcript characteristics, label frequencies, and diagnostic coverage. The primary multi-way analyses use the complete 925-case evaluation set and the 878-case in-scope subset for which at least one benchmark diagnosis is represented in HiED's main configured output space.

A separate 490-case F32-versus-F41 subset is evaluated as a secondary binary task and is not treated as the main external performance estimate. The common external trace supports committed Top-1, genuine Top-3, the candidate-to-commitment gap, and same-trace primary-selection comparisons. It does not contain matched criterion-retention outputs, so the complete internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis cannot be reconstructed.

\paragraph{Diagnostic-scope expansion.}
A configuration-level scope experiment expands the diagnostic profile from fourteen to 33 configured categories by adding category-specific checker definitions without changing the model weights. The expanded categories are not used in the primary external evaluation.

This analysis distinguishes output-space representability from behavioral recovery. It tests whether making a benchmark diagnosis available to the Diagnostician and Criterion Checker is sufficient for that diagnosis to become a ranked or committed output. The experiment does not establish the general effectiveness or safety of diagnostic-scope expansion.

\paragraph{Cross-corpus lexical transfer.}
Cross-corpus lexical transfer is evaluated using the same TF--IDF classifier family trained on one synthetic corpus and tested both within that corpus and on the other corpus. The analysis examines whether corpus-derived lexical associations with benchmark labels remain predictive under a different synthetic generation process.

This experiment evaluates the portability of a lexical representation rather than the clinical validity of the diagnostic labels or the transferability of the complete HiED architecture.

\paragraph{Descriptive diagnostic error patterns.}
Diagnostic disagreements are further examined using first-listed-label confusion patterns, frequent benchmark-to-predicted diagnosis pairs, and the stage-wise profiles defined by $D_3$, $R$, and $S$. The confusion matrix uses one first-listed projected benchmark parent per case and is treated as a descriptive visualization rather than the primary multilabel endpoint.

Criterion-state, compatibility, and stage-wise output analyses are used to characterize where disagreement is recorded in the evaluated system. They do not assign clinical causes to individual disagreements or establish that the benchmark diagnosis is clinically preferable.

The model-scale and MDD-5k analyses provide the primary RQ5 evidence, while diagnostic-scope expansion, cross-corpus lexical transfer, and error-pattern analyses are interpreted as supporting sensitivity analyses.

\section{Complementary Clinical Evaluation Plan}
\label{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan}
\label{sec:psychiatrist-case-review}

The complementary clinical evaluation comprises two separate ongoing studies with different objectives. The LingxiDiag study is a small pilot review of selected synthetic benchmark disagreements, whereas the CGMH study is a real-world validation using psychiatric data collected at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital. Their populations, reference conditions, and outcomes will be analyzed separately and will not be combined into one agreement estimate.

\subsection{LingxiDiag Pilot Psychiatrist Review}
\label{sec:lingxidiag-pilot-review}

The LingxiDiag pilot is designed to test the feasibility of the psychiatrist annotation procedure and to obtain independent clinical judgments for a selected computational disagreement population. It is not a real-patient clinical validation study.

Twenty cases will be sampled without replacement from the 272 checker-eligible internal cases satisfying $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$. The random seed and selected case identifiers will be fixed before review. Cases will not be selected according to preliminary psychiatrist judgments or perceived clinical interest.

Two psychiatrists will review all twenty cases independently and in separately randomized orders. Both reviewers will remain blinded to the benchmark labels, HiED outputs, computational failure profile, selected-case rationale, and the other reviewer's responses.

The review contains two transcript-only annotation stages. In the first stage, reviewers receive the synthetic transcript and the configured diagnostic scope. For each case, they record whether the transcript supports a unique primary diagnosis, select a preferred primary diagnosis, identify any comorbid diagnoses, and provide up to three ranked differential diagnoses. These diagnostic judgments are locked before the second stage begins.

In the second stage, reviewers independently label a prespecified set of canonical evidence items and diagnosis-specific qualifiers as \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}. Shared symptom or impairment concepts are labeled once, whereas diagnosis-specific duration, frequency, temporal, relational, threshold, and exclusion conditions remain separate. The mapping from the physician-facing items to the original Criterion Checker items will be defined and frozen before annotation.

No HiED output or benchmark label will be disclosed at either stage. After both reviewers have completed and locked their independent assessments, the research team will compare the psychiatrist judgments with the LingxiDiag benchmark labels, HiED ranked and committed diagnoses, Criterion Checker states, and deterministic compatibility decisions. Pre-consensus inter-rater agreement will be retained. Any subsequent consensus or adjudication will be conducted using the transcript and independent annotations without disclosing the HiED outputs or benchmark labels.

Planned pilot outcomes include agreement on transcript-only primary diagnosis, ranked differential coverage, criterion-state agreement, inter-rater agreement, and agreement between physician-derived and system-derived compatibility decisions. Because the pilot includes only twenty synthetic cases selected from one computational disagreement population, its findings will be interpreted as feasibility and exploratory agreement evidence rather than an estimate of overall clinical diagnostic accuracy or Criterion Checker performance in an unselected population.

\subsection{Ongoing CGMH Real-World Validation}
\label{sec:cgmh-validation}

The CGMH study is an ongoing real-world validation using cases included according to prespecified clinical eligibility and inclusion procedures. The cohort will not be selected according to whether HiED agrees with the recorded diagnosis. The model checkpoint, prompts, retrieval configuration, diagnostic scope, criterion definitions, compatibility rules, transcript-preprocessing procedure, and evaluation implementation will be fixed before outcome analysis.

The CGMH evaluation contains three separate analyses.

First, HiED predictions will be compared with the diagnoses recorded during routine clinical care. This analysis measures agreement with the clinical record. Because the recorded diagnosis may incorporate information beyond the transcript available to HiED, including prior history, longitudinal follow-up, collateral information, laboratory findings, or other clinical observations, it will not be treated as equivalent to a transcript-only reference.

Second, two psychiatrists will independently review the same transcript version and diagnostic scope provided to HiED. They will remain blinded to the recorded clinical diagnosis, HiED outputs, and each other's responses. Reviewers will first record transcript sufficiency, primary diagnosis, comorbid diagnoses, and up to three ranked differential diagnoses. These diagnostic responses will be locked before they complete the prespecified canonical evidence and diagnosis-specific qualifier labels.

Original independent judgments will be retained. Disagreements will subsequently be resolved through a prespecified consensus or adjudication procedure while the recorded clinical diagnosis and HiED outputs remain concealed. This process will produce a psychiatrist-consensus transcript-only reference.

HiED will then be compared separately with each independent reviewer and with the transcript-only consensus. Planned outcomes include committed-primary agreement, ranked-differential coverage, comorbidity agreement, inter-rater agreement, Criterion Checker state agreement, diagnostic-scope coverage, and agreement between system-derived and physician-evidence-derived compatibility decisions.

Third, only after the independent transcript-only reference has been completed and locked will the psychiatrists inspect the HiED ranked differential, committed diagnosis, criterion states, supporting evidence, and compatibility results. The recorded clinical diagnosis will remain concealed during this audit-assisted review. Reviewers will assess the consistency of the trace with the transcript, identify criterion-level errors when present, and record whether exposure to HiED changes their original diagnosis.

The audit-assisted outcomes will be treated as exploratory human--AI review and usability evidence. Decisions made after disclosure of HiED are not independent reference judgments and will not be used as the clinical gold standard or as proof that the audit trace improves diagnostic accuracy.

Agreement with the recorded clinical diagnosis, agreement with the blinded transcript-only psychiatrist reference, and audit-assisted psychiatrist decisions represent different information conditions and will be reported separately. Data collection and clinical review are ongoing, and incomplete CGMH results are not included in the present quantitative conclusions.

The result therefore does not establish the clinical superiority of the lexical model, but it shows that the remaining HiED disagreements cannot be explained only by an absence of predictive information in the transcripts.

HiED's additional empirical value lies instead in the diagnostic artifacts unavailable from the direct baselines: an explicit ranked differential, criterion-level verification outputs, compatibility decisions, and a traceable committed diagnosis. The following sections use these artifacts to examine whether benchmark disagreement arises mainly during candidate detection, criterion verification, or primary selection.

\paragraph{Criterion verification.}

The criterion states and supporting text are model-generated audit outputs and should not be interpreted as clinician-validated criterion annotations.

\paragraph{Supplementary oracle analysis.}

A supplementary gold-informed oracle estimates an optimistic upper bound on primary-selection headroom. Criterion-supported candidates with a met-ratio of at least 0.5 are ordered by met-ratio. Adjacent candidates whose met-ratio difference is below $T=0.1$ are grouped into a close supported cluster. An initially incorrect case is counted as oracle-eligible when at least one benchmark gold parent appears in any such cluster.

Under this definition, 349 of the 1,000 cases are initially incorrect but contain a benchmark gold parent in a close supported cluster. If all 349 cases were corrected while all 518 initially correct cases remained unchanged, committed Top-1 agreement would reach the optimistic upper bound

\[
\frac{518+349}{1000}=0.867.
\]

This is not the performance of an implemented selector. The oracle uses the benchmark gold label to identify the preferred candidate, assumes that every eligible disagreement can be corrected, and assumes that no initially correct case is harmed. It is therefore a non-deployable, no-harm correction upper bound rather than an estimate of expected recoverable performance. The broader oracle candidate window also differs from the genuine Top-3 used to define the 284-case candidate-to-commitment gap. Full operational details and threshold sensitivity results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\paragraph{Recorded-output methods.}

Nominate-then-Select and deterministic fusion both reuse the ranked and criterion-derived outputs recorded in the common DA trace. Their differences from DA can therefore be attributed more directly to how the available outputs are converted into a committed primary diagnosis.

Both methods reduce Top-1 agreement, and their confidence intervals exclude zero. The external results therefore reinforce the internal finding that high candidate coverage and criterion support do not by themselves provide a sufficiently selective signal for deterministic primary re-selection. Reusing the criterion-compatible set or combining the recorded signals changes correct decisions as well as incorrect ones.

\paragraph{Additional-inference methods.}

The conservative pairwise gate produces no committed changes under its predefined eligibility conditions and therefore reproduces the DA result. It provides no evidence of improvement, but it also does not test the effect of pairwise comparison on a substantial changed subset.

Two-view debate performs below DA. Because this method introduces additional prompts, model calls, context, and a judging step, the result should be interpreted as the performance of the complete debate configuration rather than as the isolated causal effect of debate.

\paragraph{Sampling-based methods.}

Self-consistency and confidence-informed self-consistency produce small positive point estimates relative to DA at both sampling budgets. However, all corresponding confidence intervals include zero, so the observed differences do not provide clear evidence of an external improvement.

SC and CISC produce identical committed predictions in the retained external records. Under these runs, confidence weighting therefore does not change the decision obtained from the underlying samples. The results should not be interpreted as evidence that confidence weighting and unweighted voting are generally equivalent.

Taken together, none of the evaluated strategies provides a clear and reliable improvement over DA on MDD-5k. Recorded-output re-selection substantially reduces benchmark agreement, debate also performs below the reference configuration, and sampling-based aggregation yields only small and statistically uncertain point estimates. The external candidate-to-commitment gap identified in Section~\ref{sec:mdd-multiway} therefore represents potential decision headroom, but it does not imply that the tested selectors can recover that headroom.

Detailed discordant counts, exact McNemar tests, and paired-bootstrap results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}, Table~\ref{tab:external-paired-tests}. Set-level metrics are not used to isolate primary-selection effects because DA may emit an optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas several alternative policies emit only one committed diagnosis.

\chapter{Stage-Wise Failure Analysis}
\label{ch:error}

This chapter characterizes the benchmark-disagreement locations identified in Chapter~\ref{ch:results} by examining candidate ranks, diagnostic directions, criterion-state patterns, diagnostic-set construction, and selected output traces. The primary analysis uses the validation-selected parent-balanced HiED--DA trace on the fixed internal test set, with the applicable population stated for each analysis. Throughout this chapter, a \emph{failure} denotes disagreement with the projected benchmark gold labels at a recorded system stage; it does not establish a psychiatrist-confirmed diagnostic error or identify its clinical cause.

\section{Stage-Wise Failure Taxonomy}
\label{sec:ch8-stagewise-taxonomy}

This chapter separates benchmark disagreements according to the recorded stage at which a benchmark gold parent is no longer preserved. The analysis uses three indicators introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}: $D_3$ indicates whether a benchmark gold parent appears in the Diagnostician's genuine Top-3, $R$ indicates whether a benchmark gold parent is retained in the criterion-compatible set, and $S$ indicates whether the committed primary diagnosis matches a benchmark gold parent.

Candidate ranking and committed-primary agreement are available for all 1,000 internal cases. Criterion retention is defined only for the 915 checker-eligible cases. The joint $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis is therefore restricted to these 915 cases. \subsection{Candidate-Detection Failures}

Across all 1,000 internal cases, 198 cases do not contain a benchmark gold parent in the genuine Top-3. Of these cases, 113 are checker-eligible and also have an incorrect committed primary diagnosis, placing them in the mutually exclusive candidate-detection failure group. The other 85 cases are scored as the evaluation-only \emph{Others} category and do not have a corresponding gold-parent checker.

Full-scope criterion verification retains a benchmark gold parent in 79 of these 113 cases, even though that diagnosis is absent from the Diagnostician's Top-3. The remaining 34 cases neither rank nor retain a benchmark gold parent. This difference reflects the non-nested system design: the Diagnostician produces a limited differential, whereas the Criterion Checker evaluates the complete configured diagnostic scope.

Retention outside the Top-3 does not provide a deployable correction under Direct-Answer, because the committed diagnosis is selected from the Diagnostician's ranked candidates. These cases therefore remain candidate-generation failures under the recorded output contract.

\subsection{Criterion-Verification Failures}

Twenty-four of the 915 checker-eligible cases contain a benchmark gold parent in the genuine Top-3 but not in the criterion-compatible set. Twelve of these cases nevertheless commit a benchmark-consistent primary diagnosis under Direct-Answer. The remaining twelve cases have both gold-label non-retention and an incorrect committed primary and therefore form the mutually exclusive criterion-verification failure group.

The checker traces in these cases contain a high proportion of \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} states for criteria associated with the benchmark diagnosis. This describes how the system recorded the available evidence but does not establish whether the relevant information was absent from the transcript or whether the checker judgments were clinically correct.

The largest remaining failure group consists of the 272 cases in which a benchmark gold parent is both ranked and retained but another diagnosis is committed as primary. The following section examines the ranking positions, diagnostic directions, and compatibility patterns associated with this ranked-and-retained nonselection group.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

The results support a stage-wise interpretation of HiED rather than an accuracy-leading claim. Its ranked, criterion, compatibility, and finalization outputs reveal where benchmark disagreement remains after a plausible diagnosis has entered the differential. The external synthetic evaluation reproduces the candidate-to-commitment gap but lacks matched criterion retention, so it provides partial rather than complete replication of the internal localization.

This distinction matters because the same benchmark error can arise under different recorded conditions. A missing candidate calls for better candidate generation, a rejected candidate calls for scrutiny of the evidence representation or compatibility rule, and a ranked-and-retained alternative calls for comparative selection. The contribution of the trace is to separate these questions without treating the recorded stages as a causal model of the language model's internal reasoning.

\section{Why Compatibility Does Not Determine Primacy}
\label{sec:discussion-primary-selection}

Candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection answer different questions. Candidate detection asks whether a plausible diagnosis appears among the leading alternatives. Criterion verification asks whether each configured diagnosis is compatible with the available transcript under its study-specific operational rule. Neither stage directly compares the plausible diagnoses to determine which one should be committed as primary.

This distinction is important because psychiatric disorders frequently share symptoms. Sleep disturbance, fatigue, impaired concentration, low mood, worry, avoidance, and functional impairment may support several diagnostic categories at the same time. A transcript may therefore be compatible with more than one diagnosis. Satisfying an individual compatibility rule does not establish whether a diagnosis is primary, secondary, comorbid, or less explanatory than another compatible alternative.

The stage-wise analysis in Chapter~\ref{ch:error} makes this limitation visible. Ranked-and-retained nonselection often occurs when a benchmark gold parent is already positioned near the top of the differential. At the same time, compatible-set size and aggregate proportions of \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} states are similar between these cases and cases with a benchmark-consistent committed primary. Broad checker-derived quantities therefore do not provide a clear rule for choosing among compatible diagnoses.

Reliable primary selection requires comparative relations that independent criterion counts do not encode. These relations may include temporal order, illness course, episode structure, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity. For example, the system must distinguish whether anxiety symptoms form the main syndrome, occur within a depressive episode, represent an independent comorbidity, or remain insufficiently characterized by the available transcript.

The concentration of disagreements in particular diagnostic directions, especially F41-to-F32, indicates that the observed pattern is not uniformly distributed. Possible explanations include corpus-specific wording, learned class preferences, overlapping symptom descriptions, and missing temporal or exclusionary evidence. The present experiments do not isolate these causes, and independent psychiatrist review is required to determine their clinical interpretation.

Primary selection is therefore the largest remaining \emph{recorded} bottleneck under the evaluated HiED configuration and synthetic benchmark contract. This claim is deliberately bounded. The $D_3$, $R$, and $S$ indicators describe related system outputs rather than a causal runtime funnel, and the analysis does not establish that every benchmark disagreement is a clinically incorrect or automatically recoverable decision.

\section{Why the Evaluated Selection Strategies Did Not Reliably Close the Gap}
\label{sec:discussion-selection-strategies}

The candidate-to-commitment gap indicates that a benchmark-consistent diagnosis is often already available among the leading candidates. However, candidate availability does not imply that the recorded outputs contain a simple and reliable rule for replacing the committed primary diagnosis.

The recorded-output methods provide the clearest test of this possibility because they reuse the existing candidate ranking, criterion states, and compatibility decisions. Nominate-then-Select and deterministic fusion nevertheless did not improve Direct-Answer. These methods rely on rank position, compatibility status, and criterion-support summaries, but none of these signals directly represents why one compatible diagnosis should be preferred over another. A rule that overturns an incorrect commitment may therefore also replace an initially correct decision.

Pairwise comparison and debate attempt to make the comparison among candidates more explicit. However, these methods introduce new prompts, inference calls, context arrangements, and decision procedures and must be interpreted as complete configurations rather than isolated selection mechanisms. Under the evaluated settings, additional comparison did not reliably improve the committed diagnosis.

One important limitation is that all methods reason over the same fixed transcript. Additional inference may reorganize or reinterpret the available evidence, but it cannot acquire missing information about symptom onset, illness course, episode history, exclusionary causes, or causal relationships. When these distinctions are absent or ambiguous, another reasoning call may change the model's preference without resolving the underlying diagnostic uncertainty.

Sampling-based methods address preference stability rather than comparative validity. A diagnosis selected consistently across samples may reflect a stable clinical interpretation, but it may also reflect a stable corpus prior or model bias. Majority voting cannot correct a preference that is consistently misaligned with the benchmark, and confidence weighting is useful only when the model's stated confidence is associated with comparative correctness.

The observed gap should therefore be interpreted as analytical decision headroom rather than demonstrated automatic recoverability. The results do not show that criterion-based selection, pairwise comparison, debate, or sampling are generally ineffective. They show that the specific prompts, models, thresholds, inference budgets, and aggregation rules evaluated in this thesis did not reliably convert candidate availability into improved committed-primary agreement.

A more effective selector may require explicit representations of temporal order, syndrome dominance, explanatory dependence, exclusionary evidence, and comorbidity. When the available transcript does not support a unique primary diagnosis, requesting targeted follow-up information or abstaining may be more appropriate than forcing another selection from the same evidence.

\section{Boundaries Beyond Primary Selection}
\label{sec:discussion-set-scope-transfer}

Primary selection is not the only decision involved in producing a diagnostic output. The results also identify separate limitations in diagnostic-set construction, diagnostic scope, and transfer across data distributions. These limitations occur at different points and should not be interpreted as additional instances of the primary-selection bottleneck.

Diagnostic-set construction determines which diagnoses should accompany the committed primary diagnosis. Chapter~\ref{ch:error} shows that a benchmark-consistent primary can coexist with an incorrect complete diagnosis set. Primary diagnoses, supported comorbidities, and differential alternatives therefore require distinct output roles. Set-level measures such as Exact Match and label-level F1 also reflect output cardinality and comorbidity construction rather than primary selection alone.

Diagnostic scope defines which diagnoses the system is able to rank, verify, or emit. A diagnosis that is absent from the configured profile cannot be recovered through improved candidate ranking or finalization. However, the scope-expansion experiment in Chapter~\ref{ch:external} also shows that making a diagnosis representable does not automatically cause the system to rank or select it. Broader coverage may require corresponding examples, retrieval support, prompt adaptation, or training that changes system behavior rather than only its available labels.

External transfer presents a further boundary. MDD-5k reproduces the candidate-to-commitment gap under a second synthetic data-generation process, but its retained artifacts do not support the complete internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis. The strong degradation of the lexical classifier across corpora also indicates that predictive signals learned within one synthetic corpus may depend strongly on corpus-specific wording, generation procedures, and label distributions.

These findings place complementary limits on the main conclusion. Primary selection is the largest remaining recorded bottleneck within the evaluated internal HiED trace, but it does not account for errors in comorbidity construction, unavailable diagnostic categories, or distribution-specific prediction behavior. The external evidence supports partial persistence across synthetic corpora, not transfer to real patients or clinical workflows.

\section{Implications for Auditable Diagnostic Systems}
\label{sec:discussion-clinical-implications}

The completed computational analyses cannot determine whether an individual disagreement is primarily attributable to the system, the available transcript, or the benchmark reference. The blinded LingxiDiag pilot and the ongoing CGMH real-world validation provide separate pathways for clinical interpretation, as described in Section~\ref{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan}. Neither study contributes completed results to the present quantitative conclusions.

HiED's main auditability contribution is to preserve distinct diagnostic artifacts rather than only a final label. The ranked differential, criterion states, supporting text, compatibility decisions, and finalization source allow a reviewer to determine whether a benchmark diagnosis was unavailable, omitted from the leading differential, rejected by an operational compatibility rule, or retained but not committed as primary.

This inspectability should not be confused with correctness. The retained artifacts do not reveal the language model's hidden reasoning, validate the Criterion Checker, or establish that the committed diagnosis is clinically appropriate. A coherent audit trace may still contain unsupported evidence, an incorrect criterion state, or a plausible but clinically suboptimal diagnosis. Auditability is therefore an analytical and review capability rather than evidence of clinical reliability.

The results also suggest that future diagnostic systems should support evidence acquisition rather than relying only on repeated inference over a fixed transcript. When the leading diagnoses differ in duration, temporal sequence, episode history, exclusionary causes, or the relationship among mood, anxiety, trauma, and sleep symptoms, the system could identify the unresolved distinction and request a targeted follow-up question.

When additional information cannot be obtained, the safer output may be to state that the available transcript does not support a unique primary diagnosis. An abstention, uncertainty, or referral mechanism may be more appropriate than forcing a commitment among similarly supported alternatives. These behaviors were not evaluated in the present experiments and remain design directions for future clinical studies.

Overall, the findings support a distinction between making diagnostic alternatives inspectable and establishing that a final decision is clinically reliable. HiED contributes the former under the evaluated synthetic benchmark conditions; the latter remains dependent on independent clinical validation, workflow evaluation, and safety assessment.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

This chapter defines five boundaries on the reported claims: benchmark and measurement validity, dataset generalizability, experimental-comparison validity, statistical and reproducibility limits, and clinical deployment. They do not invalidate the benchmark results, but specify how far those results can be interpreted.

\section{Benchmark and Measurement Limitations}

The primary evaluation target is agreement with the dataset-provided diagnoses after the parent-level projection defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}, rather than agreement with an independently adjudicated clinical diagnosis. Some cases contain more than one projected benchmark parent, and committed Top-1 is counted as correct when the selected diagnosis matches any of them. The projection provides a common scoring space but removes subtype-level distinctions. The first-listed benchmark parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses, such as the confusion matrix, and is not the primary multilabel reference.

The reported metrics evaluate different system outputs. Committed Top-1 evaluates the selected primary diagnosis, genuine Top-3 evaluates the ranked differential, criterion retention evaluates the criterion-compatible set, and Exact Match and label-level F1 evaluate the complete emitted diagnosis set. These measures are not interchangeable. In particular, set-level metrics are influenced by comorbidity construction and output cardinality and should not be interpreted as isolated measures of primary selection.

The $D_3$, $R$, and $S$ indicators are analytical views of recorded system artifacts rather than stages in a strictly causal runtime funnel. Direct-Answer commits its primary diagnosis before criterion verification, while the Criterion Checker evaluates the complete configured diagnostic scope rather than only the ranked Top-3. Analyses based on candidate coverage, criterion retention, and committed selection therefore use different operational definitions and, in some cases, different eligible populations.

The gold-informed oracle is likewise an analytical upper bound rather than an implemented selection method. It uses benchmark labels to identify favorable alternatives and assumes that eligible disagreements can be corrected without harming initially correct cases. It therefore estimates optimistic decision headroom but does not represent expected deployable performance.

Overall, the measurement framework can localize where benchmark disagreement appears in the recorded outputs. It cannot establish the language model's internal causal reasoning, identify a uniquely correct clinical primary diagnosis, or show that the localized disagreements can be automatically recovered.

\section{Dataset and Generalizability Limitations}

LingxiDiagBench and MDD-5k consist of synthetically generated Chinese psychiatric dialogues. They provide controlled and reproducible evaluation settings but may contain regular generation patterns, simplified diagnostic ambiguity, limited comorbidity, clearer symptom cues, and less conversational and longitudinal complexity than real psychiatric consultations. Similar findings across the two corpora may therefore reflect properties shared by synthetic dialogue generation rather than general characteristics of clinical diagnosis.

Because the official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable, the main internal evaluation uses a fixed 1,000-case held-out subset sampled from the training-source corpus. Case identifiers were excluded from training and retrieval, but the evaluation cases still share the source generation process, language style, label distribution, and annotation procedure with the development data. A complete semantic near-duplicate audit was not performed. The internal results should therefore be interpreted as same-source held-out performance rather than independent external validation.

The public-validation split was used repeatedly for prompt development, compatibility-rule refinement, sensitivity analysis, and retrieval selection. It consequently represents development evidence rather than an independent estimate of generalization. The reversal between the validation-selected retrieval ordering and the held-out results further illustrates the uncertainty associated with configuration selection from a limited development split.

MDD-5k introduces a second synthetic generation process with different transcript characteristics, label frequencies, and diagnostic coverage. It reproduces the candidate-to-commitment gap, but its common trace does not contain the matched criterion-retention outputs required to reconstruct the complete internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis. Its results also combine corpus shift with differences in diagnostic scope and possible effects of transcript truncation that were not separately quantified.

The cross-corpus lexical results further indicate that strong within-corpus prediction may rely on corpus-specific wording, generation patterns, and label priors. Overall, the completed evidence demonstrates same-source behavior and partial persistence under a second synthetic distribution. It does not establish generalization to naturally occurring psychiatric conversations, real patients, independent hospitals, or Taiwanese clinical practice.

\section{Model and Experimental-Comparison Limitations}
\label{sec:model-comparison-limitations}

The main same-split comparison evaluates complete predictive configurations under a common case set, label projection, and scoring implementation. TF--IDF, Single, and HiED differ in their training procedures, retrieval exposure, prompts, number of inference calls, intermediate outputs, and output contracts. Their performance differences therefore represent comparisons among complete systems rather than isolated evidence for or against the multi-agent architecture. Published LingxiDiagBench results differ further in inference and scoring procedures and are included only for descriptive benchmark positioning.

The retrieval analysis is more controlled because the backbone model, prompt family, diagnostic scope, and output contract remain fixed within each architecture. However, global Top-5 and parent-balanced retrieval differ not only in label balance, but also in the number, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, and total token length of the retrieved examples. Their comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than the isolated causal effect of parent balancing. In addition, the primary retrieval settings for Single and HiED were selected independently on the validation split, so the main system comparison is not retrieval matched.

The DA--NtS comparison provides the clearest test of finalization because both policies reuse the same ranked candidates and criterion-checking artifacts. Nevertheless, the result concerns one deterministic NtS rule and should not be generalized to all criterion-based selection strategies. Set-level measures are also affected by the fact that DA may emit an optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits only one committed diagnosis.

The additional selection analyses introduce broader configuration changes. Deterministic fusion, pairwise comparison, debate, and sampling differ from DA in one or more of their prompts, inference calls, decoding procedures, context organization, aggregation rules, or computational budgets. Diagnostic-scope expansion also changes the available categories, prompts, checker definitions, and compatibility rules without additional model training. These results should therefore be interpreted as evaluations of the declared complete configurations rather than causal comparisons of isolated reasoning mechanisms.

Finally, the model-scale analysis is restricted to the evaluated Qwen3 model family and serving setup. Similar behavior across these sizes does not establish robustness across unrelated architectures, training procedures, quantization schemes, or deployment environments.

\section{Statistical and Reproducibility Limitations}
\label{sec:statistical-reproducibility-limitations}

The reported confidence intervals and statistical tests quantify uncertainty for the observed benchmark cases under fixed system outputs and evaluation configurations. They do not account for uncertainty in benchmark validity, prompt design, checkpoint selection, synthetic data generation, or performance in a new population. Statistical uncertainty within the evaluated sample should therefore not be interpreted as uncertainty over all possible models, datasets, or clinical settings.

Several analyses also involve small or imbalanced subgroups. Rare diagnostic categories and the strict criterion-verification group, which contains only twelve cases, do not support stable class-specific or group-level conclusions. An interval containing zero indicates insufficient evidence of a difference rather than equivalence. Conversely, an interval excluding zero establishes a difference between the evaluated configurations but does not identify which component caused that difference when the configurations vary in multiple ways.

The analyses differ in their degree of prespecification and record completeness. The same-split benchmark comparison, validation-based retrieval selection, internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis, and paired DA--NtS comparison form the primary quantitative evidence. The gold-informed oracle, diagnostic-scope expansion, lexical transfer, and additional selection interventions are supporting analyses. Some secondary methods lack complete case-level paired records, limiting uncertainty estimation and preventing reconstruction of which individual cases improved or deteriorated.

Reproduction requires preservation of the complete evaluation contract, including the model and serving environment, prompts and diagnostic configuration, data splits and recorded outputs, label projections and output-view definitions, and statistical procedures. A numerically similar result obtained with a different Top-3 definition, denominator, eligible population, or compatibility rule does not reproduce the same endpoint.

Finally, computational and analytical reproducibility remain distinct from clinical validity. A prediction pipeline and its numerical results may be reproduced exactly while still relying on an ambiguous benchmark diagnosis, an incorrect criterion state, or a clinically inappropriate operational rule.

\section{Clinical Validity and Deployment Boundaries}
\label{sec:clinical-deployment-boundaries}

The computational analyses localize disagreement among diagnostic scope, candidate ranking, criterion compatibility, and primary commitment. They cannot determine whether an individual disagreement is primarily attributable to the system, incomplete transcript evidence, or the benchmark reference. The benchmark diagnoses have not been independently adjudicated as unique clinical primaries, and the Criterion Checker states and supporting text remain model-generated rather than clinician-validated evidence. The reported failure groups must therefore be interpreted as computational benchmark-disagreement profiles rather than psychiatrist-confirmed diagnostic errors.

The blinded LingxiDiag pilot and the CGMH real-world validation described in Section~\ref{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan} are ongoing and contribute no completed results to the present thesis. The LingxiDiag pilot provides exploratory agreement evidence for selected synthetic cases. The CGMH study separately evaluates agreement with recorded clinical diagnoses, agreement with a blinded transcript-only psychiatrist reference, and exploratory audit-assisted review. Decisions recorded after disclosure of the HiED output are not independent reference judgments and cannot by themselves establish improved clinical accuracy.

HiED does not implement a validated mechanism for identifying unknown or out-of-scope presentations. The \texttt{Others} label is assigned only during evaluation when none of the benchmark diagnoses is represented in the scoring space; it is not a diagnosis emitted by the system. The current confidence fields are not calibrated to diagnostic correctness or clinical risk, and no clinically validated abstention, referral, or targeted follow-up policy has been evaluated.

Taiwan is the intended application context rather than a population in which clinical effectiveness has been demonstrated. The completed evidence does not establish performance for naturally occurring psychiatric consultations, spontaneous or mixed Mandarin--Taiwanese speech, local coding practices, hospital-system integration, clinician workload, subgroup fairness, treatment decisions, patient outcomes, or safety. The audit-assisted CGMH component is exploratory and does not constitute a prospective clinical-effectiveness study.

Auditability and reproducibility do not establish clinical validity. An inspectable and repeatable trace may still contain unsupported evidence, an incorrect criterion judgment, or an inappropriate committed diagnosis. HiED should therefore remain an offline research and review framework until independent clinical adjudication, prospective workflow and safety evaluation, appropriate privacy and governance controls, and ongoing monitoring and revalidation have been completed.

\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis introduced HiED, an auditable multi-stage framework for differential diagnosis from Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts. Rather than reducing diagnosis to a single final label, HiED preserves the ranked differential, diagnosis-specific criterion states, deterministic compatibility decisions, and committed primary diagnosis as separately inspectable artifacts. Its main contribution is therefore a system and evaluation framework for locating benchmark disagreement, rather than a state-of-the-art predictive result.

On the fixed internal evaluation set, TF--IDF + logistic regression was the strongest same-split predictor, while the validation-selected Single and HiED--DA configurations achieved similar committed-primary performance. HiED nevertheless exposed a substantial candidate-to-commitment gap. The largest strict recorded disagreement group consisted of 272 checker-eligible cases in which a benchmark gold parent appeared in the genuine Top-3 and was retained as criterion-compatible, but another diagnosis was committed as primary.

The analysis further showed that candidate availability and individual criterion compatibility do not by themselves determine comparative primacy. Ranked-and-retained nonselection cases often involved nearby alternatives and were strongly concentrated in particular diagnostic directions, while compatible-set size and aggregate criterion-state composition did not clearly distinguish them from cases with a benchmark-consistent commitment.

None of the evaluated deterministic, pairwise, debate, fusion, or sampling-based selection strategies provided a clear and attributable improvement over Direct-Answer. The candidate-to-commitment pattern remained observable across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k, although the external trace did not provide the criterion-retention outputs required for a complete replication of the internal stage-wise analysis.

These findings identify primary selection as the largest remaining \emph{recorded} bottleneck under the evaluated HiED configuration and synthetic benchmark contract. They do not establish that every benchmark disagreement is a clinically incorrect or automatically recoverable decision. The completed contribution of HiED is to make candidate generation, criterion verification, compatibility, and commitment separately observable; reliable comparative diagnosis and clinical validity remain to be established through independent psychiatrist review and real-world validation.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}

The following answers are restricted to the evaluated synthetic benchmarks, declared output contracts, and recorded system artifacts. They do not represent completed clinical-validation findings.

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=1.5cm,labelwidth=1.1cm,itemsep=0.2em,parsep=0pt]

\item[RQ1] \textbf{How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?}

HiED did not outperform the strongest same-split predictor. TF--IDF + logistic regression achieved the highest committed Top-1 Accuracy, while the validation-selected Single and HiED--DA configurations produced nearly identical committed-primary performance. HiED's distinct empirical value lies in the ranked differential, criterion states, compatibility decisions, and finalization trace that enable stage-wise analysis rather than in an overall accuracy advantage.

\item[RQ2] \textbf{How often is a benchmark diagnosis present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?}

A benchmark gold parent appeared in the genuine Top-3 substantially more often than it was selected as the committed primary diagnosis. Among the 915 checker-eligible cases, 272 satisfied $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$: a benchmark gold parent was both ranked and retained as criterion-compatible, but another diagnosis was committed as primary. This ranked-and-retained nonselection group was the largest strict recorded disagreement subset. It identifies primary selection as the largest remaining recorded bottleneck under the evaluated contract, but does not establish that all 272 cases are clinically incorrect or automatically recoverable.

\item[RQ3] \textbf{How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?}

Retrieval benefited Single more consistently than HiED. Global Top-5 was selected for Single on validation, and both retrieval configurations improved its held-out Top-1 result relative to no retrieval. HiED showed mixed validation outcomes, and parent-balanced retrieval was retained according to the predefined selection rule; however, global Top-5 produced higher held-out point estimates across the reported HiED endpoints. The results therefore do not identify a stable best retrieval configuration for HiED or isolate a causal benefit of parent balancing.

\item[RQ4] \textbf{Do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve over Direct-Answer?}

No evaluated intervention produced a clear and attributable improvement over Direct-Answer. NtS provided the cleanest finalization comparison because it reused the same upstream ranking and criterion-checking artifacts, but it reduced committed Top-1 agreement under all three retrieval conditions. Deterministic fusion, pairwise comparison, debate, and sampling-based methods likewise produced negative or statistically uncertain results under the tested configurations. These findings apply to the evaluated rules, prompts, models, and inference budgets rather than to the broader method families in general.

\item[RQ5] \textbf{To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and what error patterns characterize the remaining failures?}

The candidate-to-commitment pattern remained observable across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k. The external trace did not contain matched criterion-retention outputs, so the complete internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ pathway could not be replicated. Diagnostic-scope expansion increased output-space representability without producing committed-primary recovery, while cross-corpus TF--IDF performance degraded substantially.

Within the internal ranked-and-retained nonselection group, the highest-ranked benchmark gold parent was usually the second-ranked candidate, and disagreements were strongly concentrated in the F41-to-F32 direction. Compatible-set size and aggregate criterion-state composition did not clearly distinguish these cases from cases with a benchmark-consistent commitment. These findings demonstrate persistence and concentrated error patterns only under the tested synthetic conditions, not robustness across unrelated models or clinical populations.

\end{description}

\section{Future Work}

\paragraph{Completion of the planned clinical evaluations.}

The LingxiDiag pilot and CGMH real-world validation should be completed and reported as separate studies. The LingxiDiag pilot should report the frozen case sample, reviewer characteristics, independent transcript-only diagnoses, canonical evidence and diagnosis-specific qualifier labels, pre-consensus inter-rater agreement, consensus or adjudication outcomes, and offline agreement with the benchmark and HiED outputs. Because the cases are selected from one synthetic disagreement population, the findings should remain framed as feasibility and exploratory agreement evidence.

The CGMH study should separately report agreement between HiED and the recorded clinical diagnoses, agreement with the blinded transcript-only psychiatrist consensus, criterion-state and compatibility agreement, diagnostic-scope coverage, and exploratory audit-assisted review outcomes. The recorded diagnosis, blinded transcript-only reference, and psychiatrist decisions made after viewing HiED should remain separate because they represent different information conditions.

\paragraph{Comparative primary selection and evidence representation.}

Future primary-selection methods should model the comparative relations among plausible diagnoses rather than relying only on independent criterion satisfaction, compatible-set membership, ranking position, or criterion counts. Relevant relations include temporal order, illness course, episode structure, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, explanatory dependence, and the distinction among a primary disorder, a comorbid condition, and a differential alternative.

These methods should be evaluated using fixed upstream artifacts whenever possible so that changes in finalization can be distinguished from changes in candidate generation, prompting, or inference budget. Complete case-level records should also be retained to support paired gain--loss analysis and reproducible error review.

\paragraph{Targeted evidence acquisition, abstention, and broader validation.}

When the available transcript does not contain the information required to distinguish the leading diagnoses, future systems should identify the unresolved distinction and request a targeted follow-up question. Such questions may concern symptom duration, temporal sequence, previous episodes, substance exposure, medical explanations, functional impairment, or the relationship among mood, anxiety, trauma, and sleep symptoms.

When additional evidence cannot be obtained, an explicit insufficient-evidence, abstention, or referral output may be safer than forcing a unique primary diagnosis. These mechanisms require direct evaluation together with calibrated uncertainty and validated detection of unknown or out-of-scope presentations.

Broader technical evaluation should include unrelated model families, independently collected corpora, naturally occurring clinical transcripts, complete ranking and criterion-retention traces, and direct evaluation of diagnostic-set construction. Clinical development should subsequently examine clinician interaction, automation bias, review burden, subgroup performance, privacy, governance, patient safety, and prospective clinical outcomes before any deployment claim is made.

HiED demonstrates that making plausible diagnoses visible and criterion-compatible is not equivalent to selecting the most appropriate primary diagnosis. Its current contribution is to make this distinction observable and auditable; establishing reliable comparative diagnosis and safe clinical use remains the central direction for future work.
