\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial-consultation psychiatric transcript is a comparative decision under incomplete evidence. Multiple disorders may be compatible with the same transcript, yet only one can typically be judged the primary diagnosis. Key discriminating information---illness course, onset timing, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity relationships---may not be fully elicited within a single initial consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, and recent research has applied them to psychiatric diagnostic tasks, but linguistic fluency does not equate to reliable differential diagnostic ability.

Existing evaluations show a consistent gap: models perform well on coarse-grained classification, but accuracy drops sharply on fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis. This gap motivates an auditable architecture that locates the stage at which a diagnostic error occurs. A model may generate a plausible disorder supported by diagnostic criteria, yet fail to select it as the primary diagnosis. The distinction among candidate-diagnosis generation, criterion-based verification, and primary-diagnosis selection is usually obscured by a single accuracy metric.

To address this problem, we propose HiED, a differential-diagnosis framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts that keeps candidate-diagnosis detection, ICD-10 criterion-by-criterion verification, and primary-diagnosis selection as separately observable stages, rather than a single classification event. The system outputs a criterion-level audit trace with every prediction, allowing the diagnostic inference to be examined. It is not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic tool, but a research instrument for analyzing where transcript-based psychiatric diagnosis fails. The Taiwanese context in the title denotes the intended deployment setting; real-patient Taiwanese validation remains future work.

This thesis therefore makes two main contributions: an auditable failure-localization framework that keeps these stages separately observable, and reproducible evidence that primary-diagnosis selection constitutes a core bottleneck in psychiatric large-language-model diagnosis using only initial-consultation transcripts. Under this study's synthetic transcript-only setting, the system's primary limitation lies not merely in whether it can generate plausible candidate diagnoses, but more critically in whether it can compare among multiple criterion-supported candidates and determine which is best suited as the primary diagnosis.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric care in Taiwan operates inside a highly accessible single-payer system, and that accessibility carries a workload cost. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit any tier of medical facility without a referral, and some medical centers receive over ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in a single day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A clinic session is therefore shared among many patients, leaving little time for any single consultation. A psychiatric first visit must compress history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and a provisional differential judgment into that window, with documentation completed afterward. What the encounter leaves behind---a short free-form note, or at best a transcript of the dialogue itself---rarely contains a complete account of onset, duration, clinical course, exclusionary medical or substance-related causes, or prior treatment response.

How distress is expressed adds a second layer of difficulty. In Chinese-speaking clinical contexts, psychological distress is frequently presented through somatic complaints---insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, gastrointestinal discomfort---before, or instead of, explicit mood or anxiety language~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. In Taiwanese outpatient encounters, patients and clinicians also shift among Mandarin, Taiwanese registers, and institution-specific clinical phrasing, so the same underlying syndrome can surface under several different local vocabularies within one conversation. A first-visit diagnostic judgment therefore begins from evidence that is not only incomplete but also linguistically and culturally indirect.

The diagnostic task itself is demanding even when the evidence is complete. Depressive, anxiety, stress-related, obsessive-compulsive, somatic symptom, and sleep disorders share presenting symptoms such as insomnia, fatigue, impaired concentration, and functional decline. Diagnostic manuals such as ICD-10 supply symptom, duration, impairment, course, and exclusion requirements~\citep{who2019icd10}, but a presentation that supports both depression and anxiety may reflect genuine comorbidity or a single syndrome expressed through shared symptoms~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}, and even structured field trials have found only modest test--retest reliability for common diagnoses such as major depressive disorder~\citep{regier2013dsm5}. Under the time pressure described above, this comparative decision necessarily runs on fast clinical heuristics, which are exposed to well-documented cognitive failure modes in diagnosis, including anchoring, premature closure, and availability bias~\citep{croskerry2003cognitive}. The clinician must simultaneously hold multiple candidate disorders in mind, track which criteria each one satisfies, lacks, or leaves undetermined, decide which compatible disorder is primary, and judge whether the remainder are comorbidity or redundant description. The cognitive burden concentrates in this comparative bookkeeping and selection, not in recognizing that a patient is distressed.

\section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

This thesis frames the resulting requirement as an assistance problem rather than an automation problem: supporting the comparative step between recognizing that a patient is distressed and committing a primary diagnosis. Large language models are the natural starting point for such assistance. They process free-form clinical dialogue, summarize symptom evidence, generate differential diagnoses, and produce structured output~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. Evaluations in mental-health settings, however, expose a consistent technical gap: performance that is strong for coarse-grained judgments declines substantially when the task requires fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. Fluent explanation does not close this gap. A model-generated rationale may read coherently without faithfully reflecting the process that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}, so a system that names a plausible disorder has not thereby solved the diagnostic task.\ct{Does our model deal with all these problems?}

This thesis treats this coarse-to-fine gap as a process-level failure to be localized, rather than as a single endpoint classification error. We decompose the diagnostic process into three stages at which an error can occur: candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection. We define a \emph{selection bottleneck} as the case in which the correct diagnosis is generated as a candidate and survives criterion verification, yet is not the one finally committed as primary---a failure not in generating or verifying the diagnosis, but in choosing it.\ct{what problem?}

This decomposition is necessary because psychiatric differential diagnosis from an initial consultation transcript is a structured comparison under uncertainty, not flat text classification. Three distinctions make this precise. The first distinction is between evidence of absence and absence of evidence. If a transcript does not mention substance use or duration, that omission does not prove the criterion is false. Treating every unmentioned feature as negative over-excludes; treating every omission as compatible over-retains. The second distinction is between diagnostic compatibility and diagnostic primacy. Several disorders can be compatible with the same transcript, but only one can be committed as the primary diagnosis when a single label must be recorded. The third distinction is between comorbidity and redundant description. A transcript that supports both depression and anxiety may reflect independent comorbidity, or it may reflect one syndrome described through overlapping symptoms~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}.

These distinctions make the bottleneck observable without formal apparatus. For each case, three questions can be asked of the same system output: whether the correct diagnosis appears among the generated candidates, whether it survives criterion verification, and whether it is the one finally committed as primary. Averaged over cases, these three quantities---candidate coverage, verification retention, and committed selection---localize failure. High coverage is not clinical correctness; it only means the correct answer is often present somewhere in the output. Low committed accuracy is not necessarily a failure to generate the correct diagnosis; it may be a failure to prefer it over a close alternative. The selection bottleneck exists precisely when coverage or retention substantially exceeds committed selection. The formal definitions of these quantities, and their generalization to multilabel gold standards, are given with the evaluation protocol in \Cref{sec:gold-conventions}.

These three quantities concern the primary diagnosis alone; none of them determines whether the full predicted diagnostic set is calibrated. A system can retain the correct candidate while over-retaining redundant criterion-compatible alternatives, and it can commit the correct primary while over-emitting secondary labels that should not be read as independent comorbidity. Set-level agreement is therefore evaluated separately from primary selection, under the same evaluation protocol.

This decomposition changes how performance should be interpreted. A single accuracy number cannot distinguish a system that never considered the correct diagnosis from one that considered and verified it but preferred a close alternative. These are different failures with different remedies: the first calls for better candidate generation or retrieval, the second for better comparative selection or better evidence acquisition. The relevant question is therefore not simply ``how accurate is a system?'' but ``at which stage, if any, does the correct diagnosis disappear?''

Answering that question requires an approach that keeps candidate generation, criterion verification, and primary selection observable as separate quantities on the same cases, rather than collapsed into one classification event. It also requires that the evidence behind a commitment be reviewable: for clinical decision support, the final label is not enough on its own, since the relationship among transcript evidence, candidate diagnoses, diagnostic criteria, and the committed output must be inspectable rather than taken on faith. Building and evaluating such an approach for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis, in the Taiwanese deployment context described above, is therefore the objective, with the aim of locating, rather than assuming away, the stage at which current systems actually fail.

\section{System Overview}

To operationalize this process-level view, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid evidence-grounded system for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis from initial-consultation transcripts, with the Taiwanese outpatient setting described in this chapter as its intended deployment context. HiED is designed to keep candidate detection, ICD-10 criterion verification, and committed primary selection observable rather than collapsing them into a single classification event.

At the pipeline level, HiED processes each transcript through five stages. The input is a turn-based clinician--patient dialogue, normalized while speaker turns are preserved. Class-balanced retrieval then supplies supporting diagnostic context without letting frequent disorders dominate the prompt. An LLM diagnostician reads the transcript together with the retrieved context and emits a committed primary diagnosis, an optional comorbidity call, and a ranked differential. Candidate-specific checkers next evaluate each generated candidate against operationalized ICD-10 criteria, assigning every criterion one of three evidence states: met, not\_met, or insufficient\_evidence. Finally, a deterministic logic engine summarizes criterion support into a logic-confirmed candidate set and assembles the criterion-level audit trace.

HiED deliberately separates the committed decision from the record used to review it. Under the Direct-Answer policy---the selected reference configuration---the committed primary is preserved from the diagnostician's structured output, and the checker and logic layers serve as an output-side audit. Under the alternative Nominate-then-Select policy, the logic-confirmed set instead participates in finalization, which tests whether criterion verification can act as a selector rather than only as an audit layer. The two policies share every upstream output and differ only in the finalization rule, so their comparison isolates what explicit criterion compatibility contributes to primary selection. The full architecture, including retrieval, checker design, logic rules, and finalization policies, is described in Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions: an auditable hybrid architecture, and a stage-wise decomposition that turns the selection bottleneck into a reproducible empirical finding. The first is the artifact; the second is what the artifact makes measurable.

\subsection{An Auditable Hybrid Architecture with a Three-State Criterion Audit}

First, this thesis contributes an auditable hybrid architecture for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis from initial-consultation transcripts. The architecture combines learned diagnosis generation with explicit ICD-10 criterion checking and deterministic compatibility logic, allowing the system to produce both a committed diagnostic output and a criterion-level audit trace.

The audit trace is where the architecture departs from rationale-based transparency. Much prior work in this space makes a model explain why a diagnosis fits---stepwise reasoning and matched symptoms---a positive account whose faithfulness cannot be checked from outside the model (Chapter~\ref{ch:related}). The checker bank instead records, for every criterion of every candidate disorder, one of three evidence states---\texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, and \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}---so the trace also states what the transcript contradicts and, critically, what it never establishes. The third state operationalizes the distinction between evidence of absence and absence of evidence introduced in Section~\ref{sec:problem-definition}, which matters in a first-visit setting where discriminative facts are routinely unelicited. At the same time, the architecture deliberately separates the final selector from the audit subsystem. Under the selected Direct-Answer policy, the committed primary diagnosis is the diagnostician's output; the criterion trace is an output-side audit---a record that can be inspected, reproduced, and challenged---not proof of the model's internal reasoning. HiED therefore exposes the relationship among transcript evidence, diagnostic criteria, and system output, including where evidence is absent, while avoiding the overclaim that an opaque LLM decision has become inherently interpretable.

\subsection{A Stage-Wise Decomposition and the Selection Bottleneck}

Second, this thesis proposes a stage-wise evaluation framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, and committed primary selection, and uses it to establish the selection bottleneck as a reproducible empirical finding. The framework measures whether the gold parent diagnosis appears in the candidate list, survives criterion verification, and is ultimately selected as the primary diagnosis. This decomposition reveals a failure mode that single-number accuracy reporting collapses: high candidate coverage and high verification retention can coexist with substantially lower primary-selection accuracy. The resulting gap is the empirical target of this thesis.

The finding is defended rather than merely observed. A failure-mode-driven repair battery tests competing explanations for the bottleneck, examining whether it can be reduced through retrieval, logic-based re-selection, pairwise differential reasoning, debate, deterministic fusion, learned selectors, explicit evidence extraction, self-consistency, or confidence-weighted self-consistency. Each repair corresponds to a specific hypothesis about why the correct diagnosis disappears before final commitment; if the bottleneck were caused by a missing selection rule, a simple fusion error, insufficient reasoning rounds, or unstable sampling, the corresponding probe should improve committed primary accuracy under the frozen protocol. The negative results are therefore informative rather than incidental: they narrow the explanation space by showing which plausible remedies are insufficient under controlled conditions, and they motivate future work on structured comparative selection, transcript-matched human ceilings, and active evidence acquisition, rather than simply escalating agent interaction without grounding the additional reasoning in more diagnostic evidence.

This contribution is methodological as much as empirical. It provides a reusable way to ask whether a psychiatric LLM system fails because it never generates the correct candidate, because it rejects that candidate during criterion verification, or because it cannot choose the correct diagnosis among supported alternatives. These are distinct scientific claims with different remedies, and the proposed evaluation keeps them separate.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Overview}

Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction} formulated psychiatric diagnosis from an initial consultation transcript as a stage-wise process in which a system can fail during candidate detection, criterion verification, or committed primary selection. This chapter reviews prior work against that decomposition. Prior work has shown that LLMs can answer psychiatric diagnostic questions, simulate clinical dialogue, support criterion-grounded reasoning, and coordinate multiple agents, but existing systems rarely make observable, on the same cases, whether the correct diagnosis was generated, whether it survived criterion verification, and whether it was finally selected as the primary, in the sense introduced in Section~\ref{sec:problem-definition} and formalized in \Cref{sec:gold-conventions}.

This chapter is organized by mechanism rather than by chronology, and each section is read against that same problem definition. Psychiatric LLM systems define the clinical task family; medical multi-agent systems define the collaboration design space; interpretability and diagnostic-chain methods define the explanation problem; knowledge-graph and structured-diagnosis work define the future direction the selection bottleneck motivates. Across these lines, a single question recurs: where does a system fail when candidate generation, criterion verification, and primary selection are measured separately? The chapter closes by stating this gap explicitly and posing the research questions it motivates.

Because authentic psychiatric transcripts are difficult to obtain due to privacy and access constraints, evaluation resources in this area---including the corpora used in this thesis---are frequently synthetic or reconstructed rather than collected from real clinical encounters~\citep{wu2024callm}; Chapter~\ref{ch:data} details the specific corpora, their construction, and their limitations.

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

This line of work follows a broader shift toward large AI models in health informatics, where foundation-scale systems have been discussed for diagnosis, medical informatics, education, public health, and related clinical applications~\citep{qiu2023largeai}. Psychiatric LLM studies have evaluated general models on licensing examinations, differential-diagnosis vignettes, clinical reasoning tasks, and comparisons with psychiatrists~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. These studies establish that LLMs can produce clinically plausible answers, but they also show that task setting and reference standards strongly affect interpretation. A model that performs well on an exam or vignette is not thereby validated for transcript-only outpatient decision support. Automated depression-detection work has also been reviewed across text and audio modalities~\citep{li2025addreview}, but detecting a target condition is different from fine-grained, multi-class psychiatric differential diagnosis from a fixed initial-consultation transcript.

Recent knowledge-guided psychiatric systems integrate diagnostic criteria, inquiry, or progressive reasoning. MIND connects inquiry and diagnosis with criteria-grounded supports~\citep{li2026mind}; WiseMind/ProAI uses knowledge-guided multi-agent reasoning for psychiatric diagnosis~\citep{wu2026wisemind}; MentalSeek-Dx emphasizes progressive hypothetico-deductive reasoning for real-world psychiatric diagnosis~\citep{sun2026mentalseek}. These systems often gain power by asking follow-up questions or actively acquiring missing information, which trades a fixed-transcript evaluation for a harder-to-control acquisition policy. Holding the transcript fixed instead removes that degree of freedom but isolates a narrower, complementary question: what can be recovered from a single completed session alone.

Other psychiatric multi-agent pipelines operationalize structured interviews or clinical scales. Systems such as MAGI, MoodAngels, and DSM5AgentFlow use agent roles to navigate interviews, evaluate symptoms, or provide stepwise explanations~\citep{bi2025magi,xiao2025moodangels,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. These systems share a criterion-grounded, multi-agent motivation with the problem definition above, but none of them measures whether detection, verification, and selection diverge once acquisition is held fixed; that measurement gap, rather than any single design choice, is what the research questions below target.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

General medical multi-agent systems use multiple LLM roles to debate, consult, or adaptively collaborate~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. These systems demonstrate that structured collaboration can improve some medical reasoning tasks, but they do not imply that more agents improve every decision. When agents share a base model, their errors may be correlated; when they exchange free-form arguments, it can be unclear which component caused a final change.

The most capable diagnostic-reasoning systems set an informative reference point. AMIE, a large language model optimized for diagnostic reasoning, generated differential lists that improved clinicians' diagnostic accuracy on complex real-world cases~\citep{mcduff2025amie}. A companion study extends AMIE to the consultation itself, where its diagnostic dialogue with validated patient-actors was rated favorably against primary-care physicians in a randomized, double-blind crossover design~\citep{tu2025conversational}. AMIE differs in setting and goal from the problem defined above: it targets open-ended differential quality on English internal-medicine cases and is evaluated by expert raters, whereas the problem definition above holds a Chinese psychiatric transcript fixed and asks where a decomposed pipeline diverges across detection, verification, and committed selection. The two are also complementary on interpretability. AMIE's own evaluation notes that for complex cases it can draw conclusions from isolated symptoms rather than viewing the case holistically, and its reasoning is not auditable at the level of individual criteria---a gap addressed below through an explicit ICD-10 criterion trace.

More generally, multi-agent decomposition is useful for causal attribution only when stages have separate roles and stable interfaces, so that one stage can be held fixed while another is varied. Little of the surveyed work isolates finalization this way: most report a pipeline's aggregate accuracy rather than asking whether additional comparison or adversarial reasoning changes a specific commitment. This should not be read as showing that multi-agent reasoning is useless in general; it shows that whether uniformly applied debate or pairwise override can close a transcript-only selection gap is, at present, untested rather than resolved.

\section{Interpretability and Diagnostic Chains}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

The central risk motivating this line of work is that fluency can mimic reasoning: a model-generated rationale may be coherent without being faithful to the hidden process that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. This risk is especially important in high-stakes settings, where prior work has argued against relying on post-hoc explanations of black-box models as substitutes for genuinely interpretable systems~\citep{rudin2019stop}. In healthcare NLP, recent reviews similarly distinguish explainability from interpretability and emphasize that transparent reasoning claims require careful evaluation rather than fluent presentation alone~\citep{huang2024xiai}. Diagnostic-chain methods respond to this risk by making clinical reasoning more transparent. Chain-of-Diagnosis produces stepwise diagnostic chains and confidence distributions~\citep{chen2025cod}; interpretable depression-assessment systems decompose symptoms or factors in clinically meaningful ways~\citep{lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. These approaches improve transparency, but many still depend on model-generated reasoning, which does not by itself resolve the faithfulness risk above: a stepwise chain can still be a fluent post-hoc account rather than a faithful one.

This motivates a narrower and more defensible target than interpretability or explainability in the broad sense: \emph{auditability}. An audit trace is a structured record that can be inspected, reproduced, and challenged. It does not prove that the trace caused a model's final decision, and it does not prove that every criterion judgment is clinically correct; it provides a review object whose relationship to the transcript can be examined independently of the model's internal reasoning. This is a weaker claim than interpretability, but it is more defensible for clinical decision support, and it avoids two overclaims that the surveyed methods are prone to: that attaching a deterministic component to an opaque selector makes the entire system interpretable, and that reproducibility of a trace implies correctness.

Logic-based diagnosis provides the closest conceptual analogue to an audit-oriented approach. Constraint-logic approaches translate diagnostic manuals into explicit rules that can be inspected and edited~\citep{kim2025clp}. Such approaches share a commitment to explicit criterion structures, but existing instances either operate outside a hybrid LLM pipeline or use the logic layer as the sole final selector rather than as an audit and failure-decomposition layer alongside a learned selector---a combination the literature has not evaluated for Chinese-language psychiatric transcripts.

\section{Knowledge Graphs and Structured Diagnosis}

Structured diagnosis methods use knowledge graphs, heterogeneous relations, or information-guided questioning to model dependencies among symptoms, criteria, disorders, and missing information. MedKGI combines knowledge structure with information-guided inquiry~\citep{wang2025medkgi}; DKEC uses heterogeneous graphs and label-wise attention to support diagnosis, especially for rare labels and smaller models~\citep{ge2024dkec}. These methods are relevant precisely because, per the detection--verification--selection decomposition above, the residual difficulty in this setting is expected to be comparative selection among already-supported candidates rather than candidate generation itself.

A graph over symptoms, criteria, temporal relations, exclusions, and disorders is a natural future response to the selection bottleneck. It could represent relationships that scalar met-ratio thresholds cannot capture. No such selector is implemented here; graph-based selection remains motivated future work rather than a demonstrated solution, and showing why that structure is needed is the present contribution.

\section{Research Gap}

The literature establishes that LLMs can diagnose, consult, explain, integrate criteria, generate dialogue, and coordinate agents. What remains under-specified, across every line surveyed above, is the stage-wise relationship among candidate coverage, criterion verification, and primary selection on the same cases, in the sense defined in Section~\ref{sec:problem-definition}. Prior work often reports final accuracy or qualitative explanations rather than asking, on the same cases, whether the correct diagnosis was present, whether it survived verification, and whether it was selected.

This gap motivates the research questions this thesis addresses, pursued through the architecture introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} and evaluated in the chapters that follow:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] Can a hybrid LLM--rule architecture produce a ranked psychiatric differential, a committed primary diagnosis, optional comorbidity, and a criterion-level audit trace?
\item[RQ2] How large is the gap among candidate coverage, criterion verification, and realized primary-selection accuracy?
\item[RQ3] Can logic re-selection, pairwise comparison, debate, deterministic fusion, learned selection, calibration, self-consistency, or confidence-weighted aggregation close that selection gap?
\item[RQ4] Does the same pattern persist across model size, retrieval sensitivity and selector-side similarity probes, external synthetic distribution shift, label-space expansion, and major confusion boundaries?
\item[RQ5] What do benchmark and audit results establish, and what remains unresolved without real-patient data or a transcript-only human reference?
\end{description}

These questions frame HiED not only as a diagnostic system but also as an experimental instrument: its purpose is to reveal which stage contributes which kind of evidence, and which stage remains limiting under a frozen evaluation protocol.

\chapter{HiED System Architecture}
\label{ch:architecture}

\section{Input and Transcript Format}

HiED takes as input a single Chinese-language psychiatric consultation transcript: a turn-based dialogue between clinician and patient, normalized while speaker turns are preserved. The task is scoped to initial outpatient consultations. LingxiDiagBench, and therefore LingxiDiag-16K, targets initial psychiatric diagnosis rather than follow-up assessment or longitudinal care coordination~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. Transcripts accordingly do not carry a prior diagnostic history beyond what a patient volunteers within the session itself, and HiED is evaluated within this same initial-visit setting rather than across a treatment course.

Each diagnostician invocation is built from four parts: brief task instructions describing the differential-diagnosis objective and required output fields, the normalized transcript above, the class-balanced supporting cases retrieved for that transcript (\Cref{sec:retrieval}), and a structured output-schema specification. The diagnostician therefore never sees the transcript alone; retrieval context is assembled into the prompt before the model runs, rather than requested by the model itself mid-generation.

\section{Model Overview}

\ThesisFigure{fig_pipeline.pdf}{HiED pipeline. Given the prompt assembled above, HiED processes each case through the following steps:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item Prompt the diagnostician to emit a ranked differential, committed primary, optional comorbidity, confidence, and rationale.
\item Run disorder-specific ICD-10 criterion checkers over candidate diagnoses.
\item Convert checker outputs into \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, and \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} states.
\item Apply deterministic threshold logic to produce a logic-confirmed set.
\item Emit the primary diagnosis, ranked differential, optional comorbidity, criterion trace, logic results, and decision trace.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Class-Balanced Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

Retrieval-augmented generation supplements an LLM prompt with relevant examples or knowledge~\citep{lewis2020rag}. In psychiatric dialogue, retrieval can remind a model of less salient disorders, provide lexical anchors, or expose similar presentations, and can therefore improve candidate detection; it can also amplify corpus-specific wording and does not by itself resolve primary selection among already-supported candidates.

The retrieval component is designed to support candidate breadth without letting frequent disorders dominate the prompt. Each query transcript is first encoded by BGE-M3, a multilingual text-embedding model used here to convert transcripts into dense retrieval vectors~\citep{chen2024bgem3}. These vectors are searched with FAISS, a local vector-similarity indexing library rather than a separate model or managed database~\citep{douze2024faiss}. Instead of returning the globally nearest cases, the retriever returns the top case per candidate disorder class. This class-balanced design keeps rare disorders visible and prevents common depression-like presentations from crowding out alternatives.

Retrieval therefore plays a bounded role in the architecture. It can improve candidate coverage by supplying examples and lexical anchors to the diagnostician, but it is not treated as a selector. This distinction is important for the thesis claim: retrieval may help the system preserve the correct diagnosis among its candidates, but it does not by itself solve the problem of committing to the correct primary diagnosis.

\section{Diagnostic Agent}

The diagnostic agent is a local Qwen3-32B-AWQ model---a 32-billion-parameter model compressed via activation-aware weight quantization (AWQ) to run efficiently on local hardware---served with vLLM, an LLM inference engine used here to run the model locally under the canonical configuration. It reads the transcript and retrieved context, then emits a structured object with the following fields:

\begin{itemize}
\item a ranked differential diagnosis list;
\item a committed primary parent diagnosis;
\item an optional comorbid parent diagnosis;
\item a confidence score;
\item a brief rationale;
\item optional uncertainty or abstention flags.
\end{itemize}

The committed primary is constrained to the ranked differential. This constraint lets Top-1 and Top-3 be interpreted together: the primary is the first commitment, while the ranked list records the nearby alternatives the model considered. A wrong Top-1 with a correct Top-3 is therefore not a total generation failure; it is a ranking failure among generated candidates. The confidence score is self-reported by the model in its own generated text---a verbalized estimate rather than a value read from an internal probability distribution---and later experiments (Appendix~\ref{app:interventions}) test both aggregating and calibrating this self-reported value.

\section{Criterion Checkers}

The International Classification of Diseases provides a standardized vocabulary for diagnostic categories~\citep{who2019icd10}, but computational use requires operational decisions: which criteria are required, which are optional, how symptom counts are computed, how duration requirements are represented, and how exclusions are handled when evidence is missing. These decisions transform narrative diagnostic criteria into the checkable structures used below. Operationalization improves reproducibility but narrows clinical judgment: a deterministic rule can state whether a set of criterion states satisfies a threshold, but it cannot recover context the transcript never supplied, nor determine whether two compatible disorders should be treated as primary-plus-comorbid or as competing explanations for the same syndrome. The ICD-10 logic is therefore used here as an audit scaffold rather than as a replacement for expert diagnosis: it records what the transcript appears to support, contradict, or leave unresolved.

For each candidate disorder, criterion-checking agents evaluate transcript evidence against the operationalized ICD-10 criteria. Each criterion receives one of three states: \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}. The checker may also provide an evidence quote, confidence value, and short justification.

The three-state design is the clinical core of the audit. A missing duration statement, unasked substance history, or absent exclusion clause should not automatically become a negative criterion. Recording insufficient evidence preserves the uncertainty that a single transcript creates. It also allows the results to measure whether the checker bank is high recall, high selectivity, or neither.

Checker outputs are not clinician-level gold. They are structured model outputs subject to error. Their value is that they can be inspected and replayed through deterministic logic. A reviewer can challenge whether a cited span supports a criterion without accepting or rejecting the entire final diagnosis.

\section{Deterministic Logic Engine}

The logic engine applies count-based disorder-specific rules to criterion states and records which diagnoses are logic-confirmed. Determinism ensures that the same checker trace always yields the same logic result. This makes the audit reproducible and allows controlled comparisons of finalization policies.

The logic engine is intentionally limited. It summarizes compatibility with diagnostic criteria; it does not by itself solve primacy. Several disorders can satisfy threshold logic in the same transcript, particularly when symptoms overlap and exclusions remain unobserved. The results later show that the logic-confirmed set often retains the gold parent but also retains many alternatives. This is exactly why verification and selection must be measured separately.

\section{Finalization Policies}

HiED evaluates multiple ways to produce the final primary diagnosis. Direct-Answer preserves the diagnostician's committed primary and comorbidity call, and is the selected reference policy. Nominate-then-Select (NtS) instead selects the diagnostician's highest-ranked candidate that is logic-confirmed; if none of the diagnostician's top-ranked candidates is confirmed, it falls back to the confirmed candidate with the highest met ratio, and if no candidate is confirmed at all, it falls back to the diagnostician's original top-ranked candidate rather than abstaining. NtS does not emit a secondary diagnosis. Pairwise comparison and debate are evaluated as repair probes that attempt to resolve close alternatives.

Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select share upstream outputs. The diagnostician candidates, criterion states, and logic results are identical; only the finalization rule changes. This shared-upstream design isolates the causal effect of finalization. If Nominate-then-Select improves or degrades Top-1, that difference cannot be attributed to candidate generation or checker variation.

Pairwise reasoning is evaluated in three modes: an independent run, a controlled replay over identical upstream outputs, and a forced override. The independent run tests local usefulness on activated cases. Controlled replay tests whether the stage actually changes committed primaries when everything upstream is fixed. Forced override tests global safety. This separation prevents a locally plausible comparison from being mistaken for a safe system-wide selector.

\section{Output and Audit Trace}

The complete output contains the committed primary, optional comorbidity, ranked differential, criterion states, cited evidence, logic-confirmed codes, and decision trace. These objects support different forms of review. A clinician or researcher may accept the differential but reject the primary, accept the primary but question a criterion quote, or accept the evidence but disagree with the finalization policy.

This decomposition is the practical meaning, in HiED specifically, of the auditability defined in Section~\ref{sec:auditability-definition}. The system does not ask the user to trust a single opaque recommendation. It exposes where the recommendation came from, which alternatives were considered, which criteria were supported, which criteria were contradicted, and which remained unobserved. Concretely, this separates evidence review from final commitment and allows an error to be attributed to candidate detection, criterion verification, or primary selection rather than reported only as a wrong label---the same three-way attribution the detection--verification--selection decomposition of Section~\ref{sec:problem-definition} measures in aggregate. The audit does not make the decision correct, but it makes disagreement localizable, and it is a precondition for the kind of systematic review a future clinician-facing interface would need, not a substitute for validation.

\section{Ontology-Driven Scope}

The ontology contains a broader set of ICD-10 parent codes and entries than the selected benchmark profile activates. Expanding the active profile is a configuration change rather than weight retraining. This is an engineering property: the system can represent additional disorders when the ontology and prompt configuration include them.

The thesis does not treat mechanical representability as behavioral adaptation. A newly activated disorder may be available in the output space yet never selected as primary. External scope-expansion experiments later demonstrate this distinction. The claim is therefore precise: HiED can be reconfigured to audit a broader ontology, but successful recovery of newly representable diagnoses requires empirical validation and likely selector adaptation.

\section{Summary}

HiED separates the diagnostic pipeline into stages that can be measured and challenged: retrieval-supported candidate generation, criterion verification, deterministic compatibility logic, and committed primary selection. This architecture makes the later experiments possible. Because the stages are distinct, the thesis can ask whether failures arise from missing candidates, failed verification, or wrong comparative commitment.

\section{Data, Datasets, Splits, and Roles}

Two synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogue corpora are used, each assigned a distinct evaluation role: LingxiDiagBench for in-domain development and the frozen internal test split, and MDD-5k as an external synthetic distribution-shift corpus.

LingxiDiagBench provides synthetic, EMR-aligned Chinese psychiatric consultation dialogues with multiple task granularities~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. Its reported evaluation shows a coarse-to-fine difficulty gradient: the evaluated LLMs reached 92.3\% on binary depression--anxiety classification but only 28.5\% on the full 12-way differential diagnosis, where ``12-way'' means choosing among twelve scored parent diagnosis classes after parent-level label projection, as defined in \Cref{sec:gold-conventions}. Dynamic consultation often underperformed static evaluation. It nominally contains 16,000 dialogues; because the official 1000-case test split was unavailable at freeze, the accessible corpus comprises 15,000 cases, assigned three disjoint on-disk roles (\Cref{tab:data-roles-rewrite}):

\section{Label Projection and Metrics}
\label{sec:gold-conventions}

For each transcript the system emits one committed primary parent diagnosis, an optional comorbid parent diagnosis, a ranked candidate list, criterion states with evidence, deterministic logic results, and a decision trace. The benchmark taxonomy contains twelve scored parent classes:
\[
\mathcal{C}=\{\mathrm{F20},\mathrm{F31},\mathrm{F32},\mathrm{F39},\mathrm{F41},
\mathrm{F42},\mathrm{F43},\mathrm{F45},\mathrm{F51},\mathrm{F98},
\mathrm{Z71},\mathrm{Others}\}.
\]
Raw ICD-10 subcodes are projected deterministically to this parent space before scoring. Codes whose parents are outside the active target set are folded to \emph{Others} unless an external scope-expanded configuration explicitly activates them. The selected benchmark profile activates fourteen disorder entries, including subtype-level entries for F41 and F43, while scoring after parent projection into the twelve-class parent space. Thus, ``fourteen disorders'' refers to active diagnostic entries, whereas ``twelve classes'' refers to the scored label space. This distinction prevents a granularity decision from being confused with diagnostic discrimination. The full active profile, with per-entry criterion counts and confirmation rules, is listed in \Cref{app:active-profile}.

Multiple gold parents are retained for official multilabel metrics. For case $x$, let $G(x)\subseteq\mathcal{C}$ denote the deduplicated gold parent set. The committed primary prediction is projected to $p_1(x)$, and the canonical ranked list is formed by placing the committed primary first and then appending deduplicated ranked candidates.

The thesis reports three gold conventions when appropriate. Any-gold Top-1 credits a prediction if the committed parent appears anywhere in the gold set. First-listed analyses use the first listed gold parent as a single-label convention. Exact Match requires the predicted parent set to equal the gold set. These conventions answer different questions: whether the committed diagnosis is acceptable under a multilabel gold set, whether the model matches a designated primary, and whether the full predicted set is calibrated. They are never substituted for one another without labeling.

Building on these conventions, Top-1 measures commitment. It is correct when the committed primary parent appears in the gold parent set:
\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}1}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)].
\]
Top-3 measures recoverable coverage. Let $R_3(x)$ be the deduplicated top-three parent list with the committed primary first. Then
\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}3}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing].
\]
Exact Match measures set calibration:
\[
\mathrm{Exact}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[M(x)=G(x)],
\]
where $M(x)$ is the deduplicated multilabel prediction set.

These multilabel definitions have a single-parent counterpart that formalizes the stage-wise decomposition of Section~\ref{sec:problem-definition}. For transcript $x$ with a single gold parent $y$, let $C_k(x)$ denote the top-$k$ candidate diagnoses a system produces, $V^{+}(x)$ the set of diagnoses that survive criterion verification, and $\hat{y}(x)$ the committed primary prediction; define candidate coverage $D_k(x)=\mathbb{1}[y\in C_k(x)]$, verification retention $R(x)=\mathbb{1}[y\in V^{+}(x)]$, and committed selection $S(x)=\mathbb{1}[\hat{y}(x)=y]$. The multilabel definitions above generalize this single-parent formalization rather than replacing it, under the any-gold convention introduced above. $D_k(x)$ generalizes to $\mathbb{1}[G(x)\cap C_k(x)\neq\varnothing]$, which the Top-3 definition above instantiates with $C_3(x)\approx R_3(x)$; $S(x)$ generalizes to $\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)]$, which is exactly the Top-1 definition above; and $R(x)$ generalizes to $\mathbb{1}[G(x)\cap V^{+}(x)\neq\varnothing]$, reported later as Gold-in-confirmed (Appendix~\ref{app:metrics}). Later chapters therefore write $\bar D_3$, $\bar R$, and $\bar S$ for the sample means of these multilabel-generalized quantities, identifying them respectively with Top-3, Gold-in-confirmed, and Top-1.

Macro-F1 and weighted-F1 are computed over the twelve-class multilabel indicators. Macro-F1 weights classes equally and is sensitive to rare-class failure. Weighted-F1 weights by gold support and is more influenced by frequent labels. Reporting both prevents an apparently moderate aggregate result from hiding poor rare-class recall.

The selection-bottleneck analysis interprets these metrics jointly. High Top-3 does not mean the system is clinically correct; it means the correct diagnosis is often present in the output. Low Top-1 does not necessarily mean the system failed to consider the correct diagnosis; it may indicate failure to select it as primary.

Uncertainty for single-system headline proportions is reported with 95\% Wilson score intervals, attached to the frozen architecture ladder and to the external Top-1 summary (\Cref{tab:ladder-ci} and \Cref{tab:external-summary-ci}). Intervals are deliberately not attached to every derived cell: within-battery and between-policy contrasts are adjudicated by the paired McNemar protocol described next, so the single-system intervals provide scale context for the headline estimates rather than serving as the comparison instrument.

Paired binary outcomes use exact McNemar tests, appropriate because compared systems are evaluated on the same cases; all reported $p$-values in the main text are exact McNemar paired tests unless a table notes otherwise, and Appendix~\ref{app:metrics} details the correction applied across related comparison families. A non-significant result is not evidence of equivalence, which matters for the repair battery reported in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}: a convergent pattern of no stable gain across mechanistically different probes, rather than any single non-significant result, constrains simple explanations for the bottleneck.

\section{Run Environment}

Every run is checked for expected case count, schema validity, abstention and parse-recovery events, frozen ordering, configuration identity, and candidate integrity; these checks prevent malformed outputs, silent dropped cases, or reorderings from contaminating paired comparisons. A system that fails to produce valid outputs on difficult cases could appear more accurate than it is if run health were ignored.

Reproducibility across the data protocol rests on the same computational and semantic lineage that later experimental results depend on. The selected system runs on one NVIDIA RTX 5090 and uses Qwen3-32B-AWQ served through vLLM, a high-throughput LLM inference engine, with greedy decoding, an 8192-token context (a token is roughly a word or sub-word unit, so this bounds the combined length of the input transcript, retrieved context, and any multi-turn exchange the model can process at once), maximum output length 1536, disabled thinking mode, and bounded retries. Reproducibility further requires matching split, policy, metric definition, and artifact identity: the same numerical accuracy can mean different things if computed on a different split, under a different finalization policy, or with a different gold convention. The evaluation protocol therefore treats a result as a tuple rather than as a standalone number---what data were scored, how labels were projected, which system policy emitted the output, which metric was computed, and which statistical test was applied.

\chapter{Experimental Design}
\label{ch:experimental}

\section{Design Logic and Experimental Ladder}

Having fixed the data, labels, and metrics in the previous chapter, this chapter turns the selection-bottleneck hypothesis into a controlled experimental battery. Each variant is included because it corresponds to a specific alternative explanation: perhaps the system lacks retrieval, perhaps verification should re-select the primary, perhaps local comparison is needed, perhaps debate or sampling reveals hidden consensus, or perhaps a simple deterministic fusion rule is missing. A negative result is interpretable only when the intervention is tied to the mechanism it tests.

The design therefore separates the proposed architecture from the repair probes. Single establishes a direct baseline with no HiED-specific component. Single+RAG then isolates HiED's own class-balanced retrieval, testing its contribution before the full structured architecture is introduced; it is therefore an ablation step in the architecture ladder rather than an external baseline. HiED--DA and HiED--NtS test the structured architecture and finalization policy. Pairwise comparison, debate, fusion, learned selection, evidence extraction, and self-consistency are not treated as moving definitions of HiED; they are controlled probes of possible failure causes.

\section{Detection--Selection Decomposition and Verification Experiments}

The primary evidence for a selection bottleneck is the gap between Top-3 candidate coverage and Top-1 committed accuracy. This gap requires no threshold and no oracle: it is computed directly from the ranked output the system already emits. When Top-3 is much higher than Top-1, the system often has the correct parent diagnosis in view but fails to commit it.

A close-cluster recoverability analysis is reported only as a secondary upper-bound analysis. It counts wrong Direct-Answer commitments for which the gold parent lies among score-adjacent candidates that a better selector might plausibly recover. Because it presupposes access to the gold label, it is not a deployable method. Its role is to size the opportunity available to improved selection, not to claim that the current system recovers that opportunity.

Verification experiments ask whether criterion checking improves primary selection or instead functions mainly as an audit and set-calibration mechanism. The Direct-Answer versus Nominate-then-Select contrast is central because it holds upstream candidates and criterion traces fixed while changing only the finalization rule. If verification can choose the primary, NtS should improve Top-1 over DA. The checker factorial varies checker granularity and verification scope. Exact Match versus Top-1 analyses test whether a policy improves diagnostic-set calibration at the cost of primary commitment. Together, these experiments distinguish three possibilities: verification may reject the gold diagnosis, retain it but also retain too many alternatives, or sharpen the primary. The results later show the second pattern dominates.

\section{Selection Repairs and Robustness Axes}

This section separates three layers of evidence that are easy to conflate. The first layer is the frozen repair battery: interventions evaluated as candidate remedies for the primary-selection bottleneck under a fixed scoring protocol. The second layer consists of non-headline learned selectors and exploratory probes, which test whether a recoverable selection signal exists but are not part of the selected HiED architecture. The third layer consists of robustness axes, which test whether the bottleneck interpretation persists across definition-similarity signals, selector-side case-neighbor reranking, retrieval sensitivity, corpora, confusion boundaries, ontology scope, and backbone size. This separation matters because a failed repair, a non-transferable selector, and a robustness perturbation answer different questions. All three layers are read under a common freeze discipline, stated at the end of this section.

\paragraph{Pairwise reasoning.} Pairwise comparison uses adjacent ranked candidates that are sufficiently supported and close under the pre-specified met-ratio gate: each candidate must have met ratio at least $0.5$, and the adjacent met-ratio distance must be less than $0.10$. The close-cluster scan is applied over the ranked list rather than only around the committed primary. In controlled replay, the eligible close clusters are derived from the Direct-Answer (DA) reference output: DA provides the ranked candidates, candidate adjacency, and criterion-derived met ratios, while candidate generation and criterion verification are not rerun. Only the pairwise comparison and finalization step is applied on top of these fixed DA outputs. The independent run tests raw pairwise performance; controlled replay tests whether pairwise comparison causally changes committed primaries when upstream evidence is held fixed; forced override tests whether applying the comparison globally is safe.

\paragraph{Debate.} Debate uses a bounded multi-round protocol in which advocates argue for leading candidates and a judge resolves the exchange~\citep{du2024debate}. It tests whether additional adversarial reasoning supplies comparative information missing from the direct output.

\paragraph{Deterministic fusion.} Deterministic fusion tests whether the selection bottleneck can be repaired by a fixed rule over signals the system already records, rather than by adding new evidence, retraining the diagnostician, or introducing another reasoning agent. The candidate rules combine the diagnostician's rank order, criterion met ratios, and deterministic threshold-satisfaction indicators. Rules are selected only on the development split and then frozen before test evaluation; full rule-sweep details are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:interventions}. The selected rule preserves the diagnostician's committed primary unless that primary is not logic-confirmed and a logic-confirmed alternative exceeds it by a pre-specified margin, in which case the confirmed alternative becomes the committed primary. This probe asks whether a simple rule-based recombination of existing ranking and verification signals is sufficient to improve committed primary selection.

\paragraph{Self-consistency and CISC.} Self-consistency samples the diagnostician multiple times and aggregates the committed primary by majority vote. Confidence-informed self-consistency weights votes by reported confidence. These probes test whether the direct commitment is unstable and whether a hidden sample majority exists.

\paragraph{Learned selector.} This probe is not part of the frozen repair battery, so it is not included in Table~\ref{tab:repair-battery-rewrite} or Table~\ref{tab:mdd-repair-rewrite}; its design is summarized here because RQ3 names it as a tested axis, and its verdict appears in \Cref{fig:repairs-thesis-rewrite}. It tests whether a lightweight external model, fitted or selected on development data without modifying the diagnostician or checker bank, can recover a transferable primary-selection signal across three signal families: lexical (a TF--IDF with logistic-regression selector), pipeline-derived (a gradient-boosted ranker, LightGBM), and dense definition-similarity (BGE-M3). Only the lexical family is quantified directly in the main text, in \Cref{tab:lexical-transfer-rewrite}, where the same model family is applied to raw transcript text; the remaining two families, together with three further exploratory probes (evidence extraction, a residual-evidence loop, and post-hoc confidence calibration) that produced no comparable headline number, are reported in full in Appendix~\ref{app:interventions}. Improvement confined to the development lineage would indicate corpus-specific fitting rather than a transferable selection rule.

\paragraph{Robustness axes.} Robustness is evaluated across definition-similarity signals, selector-side case-neighbor reranking, retrieval sensitivity, in-domain and external corpora, confusion boundaries, and ontology scope, each stressing a different explanation for the bottleneck. If embedding representation is the problem, a different similarity signal should improve selection. If similarity to development cases carried the missing selection signal, case-neighbor reranking should transfer to the external corpus. If the finding is corpus-specific, it should not persist on MDD-5k. If scope is the issue, mechanical expansion should recover newly representable labels. The thesis uses robustness to test the stability of the interpretation, not to claim that all perturbations are inconsequential. A result can be robust in the sense that the bottleneck persists while still varying in magnitude across settings.

\paragraph{Backbone size.} If scale alone solves the selection bottleneck, larger backbones should escape it. The size ladder spans 4B, 8B, 14B, and 32B parameters under the same architecture and protocol. The 4B HiED run required elevated parse recovery, and the 4B single run produced abstentions, but all reported size-ladder runs produced 1000 valid outputs and passed health gates; these events are part of system behavior worth reporting, since structured-output fragility is not the same as diagnostic judgment, though it affects deployability and the interpretation of small-backbone results.

\paragraph{Freeze discipline.}
\label{sec:freeze-discipline}
The internal test split is excluded from retrieval indexes, and development and internal-test fingerprints are stored separately. Sweeps use development lineage; any rule that faces the internal test split is committed before scoring, and post-freeze probes are labeled as such. External results are never used for tuning. Quantitative claims are linked to frozen artifacts, expected case counts, and metric definitions. This discipline is what makes the repair battery evidence rather than trial and error, and what makes negative results scientifically useful: a failed repair selected after seeing internal-test outcomes would not test a general mechanism, and a successful one could reflect test-set tuning instead of a real effect. By freezing rules before headline scoring and labeling post-freeze probes explicitly, the internal test split is preserved as confirmatory evidence, and the battery narrows the solution space even when individual interventions fail.

\section{Summary}

The experimental design turns a broad diagnostic-system evaluation into a sequence of causal questions. Each result will be interpreted by which stage it moves: candidate detection, criterion verification, primary selection, set calibration, transfer, or auditability. The next chapter applies this design to the in-domain internal test split.

\section{Candidate Coverage and Recoverable Opportunity}

\ThesisFigure{fig_b_selection_bottleneck.pdf}{Internal test split detection--selection decomposition: committed Top-1, ranked Top-3 coverage, and recoverable close-cluster misclassifications.}{fig:bottleneck-thesis-rewrite}

HiED--DA reaches 0.802 Top-3 while its Top-1 is 0.518. The resulting 28.4-point gap is the threshold-free evidence for the selection bottleneck. This gap measures recoverable opportunity under the benchmark label convention, not clinical recoverability without additional information. The correct parent diagnosis is often present in the ranked output even when it is not selected as primary.

A close-cluster recoverability analysis sharpens this interpretation. This analysis is an oracle-style diagnostic of missed opportunity, not a deployable prediction rule: after the system has already produced its ranked candidates and criterion-derived support scores, the analysis asks whether a misclassified case would have been correct if the gold parent had been selected from a nearby cluster of similarly supported candidates. Under the registered met-ratio gate, using the close-cluster scan over the ranked candidate list rather than only the Top-3 entries, 349 of 1000 cases are recoverable misclassifications: the committed Direct-Answer primary is wrong, but the gold parent is reachable within a nearby supported candidate cluster. This corresponds to a $+34.9$ percentage-point upper-bound lift over the deployed Direct-Answer output. Because the scan spans the full ranked candidate list rather than only the top three entries, this upper bound (0.518+0.349=0.867) can exceed the Top-3 coverage rate of 0.802 reported above; the two quantities are not directly comparable, since they are computed over different candidate windows and answer different questions. The analysis uses gold labels only to measure how often the correct answer was already available within the candidate pool; it is not a deployed method.

The Top-3 gap and recoverability result rule out a simple generation-only explanation for many errors. The system often has already generated the correct parent. The residual problem is deciding that it should be first.

\section{Criterion Verification}
\label{sec:criterion-verification}

The logic-confirmed set contains the gold parent in 93.7\% of non-Others cases, but it confirms a median of six of fourteen active disorder entries per case. Criterion states are distributed 48.3\% \texttt{met}, 23.5\% \texttt{not\_met}, and 28.3\% \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}: \texttt{met} is the largest single state, but \texttt{not\_met} and \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} together still account for over half of all criterion decisions. The checker bank is therefore high recall but low selectivity. In the notation of \Cref{sec:gold-conventions}, this chapter's internal test split gives $\bar D_3=0.802$, $\bar R=0.937$ (computed, as above, over the non-Others cases for which a checker entry exists), and $\bar S=0.518$: both candidate detection and verification retention substantially exceed committed selection, the operational form of the selection bottleneck introduced there.

\ThesisFigure{fig_c_criterion_states.pdf}{Distribution of per-criterion checker states on the internal test split.}{fig:criterion-states-thesis-rewrite}

This result explains why verification does not automatically solve primary selection. Retaining the gold diagnosis is useful, but retaining many competitors leaves the final comparison unresolved. Verification establishes compatibility with the transcript; primacy requires choosing among compatible diagnoses.

Nominate-then-Select makes this causal. NtS re-derives the primary from the confirmed set using the same upstream diagnostician outputs and checker traces as DA. It lowers Top-1 to 0.490 but raises Exact Match from 0.409 to 0.437 ($p=0.008$) by emitting fewer labels. Verification therefore improves diagnostic-set control while degrading primary commitment. This is a trade-off, not a selection improvement.

A checker-granularity by verification-scope factorial (Appendix~\ref{app:inventory}) confirms this is not merely close but exact: Top-1, Top-3, and macro-F1 are identical to three decimal places across all tested checker configurations, the expected consequence of checker and logic outputs sitting downstream of the Direct-Answer commitment rather than an empirical coincidence.

The largest first-listed-gold confusion is F41 to F32, followed by F39 to F32 and Others to F32. Errors concentrate around depression, anxiety, and mood boundaries.

\section{Scope Coverage and Mechanical Expansion}
\label{sec:scope-coverage}

Forty-seven MDD cases fall outside the selected fourteen-disorder active scope. These are coverage limitations rather than recoverable selection errors. Under the selected profile, the system cannot emit the gold parent for these cases regardless of how the selector is changed.

This distinction matters because external evaluation can otherwise mix two different failures. A within-scope error means the system could have emitted the gold diagnosis but did not. An out-of-scope case means the diagnosis was not available in the active output space. The coverage-aware reporting separates configuration limits from selection limits so that the in-scope Top-1 result measures behavior within the system's declared scope.

The 47 out-of-scope cases are not concentrated in one alternative disorder; their raw (pre-projection) gold parent codes span fifteen distinct ICD-10 categories: F50 (7), G47 (5), F23, F30, F34, F93 (4 each), F28, F48, F90 (3 each), F22, F33, F38, F40 (2 each), and F01, F06 (1 each). No single excluded category would have closed a large fraction of the scope gap by itself.

This same representability-versus-recovery distinction can be tested directly by deliberately expanding scope rather than merely accounting for it. The active profile can be expanded to thirty parent codes across thirty-nine ontology entries without retraining; the expanded set covers all fifteen raw parent categories listed above. This confirms the architectural claim that representable scope is configured through the ontology and active profile rather than by changing model weights.

However, mechanical expansion does not produce behavioral adaptation. In the expanded external setting, newly representable cases are not recovered as primaries: across the 58 external cases whose raw gold parent lies in a newly activated code---a set that includes all 47 out-of-scope cases above---the retargeted run never commits the newly activated parent as primary (0/58), emits a newly activated code as primary exactly once anywhere in the run, and leaves paired in-scope controls statistically unchanged (13/20 versus 12/20; exact McNemar $p=1.0$). This result separates two claims that are often conflated: a system can represent a diagnosis, but that does not mean it can select it correctly. The engineering precondition for broader scope is satisfied; the behavioral evidence for broader recovery is not.

This finding reinforces the thesis's main decomposition. Adding labels to the output space affects representability, but selection still determines whether those labels are committed. A broader ontology without a better selector can enlarge the candidate space while leaving the primary-selection bottleneck intact.

\section{Selector-Side Case-Neighbor Reranking}

This section evaluates a selector-side case-neighbor reranking probe, not an ablation of the canonical class-balanced case retriever used to construct the diagnostician prompt. The probe asks whether similarity to training cases can serve as a transferable primary-selection signal under external synthetic distribution shift. Concretely, each query transcript retrieves its nearest-neighbor cases from the in-domain development corpus in embedding space; the retrieved neighbors' gold labels, not the diagnostician's own candidates, are then aggregated by vote to rerank the diagnostician's existing candidate list. Unlike the canonical class-balanced retrieval in \Cref{sec:retrieval}, which retrieves one case per candidate disorder class \emph{before} the diagnostician generates its output and supplies those cases as prompt context to broaden candidate coverage, this probe retrieves unbalanced nearest neighbors \emph{after} candidates already exist and uses only the neighbors' labels---not their content---to vote on which existing candidate to select. Dense, sparse, ColBERT, and hybrid similarity modes---differing in how the embedding space used for neighbor retrieval is constructed---are selected on the in-domain development split and then frozen before application to the in-scope MDD subset, without using external labels for tuning.

\section{Interpretation}

The external evidence supports four conclusions. First, useful synthetic transfer exists: the system performs above the majority-class baseline on every external subset (by roughly fourteen points even on the depression-skewed binary subset) and transfers coarse F32/F41 discrimination. Second, scope accounting is essential because out-of-scope cases are not recoverable selector errors. Third, ontology expansion provides representability but not behavioral adaptation. Fourth, selection repairs that fail in domain also fail, remain inert, or degrade externally.

The external chapter therefore strengthens the bounded claim of the thesis. HiED is not clinically validated by MDD-5k, but the selection-bottleneck pattern is not merely an artifact of the in-domain split. The next chapter translates this aggregate pattern into concrete error archetypes.

\section{Depression--Anxiety Boundary}

A typical boundary case contains insomnia, fatigue, worry, somatic tension, low motivation, and impaired functioning. F32 and F41 can both be plausible, both may appear in the ranked list, and both may satisfy enough criteria to remain logic-confirmed. The final error is not failure to notice anxiety or depression; it is failure to decide which syndrome is primary under incomplete temporal and exclusion evidence.

This archetype is the qualitative face of the largest confusion structure. Depression and anxiety share many surface symptoms, and the differentiators often depend on onset, persistence, worry generalization, mood primacy, and whether symptoms are better explained by another disorder. When the transcript does not establish those facts, a selector may commit the wrong primary even though candidate detection and verification are working as designed.

The implication is that better symptom extraction alone is unlikely to solve the boundary. The missing object is comparative structure: evidence that ranks supported disorders against one another rather than evaluating each disorder against the transcript in isolation.

\section{Missing Exclusion Information}

Some transcripts contain symptoms compatible with obsessive-compulsive, somatic, sleep, or stress-related disorders while omitting substance use, medical causes, medication effects, prior episodes, or longitudinal course. A strict verifier that treats missing exclusions as failures will over-exclude. A permissive verifier that treats them as compatible will retain too many alternatives. Neither setting creates information that was never elicited.

This archetype explains why checker thresholds are not enough. The checker bank can record insufficient evidence, and deterministic logic can summarize compatibility, but the transcript may not contain the fact needed to resolve the diagnosis. When the limiting factor is missing evidence, the natural remedy is not a different global threshold; it is evidence acquisition through follow-up questioning or richer clinical data.

\section{Correct Candidate, Wrong Commitment}

The canonical selection-bottleneck case occurs when the gold parent appears in Top-3 and survives verification, but another candidate is committed as primary. In such a case, the system has not failed to generate the correct diagnosis. It has failed to rank it first.

This archetype directly connects to the Top-3/Top-1 gap and the high gold-in-confirmed rate. It also explains why pairwise reasoning can look promising locally but fail globally. A local comparison among two close candidates may resolve some cases, but applying the same override everywhere can harm cases where the diagnostician's original commitment was already correct or where the pairwise prompt receives insufficient evidence.

The implication is that future selectors should be conditional. Heavy comparative reasoning should be routed to cases where coverage is high, confidence is low, and the competing candidates are close, rather than applied uniformly.

\section{Conservative Set Control}

Nominate-then-Select emits fewer labels and improves Exact Match while lowering Top-1. This is not merely a failure; it is a different operating point. NtS makes the diagnostic set more conservative but does not choose the primary better.

This archetype shows why Top-1 and Exact Match must be interpreted together. A system can improve set calibration while harming the committed primary. Conversely, a system can choose the primary more often while emitting too many comorbid or secondary labels. In clinical decision support, both properties matter, but they are not the same property.

The implication is that finalization policy should be selected according to intended use. If the goal is a conservative list for clinician review, NtS-like behavior may be useful. If the goal is benchmark primary accuracy, it is not an improvement.

\section{Out-of-Scope Diagnosis}

When the gold code lies outside the active profile, the system cannot emit it. This is a scope failure, not a selection error. External evaluation must therefore separate out-of-scope cases from within-scope cases.

Mechanical ontology expansion makes additional codes representable, but the expanded-scope experiment shows that representability does not imply recovery. The selector must still choose those labels as primary, and the existing diagnostician may not be behaviorally adapted to the enlarged space.

This archetype prevents two opposite misreadings. The selected-scope system should not be punished as though it could emit labels outside its profile. But an expanded ontology should not be celebrated as adaptation unless newly representable diagnoses are actually recovered.

\section{Rare-Class Failure}

Overall accuracy can remain moderate while rare-class recall is poor. Weighted-F1 is dominated by frequent labels, whereas macro-F1 keeps rare labels visible. This is why the thesis reports both metrics and does not rely on a single aggregate score.

Rare-class failure is especially important in psychiatric decision support because infrequent diagnoses may be exactly where assistance is most needed. A system that performs well on common depression-like presentations but fails rare categories can appear adequate in aggregate while providing limited clinical value.

The current evidence does not show that larger models, retrieval, or graph-based selectors necessarily solve this problem. It shows that rare-class behavior must be measured explicitly and that future structured selectors should be evaluated under macro-averaged and class-specific views.

\section{Audit Checklist}

For each error, the audit trace should be reviewed in a fixed order:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item Is the gold diagnosis inside the active scope?
\item Does the gold parent appear in the candidate list?
\item Does the gold parent survive criterion verification?
\item How many competing diagnoses are also confirmed?
\item Which temporal, course, exclusion, or substance-use facts are missing?
\item Which finalization policy produced the committed primary?
\item Does the same pattern appear under external distribution shift?
\end{enumerate}

This checklist turns the audit trace into a research instrument. It localizes each failure rather than attributing all errors to the system as a whole. The chapter's archetypes show that many errors are not generation failures; they are compatibility, scope, or comparative-selection failures that require different remedies.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Main Finding}

The main lesson of HiED is not a claim of autonomous psychiatric diagnosis (Chapter~\ref{ch:limitations} details that boundary); it is that transcript-only psychiatric diagnosis can be decomposed into stages whose failures differ. HiED detects and verifies plausible diagnoses more often than it selects the correct primary, and this mismatch persists across repair probes and external synthetic shift.

This reframes the system's contribution. The architecture is not justified primarily by a leaderboard-style Top-1 improvement. It is justified because it turns a single final accuracy number into a stage-wise account of behavior. Retrieval improves candidate support. Verification retains the correct diagnosis at high rates but also retains many alternatives. Final selection remains the dominant measured bottleneck. The contribution is therefore failure localization under a reproducible protocol.

\section{What Decomposition Achieves}

A flat classifier can only be right or wrong. A decomposed system can be wrong in diagnosable ways. If the gold parent is absent from the candidate list, the failure is candidate detection. If it appears but fails criterion verification, the failure is verification or evidence interpretation. If it appears and survives verification but is not committed, the failure is primary selection. This distinction changes the implied remedy.

The internal results show that candidate coverage and verification retention exceed committed Top-1. The external results preserve the same pattern under distribution shift. This means the system's strength and weakness coexist: it can often keep the right diagnosis in view, but it cannot reliably choose it as primary. The decomposition makes that coexistence visible.

\section{Why Verification Does Not Select}

Verification fails to select because compatibility is not primacy. A criterion checker asks whether a disorder is supported by the transcript. Primary selection asks which supported disorder best explains the case. These are different comparisons. The first is one diagnosis against the evidence; the second is one diagnosis against other plausible diagnoses.

The distinction is especially important in psychiatric transcripts. Several disorders share sleep disturbance, fatigue, concentration difficulty, somatic tension, avoidance, and functional impairment. The facts that resolve primacy often involve duration, onset, exclusion, recurrence, and longitudinal course. When those facts are missing, a checker can correctly retain multiple disorders without being able to rank them.

NtS demonstrates this point causally. Re-selecting from the verified set improves diagnostic-set control but lowers Top-1. Verification therefore contributes auditability and set calibration, not primary selection. This does not make verification useless; it defines what verification is good for.

A complementary case-level autopsy asks a narrower question: in the specific cases where the logic engine excludes the gold parent from the confirmed set, what does the gold disorder's own checker record show? On the internal test split, when the analysis is restricted to cases whose gold diagnosis has a corresponding checker-bank entry, insufficient evidence accounts for approximately 46--50\% of the gold disorder's own criterion states, compared with roughly 28--30\% not-met and 23--24\% met; the exact share depends on how multi-label gold cases are counted, and this range reflects that sensitivity rather than a single precisely reproduced figure. On the external MDD-5k corpus, the same autopsy is better determined and converges tightly across independent computations at approximately 62\% insufficient evidence, 22\% met, and 15\% not-met. In both corpora, not-met is smaller than insufficient evidence: when verification wrongly excludes the correct diagnosis, the checker is rarely contradicting it outright. More often, the transcript simply does not contain the fact the checker needed to confirm it. This is consistent with the missing-exclusion-information archetype (Chapter~\ref{ch:error}) and sharpens its implication: the remedy for these specific failures is unlikely to be a stricter or looser confirmation threshold, since the limiting factor is absent evidence rather than contradicted evidence. It points instead toward evidence acquisition--asking the transcript for the missing fact--as a more direct response than re-tuning the verification gate.

\section{Why Simple Repairs Fail}

The repair battery constrains the solution space. Pairwise comparison does not provide a safe global override. Debate does not add useful comparative evidence on average and can degrade external performance. Deterministic fusion does not uncover a missing rule over existing signals. Self-consistency does not reveal a hidden majority consensus. Confidence-weighted aggregation does not correct the commitment.

These failures are mechanistically diverse, which makes their convergence informative. If the bottleneck were correctable by a simple post-hoc signal, a local pairwise override, unstable sampling, or additional reasoning rounds, at least one of the evaluated repairs should produce a stable causal gain under the frozen protocol. The absence of such a gain across mechanistically distinct probes supports the interpretation that the residual problem is comparative selection under incomplete evidence.

This conclusion is bounded. The thesis does not show that logic, pairwise reasoning, debate, fusion, or learned selection can never help. It shows that the tested implementations do not close this gap. The result motivates more structured selectors and more selective routing of difficult cases rather than unconditional escalation.

\section{Method Limitation versus Modality Limitation}

Some failures are method-limited: the transcript contains subtle comparative evidence, but the current selector does not use it. Other failures are modality-limited: the transcript simply lacks the facts needed to distinguish primary from secondary diagnoses. Benchmark performance alone cannot separate these possibilities.

The structural shape of this result is not unique to psychiatry. A rare-disease gene-prioritization pipeline shows the same division: a high-recall retrieval stage keeps the causal gene available, but the effective gain comes from improving the ranking stage rather than from retrieval alone~\citep{lee2025lamarrvel}. That two independent clinical domains locate the bottleneck at ranking rather than retrieval suggests this pattern is not an artifact specific to the psychiatric setting, though two domains cannot by themselves establish how general a property it is.

Psychiatric reference judgments are themselves imperfect: DSM-5 field-trial reliability varies across diagnostic categories~\citep{regier2013dsm5}. A transcript-only human ceiling is therefore a central future measurement. If blinded clinicians reading the same transcripts also struggle to identify the benchmark primary, the ceiling may reflect the modality. If clinicians can reliably resolve cases that HiED misses, the bottleneck is more likely methodological. This distinction is necessary before interpreting the remaining gap as a model failure, a data limitation, or an inherent ambiguity of the diagnostic labels.

The planned clinical arm and the transcript-only ceiling answer different questions. Treating clinicians observe more than the transcript and can ask follow-up questions, so disagreement with them cannot be attributed solely to model error. Blinded transcript-only experts hold the input fixed and directly test what can be inferred from the same evidence.

\section{External Robustness and Transfer}

The external MDD-5k evaluation shows useful but distribution-dependent transfer. Coarse F32/F41 discrimination transfers strongly, and in-scope external Top-1 exceeds the majority baseline. At the same time, full multi-way selection remains difficult, lexical selectors fail across corpora, and case-similarity reranking degrades external performance.

This pattern distinguishes portable structure from corpus-specific shortcuts. The ontology, checker logic, and transcript pipeline can run on an external corpus, but fitted lexical or similarity signals may encode source-corpus regularities. The external results therefore support the value of structured evaluation while warning against overinterpreting in-domain reranking gains.

\section{Auditability versus Trust}

Auditability is necessary for review but insufficient for trust. A criterion trace allows a reviewer to inspect evidence, criterion states, and deterministic logic outputs. It does not prove clinical correctness, calibration, fairness, or safety. Trustworthy clinical use requires validated data, human-factor evidence, monitoring, governance, and demonstrated safety under the intended workflow.

This distinction is not a disclaimer added after the fact; it follows from the results, and Chapter~\ref{ch:limitations} states the resulting boundary in full. HiED's appropriate role is to organize a differential, expose evidence, and flag missing information for clinician review.

\section{Taiwanese Clinical Informatics}

The Taiwanese context motivates the deployment objective but is not established by the current evidence. The experiments use synthetic Chinese corpora rather than real Taiwanese patient data. The title and system design reflect intended use in a Taiwanese clinical setting: Traditional-Chinese interaction, local governance, offline inference, and clinician-facing audit. They do not imply completed Taiwanese validation.

This boundary must remain visible because cultural and institutional factors can affect symptom expression, somatization, language choice, and diagnostic practice. Future Taiwanese evaluation should therefore be stratified by language variety, age, socioeconomic context, institution, and presentation style. The current thesis supplies the architecture and failure analysis needed to design that evaluation, not the final clinical answer.

\section{Synthesis}

The thesis supports five measured statements. Candidate coverage exceeds committed primary accuracy. Criterion verification retains the gold parent but also retains many competitors. Tested repairs do not provide a stable causal gain. External synthetic transfer is useful but scope-sensitive. Mechanical ontology expansion is not behavioral adaptation.

The strongest interpretation is therefore modest but sharp: under transcript-only evidence, psychiatric LLM diagnosis is not limited only by generating plausible candidates. The correct diagnosis is often detected and criterion-supported, while the remaining failure is comparative primary selection. Future systems must either supply better comparative structure or acquire the missing evidence that current transcripts omit.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

\section{Overview}

The limitations define the evidentiary boundary of the thesis. The experiments support a claim about decomposed failure localization on synthetic transcript corpora; they do not establish Taiwanese clinical effectiveness, clinician-level performance, or autonomous diagnostic safety. Stating these boundaries is necessary because the system's domain is clinical and the temptation to overread benchmark performance is high.

The chapter therefore treats limitations as part of the argument rather than as an afterthought. Each limitation specifies what the evidence can and cannot support. The central contribution remains valid within these boundaries: HiED provides an auditable decomposition showing that detection, verification, and selection can diverge. The boundaries prevent that contribution from being misread as clinical validation.

\section{Data and Construct Limitations}

Both LingxiDiag-16K and MDD-5k are synthetic corpora. Synthetic dialogues are useful for controlled development and ablation, but they may encode generator priors, simplify comorbidity, omit nonverbal signals, and fail to reproduce the longitudinal and institutional context of real consultations. Every quantitative result in the thesis is therefore a result on synthetic transcript data, not a direct estimate of real-world clinical performance. Both corpora are, moreover, written in Simplified Chinese, whereas the intended deployment setting is Traditional Chinese with Mandarin--Taiwanese register mixing; script and register shift is therefore untested in this evidence base, and is one reason the planned clinical arm specifies Traditional-Chinese prompting (Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}).

Public release of the corpora before the experiments means pretraining exposure cannot be excluded. If the underlying model has seen related examples during pretraining, apparent performance could be inflated. The thesis mitigates this by focusing on within-system decomposition, paired comparisons, and external synthetic shift rather than claiming absolute clinical accuracy. It cannot fully eliminate the possibility of pretraining overlap.

The official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable at freeze, so the headline in-domain results use a pre-registered internal carve. The split is excluded from retrieval and selection procedures, but it remains an internal split rather than a community-standard public test set. This qualifies leaderboard-style comparison and is why public-validation positioning is not treated as the thesis headline.

These data limitations carry directly into what the metrics can be read as measuring. Top-1 measures whether the committed primary parent appears in the gold set. Top-3 measures whether a gold parent appears in the ranked shortlist. Exact Match measures set equality. None of these metrics alone equals clinical correctness.

Top-3 is especially easy to misread. It is a coverage measure, not a safe-deployment measure. A correct diagnosis in the top three may help a clinician review the differential, but it does not mean the system selected the correct primary. Conversely, a low Top-1 does not prove that candidate generation failed. The thesis uses the gap between the two as evidence of recoverable opportunity, not as evidence that the deployed output is correct.

Gold labels themselves may reflect uncertainty. Multilabel cases can be scored under any-gold, first-listed, or exact-set conventions, and each convention answers a different question. The thesis labels these views explicitly, but no convention removes the clinical ambiguity inherent in psychiatric diagnosis.

\section{Model, Method, and Statistical Limitations}

The selected system uses a quantized Qwen3-32B-AWQ model under one serving stack, decoding configuration, prompt family, and hardware regime. Behavior under other models, model versions, serving frameworks, or prompting strategies is not fully characterized. Size-ladder results test some robustness, but they do not prove that capacity or model family is irrelevant.

Checker states are not clinician-validated criterion gold. They are model-generated audit outputs. A reproducible checker trace can still be clinically wrong if the checker misreads a symptom, misses a negation, or over-infers from weak evidence. Deterministic logic faithfully summarizes checker states but also inherits checker errors. The auditability claim is therefore about inspectability and reproducibility, not criterion-level truth.

Several repair probes originated as bounded research implementations rather than production components. Their negative results are useful because they test specified mechanisms under a frozen protocol, but they should not be read as exhaustive rejection of entire method families. A different pairwise policy, debate protocol, selector architecture, or evidence representation might behave differently and would require fresh evaluation.

These method-level caveats are compounded by statistical ones. Several analyses are exploratory or post-freeze, and rare-class estimates and small strata can have high variance; this is why the thesis emphasizes convergent patterns across probes rather than isolated marginal differences.

The negative repair results should therefore be interpreted as bounded eliminations. They rule out specific simple explanations under the tested conditions; they do not prove that no future method can improve selection. The strongest evidence comes from convergence: verification, fusion, debate, pairwise override, and sampling aggregation all fail to provide stable causal gains.

\section{Scope and External Validity}

Out-of-scope labels cannot be recovered by selection. If the active profile excludes a diagnosis, no finalization rule can emit it. This is why external evaluation reports coverage-aware views. Within-scope errors and out-of-scope coverage failures require different remedies.

Mechanical ontology expansion does not adapt the diagnostician. Adding codes to the active profile makes them representable, but the selector must still choose them. The external expansion result shows that representation alone does not produce recovery. Broader scope therefore requires behavioral validation and likely selection adaptation.

The transcript-only design omits nonverbal behavior, medical history, medication records, structured rating scales, collateral reports, and longitudinal course. Many psychiatric distinctions depend precisely on these missing sources. The thesis studies the transcript-only regime because it is experimentally controlled and clinically relevant, but that regime is also a hard boundary on what any method can infer.

This scope boundary extends to the external corpus itself. MDD-5k is external to LingxiDiag but remains synthetic and depression-skewed. It provides distribution-shift evidence, not real-patient validation. Strong performance on an external synthetic subset does not establish Taiwanese clinical effectiveness. No real-patient results are included; the planned Chang Gung arm is prospective and outside the current evidence base.

\section{Reproducibility and Remaining Boundary}

Reproduction requires matching the computational and semantic lineage fixed in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}---model, software, hardware, split, policy, prompt family, label projection, and metric definition. A number reproduced under a different convention is not the same result. The thesis mitigates this through run-health gates, split discipline, artifact lineage, and explicit labeling of canonical paths versus probes; these safeguards improve credibility but do not remove the practical dependence on a specific computational stack.

The main mitigations across this chapter are pre-registration of the internal split, retrieval exclusion, paired tests, full metric reporting, external synthetic evaluation, health gates, negative-result disclosure, controlled replay, and explicit claim boundaries. These practices make the failure-localization claim credible within the data setting.

They do not turn benchmark evidence into clinical validation. The final boundary is therefore clear: HiED demonstrates an auditable decomposition and a persistent selection bottleneck on synthetic transcript corpora. It remains future work to establish clinician-level performance, real-patient effectiveness, calibrated abstention, fairness, and safety under clinical workflow.


\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Overview of Findings}

This thesis introduced HiED to study a specific failure mode in initial-consultation, transcript-only psychiatric LLM diagnosis. By separating candidate detection, criterion verification, and committed primary selection, the system shows that the correct diagnosis can be present and criterion-supported while still not being selected as the primary.

HiED combines a local LLM diagnostician, class-balanced retrieval, ICD-10 criterion checkers, and deterministic threshold logic. It emits a committed primary, optional comorbidity, ranked differential, criterion states, evidence, logic results, and decision trace. The architecture is therefore both a decision-support prototype and an experimental instrument for localizing failure.

The main empirical result is a stage-wise mismatch. Candidate coverage and verification retention substantially exceed primary-selection accuracy. Retrieval improves the direct baseline, but the residual Top-3/Top-1 gap remains. Verification retains the gold parent at high rates, but it also retains many competitors. A broad repair battery does not yield a stable causal gain over Direct-Answer. External synthetic evaluation preserves the same pattern under distribution shift.

\section{Answers to Research Questions}

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] HiED can produce a committed primary, optional comorbidity, ranked differential, and inspectable criterion-level audit trace. The trace is an output-side audit, not proof of faithful model reasoning.

\item[RQ2] Candidate coverage and verification retention substantially exceed realized primary accuracy. This establishes the selection bottleneck: the correct diagnosis can be present and criterion-supported but not selected.

\item[RQ3] No tested repair provides a stable, causally attributable improvement over Direct-Answer on the internal test split. Logic re-selection, pairwise comparison, debate, deterministic fusion, learned selection, calibration, self-consistency, and confidence-weighted aggregation each fail to close the gap under the tested conditions.

\item[RQ4] The pattern persists across model sizes, retrieval sensitivity and selector-side similarity probes, external synthetic distribution shift, scope analysis, and multiple repair families. The external evidence supports useful transfer for some distinctions---most concretely, the F32/F41 depression--anxiety confusion boundary transfers well as a binary discrimination (\Cref{ch:external})---but does not eliminate the primary-selection bottleneck.

\item[RQ5] The evidence supports an auditable research architecture and a failure-localization claim; Chapter~\ref{ch:limitations} details what this does and does not establish.
\end{description}

\section{Future Priorities}

The next research step is not simply a larger model or another round of debate. The thesis motivates six specific priorities.

\subsection{Transcript-Only Human Ceiling}

A blinded transcript-only human reference is the highest-priority evaluation. It is needed to separate method limitations from modality limitations. If psychiatrists reading the same transcript cannot reliably choose the benchmark primary, the ceiling may reflect missing evidence or label ambiguity. If psychiatrists can resolve cases that HiED misses, the bottleneck is more likely methodological.

This study should use matched inputs, blinded review, and explicit disagreement analysis. Treating-clinician comparison remains useful but answers a different question because treating clinicians observe more information than the transcript alone.

\subsection{Prospective Clinical Validation}

The planned clinical arm compares HiED's committed primary with the attending psychiatrist's recorded principal diagnosis on de-identified Chang Gung Memorial Hospital outpatient transcripts, under an IRB amendment and a pre-registered analysis plan frozen ahead of any data access. The system configuration is frozen at the same methods tag used for the benchmark results; the only adaptation between the benchmark and clinical configurations is rendering prompts in Traditional Chinese rather than Simplified Chinese, matching the clinical corpus.

Disagreement cases are adjudicated by the clinical PI into three buckets, designed to mirror this thesis's own decomposition rather than treat every disagreement as a system failure: the transcript did not carry sufficient signal for the principal to be derivable (a modality limit, not counted against the system); the transcript did carry sufficient signal and the system missed it (a system-attributed miss); or the disagreement reflects a scope or code-mapping mismatch, such as a principal diagnosis outside the deployed active profile (a deployment-scope limit, not counted against the system). Only the middle category is reported as system-attributable error, so the clinical result cannot be inflated by cases the system was never positioned to resolve. This adjudication is a direct clinical analogue of the detection--verification--selection decomposition used throughout the benchmark chapters: it separates whether the correct information was available, whether the system used it, and whether the case was in scope.

A secondary usability endpoint has the clinical PI rate a random sample of audit reports for faithfulness, clarity, and utility on a descriptive Likert scale, following structured reporting practices for early-stage clinical AI evaluation. This targets the auditability-supports-review claim directly: that the audit trace helps clinician review, not that it proves diagnostic correctness.

This treating-clinician comparison is complementary to, not a substitute for, the transcript-only human ceiling above: treating clinicians observe more than the transcript, so their agreement or disagreement with HiED cannot alone isolate whether the remaining gap is methodological or reflects missing information in the transcript itself. The two studies together are needed to answer that question.

The clinical arm is explicitly not a clinical trial. It is a transcript-only secondary-use study; no patient-care decisions are made on the basis of HiED's output, and the frozen configuration is not tuned on any clinical-arm data. Results from this arm remain outside the evidence base of the present thesis and will be reported once the IRB-approved data collection and adjudication are complete.

\subsection{Structured Comparative Selection}

Future selectors should operate over structured relations among symptoms, criteria, exclusions, temporal facts, and disorders. Scalar met-ratio thresholds are high recall but low selectivity. A graph or structured reranker could represent which evidence distinguishes one supported disorder from another rather than only whether each disorder is compatible with the transcript.

Such work requires fresh pre-registration and an untouched confirmatory split. The current thesis motivates structured selection but does not demonstrate it. Future systems should be evaluated by whether they convert existing Top-3 coverage into higher committed Top-1 without harming rare-class behavior or set calibration.

\subsection{Evidence Acquisition}

Several observed errors trace to missing duration, exclusion, course, medication, or substance-use information. When the transcript lacks the discriminating fact, a better selector may not be enough. An inquiry-capable system should ask the next clinically discriminative question instead of forcing a primary commitment from incomplete evidence. Conversational diagnostic systems such as AMIE demonstrate that an LLM can conduct the history-taking dialogue itself~\citep{tu2025conversational}; the complementary question raised here is which single question most reduces ambiguity among criterion-supported candidates.

Evidence acquisition is complementary to selection. Structured selection improves how the system decides when evidence is fixed; inquiry improves what evidence is available. The decomposition in this thesis helps decide when each response is appropriate.

\subsection{Audit Reliability and Abstention}

The audit trace must itself be validated. Future work should compare checker states with clinician-rated criterion judgments, evaluate evidence-span correctness, and calibrate the relationship among confidence, insufficient-evidence burden, ambiguity, and error risk. A concrete first step is a bounded reliability study: sample 50--100 criterion states stratified across disorders and evidence-state categories (\texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}), have a psychiatrist or trained rater independently judge whether each cited evidence span supports the assigned state, and report percent agreement or Cohen's $\kappa$ together with a breakdown of disagreement types (for example, span misidentification versus criterion misapplication). This study is deliberately small relative to the benchmark evaluation; its purpose is to establish whether the audit trace is reliable enough to support a clinician-facing review workflow, not to re-validate the diagnostic labels themselves. A clinical abstention policy should distinguish low confidence, missing evidence, out-of-scope diagnosis, and runtime failure.

\subsection{Clinical Workflow and Governance}

A prospective clinical study should evaluate not only agreement with clinicians but also how clinicians use the audit. Important outcomes include review time, comprehension, disagreement resolution, detection of incorrect evidence, automation bias, and whether the system changes follow-up questioning. Privacy, logging, monitoring, fairness, and revalidation requirements must be evaluated as part of the workflow rather than assumed from benchmark performance.

\section{Final Conclusion}

HiED's contribution is an auditable architecture and a reproducible demonstration that psychiatric LLM diagnosis can fail after the correct diagnosis has already been detected and criterion-supported. Under the tested transcript-only setting, the dominant measured bottleneck is comparative primary selection. The work therefore identifies what future systems must improve and what future evaluations must measure, while stopping short of claiming clinically validated autonomous diagnosis (Chapter~\ref{ch:limitations}).

\section{Paired Testing}

Exact McNemar tests compare paired binary outcomes. Paired bootstrap resamples case indices jointly. Non-significance is not equivalence. Where multiple related comparisons are drawn from the same family (for example, the retrieval-mode sweep in \Cref{tab:case-knn-rewrite} or the calibration metrics in Appendix~\ref{app:interventions}), a Holm--Bonferroni correction is applied within that family; single headline comparisons such as the architecture ladder are not corrected because each tests a distinct, pre-specified hypothesis rather than one of a multiple-testing family.

\chapter{Prompt Families and Output Schemas}
\label{app:prompts}

This appendix documents the scientific structure of the prompts and output schemas used by each language-model component of HiED. It describes the fields each agent is required to emit and the design of each prompt family at the level needed to understand and reproduce the method, rather than reproducing the verbatim templates. The full frozen prompt templates and their hashes are preserved in the project repository; this appendix deliberately reports prompt-family structure rather than implementation artifacts.

\section{Diagnostic Agent Output Schema}

The diagnostician is prompted to return a single structured object with the following fields:
\begin{itemize}
\item a ranked differential---an ordered list of candidate parent diagnoses, most plausible first;
\item a committed primary diagnosis, drawn from the ranked differential;
\item an optional comorbid diagnosis where the presentation supports more than one independent disorder;
\item a confidence value for the committed primary;
\item a brief free-text rationale; and
\item optional uncertainty or abstention flags when the transcript does not support a confident commitment.
\end{itemize}
The committed primary is emitted by the diagnostician and is constrained to the ranked differential; it is not mechanically selected by the criterion checker or the logic engine. This is the schema-level counterpart of the architectural claim made in the main text: under the selected Direct-Answer policy the final label is the diagnostician's own output, and the criterion trace is an output-side audit rather than the mechanism that produced the primary.

\section{Criterion Checker Output Schema}

For each criterion of each candidate disorder, the checker returns:
\begin{itemize}
\item a \texttt{criterion\_id} identifying the criterion under evaluation;
\item a \texttt{status} drawn from \{\texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, \texttt{insufficient\_evidence}\};
\item a confidence value;
\item an evidence quote drawn from the transcript, or an empty field when none is available; and
\item a short justification for the assigned status.
\end{itemize}
The checker output is an audit state, not clinician-validated criterion gold. A \texttt{met} verdict records that the transcript, as read by the checker, is compatible with the criterion under the operational definition used; it does not certify clinical correctness, and the \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} state is retained precisely so that absence of evidence is not silently recorded as evidence of absence.

\section{Finalization Policies}

Two finalization policies operate over identical upstream traces:
\begin{itemize}
\item Direct-Answer (DA) preserves the diagnostician's committed primary and comorbidity call as the final label; and
\item Nominate-then-Select (NtS) sets aside the diagnostician's emitted primary and comorbidity call and applies a deterministic finalization rule to the same upstream ranked differential, checker states, and logic-confirmed set used by DA. It first scans the diagnostician's top-ranked candidates in order and selects the highest-ranked candidate that is logic-confirmed. If none of those top-ranked candidates is confirmed but the logic-confirmed set is non-empty, it instead selects from the full confirmed set the candidate with the highest met ratio. If the logic-confirmed set is empty, it falls back to the diagnostician's original top-ranked candidate rather than abstaining. NtS never emits a secondary diagnosis.
\end{itemize}
Because DA and NtS are applied to the same diagnostician candidates and the same criterion and logic traces, any difference between them is attributable to the finalization step alone rather than to upstream variation. This shared-upstream construction is what allows the main text to isolate selection as the locus of the bottleneck.

\section{Pairwise and Debate Prompt Skeletons}

Two comparison prompts are evaluated as repair probes rather than as deployed policies.
\begin{itemize}
\item The pairwise prompt compares two candidate diagnoses that fall inside a close cluster under a pre-specified score rule, and returns a preferred candidate with a justification grounded in the transcript.
\item The debate prompt stages a two-advocate, multi-round adversarial comparison in which each candidate is argued for in turn before a final adjudication.
\end{itemize}
Both are repair probes used to test whether localized comparison can improve primary selection; neither is the finalization policy used for the selected reference system.

\section{Self-Consistency and Confidence-Weighted Aggregation}

The self-consistency probe samples the diagnostician multiple times under the same prompt and aggregates the committed primaries:
\begin{itemize}
\item self-consistency takes a majority vote over the sampled primaries; and
\item the confidence-weighted variant (CISC) weights each sampled primary by the diagnostician's verbalized confidence before aggregation.
\end{itemize}
Neither variant modifies the criterion checker traces, and both are reported as selection-repair probes; as reported in the results, neither improved primary selection over the single-sample baseline.

\section{Reproducibility Note}

Full prompt templates and frozen prompt hashes are preserved in the project repository. This appendix reports the scientific structure of each prompt family rather than repository paths or implementation filenames.

\chapter{Detailed Intervention Implementations}
\label{app:interventions}

\section{Evidence Reasoning}

The evidence-reasoning intervention is a four-stage pipeline whose stage order is extract $\rightarrow$ somatize $\rightarrow$ match $\rightarrow$ assemble. \emph{Extraction} prompts the language model to pull symptom spans from transcript turns, with the transcript clipped to a per-language character budget that preserves turn boundaries and with separate Chinese and English extraction prompts. \emph{Somatization mapping} routes extracted spans to somatic criterion groups, with a dedicated fallback prompt for Chinese somatic presentations. \emph{Criterion matching} scores each span against ICD-10 criterion text by token overlap (Jaccard similarity) rather than by dense embedding, after NFKC text normalization; a syntactic, scope-aware Chinese negation detector that handles positive-negation idioms (for example, 睡不着, 吃不下) and clause boundaries flags genuine contradictions, and when a criterion is negated a fixed contradiction penalty of $0.20$ is subtracted from the fused score. \emph{Assembly} compiles the surviving evidence into a per-candidate evidence brief at a minimum confidence of $0.1$ and caches it. The intervention improved inspectability but introduced extraction and negation noise and did not yield a stable selection gain; the main text attributes this to all evidence deriving from a single transcript modality.

\section{Calibration}

The calibration intervention fits post-hoc Platt scaling (a one-dimensional logistic map)~\citep{guo2017calibration} to the diagnostician's verbalized confidence under five-fold cross-validation, projecting predictions to the paper's parent classes, and reports Expected Calibration Error (ten-bin) and the Area Under the Risk--Coverage curve~\citep{geifman2017selective} before and after calibration. Calibration did not improve selective prediction and did not consistently improve the calibration metrics themselves, so it is excluded from the reference configuration: a monotonic confidence map can reorder confidences but cannot manufacture the ranking information that selection requires.

\section{Learned Selector}

Three learned selectors were evaluated, none of which fine-tunes the diagnostician or any other HiED component. Each fits a lightweight external model: a gradient-boosted pairwise ranker~\citep{burges2010ranknet} (LightGBM)~\citep{ke2017lightgbm} trained out-of-fold under five-fold cross-validation with deliberately conservative capacity ($50$ trees, maximum depth $3$), combining pointwise features in four families---checker (criteria-met count, required count, average checker confidence), evidence (number of spans, average span confidence), somatization (number of somatic mappings, somatic criteria met), and cross-disorder (confidence margin, rank position, number of confirmed disorders)---with pairwise shared-criteria features (such as a shared-criteria count and a criteria-overlap ratio) and one-hot disorder-identity features; a BGE-M3 dense definition-similarity reranker; and a TF--IDF with logistic-regression selector. All three improved development-lineage accuracy but failed to transfer across corpora, indicating that the fitted gains reflect corpus-specific regularities rather than transferable diagnostic signal; the quantified cross-corpus evidence for the lexical channel appears in \Cref{tab:lexical-transfer-rewrite}.

\section{Checker Fine-Tuning}

Unlike the learned selector, which fits an external model without touching any HiED component, this probe fine-tunes a HiED component directly. It uses LoRA to fine-tune an independent Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct model on a supervised criterion-checking dataset, then substitutes this fine-tuned model for HiED's prompt-based checker bank and measures cross-corpus generalization on the four-cell train/test matrix spanning the two corpora (LingxiDiag$\rightarrow$LingxiDiag, LingxiDiag$\rightarrow$MDD, MDD$\rightarrow$LingxiDiag, MDD$\rightarrow$MDD). A fine-tuned checker improved in-corpus point accuracy in the matching cells but did not transfer to the off-diagonal cells, reinforcing that the bottleneck is not closed by fine-tuning a stronger checker on a single corpus.

\section{Definition-Similarity Fusion Probes}

These probes test whether disorder-definition similarity can serve as a post-hoc selector signal. They are not ablations of the canonical class-balanced case retriever used in HiED's diagnostician prompt (\Cref{sec:retrieval}), which retrieves training-case examples rather than matching against criterion-definition text. Dense, sparse, hybrid, and ColBERT similarity modes are applied as selector-side fusion signals over already produced candidate diagnoses. The purpose is to test whether a different similarity representation can repair primary selection, not whether the pipeline retrieval component should use a different case-retrieval mode. None of the four modes improves Top-1 over the Direct-Answer baseline.

\section{Self-Consistency and CISC}

The test-time aggregation intervention samples the diagnostician $K\in\{3,5\}$ times and aggregates its committed primary by plain majority vote (self-consistency)~\citep{wang2023selfconsistency} and by confidence-weighted vote using the diagnostician's verbalized per-diagnosis confidence (CISC)~\citep{taubenfeld2025cisc}. A single-sample temperature ladder (temperatures $0.0$, $0.3$, $0.7$) is run as a control. Aggregation is applied at the diagnostician because that is the locus of final primary commitment; checker-level aggregation is left to future work. No variant improved Top-1, and higher-temperature sampling reduced the point estimate; the per-case prediction artifacts for these runs are not part of the committed tree.

\section{Pairwise Differential}

The pairwise differential intervention activates on adjacent ranked candidates that are both sufficiently supported and close: a close cluster is assembled from ranked neighbors with met ratio at least $0.5$ and adjacent met-ratio distance less than $0.10$, the recorded checker outputs are reconstructed from the decision trace, and a differential-comparison agent re-decides the primary among the clustered alternatives. The scan is applied over the ranked list rather than only around the committed primary. It was evaluated three ways---an independent run, a controlled replay over the Direct-Answer (DA) reference policy's identical upstream outputs, and a forced override---plus a committed-policy variant in which the re-decided top candidate overrides the diagnostician's primary. For the controlled replay, close clusters are constructed from the DA reference artifacts rather than from a newly generated pairwise run: the DA ranked list determines adjacent candidates, and the DA checker trace determines each candidate's met ratio, so the differential-comparison agent is applied only as a replayed finalization step. This design isolates the effect of pairwise selection from changes in candidate generation or criterion verification. A second phase measured conditional recovery on close-cluster subsets. The controlled replay changed no committed primary under the DA reference policy, so local subset accuracy did not translate into a full-evaluation gain, and the forced override was harmful.

\section{Debate}

The debate intervention stages a bounded two-advocate, multi-round adversarial protocol~\citep{du2024debate}: each advocate argues for one of the leading candidate diagnoses in turn, citing transcript evidence, and a judge model issues a final adjudication after a fixed number of rounds. It is evaluated as a repair probe, not as the finalization policy of the selected system. Under the shared 8192-token budget used by every other method in the battery, all 925 external-corpus cases exceed the available context once the two-advocate, multi-round transcript accumulates, so debate is infeasible at that budget on the external corpus and is re-evaluated separately at an enlarged context budget sufficient for every case. At both the internal-test budget and the enlarged external budget, debate lowers Top-1 relative to Direct-Answer rather than improving it; additional adversarial reasoning rounds do not supply comparative information that the direct commitment lacks, and on the external corpus the degradation is the largest of any tested repair.

\section{Deterministic Fusion}

The deterministic-fusion intervention enumerates post-hoc rules that combine already-recorded signals---ranked position, per-criterion met ratios, and threshold satisfaction---into an alternative finalization rule, on the reasoning that a simple combination of existing signals might recover primacy information that no single signal expresses alone. The sweep spans five rule families over a shared parameter grid (seven met-ratio thresholds, a 21-point weighting coefficient, four confirmation-boost and margin values, and top-2/3/5 candidate windows): ten single-signal ranking or threshold rules, fifty-five rank-based rescue-and-veto rules, one hundred eighty-nine weighted-fusion rules combining rank and met-ratio scores, twenty-four confirmed-candidate-set rules, and fifteen conditional override-DA-unless rules, for 293 candidate rule definitions on the development split in total. Each rule is scored against the development-lineage Top-1 before any rule is frozen under the freeze discipline of \Cref{sec:freeze-discipline}, and the sweep finds no non-identical rule that beats Direct-Answer on development. This frozen rule---called the frozen override rule in the main text, and labeled E3 in the implementation and below---is not the best-performing development rule (none beat DA), but the rule selected by the pre-registered selection criterion prior to internal-test scoring. On the internal test split, E3 loses four net correct cases relative to Direct-Answer; on the external corpus it transfers as a significant loss. The consistent negative result across both corpora indicates the missing information is not recoverable by re-combining signals the system already records, whether the combination rule is a weighted fusion, a rank-based veto, or a conditional override.

\section{Residual-Evidence Loop}

The residual-evidence loop iteratively feeds the checker outputs and the criteria left unresolved by verification back into the diagnostician for re-invocation, in the hope that a second pass conditioned on the verification result would sharpen the primary. Across tested model families the loop produced unstable trade-offs between over-emission (adding diagnoses) and under-recovery (dropping the gold parent), without a stable net gain. It was developed as ad-hoc orchestration rather than a canonical, committed probe; its negative result is reproducible from archived development artifacts but a clean re-implementation remains an open task, so it is reported as a bounded experimental probe rather than a system component.

\chapter{Worked Audit-Trace Example}
\label{app:worked}

This appendix makes the audit trace concrete by walking a single constructed case through the system's recorded output. The case is synthetic and illustrative; it is not a patient record, and the confidence values shown are indicative rather than measured. It instantiates the depression--anxiety boundary archetype of Chapter~\ref{ch:error} so that the abstract claim of auditability can be inspected as a concrete object.

\section{Constructed Input}

A help-seeker describes roughly two weeks of persistent low mood with loss of interest in usual activities, early-morning waking, constant tiredness, difficulty concentrating at work, and a sense of being a burden; they also report worrying about many things and feeling unable to stop, feeling keyed up, and tension headaches. The account does not establish how long the worry has been present, does not mention appetite or weight change, and denies thoughts of self-harm. The distinguishing information that separates a primary depressive episode from a primary anxiety disorder---the relative onset and duration of worry versus mood, and the exclusion of medical or substance contributions---is not present in the account.

\section{Verdict, Ranking, and Committed Output}

Both candidates satisfy the same core-total confirmation rule in the ontology: at least two core criteria and at least four total criteria are met. The committed output in this worked trace is therefore not produced by sorting the confirmed set by met-ratio or by confidence. Instead, finalization follows the diagnostician's recorded ranking among confirmed candidates: because the depressive candidate appears before the anxiety candidate in the diagnostician's ranked differential and both are logic-confirmed, the depressive candidate remains the committed primary, and the anxiety candidate meets the comorbidity gate and is placed as a secondary diagnosis. The confidence values are reported as part of the audit trace, but they are not the tie-breaker that determines this commitment.

The recorded output therefore contains the committed primary (F32), the secondary diagnosis (F41), the ranked confirmed set, the full per-criterion states and grounding spans above, the logic-confirmed codes, and the decision trace that names the rule producing each placement. A reviewer can check each layer independently: whether each grounding span actually supports its criterion, whether the criterion states are clinically plausible, and whether the placement rule was applied correctly.

The example also exposes the selection bottleneck in its concrete form. Nothing in the detection or verification record is wrong: both disorders are genuinely supported, both are confirmed, and the cited evidence holds. What decides primacy is the comparison between two confirmed candidates, and the criterion that would settle it---whether the worry has persisted long enough, and with what onset relative to the mood disturbance, to make anxiety rather than depression primary---is recorded as \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} because the account does not contain it. The commitment to depression as primary thus rests on information the transcript never establishes, which is exactly the configuration in which the aggregate error analysis shows the dominant confusions arise. The trace does not resolve the ambiguity, but it makes the basis of the commitment fully visible, which is the property the auditable design is intended to provide.

\section{Post-Freeze Prompt--Retrieval Interaction Probe}

A post-freeze true-retriever prompt--retrieval probe examines how
retrieval interacts with prompt structure under the same FAISS/BGE-M3
case retriever used by the canonical in-domain runs (\cref{tab:prompt-rag-true-retriever}). Retrieval raises
the direct Single baseline from 0.466 to 0.508
(McNemar $p=4.0\times10^{-4}$), confirming that case retrieval helps
direct candidate support. In contrast, a historical rank-only
Diagnose-then-Verify (DTV v1) variant does not benefit from retrieval
(0.456 to 0.451, $p=0.73$), and the deployed HiED--DA configuration
shows only a small, non-significant marginal gain when retrieval is
added back (0.511 to 0.518, $p=0.43$). The probe supports the main
interpretation: retrieval helps direct candidate support, but the
structured HiED ceiling is dominated by primary selection rather than
by absence of retrieved examples. This finding does not weaken the
headline retrieval contribution reported in the architecture ladder
(\Cref{tab:ladder-rewrite}): the Single-to-Single+RAG gain is
unaffected by this probe and remains the thesis's primary evidence
that case retrieval supports candidate coverage. What this probe adds
is a narrower, later-stage observation: once the checker and logic
layers already support candidate quality, retrieval's additional
marginal contribution to HiED--DA's Top-1 shrinks and is no longer
statistically significant. The two findings answer different
questions---whether retrieval helps a bare model, and whether
retrieval still moves the needle once the full pipeline is already in
place---and are not in tension. This probe is therefore used only to
interpret retrieval sensitivity; it does not replace the frozen
architecture ladder, where the Single, Single+RAG, and HiED--DA rows
remain the thesis headline comparisons.

\Cref{tab:checker-factorial} reports the $2\times3$ checker-granularity by verification-scope factorial. Cells 1--5 (matched $N=1000$, identical case order) are not merely close: Top-1, Top-3, and macro-F1 are identical to three decimal places regardless of checker granularity or verification scope. Cell 6 (a smaller registered $N=200$ slice) differs only by sampling variation consistent with its size. This is not an approximate flatness; it is the expected consequence of the architecture under Direct-Answer finalization. The diagnostician's committed primary is generated before the checker or logic layer runs, and Direct-Answer emits that primary unchanged; checker granularity and verification scope govern what the audit layer records, not what the diagnostician commits. This result is important because it prevents a tempting but incorrect interpretation. The checker bank is imperfect, but simply tuning how many criteria must be met does not supply the missing comparative ordering, and it could not, since verification sits downstream of the committed decision under Direct-Answer. The problem is not only whether each diagnosis is compatible; it is which compatible diagnosis should be primary.
