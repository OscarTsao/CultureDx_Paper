\chapter*{中文摘要}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{中文摘要}
初診精神科晤談逐字稿中的鑑別診斷，本質上是一種在證據不完整條件下進行的比較式決策。多個疾病診斷可能同時與同一份逐字稿相容，但最終通常只能有一個被判定為主診斷。然而，用以區分病程、發病時序、排除病因與共病關係等關鍵鑑別資訊，未必會在單次初診晤談中被完整引出。大型語言模型雖然能流暢地閱讀並理解這類逐字稿，近期也已有大量研究嘗試將其應用於精神科診斷任務，但語言理解的流暢性並不等同於可靠的鑑別診斷能力。

既有評測呈現出一致的落差：模型在粗粒度分類任務上往往表現良好，但一旦進入細粒度的疾病層級鑑別診斷，準確率便會大幅下降。這個落差促使本研究提出一套可稽核的系統架構，用以明確定位錯誤發生於診斷流程中的哪一個階段。模型可能生成一個合理且受到診斷準則支持的候選疾病，卻仍未能將其正確選為主診斷。候選診斷生成、依準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇三者之間的差異，通常會被單一準確率指標所掩蓋，使研究者難以判斷系統究竟在哪個環節失敗。

為了解決這個問題，本研究提出 HiED——一套面向中文精神科初診對話逐字稿的鑑別診斷框架，將候選診斷偵測、ICD-10 逐準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇維持為可分別觀察的階段，而非將整個流程簡化為單一分類事件。同時，系統會在每次預測時輸出逐準則的稽核軌跡，使診斷推論過程能夠被檢視與分析。本研究中的系統並非宣稱已完成臨床驗證的自主診斷工具，而是定位為一個研究工具，用以分析在逐字稿條件下，精神科診斷流程究竟在哪一個階段發生失敗。

因此，本論文有兩項主要貢獻：其一，提出一套可稽核的失敗定位框架，使上述各診斷階段得以分別觀察；其二，提供可重現的證據指出，在僅使用初診逐字稿進行的精神科大型語言模型診斷任務中，主診斷選擇本身構成一個核心瓶頸。換言之，在本研究之合成逐字稿設定下，系統的主要限制不僅在於能否生成合理的候選診斷，而更在於能否在多個皆具一定準則支持的候選之間，進行有效比較並判定最適合作為主診斷者。最後需說明，本文題目所稱之臺灣脈絡，指涉系統之設計動機與預期部署場域（繁體中文互動、本地端推論、面向臨床人員之稽核），而非已完成之臺灣真實病人臨床驗證；本文所有量化結果均來自合成中文逐字稿語料，真實病人之臨床驗證為後續工作。

\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial-consultation psychiatric transcript is a comparative decision under incomplete evidence. Multiple disorders may be compatible with the same transcript, yet only one can typically be judged the primary diagnosis. Key discriminating information---illness course, onset timing, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity relationships---may not be fully elicited within a single initial consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, and recent research has applied them to psychiatric diagnostic tasks, but linguistic fluency does not equate to reliable differential diagnostic ability.

Existing evaluations show a consistent gap: models perform well on coarse-grained classification, but accuracy drops sharply on fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis. This gap motivates an auditable architecture that locates the stage at which a diagnostic error occurs. A model may generate a plausible disorder supported by diagnostic criteria, yet fail to select it as the primary diagnosis. The distinction among candidate-diagnosis generation, criterion-based verification, and primary-diagnosis selection is usually obscured by a single accuracy metric.

To address this problem, we propose HiED, a differential-diagnosis framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts that keeps candidate-diagnosis detection, ICD-10 criterion-by-criterion verification, and primary-diagnosis selection as separately observable stages, rather than a single classification event. The system outputs a criterion-level audit trace with every prediction, allowing the diagnostic inference to be examined. It is not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic tool, but a research instrument for analyzing where transcript-based psychiatric diagnosis fails. The Taiwanese context in the title denotes the intended deployment setting; real-patient Taiwanese validation remains future work.

This thesis therefore makes two main contributions: an auditable failure-localization framework that keeps these stages separately observable, and reproducible evidence that primary-diagnosis selection constitutes a core bottleneck in psychiatric large-language-model diagnosis using only initial-consultation transcripts. Under this study's synthetic transcript-only setting, the system's primary limitation lies not merely in whether it can generate plausible candidate diagnoses, but more critically in whether it can compare among multiple criterion-supported candidates and determine which is best suited as the primary diagnosis.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric outpatient care in Taiwan is widely accessible, but this access also creates a heavy clinical workload. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit different levels of medical facilities without referral, and large medical centers may receive more than ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in one day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A first psychiatric visit must cover history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and an initial diagnostic judgment within limited time. The resulting note or transcript often contains incomplete information about onset, duration, course, medical or substance-related causes, and previous treatment.

The evidence may also be indirect. In Chinese-speaking settings, patients often describe distress through insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, or gastrointestinal symptoms before using explicit mood or anxiety terms~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. Taiwanese consultations may also mix Mandarin, Taiwanese expressions, and local clinical wording. The same condition can therefore be described in several ways within one visit.

Differential diagnosis remains difficult because common psychiatric disorders share many symptoms. Depression, anxiety, stress-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, somatic symptom disorders, and sleep disorders may all involve insomnia, fatigue, poor concentration, and impaired daily function. ICD-10 provides requirements for symptoms, duration, impairment, course, and exclusions~\citep{who2019icd10}, but several disorders may still fit the same transcript. Evidence for both depression and anxiety, for example, may reflect comorbidity or overlapping symptoms from one main condition~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}. Even structured diagnostic studies report limited agreement for some common disorders~\citep{regier2013dsm5}.

These features make first-visit psychiatric diagnosis a problem of comparative bookkeeping under incomplete evidence. The clinician must document a best-supported working diagnosis, keep plausible alternatives visible, and identify which missing information should be collected at follow-up. Time pressure can increase the risk of anchoring or premature closure when several disorders share the same symptoms~\citep{croskerry2003cognitive}.
\section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

Large language models and medical multi-agent systems are attractive for psychiatric transcript analysis because they can process free-form dialogue, summarize symptoms, retrieve relevant examples, assign specialized roles, and return structured outputs~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. However, these capabilities do not by themselves ensure reliable differential diagnosis from a fixed transcript. A fluent answer may still fail because the model never proposes a gold label, because its criterion audit does not support that diagnosis, or because it proposes the diagnosis but commits another one. HiED studies this narrower technical problem; it does not solve short consultation time, missing history, or clinical uncertainty.\ct{Does our model deal with all these problems?}

We study this question with three measures. \emph{Candidate detection} asks whether a gold parent label appears in the ranked Top-3. \emph{Criterion verification} asks whether a gold parent appears in the rule-confirmed set when that diagnosis has a corresponding checker. \emph{Committed selection} asks whether the committed diagnosis belongs to the gold parent set. These measures are used to locate disagreements among output views; they do not form a fixed chronological funnel for every policy.

We use the term \emph{primary-selection bottleneck} for the operational pattern in which a gold label is present among leading candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but the system commits a different diagnosis. The bottleneck is therefore a benchmark-local pattern showing that candidate ranking or criterion compatibility can exceed final commitment; it is not a claim about the true clinical primary diagnosis. It does not show that every error passes both earlier checks, that the corpus identifies a unique clinical primary diagnosis, or that every transcript contains enough evidence for one.\ct{what problem?}

This distinction matters because different errors need different solutions. Missing candidates call for better generation or retrieval. A candidate that fails criterion checking points to the checker, the rules, or missing evidence. A candidate that remains available but is not selected points to the final comparison among plausible diagnoses. The detailed metric definitions are given in \Cref{sec:gold-conventions}.

\section{Overview of HiED and Study Scope}

To make these failure modes observable, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid multi-agent system for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis from initial-consultation transcripts. The intended context is Taiwanese outpatient care, but all quantitative results in this thesis use synthetic Chinese transcript datasets; no Taiwanese patient results are reported.

HiED has five main parts. It formats the dialogue while preserving speaker turns, optionally retrieves similar examples, asks an LLM diagnostician for a ranked differential and a primary diagnosis, checks each configured diagnostic category against operationalized ICD-10 criteria, and applies deterministic rules to form a rule-confirmed set. Each criterion is marked as \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}.

The Direct-Answer policy keeps the diagnostician's primary diagnosis and uses outputs from the Criterion Checker and Compatibility Auditor as audit artifacts. The alternative Nominate-then-Select policy uses the same upstream outputs but allows the rule-confirmed set to determine a different committed diagnosis. Their comparison tests whether criterion compatibility is more useful as an audit or as a selector. Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} describes the architecture in detail.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions.

First, it presents an auditable hybrid architecture that combines LLM diagnosis, explicit ICD-10 criterion checking, and deterministic rules. The system records criterion states and supporting evidence instead of relying only on a free-form rationale. The trace can be inspected and replayed, but it does not prove that the LLM's hidden reasoning is faithful or that the checker judgments are clinically correct~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}.

Second, it presents a stage-wise failure-localization and empirical evaluation framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, and committed selection, quantifies the primary-selection bottleneck, and tests whether criterion-based finalization and several repair methods close the observed gap. The negative repair results narrow several simple explanations under the tested settings, but they do not show that better selectors or additional clinical information can never help.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

Psychiatric LLM studies cover several different tasks, including examination questions, short vignettes, diagnostic reasoning, and comparison with clinicians~\citep{qiu2023largeai,li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. These studies show that LLMs can produce plausible clinical answers, but performance depends on the input, label space, and reference standard. Detecting depression from text or audio is also different from choosing one primary diagnosis among several overlapping disorders~\citep{li2025addreview}.

Chinese psychiatric datasets make disorder-level evaluation possible. LingxiDiagBench includes static diagnosis and dynamic consultation over the synthetic LingxiDiag-16K corpus and reports a clear drop from broad depression--anxiety classification to fine-grained diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. MDD-5k provides a second synthetic Chinese diagnostic-dialogue resource based on anonymized cases~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. These datasets support reproducible experiments, but they do not replace real-patient validation.

Existing systems also differ in whether they can ask questions. MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, and DSM5AgentFlow study inquiry or interactive diagnostic workflows~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels focus more on diagnosis from information already available in records or transcripts~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. Interactive systems can obtain missing evidence, while fixed-input systems isolate how well a model uses the same evidence. This thesis studies the fixed-input setting.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning}

Retrieval-augmented generation conditions a model on external records selected for the current query rather than relying only on information encoded in model parameters~\citep{lewis2020rag}. In-context-learning studies similarly show that semantically related demonstrations can be retrieved to construct a query-specific prompt and that performance depends on which examples are selected~\citep{liu2022incontext}. In transcript-based diagnosis, retrieved cases can therefore provide prompt-side examples of symptom descriptions and diagnostic labels. They are demonstrations rather than patient evidence and should not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

Similarity retrieval can expose useful corpus-local regularities, but it can also reproduce the frequency and wording patterns of the indexed data. Ordinary nearest-neighbor retrieval may repeatedly select examples from common labels, while a parent-balanced policy changes the label composition of the prompt. It may also change the number, ordering, and total token length of the retrieved examples. A comparison between global and parent-balanced retrieval consequently evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating balancing as a single causal factor.

These considerations motivate the retrieval experiments in this thesis. No retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval are compared using the same training-set retrieval index, evaluation cases, label projection, and scoring implementation. The comparison is repeated in both the Single and HiED pipelines so that retrieval behavior is not inferred from only one architecture.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

Medical multi-agent systems assign different roles to LLMs and combine their outputs through consultation, debate, or adaptive collaboration~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. AMIE and related work also show the value of broad differential diagnosis, realistic consultation, and expert review~\citep{mcduff2025amie,tu2025conversational}. These systems demonstrate that structured interaction can help, but adding agents does not guarantee a better final answer. Agents may repeat the same model bias, and free-form discussion can make it difficult to identify which component changed the decision.

For this thesis, the important issue is controlled attribution. If ranking, criterion checking, debate, and finalization all change at once, an accuracy difference cannot show which change helped. HiED therefore compares two policies that reuse the same ranked candidates and checker outputs: Direct-Answer keeps the diagnostician's decision, whereas Nominate-then-Select uses criterion compatibility to choose the primary.

\section{Auditability and Criterion Grounding}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

A fluent explanation is not proof of faithful reasoning. Model-generated chains can sound coherent while not reflecting the factors that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. In healthcare, explanation quality, interpretability, and clinical validity therefore need separate evaluation~\citep{rudin2019stop,huang2024xiai}.

Diagnostic-chain and symptom-based methods make outputs easier to inspect~\citep{chen2025cod,lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. However, a generated reasoning chain is still another model output. This thesis uses the narrower term \emph{auditability}: an external record that links transcript evidence, criterion states, rule results, and the final output. The record can be checked and replayed, but it does not reveal the model's hidden reasoning or turn checker outputs into clinical ground truth.

Constraint-logic systems are the closest related approach because they translate diagnostic criteria into explicit rules~\citep{kim2025clp}. HiED keeps the learned diagnostician separate from the criterion and rule layer. Under Direct-Answer, the structured layer audits the decision; under Nominate-then-Select, it is tested as a selector. Among the reviewed studies, we did not identify an evaluation that uses this separation to compare gold-label retention with primary selection on the same Chinese psychiatric transcripts.

\section{Structured Diagnostic Knowledge and Primary Selection}

Knowledge-based diagnosis methods represent relations among symptoms, disorders, and missing information. MedKGI supports information-guided inquiry, and DKEC uses structured domain knowledge for label prediction~\citep{wang2025medkgi,ge2024dkec}. Such relations may help when disorders share symptoms but differ in onset, duration, exclusions, or causal direction.

HiED does not implement a full knowledge-graph selector. Instead, this literature motivates future work if simple criterion counts and local re-ranking are not enough. A stronger selector may need to compare which diagnosis best explains the full presentation, not only how many criteria each diagnosis satisfies.

\section{Research Gap and Research Questions}

Prior work shows that LLMs can diagnose psychiatric cases, retrieve examples, use diagnostic criteria, and work in multi-agent systems. Five gaps remain. First, conventional text classifiers, single LLMs, retrieval-augmented LLMs, and structured systems are not always compared under the same split and endpoint. Second, final accuracy does not show whether a gold label was absent from the candidate list or present but not committed. Third, global nearest-neighbor retrieval and parent-balanced retrieval require matched comparison across architectures. Fourth, criterion-based finalization and later repair methods must be evaluated separately from candidate generation and retrieval. Fifth, it remains unclear whether the observed ranking-to-commitment pattern persists across model sizes, external corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope. The precise gap addressed here is that HiED retains ranked candidate generation, criterion compatibility, and final commitment as separate outputs evaluated on the same cases.

This thesis therefore uses five research questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?
\item[RQ2] How often is a gold label present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?
\item[RQ3] How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?
\item[RQ4] Do criterion-based re-selection or the tested selection-repair methods improve over Direct-Answer?
\item[RQ5] To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and what error patterns characterize the remaining failures?
\end{description}

RQ1 and RQ3 use matched comparisons on the same fixed internal test set, with common label projection and evaluation code. Clinical effectiveness, transcript-only human performance, and criterion-level clinical validity remain future work.

\section{Transcript Input and Configured Diagnostic Scope}

HiED processes one Chinese psychiatric consultation transcript at a time. The input is an ordered sequence of turns, each marked as \texttt{Clinician} or \texttt{Patient}; speaker labels and turn order are preserved in the prompt. Transcripts longer than the available prompt budget are deterministically truncated by retaining complete turns from the beginning and end, with an explicit marker for the omitted middle. The system does not summarize or invent missing text. This is a fixed-input study: the system cannot ask the patient a follow-up question during inference.\ct{Is this about input and transcript format?}

ICD-10 is the diagnostic coding system used to name the disorders in this study~\citep{who2019icd10}. The system does not search all ICD-10 diagnoses. Before a run begins, it loads one fixed \emph{configured diagnostic profile} that is used for every patient. The main diagnostic profile contains fourteen configured diagnostic categories. F41 and F43 include subtype-level categories, so the fourteen categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels for scoring. Chapter~\ref{ch:data} defines how mixed in-scope and out-of-scope gold codes are projected for evaluation.

Three terms are used throughout the architecture. A \emph{configured diagnostic category} is an ICD-10 code that the diagnostician may rank and for which the system defines a corresponding criterion checker. The \emph{criterion-checker set} is the collection of those fourteen checker definitions; it is not a learned memory bank. Each criterion receives one of three model-generated evidence states: \texttt{met} when the transcript supports it, \texttt{not\_met} when the transcript contains evidence against it, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the transcript supports neither conclusion. The rules are study-specific operationalizations informed by ICD-10 descriptions, not a reproduction of the full official manual and not independently clinician-validated ground truth.

\section{Global Top-5 and Parent-Balanced Case Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

The Similar-Case Retriever adds relevant training examples to the diagnostician prompt. It follows the retrieval-augmented generation pattern~\citep{lewis2020rag}: BGE-M3 is the multilingual text-embedding model used to convert the query transcript and training transcripts into normalized dense vectors~\citep{chen2024bgem3}. FAISS is the vector similarity-search library and index used for retrieval, not a standalone data store~\citep{douze2024faiss}. FAISS inner-product search over normalized vectors corresponds to cosine-similarity ranking. The study evaluates no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval; the final retrieval setting is selected using the public validation split.

The retrieval classes are fixed by the configured diagnostic profile and are identical for every patient.\ct{Are the candidate classes pre-determined, or patient-specific?} Only the example selected within each class depends on the current transcript. Subtypes that share a parent code, such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2, share one retrieval class. Global Top-5 always returns the five nearest eligible index entries. Parent-balanced retrieval attempts to return at most one high-similarity example for each of the eleven scoring parents and can therefore supply up to eleven examples. This policy is intended to reduce domination by frequent classes, but it also changes example count, label composition, ordering, prompt length, and possibly the available transcript budget. Retrieved cases are included only in the Diagnostician prompt; they do not enter checker prompts or directly choose the final output.

The three conditions use the same training-set retrieval index and embedding model. Their comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating parent balancing as a single causal factor.

\section{Ranked Differential Generation}

The canonical diagnostician uses Qwen3-32B with 4-bit Activation-aware Weight Quantization (AWQ). Its input contains the transcript, the configured diagnostic profile, and optional retrieved examples. Its prompt requests a ranked candidate list containing five ordered diagnoses from that profile, a short reason for each, one primary field chosen from the first three, and at most one separate comorbid field. In clinical prose this is a ranked differential diagnosis; as a formal artifact it is the ranked candidate list. The primary field and the ranked list are stored separately. In the running example, F41.1 can therefore remain in the Top-3 even though F32 is emitted as primary.

The main diagnostician configuration is a system specification, not an ablation. It uses greedy decoding and does not request a calibrated probability or an abstention decision. Confidence-based sampling, calibration, and selective-output ideas are evaluated only as later repair or future-work directions.\ct{Is this an ablation study? If so, why in Appendix?}

\section{Criterion-Level Evidence Checking}

Each of the fourteen configured diagnostic categories has a corresponding criterion checker based on the study's operational rule set.\ct{Is this ICD 10?} The rules are linked to ICD-10 disorder descriptions~\citep{who2019icd10}, but several thresholds are simplified study rules. Appendix~\ref{app:configured-profile} lists the diagnostic codes and configured thresholds so readers can distinguish the study implementation from official clinical guidance.

For each criterion, the checker returns \texttt{met} when the transcript supports the criterion, \texttt{not\_met} when the transcript contains evidence against it, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the available transcript does not resolve it.\ct{What 3 states?} It may also return a short transcript span linked to the judgment. Each configured diagnostic category is checked as an independent task using the transcript and its category-specific operational criteria. Retrieved examples do not enter checker prompts, and checkers do not communicate with one another. All fourteen configured categories are checked for every case, yielding one category-level audit record for each.

Missing information is not negative evidence. A criterion is \texttt{not\_met} only when the transcript contains evidence against it; otherwise unresolved evidence remains \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}.

\section{Deterministic Diagnostic Compatibility Aggregation}

The Compatibility Auditor is a deterministic rule-based module. For each configured diagnostic category, it reads the Criterion Checker states, applies the predefined confirmation rule, adds the diagnosis to the rule-confirmed set when that rule is satisfied, and retains the rule application in the audit trace. A rule may require all criteria, a minimum number of core and total criteria, a symptom count, or particular criterion groups. The same checker trace always produces the same confirmed set. Confirmation means compatibility with the configured study rule; it is not primacy and does not mean that the diagnosis is clinically correct or should be primary. Several diagnoses may be confirmed in the same case.

Checker states are model-generated and have not been validated as clinician ground truth. They create an evidence-linked record that can be inspected and replayed, but they do not prove that the criterion judgments or evidence spans are correct.

\section{Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select Finalization}

HiED defines two finalization policies over the same upstream ranked candidates, criterion states, and rule-confirmed set. Direct-Answer (DA) is the default policy and preserves the diagnostician's emitted decision. Nominate-then-Select (NtS) tests whether criterion-derived compatibility can improve the committed primary diagnosis and emits one selected diagnosis.

Under Direct-Answer (DA), the diagnostician's primary field becomes the committed diagnosis; the first ranked diagnosis is used only when that field is missing. An optional comorbidity is retained when it differs from the primary. Outputs from the Criterion Checker and Compatibility Auditor do not replace the DA committed diagnosis.

Nominate-then-Select (NtS) uses criterion compatibility to choose the committed diagnosis. For configured category $d$, its criterion-support ratio is
\[
\operatorname{met\_ratio}(d)=\min\!\left(1,\frac{\text{number of \texttt{met} criteria for }d}{\text{required criterion count for }d}\right).
\]
The denominator is the required count stored in the configured rule, not the total number of listed criteria. NtS first selects the earliest rule-confirmed diagnosis in the five-diagnosis ranking. If none of those five is confirmed, it selects another rule-confirmed diagnosis by descending met ratio, with ties resolved by the predefined configured-category order. If no diagnosis is rule-confirmed, it falls back to the first ranked diagnosis.

NtS emits only the re-selected primary. DA and NtS therefore reuse the same ranked candidates and checker traces but differ in both the primary-selection rule and secondary-label emission. The primary analysis consequently uses Top-1 Accuracy. Exact Match and classwise F1 for NtS also reflect its single-label output contract and are not interpreted as isolated effects of primary re-selection. Additional primary-selection repair methods are defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental} and do not alter the core architecture described here.

\section{Recorded Audit Outputs}

The audit artifacts include the predicted diagnosis set, ranked differential, criterion states, short evidence spans, confirmed diagnoses, finalization source, and run failures. A reviewer can therefore challenge the candidate ranking, an individual criterion judgment, a study rule, or the final selection separately.

This record follows the definition of auditability in Section~\ref{sec:auditability-definition}. It makes the system easier to inspect, but it does not expose the LLM's hidden reasoning, prove clinical correctness, or replace real-patient validation.

\section{Model and Inference Configuration}

The main system uses Qwen3-32B with 4-bit Activation-aware Weight Quantization (AWQ). AWQ reduces model-memory requirements and allows the 32-billion-parameter backbone to be served locally without changing the prompt, diagnostic scope, or finalization logic. No main-system component fine-tunes model weights. vLLM is the inference and serving backend for loading the model and generating outputs; it is not a diagnostic component and does not alter prompts, criteria, rules, or finalization. The system uses greedy decoding, an 8,192-token context window, a 1,536-token output cap, structured JSON output, guided decoding where supported, schema validation, and checker-output recovery for duplicate or invalid criterion items.

Runs are checked for case count, valid outputs, parse failures, ranked-output availability, and candidate validity. A checker-output recovery occurs when an LLM response can be parsed but contains duplicate or invalid criterion items. The parser removes those items and retains the valid criterion records. This is a structured-output schema-adherence warning rather than a hard parse failure.

\section{Configured Output Space and Gold-Label Projection}
\label{sec:gold-conventions}

The main system uses fourteen configured ICD-10 diagnostic categories, listed in Table~\ref{tab:configured-profile-summary}. For evaluation, subtype-level categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels. Let $G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x)$ be the raw benchmark codes for case $x$, let $\pi$ map subtype codes such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2 to their scoring parent, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of eleven configured scoring parents. The in-scope gold set is
\[
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\{\pi(c):c\in G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x),\ \pi(c)\in\mathcal{C}\}.
\]
The scored gold-label set is then
\[
G(x)=
\begin{cases}
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x), & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)\neq\varnothing,\\
\{\mathrm{Others}\}, & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\varnothing.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus, a mixed case such as F90 with F41.1 is scored as $\{\mathrm{F41}\}$ rather than $\{\mathrm{Others},\mathrm{F41}\}$. A case is in scope when $G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)$ is nonempty and out of scope otherwise. Because the main system has neither an \emph{Others} output nor a trained unknown detector, it cannot correctly represent an out-of-scope case.

Following standard machine-learning evaluation terminology, benchmark-provided diagnosis codes are referred to as \emph{gold labels}. In this thesis, the term denotes the dataset annotations used for scoring and does not imply an independently adjudicated clinical gold standard. For case $x$, $G(x)$ is the deduplicated gold-label set after parent-level projection. LingxiDiag cases may contain more than one gold parent label, and neither dataset provides an independently adjudicated unique clinical primary diagnosis. The first-listed gold parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses such as the confusion matrix.

In the running example from Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}, F41.1 is scored as F41. If the gold-label set contains F41 and the system emits F32 while ranking F41.1 second, Top-1 is wrong but Top-3 is correct.

\section{Prediction Views and Metrics}

The evaluator keeps three outputs separate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item The \emph{primary view}, $p_1(x)$, is the final primary diagnosis.
\item The \emph{ranked view}, $R_3(x)$, contains the first three distinct parent labels in the method's genuine ranked output. For HiED, it comes from the recorded Diagnostician ranking and is independent of any later DA or NtS finalization decision.
\item The \emph{multilabel view}, $M(x)$, contains the primary and any emitted comorbid parent.
\end{enumerate}

TF--IDF + logistic regression obtains its Top-3 from ranked class scores. HiED obtains it from the Diagnostician ranking. A Single system without a genuine ranked output is not assigned an artificial Top-3.

Because a case may contain more than one gold label, Top-$k$ Accuracy counts a case as correct when at least one gold label appears among the first $k$ predictions. Exact Match, also called subset accuracy, requires the complete predicted label set to equal the complete gold-label set. These are benchmark-agreement measures, not independent measures of the patient's true clinical diagnosis.

\[

\]

S(x)=\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
where $L(x)$ is the rule-confirmed parent set. $D_3$ and $S$ use all cases. Gold-label retention, $R$, is calculated only for cases whose gold-label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} parent represented by a corresponding Criterion Checker. This checker-eligible subset contains 915 of the 1,000 test cases, whereas Top-1 and Top-3 use all 1,000 cases. The rates therefore have different denominators and should not be interpreted as consecutive stages of one case-level funnel.

These indicators ask three simple questions: Was a gold label in the genuine ranked Top-3? Was it retained as compatible under the configured rules? Was it selected as primary? They are analysis views of one recorded output, not a claim that every finalization policy executes the steps in that order.

\section{Statistical Analysis}

The case is the unit of analysis, and the two-sided significance level is $\alpha=0.05$. Single-system binary proportions are summarized with 95\% Wilson confidence intervals. Pairwise comparisons require identical case IDs and endpoint definitions. For binary correctness outcomes, the point estimate is the paired difference in correctness proportions, and the exact McNemar test is computed from the two discordant counts: cases correct only under the first system and cases correct only under the second. These counts are reported with the corresponding $p$-value so that the direction and magnitude of the paired difference remain visible.

For paired accuracy differences and finalization interaction contrasts, case IDs are resampled with replacement using 10,000 paired bootstrap replicates and seed 42. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles form the reported 95\% confidence interval. Reported $p$-values are interpreted together with discordant counts, confidence intervals, and effect direction rather than as standalone evidence.

Holm correction is applied separately within the comparison families defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}; unrelated research questions are not pooled into one family. A non-significant result indicates insufficient evidence of a difference and is not treated as proof of equivalence. Paired tests require identical cases and endpoint definitions. When systems also differ in context budget, number of model calls, or other execution settings, the paired result is interpreted as a comparison between complete configurations rather than as an isolated component effect.

\section{Failure Localization Across Ranking, Verification, and Commitment (RQ2)}

RQ2 uses the $D_3$--$R$--$S$ measures defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}. Under the DA output contract, the emitted primary must be selected from the Diagnostician's first three candidates. Every Top-1-correct DA case is therefore also Top-3 correct, even though the committed primary is stored separately from the genuine ranking. When both metrics use the same 1,000 cases, the Top-3--Top-1 difference consequently counts cases in which at least one gold parent appears within the Top-3 but the committed diagnosis is not a gold parent.

Criterion retention cannot be added as a third marginal stage in the same 1,000-case funnel because it is defined only for the 915 checker-eligible cases. The case-level analysis therefore constructs a $2\times2\times2$ joint table on those 915 cases, where $D_3$ indicates that a gold parent appears in the Top-3, $R$ indicates that a gold parent appears in the rule-confirmed set, and $S$ indicates that the committed diagnosis is a gold parent. The joint table supports quantities such as $P(R\mid D_3)$, $P(S\mid R)$, and $P(S\mid D_3,R)$ without mixing denominators. Confirmed-set size and criterion-state counts additionally show whether the audit rejects the gold label or keeps many diagnoses at the same time.

A supporting broad gold-in-supported-cluster oracle analysis considers diagnoses appearing in either the Diagnostician ranking or the rule-confirmed set. Diagnoses with met ratio at least 0.50 are sorted by support, adjacent diagnoses are linked when their ratio difference is below 0.10, and consecutive links form clusters. A wrong DA case is counted when any cluster contains a gold parent. The definition neither requires the DA primary to belong to that cluster nor represents a deployable local selector; it is reported only as an optimistic opportunity upper bound.

\section{Primary-Selection Repair Methods}

The repair analysis separates downstream selection on common recorded DA outputs from complete configurations that require new inference. This distinction determines whether a result can be interpreted as a selector-level intervention or only as the performance of the full configuration.

\subsection{Downstream Selection on Common DA Outputs}

NtS and deterministic fusion reuse recorded upstream DA artifacts. A conservative application of recorded pairwise preferences accepted no replacement and reproduced DA exactly; it is retained only as an implementation check rather than a primary method.

\subsection{New Inference Configurations}

The independent pairwise run, two-view debate, SC, and CISC require new model generation. They may change sampling, upstream outputs, number of calls, or context budget and are therefore evaluated as complete configurations.

\paragraph{Pairwise comparison.} The gate considers ranked diagnoses with met ratio at least 0.50, sorts them by support, and links adjacent alternatives separated by less than 0.10. When several adjacent pairs qualify, their union forms one comparison cluster; the cluster need not contain the current DA primary. The pairwise prompt receives the deterministically budgeted transcript and criterion states, evidence spans, confidence fields, and support counts for every diagnosis in that cluster. A returned diagnosis is valid only if it belongs to the cluster. The forced-override configuration mandatorily promotes a valid preference and serves as a safety stress test. Its retained artifact is a full pairwise-commit pipeline whose genuine ranking differs from the DA trace, so the paired outcome comparison evaluates the complete configuration rather than an isolated common-upstream finalizer. A conservative gate applied to recorded DA outputs produced no committed changes and is reported only as a supporting implementation check. Cases without an eligible cluster or valid preference retain the existing output.

\paragraph{Two-view debate.} Debate is a ranking reconciliation rather than a pro-versus-con argument about one diagnosis. The candidate set is the union of the Diagnostician Top-5 and the rule-confirmed diagnoses. Advocate-D receives the ranking, rationale, and retrieved-case gist. Advocate-C receives the same candidates and criterion-evidence table, but not the Diagnostician rationale or retrieved cases. Each receives the deterministically budgeted transcript and produces a ranked Top-3 for at most two rounds. Agreement on the first candidate ends the debate; otherwise a neutral judge selects from the shared candidate set. This is an independently generated pipeline run, and the debate contract emits one primary rather than preserving the DA secondary label. Because the advocate and judge prompts combine the transcript, candidate set, and intermediate arguments, debate uses a 24,576-token context window, compared with 8,192 tokens for DA. Its result is therefore a complete cross-budget configuration comparison, not a matched-budget component ablation.

\paragraph{Deterministic fusion.} The fusion rule combines the recorded DA rank, rule-confirmation status, and criterion met ratio without additional LLM inference. It was selected on the public validation split before internal-test evaluation. It keeps DA unless the DA primary is unconfirmed and a confirmed Top-3 alternative exceeds its met ratio by at least 0.20.

\paragraph{Self-consistency and CISC.} Self-consistency samples the Diagnostician $K=3$ or $K=5$ times at temperature 0.7 under fixed seed schedules and selects the primary by vote. Ties in vote count are resolved by selecting the diagnosis that first appears in the fixed sequence of sampled outputs. CISC uses the same samples but weights votes by the Diagnostician's verbalized primary confidence, clipped to $[0,1]$; a missing confidence receives weight 1. Ties in confidence-weighted scores are first resolved by unweighted vote count and then by first appearance in the fixed sample sequence. These confidences are neither model logits nor calibrated probabilities. Criterion Checkers, deterministic logic, and finalization are not rerun. The internal rows lack retained case-level sampling records and are therefore descriptive supporting evidence, excluded from core paired repair claims. The external MDD-5k matrix retains sample predictions, seeds, and aggregation records for audit.

Parent-projected Top-1 Accuracy is the common repair endpoint. Top-3, Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 are reported only when a method produces the corresponding genuine ranked or multilabel output. Methods on identical cases report wrong-to-correct and correct-to-wrong flips; the core repair family applies Holm correction to the predefined DA comparisons reported together in the result table.

Because NtS emits one diagnosis, its Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 also reflect the single-label output contract and are not used to isolate primary re-selection. A separate checker-sensitivity analysis leaves DA Top-1 unchanged across the five full-size settings, as expected because DA does not allow the downstream audit to overwrite the diagnostician's primary.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

This chapter interprets the results around one central distinction: a diagnosis may be generated and retained as compatible without being selected as primary. The discussion first examines this distinction, then considers how the lexical baseline, retrieval comparisons, repair methods, and external analyses modify its interpretation.

\section{Primary Finding: Compatibility Does Not Resolve Primacy}

The Criterion Checker evaluates each diagnosis separately. It asks whether the transcript is compatible with that diagnosis, not whether it is a better primary explanation than the alternatives. Common findings such as poor sleep, fatigue, low mood, worry, avoidance, and reduced concentration can therefore support several categories. Primary selection often depends on comparative facts such as onset, duration, previous episodes, exclusionary causes, and whether one syndrome better explains the remaining symptoms.

The internal parent-balanced trace illustrates this difference. HiED--DA reaches 0.802 Top-3 Accuracy and 0.518 Top-1 Accuracy. A gold parent is retained in the rule-confirmed set in 857 of 915 checker-eligible cases, while the median confirmed set contains six categories. These quantities use different denominators and are not a strict funnel, but the joint analysis identifies 272 cases in which a gold parent is ranked and retained without being committed.

NtS provides a controlled test of whether the recorded criterion-compatibility signals improve primary selection when the upstream ranking and checker outputs are held fixed. Under this comparison, NtS reduces rather than improves Top-1 Accuracy by 2.8--2.9 percentage points across no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Because NtS emits only one diagnosis, its set-level metrics cannot isolate primary re-selection from output-cardinality effects. The similar negative Top-1 effects indicate that the NtS penalty is not confined to one retrieval configuration; because the interaction confidence intervals include zero, they show no detected moderation rather than equivalence among the effects.

The stage-wise analysis changes how errors are interpreted. Absence of a gold parent from the genuine Top-3 identifies a candidate-generation miss. Ranking a gold parent without retaining it under the configured rules identifies a compatibility-retention miss. Ranking and retaining a gold parent while committing another diagnosis identifies a comparative-selection error. Endpoint accuracy alone cannot distinguish these failure locations.

\section{What the Lexical Baseline Changes}

The TF--IDF result prevents the ranking-to-commitment gap from being read as proof of intrinsic transcript underdetermination. A supervised lexical model reaches 0.632 Top-1 on the same internal synthetic split, above the evaluated HiED--DA trace. The benchmark therefore contains lexical signal that the tested LLM pipeline does not fully exploit. The gap remains a valid description of where HiED fails, but it is a system-level empirical finding rather than a universal ceiling on transcript-only prediction.

The lexical result has its own boundary. Synthetic corpora may contain generator-specific wording and label cues, and the cross-corpus classifier results show that fitted lexical signals do not transfer reliably. Transcript-only clinician evaluation and real-patient data are needed to distinguish model limitation, corpus shortcut, and genuine information insufficiency.

\section{Retrieval, Model Size, and Cross-Corpus Transfer}

On the internal test set, retrieval improves Single relative to no retrieval but does not produce a statistically supported gain for HiED. One possible explanation is architectural: retrieved demonstrations affect the Diagnostician, while the Criterion Checkers and finalization rules remain unchanged. Better demonstrations may improve direct diagnosis without resolving the later comparison among several compatible candidates. Global and parent-balanced retrieval also differ in example count, label composition, ordering, and prompt length, so their contrast compares complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating a balancing effect.

The ranking-to-commitment pattern also persists across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes. Increasing scale within this family substantially changes Single performance, while the HiED Top-1 and Top-3 ranges are narrower. This is evidence about scale sensitivity within Qwen3, not robustness across unrelated model families.

On the unified 925-case MDD-5k trace, the genuine Top-3 reaches 0.8508 while the committed Top-1 reaches 0.5708. This reproduces ranking headroom under synthetic corpus shift, but MDD-5k does not provide the same criterion-retention decomposition. Supporting lexical and BGE-M3 case-neighbor label-voting analyses also transfer poorly across corpora. The planned six-condition MDD retrieval matrix remains necessary before retrieval-policy transfer can be interpreted directly.

\section{Why the Tested Repair Methods Did Not Improve Selection}

The repair experiments test distinct explanations under different execution contracts. NtS and deterministic fusion reuse recorded upstream outputs. The forced pairwise-override artifact, independent pairwise reasoning, debate, and self-consistency instead evaluate complete configurations that may change ranking, generation, sampling, calls, or context. Supporting case-neighbor analyses ask whether labels from semantically similar training cases provide a transferable diagnostic signal; the standalone voting and fusion phases are kept separate.

None of the tested methods provides a clear, statistically supported, and attributable improvement under its predefined comparator. A conservative pairwise gate produces no committed flips, while the complete forced pairwise-override configuration and deterministic fusion are harmful; the larger-context debate configuration is also lower than DA. The available internal self-consistency evidence is summary-level and therefore does not support the same paired conclusion. These results do not establish that the broader method families cannot work; they show that the tested thresholds, votes, overrides, and additional reasoning calls do not combine the available signals well enough.

\section{Model Error Versus Missing Diagnostic Information}

The experiments cannot fully separate two causes of error. The transcript may contain sufficient information that the model uses poorly, or it may omit facts needed for a unique primary diagnosis. The 284 internal cases in which a gold parent is in the Top-3 but not selected show that useful candidate generation has already occurred in many errors. Broad confirmed sets and frequent \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} states are consistent with unresolved discrimination, but they do not prove that clinical information was absent; ambiguity, conflicting evidence, and checker error are also possible. The stronger TF--IDF baseline is an important counterexample to a universal missing-information account, although synthetic lexical shortcuts may contribute.

A transcript-only clinician study is needed to separate these causes. Psychiatrists should receive the same transcript and diagnostic scope as the system. If clinicians also disagree or judge the evidence insufficient, the task definition or benchmark label may be the main limit. If clinicians consistently resolve cases that HiED misses, the method is the more likely source of error.

\section{Relation to Prior Psychiatric and Multi-Agent Systems}

Interactive systems such as MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, and DSM5AgentFlow can ask additional questions and therefore address evidence acquisition as well as diagnosis~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. HiED instead fixes the transcript so that ranking, compatibility, and commitment can be compared under identical available evidence. This controlled setting improves failure localization but cannot resolve evidence that the transcript never provides.

Fixed-record systems such as MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels demonstrate diagnosis and structured reasoning from available records~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. HiED's narrower contribution is to expose and evaluate candidate generation, criterion-level compatibility, and final commitment on the same cases. Its audit trace has demonstrated analytical value for failure localization even without a Top-1 gain. Whether the trace improves clinician review, efficiency, or decision quality remains an empirical clinical question.

Structured-knowledge systems such as MedKGI and DKEC motivate richer relations among symptoms, diagnoses, and missing information~\citep{wang2025medkgi,ge2024dkec}. HiED's independent criterion rules behave as a high-retention compatibility audit but not as an effective primary selector. This result supports studying relational representations that compare temporal course, exclusions, causal direction, and unresolved conflicts rather than independently counting support for each candidate.

\section{Implications for System Design and Clinical Use}

HiED records ranked candidates, criterion states, linked evidence, rule-confirmed diagnoses, and finalization source. These artifacts make disagreements inspectable, but they neither reveal hidden reasoning nor establish that the checker is clinically correct. The demonstrated contribution is analytical traceability, not clinical benefit or calibrated trust.

One possible next step is a structured diagnostic graph whose nodes represent diagnoses, symptoms, episodes, and findings, and whose edges represent temporal order, support, contradiction, exclusion, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity. Its purpose would be to compare which candidate explains the overall presentation with fewer unresolved conflicts, not merely to add another knowledge source. This remains a proposed direction and is not evaluated in the present thesis.

Some cases may instead require new evidence. When duration, course, substance use, exclusion, or episode history remains unresolved, a future system should identify the discriminating fact, ask a focused follow-up question when interaction is possible, or abstain. Comparative selection is relevant when evidence is sufficient; targeted evidence acquisition is needed when it is not.

Finally, Taiwan is the intended setting, not a validated population. All quantitative evidence comes from synthetic Chinese corpora. The thesis does not test real Taiwanese patients, local language use, hospital workflow, clinician behavior, or patient outcomes. HiED should therefore be treated as a research framework for auditable analysis, not an autonomous diagnostic tool.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

\section{Construct Validity and Measurement Limitations}

The evaluation target is agreement with benchmark gold labels after parent-level projection. It is not an independent judgment of the patient's true diagnosis. Parent projection makes two corpora easier to compare but removes subtype detail. Top-1 Accuracy with a gold-label set is also less strict than matching only the first listed diagnosis. Top-1 Accuracy, Top-3 Accuracy, Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 therefore measure different parts of benchmark performance; none alone measures clinical correctness or safety.

The selection bottleneck is an observed difference among output views, not a proven causal process inside the model. Under Direct-Answer, the primary diagnosis is chosen before the checker creates the audit trace. Top-1 Accuracy and Top-3 Accuracy use all 1,000 test cases, whereas gold-label retention uses 915 checker-eligible cases. These measures help locate errors but should not be read as one nested funnel. The oracle upper-bound recovery analysis is likewise an upper bound, not a deployable selector.

\paragraph{Lexical baseline and construct validity.} The strong internal TF--IDF result may reflect real transcript signal, source-corpus lexical regularities, synthetic-generator artifacts, or a mixture of all three. It narrows the interpretation of the LLM selection bottleneck but does not establish that a lexical classifier is clinically superior or transferable. Conversely, because this stronger same-split baseline exists, HiED errors alone cannot establish that primary diagnosis is intrinsically unrecoverable from a transcript.

\section{Internal Validity and Statistical Limitations}

The official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable, so the main in-domain result uses a 1,000-case held-out split from the original training-source corpus. The cases were excluded from retrieval and development, but the split still shares templates and source distribution with the training data. The thesis does not report a full template-level or embedding near-duplicate audit, and foundation-model exposure to the public corpus cannot be ruled out.

The public validation split is used repeatedly for prompt, rule, sensitivity, and retrieval-configuration selection. Its results are configuration-selection evidence rather than an independent test estimate. The separate internal split reduces direct selection bias in the main estimate, but repeated use of one public split may still favor choices adapted to its corpus-specific patterns.

Not every comparison isolates one component. Single versus HiED changes several modules and measures a package effect. Common-upstream finalization methods hold recorded DA outputs fixed more closely, whereas independent pairwise reasoning, debate, self-consistency, and case-neighbor methods change calls, sampling, context, or input signals. Their results compare the tested configurations rather than establish a causal ranking of method families.

The debate comparison also differs in inference budget. The reference Direct-Answer pipeline uses an 8,192-token context window, whereas the complete advocate--judge debate requires 24,576 tokens and additional model calls. Its result therefore evaluates the full debate configuration rather than the isolated effect of debate under a matched context or compute budget.

Several analyses are exploratory or use small samples. Rare classes have wide uncertainty, and a non-significant difference does not prove equivalence. Holm correction is applied within the predefined families in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}, not across every analysis in the thesis. Oracle, lexical, BGE-M3, scope, and repair probes therefore remain supporting or exploratory evidence even when an isolated result is favorable.

NtS is derived deterministically from the recorded DA ranking, criterion states, and rule-confirmed set without additional LLM calls. This isolates the specified primary-selection rule more closely than a newly generated pipeline run, but NtS emits only one label. Its Top-1 supports a paired primary-selection analysis, while Exact Match and classwise F1 conflate re-selection with output cardinality. The comparison remains an offline benchmark analysis and does not establish prospective behavior or clinical benefit.

Complete internal case-level sampling traces were not retained for SC and CISC. Those results provide weaker auditability than methods with case-level predictions and are treated as descriptive supporting evidence rather than paired repair evidence.

The canonical system also depends on one model family, prompt set, quantized checkpoint, serving stack, context window, and decoding setup. The Qwen3 size analysis does not establish that the same findings hold for unrelated models or runtimes.

\section{External Validity and Clinical Generalizability}

Both quantitative corpora are synthetic. They support controlled and reproducible experiments, but may contain generator-specific language, simplified ambiguity, limited comorbidity, and less noise than real practice. MDD-5k provides a useful distribution shift, but it is still synthetic and does not provide the same verification analysis as the internal set.

No psychiatrist comparator is available under the same transcript-only, configured-scope, and no-follow-up-question constraints. The study therefore cannot determine whether a unique primary is recoverable by clinicians from each transcript or whether some model--gold-label disagreements reflect task ambiguity.

The intended setting is Taiwanese outpatient psychiatry, yet the study does not evaluate real Taiwanese patients, Traditional-Chinese clinical speech, mixed Mandarin--Taiwanese language, local coding practice, or hospital workflow. The configured diagnostic scope also maps unsupported diagnoses to \texttt{Others}. Expanding the list of legal outputs does not by itself teach the model when to use new labels, as shown by the scope-expansion analysis.

No result in this thesis establishes clinical effectiveness, clinician equivalence, treatment benefit, or safe autonomous use. There is no tested clinical abstention or unknown-diagnosis detector, calibrated confidence, subgroup fairness analysis, workflow study, or measurement of automation bias. Runtime cost, per-case token use, and latency were not evaluated as a formal workflow endpoint. Real-patient validation and operational evaluation must therefore precede any deployment claim.

\section{Reproducibility, Governance, and Deployment Boundaries}

Computational reproducibility requires the datasets, exact checkpoint and quantization, retrieval index, configuration, serving environment, structured-decoding implementation, and evaluation code. Greedy decoding reduces sampling variation, but exact identity may still depend on model revision, serving-library version, hardware, and schema recovery. Analytical reproducibility additionally requires case-level predictions, label projection, endpoint definitions, and the declared paired-comparison families. Complete internal SC/CISC traces are a known exception.

Neither form of reproducibility establishes clinical validity. An exactly reproduced criterion state can still be clinically wrong, and a reproduced benchmark gain does not establish workflow value, safety, or patient benefit.

Future work with patient data will require institutional approval, privacy controls, access logging, monitoring, incident procedures, and periodic revalidation. Until these requirements and the clinical studies above are completed, HiED should remain a research and review prototype.

\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis introduced HiED, a hybrid multi-agent framework that separates ranked differential generation, criterion-level evidence checking, deterministic compatibility aggregation, and primary-diagnosis commitment.

The same-split lexical baseline achieved higher Top-1 performance than HiED on the internal benchmark. HiED nevertheless showed substantially higher ranked candidate coverage than committed Top-1 performance, and criterion checking frequently retained a gold diagnosis without determining which compatible diagnosis should be primary. Criterion-derived Nominate-then-Select finalization and the tested pairwise, debate, fusion, and sampling-based repair configurations did not provide a clear and reproducible improvement in primary selection; supporting case-neighbor analyses likewise transferred poorly. Retrieval improved the Single system, but the results did not support an advantage for parent-balanced over global retrieval or a statistically supported retrieval gain for HiED. The ranking-to-commitment gap also remained visible on the external synthetic corpus.

HiED's contribution is therefore not state-of-the-art benchmark accuracy, but an auditable architecture and evaluation framework that localizes disagreement across candidate generation, evidence verification, and final commitment. The results are limited to synthetic benchmark data and do not establish clinical validity or autonomous diagnostic safety.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}

This section summarizes how the final experiments answer the five research questions introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:related}. Each answer is limited to benchmark performance on synthetic Chinese psychiatric transcripts and should not be interpreted as evidence of clinical effectiveness.

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] HiED did not outperform the strongest same-split baseline under the common evaluation contract. The TF--IDF and logistic-regression classifier achieved higher Top-1 Accuracy, and the paired difference remained significant after correction for multiple comparisons. HiED instead provided ranked differential diagnoses, criterion-level evidence records, and explicit finalization artifacts. Its value therefore lies in decomposing the diagnostic process rather than maximizing benchmark accuracy alone.

\item[RQ2] The largest observed discrepancy lies between candidate or compatibility availability and final commitment. Gold diagnoses were frequently present in the genuine ranked output and retained under the configured compatibility rules, yet another diagnosis was committed as primary. This supports a ranking-to-commitment bottleneck in HiED under the benchmark protocol, but it does not establish the clinically correct primary diagnosis or a universal causal bottleneck in transcript-based diagnosis.

\item[RQ3] Retrieval improved the Single system relative to no retrieval, but global and parent-balanced retrieval did not differ significantly. HiED showed no statistically supported retrieval gain across the tested strategies. Because the strategies also differed in the number and composition of examples supplied to the model, the results do not establish an independent benefit of parent balancing.

\item[RQ4] NtS reduced Top-1 Accuracy relative to Direct-Answer across all three retrieval conditions, and the complete forced pairwise-override configuration was harmful. The tested selection rules did not improve primary commitment under their respective evaluation contracts. Because NtS emits one diagnosis, its set-level metrics also reflect output cardinality.

\item[RQ5] The ranking-to-commitment gap remained visible across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k, so the pattern was not confined to one model size or synthetic corpus. Expanding the configured diagnostic scope made additional diagnoses representable but recovered none of the tested newly representable cases. Errors also concentrated in a small number of diagnostic pairs, especially depression--anxiety confusions. These findings support robustness only within the tested synthetic datasets, Qwen3 family, and configured analyses; they do not establish cross-model or clinical generality.
\end{description}

\section{Future Work}

\subsection{Clinician Validation of Diagnostic Labels and Criterion Audits}

Future work should first validate both the benchmark target and the criterion-level audit. Psychiatrists should review the same transcripts and diagnostic scope provided to the system, indicate whether one primary diagnosis is adequately supported, record acceptable alternatives, and identify information that is missing for differential diagnosis. A separate annotation study should evaluate the Criterion Checker's evidence states and linked transcript spans. Inter-rater agreement, insufficient-information judgments, and adjudication outcomes should be reported separately so that disagreement is not automatically treated as annotation error. These analyses would help distinguish errors caused by the model, incomplete transcripts, ambiguous dataset labels, or incorrect criterion judgments.

\subsection{Comparative Selection with Structured Diagnostic Knowledge}

The current criterion layer evaluates each diagnosis independently and therefore represents compatibility more directly than comparative primacy. A stronger selector should explicitly model relations among diagnoses, symptoms, temporal course, exclusion conditions, causal explanations, and comorbidities. One possible direction is a structured diagnostic knowledge graph in which nodes represent diagnoses, symptoms, episodes, and clinical findings, while edges represent temporal order, support, contradiction, exclusion, and explanatory dependence. The selector could then compare which candidate explains the overall presentation with the fewest unresolved conflicts, rather than selecting the diagnosis with the largest number of independently satisfied criteria. This approach should be evaluated against the current criterion-count and finalization rules under the same candidate rankings and case sets.

\subsection{Targeted Evidence Acquisition and Abstention}

Some cases may remain ambiguous because the transcript does not establish duration, onset, previous episodes, substance use, medical causes, or the relationship between concurrent syndromes. In these cases, applying another ranking rule may not be sufficient. Future systems should identify the unresolved fact that would best distinguish the leading candidates, ask a targeted follow-up question when interaction is possible, or abstain from committing to one diagnosis when the available evidence is insufficient. Conversational diagnostic systems show that LLMs can collect history~\citep{tu2025conversational}; the value of each question should be evaluated by whether it reduces diagnostic ambiguity rather than only by conversational fluency.

\subsection{Prospective Clinical Evaluation in Taiwan}

After the target labels, criterion audit, and final system configuration have been validated, HiED should be evaluated prospectively on de-identified Taiwanese psychiatric consultation transcripts. The inclusion criteria, primary and secondary outcomes, system configuration, subgroup analyses, and statistical plan should be specified before outcome analysis and publicly registered when appropriate. The evaluation should distinguish the recorded clinical diagnosis, transcript-only clinician review, and fuller clinical adjudication. Subsequent studies should examine workflow value, subgroup performance, automation bias, privacy, monitoring, and patient safety before any deployment claim is made.

\bigskip
\noindent A system may rank a benchmark-supported diagnosis as plausible without committing to it as primary. HiED makes this distinction observable by separating candidate ranking, criterion-level compatibility assessment, and final commitment. The next step is not simply to add more agents, but to validate the evidence representation, model comparative diagnostic relations more explicitly, and evaluate the resulting system on real Taiwanese clinical data.

\section{Broad Supported-Cluster Oracle}

The broad close-cluster oracle identifies 349 misclassified internal cases whose gold parent occurs in any supported cluster. \section{Sampling-Based Repair Evidence Boundary}

Internal self-consistency and confidence-informed self-consistency results are retained only as descriptive supporting analyses because complete case-level samples, seed schedules, and aggregation records are unavailable. They are not included in the paired repair table and do not support case-level claims comparable to methods with retained predictions. External sampling runs use their separately specified execution contracts and are interpreted as complete configurations rather than isolated selector changes.
