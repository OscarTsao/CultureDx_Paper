\chapter*{中文摘要}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{中文摘要}
初診精神科晤談逐字稿中的鑑別診斷，本質上是一種在證據不完整條件下進行的比較式決策。多個疾病診斷可能同時與同一份逐字稿相容，但最終通常只能有一個被判定為主診斷。然而，用以區分病程、發病時序、排除病因與共病關係等關鍵鑑別資訊，未必會在單次初診晤談中被完整引出。大型語言模型雖然能流暢地閱讀並理解這類逐字稿，近期也已有大量研究嘗試將其應用於精神科診斷任務，但語言理解的流暢性並不等同於可靠的鑑別診斷能力。

既有評測呈現出一致的落差：模型在粗粒度分類任務上往往表現良好，但一旦進入細粒度的疾病層級鑑別診斷，準確率便會大幅下降。這個落差促使本研究提出一套可稽核的系統架構，用以明確定位錯誤發生於診斷流程中的哪一個階段。模型可能生成一個合理且受到診斷準則支持的候選疾病，卻仍未能將其正確選為主診斷。候選診斷生成、依準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇三者之間的差異，通常會被單一準確率指標所掩蓋，使研究者難以判斷系統究竟在哪個環節失敗。

為了使這些不同的失敗階段能夠被分別觀察，並檢驗主診斷選擇是否構成主要瓶頸，本研究提出 HiED——一套面向中文精神科初診對話逐字稿的鑑別診斷框架。HiED 將候選診斷偵測、ICD-10 逐準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇保留為可分別分析的階段，而非將整個流程簡化為單一分類事件。同時，系統會在每次預測時輸出逐準則的稽核軌跡，使研究者能夠檢視診斷依據並定位系統失敗的位置。HiED 並非已完成臨床驗證的自主診斷工具，而是一項用於分析與檢驗逐字稿式精神科診斷失敗模式的研究工具。

因此，本論文有兩項主要貢獻：其一，提出一套可稽核的失敗定位框架，使上述各診斷階段得以分別觀察；其二，提供可重現的證據，指出在本研究評估的 HiED 設定與合成逐字稿基準評測中，主診斷選擇構成主要的失敗瓶頸。換言之，在本研究之合成逐字稿設定下，系統的主要限制不僅在於能否生成合理的候選診斷，而更在於能否在多個皆具一定準則支持的候選之間，進行有效比較並判定最適合作為主診斷者。最後需說明，本文題目所稱之臺灣脈絡，指涉系統之設計動機與預期部署場域（繁體中文互動、本地端推論、面向臨床人員之稽核），而非已完成之臺灣真實病人臨床驗證；本文現階段的量化結論均來自合成中文逐字稿語料。臺灣真實病人資料之臨床驗證目前於長庚紀念醫院進行中，其結果不納入本論文現階段的量化結論。

\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial-consultation psychiatric transcript is a comparative decision under incomplete evidence. Multiple disorders may be compatible with the same transcript, yet only one can typically be judged the primary diagnosis. Key discriminating information---illness course, onset timing, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity relationships---may not be fully elicited within a single initial consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, and recent research has applied them to psychiatric diagnostic tasks, but linguistic fluency does not equate to reliable differential diagnostic ability.

Existing evaluations show a consistent gap: models perform well on coarse-grained classification, but accuracy drops sharply on fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis. This gap motivates an auditable architecture that locates the stage at which a diagnostic error occurs. A model may generate a plausible disorder supported by diagnostic criteria, yet fail to select it as the primary diagnosis. The distinction among candidate-diagnosis generation, criterion-based verification, and primary-diagnosis selection is usually obscured by a single accuracy metric.

To make these failure stages separately observable and to examine whether primary-diagnosis selection is a major bottleneck, we propose HiED, a differential-diagnosis framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts. HiED retains candidate detection, ICD-10 criterion verification, and primary selection as separately analyzable stages rather than reducing diagnosis to one classification event. A criterion-level audit trace records evidence and failure locations for each prediction. HiED is a research instrument for analyzing transcript-based diagnostic failures, not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic tool. The Taiwanese context in the title denotes the intended deployment setting. Real-patient validation in Taiwan is ongoing at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital (CGMH) and is not included in the present quantitative conclusions.

This thesis makes two main contributions: an auditable failure-localization framework and reproducible evidence of a primary-selection bottleneck in the evaluated HiED configurations under synthetic transcript-only benchmarks. In these settings, the observed limitation lies not only in generating plausible candidates, but in comparing criterion-supported alternatives and selecting the most appropriate primary diagnosis.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric outpatient care in Taiwan is widely accessible, but this access also creates a heavy clinical workload. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit different levels of medical facilities without referral, and large medical centers may receive more than ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in one day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A first psychiatric visit must cover history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and an initial diagnostic judgment within limited time. The resulting note or transcript often contains incomplete information about onset, duration, course, medical or substance-related causes, and previous treatment.

The evidence may also be indirect. In Chinese-speaking settings, patients often describe distress through insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, or gastrointestinal symptoms before using explicit mood or anxiety terms~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. Taiwanese consultations may also mix Mandarin, Taiwanese expressions, and local clinical wording. The same condition can therefore be described in several ways within one visit.

Differential diagnosis remains difficult because common psychiatric disorders share many symptoms. Depression, anxiety, stress-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, somatic symptom disorders, and sleep disorders may all involve insomnia, fatigue, poor concentration, and impaired daily function. In diagnostic assessment, these symptoms are interpreted through disorder-specific criteria. These criteria do not treat every symptom independently or equally: depending on the disorder, a diagnosis may require particular core symptoms, a minimum number of additional symptoms, a specified duration, functional impairment, and the absence of exclusionary causes~\citep{who2019icd10}. Diagnostic assessment therefore involves matching the available evidence to a disorder-specific combination of requirements, rather than assigning a diagnosis from the presence of one symptom alone. Even when diagnostic criteria are applied, several disorders may remain compatible with the same transcript. Evidence for both depression and anxiety, for example, may reflect comorbidity or overlapping symptoms from one primary condition~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}. Even structured diagnostic studies report limited agreement for some common disorders~\citep{regier2013dsm5}.

These features make first-visit psychiatric diagnosis a problem of comparative bookkeeping under incomplete evidence. The clinician must document a best-supported working diagnosis, keep plausible alternatives visible, and identify which missing information should be collected at follow-up. Time pressure can increase the risk of anchoring or premature closure when several disorders share the same symptoms~\citep{croskerry2003cognitive}.
\section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

Large language models offer capabilities that are well aligned with psychiatric transcript analysis: they can process free-form dialogue, organize symptom evidence, retrieve relevant examples, and produce structured outputs. Medical multi-agent systems can further divide the diagnostic task among specialized roles and combine their outputs~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. However, these capabilities do not by themselves ensure reliable differential diagnosis from a fixed transcript.\ct{Does our model deal with all these problems?}

A final diagnostic error may arise for different reasons. A clinically relevant diagnosis may never be proposed as a candidate, the available transcript may not provide sufficient criterion-level support for that diagnosis, or the diagnosis may remain plausible but lose the final comparison against competing alternatives. These failure modes require different remedies, but they are usually collapsed into a single final-accuracy score.

This creates the central technical problem studied in this thesis: existing evaluation often does not reveal whether disagreement arises during candidate generation, criterion-based verification, or primary-diagnosis selection. Without separately observable outputs for these stages, it is difficult to determine whether better retrieval, evidence checking, or comparative selection is needed. The next section introduces HiED as a framework designed to make these distinctions observable.\ct{what problem?}

\section{Overview of HiED and Study Scope}

To make these distinctions observable, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid multi-agent system for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis from initial-consultation transcripts. The intended context is Taiwanese outpatient care. The completed quantitative results reported in this thesis are based on synthetic Chinese transcript datasets. Two complementary clinical studies are ongoing: a 20-case pilot psychiatrist review of selected LingxiDiag disagreements and a real-world system--physician agreement study using CGMH data. Their populations, reference standards, and outcomes are analyzed separately, and no incomplete results are included in the present conclusions.

HiED consists of five functional components. First, the \emph{Transcript Input} component preserves speaker labels and turn order while applying the predefined prompt-budget rule. Second, the \emph{Similar-Case Retriever} optionally retrieves relevant examples from the training corpus and supplies them only to the Diagnostician. Third, the \emph{Diagnostician Agent} produces a ranked list of five candidate diagnoses, one primary diagnosis selected from the three highest-ranked candidates, and at most one optional comorbid diagnosis. Fourth, a separate \emph{Criterion Checker Agent} evaluates each configured diagnostic category against its operationalized ICD-10 criteria. For every criterion, the checker records whether the transcript provides supporting evidence (\texttt{met}), contradictory evidence (\texttt{not\_met}), or insufficient evidence to make either judgment (\texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}). Finally, the deterministic \emph{Compatibility Auditor} applies the predefined operational rules to these criterion states and forms the criterion-compatible set.

The ranked candidates, emitted primary diagnosis, optional comorbid diagnosis, criterion states, and criterion-compatible set are retained as separate system outputs. Direct-Answer preserves the Diagnostician's emitted primary diagnosis and optional comorbidity, whereas Nominate-then-Select uses the same recorded ranking and compatibility results to select a single committed primary diagnosis. Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} describes the architecture in detail.

These outputs expose three analyzable stages. \emph{Candidate detection} asks whether a benchmark diagnosis appears in the ranked Top-3. \emph{Criterion verification} evaluates each configured diagnosis against its disorder-specific criteria; diagnoses that satisfy the study's operational compatibility rules are retained in the \emph{criterion-compatible set}. \emph{Primary selection} asks which candidate is selected as the primary diagnosis; its observed output is the \emph{committed primary diagnosis}.

Throughout this thesis, \emph{primary selection} denotes the scientific task of choosing one primary diagnosis among plausible candidates. \emph{Finalization} denotes the system-level process that converts the recorded ranking, checker states, and compatibility outputs into the final output; Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select are finalization policies. \emph{Commitment} is reserved for the resulting output state, as in \emph{committed primary diagnosis} or \emph{committed Top-1 agreement}, rather than used as the name of a separate diagnostic stage.

We use the term \emph{primary-selection bottleneck} for the operational pattern in which a benchmark diagnosis is present among leading candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but the system selects a different committed primary diagnosis. The bottleneck is a benchmark-local pattern showing that candidate coverage or criterion compatibility can exceed committed Top-1 agreement; it is not a claim about the true clinical primary diagnosis. It does not show that every error passes both earlier checks, that the corpus identifies a unique clinical primary diagnosis, or that every transcript contains enough evidence for one.

This distinction matters because different errors need different remedies. Missing candidates call for better generation or retrieval. A candidate that fails criterion checking points to the checker, the rules, or missing evidence. A candidate that remains available but is not selected points to the final comparison among plausible diagnoses. The detailed metric definitions are given in \Cref{sec:gold-label-conventions}.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions.

First, it presents an auditable hybrid architecture that combines LLM-based diagnosis, structured criterion-level checking against operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria, and deterministic compatibility rules. The system records criterion states, checker-generated supporting text, and compatibility-rule outcomes instead of relying only on a free-form rationale. The trace can be inspected and replayed, but it does not prove that the LLM's hidden reasoning is faithful or that the checker judgments are clinically correct~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}.

Second, it presents a stage-wise failure-localization and empirical evaluation framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection, quantifies the primary-selection bottleneck, and tests whether criterion-based finalization and several primary-selection methods close the observed gap. The absence of improvement under the evaluated selection interventions narrows several simple explanations under the tested settings, but it does not show that better selectors or additional clinical information can never help.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

Psychiatric LLM studies cover several different tasks, including examination questions, short vignettes, diagnostic reasoning, and comparison with clinicians~\citep{qiu2023largeai,li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. These studies show that LLMs can produce plausible clinical answers, but performance depends on the input, label space, and reference standard. Detecting depression from text or audio is also different from choosing one primary diagnosis among several overlapping disorders~\citep{li2025addreview}.

Chinese psychiatric datasets make disorder-level evaluation possible. LingxiDiagBench includes static diagnosis and dynamic consultation over the synthetic LingxiDiag-16K corpus and reports a clear drop from broad depression--anxiety classification to fine-grained diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. MDD-5k provides a second synthetic Chinese diagnostic-dialogue resource based on anonymized cases~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. These datasets support reproducible experiments, but they do not replace real-patient validation.

Existing systems also differ in whether they can ask questions. MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, and DSM5AgentFlow study inquiry or interactive diagnostic workflows~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels focus more on diagnosis from information already available in records or transcripts~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. Interactive systems can obtain missing evidence, while fixed-input systems isolate how well a model uses the same evidence. This thesis studies the fixed-input setting.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning}

Retrieval-augmented generation conditions a model on external records selected for the current query rather than relying only on information encoded in model parameters~\citep{lewis2020rag}. In-context-learning studies similarly show that semantically related demonstrations can be retrieved to construct a query-specific prompt and that performance depends on which examples are selected~\citep{liu2022incontext}. In transcript-based diagnosis, retrieved cases can therefore provide prompt-side examples of symptom descriptions and diagnostic labels. They are demonstrations rather than patient evidence and should not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

Similarity retrieval can expose useful corpus-local regularities, but it can also reproduce the frequency and wording patterns of the indexed data. Ordinary nearest-neighbor retrieval may repeatedly select examples from common labels, while a parent-balanced policy changes the label composition of the prompt. It may also change the number, ordering, and total token length of the retrieved examples. A comparison between global and parent-balanced retrieval consequently evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating balancing as a single causal factor.

These considerations motivate the retrieval experiments in this thesis. No retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval are compared using the same training-set retrieval index, evaluation cases, label projection, and scoring implementation. The comparison is repeated in both the Single and HiED pipelines so that retrieval behavior is not inferred from only one architecture.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

Medical multi-agent systems assign different roles to LLMs and combine their outputs through consultation, debate, or adaptive collaboration~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. AMIE and related work also show the value of broad differential diagnosis, realistic consultation, and expert review~\citep{mcduff2025amie,tu2025conversational}. These systems demonstrate that structured interaction can help, but adding agents does not guarantee a better final answer. Agents may repeat the same model bias, and free-form discussion can make it difficult to identify which component changed the decision.

For this thesis, the important issue is controlled attribution. If ranking, criterion checking, debate, and finalization all change at once, an accuracy difference cannot show which change helped. HiED therefore compares two policies that reuse the same ranked candidates and checker outputs: Direct-Answer keeps the diagnostician's decision, whereas Nominate-then-Select uses criterion compatibility to choose the primary.

\section{Auditability and Criterion Grounding}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

A fluent explanation is not proof of faithful reasoning. Model-generated chains can sound coherent while not reflecting the factors that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. In healthcare, explanation quality, interpretability, and clinical validity therefore need separate evaluation~\citep{rudin2019stop,huang2024xiai}.

Diagnostic-chain and symptom-based methods make outputs easier to inspect~\citep{chen2025cod,lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. However, a generated reasoning chain is still another model output. This thesis uses the narrower term \emph{auditability}: an external record that links transcript evidence, criterion states, rule results, and the final output. The record can be checked and replayed, but it does not reveal the model's hidden reasoning or turn checker outputs into clinical ground truth.

Constraint-logic systems are the closest related approach because they translate diagnostic criteria into explicit rules~\citep{kim2025clp}. HiED keeps the learned diagnostician separate from the criterion and rule layer. Under Direct-Answer, the structured layer audits the decision; under Nominate-then-Select, it is tested as a selector. Among the reviewed studies, we did not identify an evaluation that uses this separation to compare retention of a benchmark diagnosis with primary selection on the same Chinese psychiatric transcripts.

\section{Structured Diagnostic Knowledge and Primary Selection}

Knowledge-based diagnosis methods represent relations among symptoms, disorders, and missing information. MedKGI supports information-guided inquiry, and DKEC uses structured domain knowledge for label prediction~\citep{wang2025medkgi,ge2024dkec}. Such relations may help when disorders share symptoms but differ in onset, duration, exclusions, or causal direction.

HiED does not implement a full knowledge-graph selector. Instead, this literature motivates future work if simple criterion counts and local re-ranking are not enough. A stronger selector may need to compare which diagnosis best explains the full presentation, not only how many criteria each diagnosis satisfies.

\section{Research Gap and Research Questions}

Prior work shows that LLMs can diagnose psychiatric cases, retrieve examples, use diagnostic criteria, and work in multi-agent systems. Five gaps remain. First, conventional text classifiers, single LLMs, retrieval-augmented LLMs, and structured systems are not always compared under the same split and endpoint. Second, final accuracy does not show whether a benchmark diagnosis was absent from the candidate list or present but not selected as the committed primary. Third, global nearest-neighbor retrieval and parent-balanced retrieval require matched comparison across architectures. Fourth, criterion-based finalization and later primary-selection methods must be evaluated separately from candidate generation and retrieval. Fifth, it remains unclear whether the observed Top-3--Top-1 selection gap persists across model sizes, external corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope. The precise gap addressed here is that HiED retains candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection as separately observable stages evaluated on the same cases.

This thesis therefore uses five research questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?
\item[RQ2] How often is a benchmark diagnosis present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?
\item[RQ3] How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?
\item[RQ4] Do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve over Direct-Answer?
\item[RQ5] To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and what error patterns characterize the remaining failures?
\end{description}
\ct{Does your thesis really answer all 5 questions?}

RQ1 and RQ3 use matched comparisons on the same fixed internal test set, with common label projection and evaluation code. Clinical effectiveness and criterion-level clinical validity are not answered by the completed computational RQs; the LingxiDiag pilot review and CGMH real-world validation are separate ongoing clinical studies.

\section{Input and Diagnostic Scope}
\label{sec:input-diagnostic-scope}

HiED processes one Chinese psychiatric consultation transcript at a time. The input is an ordered sequence of turns, each marked as \texttt{Clinician} or \texttt{Patient}; speaker labels and turn order are preserved in the prompt. Transcripts longer than the available prompt budget are deterministically truncated by retaining complete turns from the beginning and end, with an explicit marker for the omitted middle. The system does not summarize or invent missing text. This is a fixed-input study: the system cannot ask the patient a follow-up question during inference.\ct{Is this about input and transcript format?}

ICD-10 is the diagnostic coding system used to name the disorders in this study~\citep{who2019icd10}. The system does not search all ICD-10 diagnoses. Before a run begins, it loads one fixed \emph{configured diagnostic profile} that is used for every patient. The main diagnostic profile contains fourteen configured diagnostic categories. F41 and F43 include subtype-level categories, so the fourteen categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels for scoring. Chapter~\ref{ch:data} defines how mixed in-scope and out-of-scope dataset-provided diagnosis codes are projected for evaluation.

The following terms define the diagnostic scope and criterion-checking process used in HiED. A \emph{configured diagnostic category} is an ICD-10 code that the Diagnostician may rank and for which the system provides a corresponding Criterion Checker. The fourteen category-specific checkers together form the \emph{criterion-checker set}.

For each criterion, the checker assigns one of three model-generated evidence states: \texttt{met} when the transcript provides supporting evidence, \texttt{not\_met} when it provides contradictory evidence, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the available information does not support either conclusion. The checker definitions and compatibility rules are study-specific operationalizations informed by ICD-10 descriptions. They do not reproduce the full official ICD-10 manual and have not been independently validated as clinical ground truth.

HiED is therefore a modular multi-stage LLM orchestration with role-specific agents and deterministic rule aggregation.

\section{Candidate Generation and Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

This section describes the two candidate-generation components shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}: the Similar-Case Retriever and the Diagnostician.

\paragraph{Similar-Case Retriever.}

The Similar-Case Retriever optionally selects training-source cases that are similar to the current transcript and supplies them to the Diagnostician as prompt-side demonstrations, following the retrieval-augmented generation pattern~\citep{lewis2020rag}. The study evaluates three conditions: no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Global Top-5 returns the five most similar eligible cases. Parent-balanced retrieval selects at most one example for each of the eleven configured scoring parents and can therefore return up to eleven examples.

The parent groups used for retrieval are fixed by the configured diagnostic profile rather than generated separately for each patient. Only the selected example within each group depends on the current transcript. Subtypes that share a scoring parent, such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2, also share one retrieval group. Parent-balanced retrieval may reduce repeated examples from frequent labels, but it also changes the number, label composition, ordering, and total token length of the demonstrations. The retrieval comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating balancing as a single causal factor.

Retrieved cases are provided only to the Diagnostician. They are not included in the Criterion Checker prompts, do not constitute evidence about the current patient, and do not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

\paragraph{Diagnostician.}

The Diagnostician receives the speaker-labeled transcript, the configured diagnostic profile, and any retrieved examples. It produces three separately recorded outputs: a ranked list of five candidate diagnoses, one emitted primary diagnosis selected from the three highest-ranked candidates, and at most one optional comorbid diagnosis. The ranked list represents the model's differential diagnosis, whereas the primary field records the diagnosis that becomes committed under the Direct-Answer policy.

The ranked candidates, emitted primary diagnosis, and optional comorbidity are stored separately. A diagnosis may therefore remain present among the leading candidates even when another diagnosis is emitted as primary. This distinction allows candidate coverage and primary selection to be evaluated independently.

\section{Criterion Verification and Compatibility Aggregation}

This section describes the two criterion-processing components shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}: the Criterion Checker and the Compatibility Auditor.

\paragraph{Criterion Checker.}

Each of the fourteen configured diagnostic categories has a corresponding Criterion Checker based on the study's operationalized ICD-10-informed criteria~\citep{who2019icd10}. Every checker receives the same speaker-labeled transcript together with the criteria defined for one diagnostic category. Retrieved examples are not included in checker prompts, and the checkers do not communicate with one another.

For each criterion, the checker assigns one of the three criterion states defined in Section~\ref{sec:input-diagnostic-scope}: \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}. It also returns a short supporting-text field, which may be \texttt{null} when no explicit supporting text is identified. Missing information is not negative evidence: an unresolved criterion remains \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} rather than \texttt{not\_met}. All fourteen configured categories are checked for every case, so criterion verification is not limited to diagnoses in the ranked Top-5.

\paragraph{Compatibility Auditor.}

The Compatibility Auditor is a deterministic module that converts criterion-level states into diagnosis-level compatibility decisions. For each configured diagnostic category, it applies the predefined operational rule associated with that category. Depending on the diagnosis, a rule may require particular core criteria, a minimum number of total criteria, specified criterion groups, or all listed requirements. A diagnosis is added to the \emph{criterion-compatible set} only when its configured rule is satisfied.

The auditor records the applied rule, the relevant criterion counts, and the resulting compatibility decision in the compatibility-rule trace. Because the aggregation is deterministic, the same checker states always produce the same criterion-compatible set. The complete category-specific rules are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:configured-profile}.

\paragraph{Interpretation boundary.}

Criterion states and supporting text are model-generated and have not been validated as clinician ground truth. The supporting text is retained for inspection but is not guaranteed to be a verbatim or span-aligned extract or a clinically validated evidence annotation. Criterion compatibility is an operational property under the study configuration; it does not establish that a diagnosis is clinically correct, uniquely supported, or the most appropriate primary diagnosis. Several diagnoses may therefore be retained in the criterion-compatible set for the same transcript.

\section{Primary Selection and Finalization}

This section describes the finalization policies and the Committed Diagnosis output shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline-thesis-rewrite}. Both policies are evaluated from the same recorded Diagnostician and criterion-processing outputs, but they use these outputs differently. Finalization does not introduce an additional LLM call.

\paragraph{Direct-Answer.}

Direct-Answer (DA) preserves the Diagnostician's emitted decision. The emitted primary field becomes the committed primary diagnosis; if this field is missing, the first-ranked candidate is used as a fallback. DA also retains at most one optional comorbid diagnosis when it differs from the primary. Criterion Checker states and Compatibility Auditor outputs remain available as audit artifacts but do not overwrite the DA committed diagnosis.

\paragraph{Nominate-then-Select.}

Nominate-then-Select (NtS) is a deterministic alternative that uses the recorded candidate ranking and criterion-compatible set to choose one committed primary diagnosis. For configured diagnostic category $d$, the criterion-support ratio is
\[
\operatorname{met\_ratio}(d)=\min\!\left(1,\frac{\text{number of \texttt{met} criteria for }d}{\text{required criterion count for }d}\right).
\]
The denominator is the minimum satisfying count defined by the configured operational rule rather than the total number of listed criteria. NtS first selects the highest-ranked criterion-compatible diagnosis within the ranked Top-5. If none of the ranked candidates is criterion-compatible, it selects from the remaining criterion-compatible diagnoses by descending $\operatorname{met\_ratio}$, with ties resolved by the predefined configured-category order. If the criterion-compatible set is empty, NtS falls back to the first-ranked diagnosis.

\paragraph{Output contracts.}

DA produces one committed primary diagnosis and may retain one optional comorbid diagnosis. NtS produces one re-selected committed primary diagnosis and does not emit a comorbid diagnosis. Thus, the two policies differ in both their primary-selection rule and final output cardinality. Additional primary-selection methods are defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental} and do not alter the core architecture described here.

\section{Configured Output Space and Gold-Label Projection}
\label{sec:gold-label-conventions}

Throughout this thesis, the dataset-provided diagnoses used for scoring are referred to as \emph{benchmark gold labels}. The term \emph{gold} denotes the official benchmark reference and does not imply a uniquely correct clinical diagnosis.

The main system uses fourteen configured ICD-10 diagnostic categories, listed in Table~\ref{tab:configured-profile-summary}. For evaluation, subtype-level categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels. Let $G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x)$ be the raw benchmark gold codes for case $x$, let $\pi$ map subtype codes such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2 to their scoring parent, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of eleven configured scoring parents. The in-scope gold-label set is
\[
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\{\pi(c):c\in G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x),\ \pi(c)\in\mathcal{C}\}.
\]
The scored gold-label set is then
\[
G(x)=
\begin{cases}
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x), & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)\neq\varnothing,\\
\{\mathrm{Others}\}, & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\varnothing.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus, a mixed case such as F90 with F41.1 is scored as $\{\mathrm{F41}\}$ rather than $\{\mathrm{Others},\mathrm{F41}\}$. A case is in scope when $G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)$ is nonempty and out of scope otherwise. Because the main system has neither an \emph{Others} output nor a trained unknown detector, it cannot correctly represent an out-of-scope case.

For case $x$, $G(x)$ is the deduplicated gold-label set after parent-level projection. LingxiDiag cases may contain more than one gold parent label, and neither dataset provides a psychiatrist-reviewed unique clinical primary diagnosis. The first-listed gold parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses such as the confusion matrix.

In the running example from Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}, F41.1 is scored as F41. If the gold-label set contains F41 and the system emits F32 while ranking F41.1 second, Top-1 is wrong but Top-3 is correct.

\section{Datasets, Case Construction, and Split Roles}

The study uses two synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogue datasets. LingxiDiagBench is the source corpus for the in-domain experiments. MDD-5k provides an external synthetic test set.

\subsection{LingxiDiagBench}

LingxiDiagBench contains synthetic Chinese psychiatric consultations generated for static diagnostic classification and interactive consultation tasks~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. It is used as the source corpus for the in-domain experiments because it provides a fixed diagnostic label space and sufficient cases for training, configuration selection, and held-out evaluation. Its published results also show that fine-grained disorder-level diagnosis is substantially more difficult than broad depression--anxiety classification.

The accessible release used in this study contained 14,000 training-source cases and 1,000 public-validation cases. The official test file was not available in the accessible release used when the experiments were designed. To obtain a held-out internal evaluation set, 1,000 cases were separated from the 14,000-case training source before the main experiments. The first-listed label was used only as a sampling variable and was not treated as a psychiatrist-validated clinical primary diagnosis. \section{Prediction Views and Metrics}

The evaluator keeps four recorded output views separate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item The \emph{primary view}, $p_1(x)$, is the committed primary diagnosis.
\item The \emph{ranked view}, $R_3(x)$, contains the first three distinct parent labels in the method's genuine ranked output. For HiED, it is taken from the recorded Diagnostician ranking and is independent of later DA or NtS finalization.
\item The \emph{compatibility view}, $L(x)$, is the set of scoring parent labels retained as criterion-compatible by the Compatibility Auditor under the configured operational rules.
\item The \emph{multilabel view}, $M(x)$, contains the committed primary diagnosis and any emitted comorbid parent label.
\end{enumerate}

TF--IDF + logistic regression obtains its Top-3 from ranked class scores. HiED obtains it from the Diagnostician ranking. A Single system without a genuine ranked output is not assigned an artificial Top-3.

Because a case may contain more than one gold label, Top-$k$ Accuracy counts a case as correct when at least one gold label appears among the first $k$ predictions. Exact Match, also called subset accuracy, requires the complete predicted label set to equal the complete gold-label set. These are benchmark-agreement measures, not independent measures of the patient's true clinical diagnosis.

DA may emit a committed primary diagnosis and one optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits one committed primary diagnosis. Their Top-1 comparison therefore evaluates the committed primary directly, but differences in Exact Match, Macro-F1, or Weighted-F1 also reflect output cardinality and cannot be interpreted as isolated effects of primary re-selection.

Top-3 and committed Top-1 are computed over all 1,000 internal test cases. Gold-label retention in the criterion-compatible set is computed over the 915 cases with at least one non-\emph{Others} gold parent covered by a corresponding Criterion Checker.

\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}1\ Accuracy}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}3\ Accuracy}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
\mathrm{Exact}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[M(x)=G(x)].
\]

Macro-F1 is the unweighted mean of the twelve one-vs-rest label-level F1 scores obtained by binarizing $G(x)$ and $M(x)$ over the fixed scoring classes. Weighted-F1 uses the same label-level F1 scores but weights each class by its gold-label support.

First-listed-label agreement compares the committed diagnosis only with the first-listed projected gold parent. It is used for the confusion matrix but not for the main result. It may differ from any-gold Top-1 Accuracy when a case contains more than one gold parent label.

\section{Failure-Localization Indicators}

The thesis defines three failure-localization indicators. Candidate detection and primary agreement are defined for all evaluation cases, whereas criterion retention is defined only for checker-eligible cases.

For case $x$,
\[
D_3(x)=\mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
R(x)=\mathbb{1}[L(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
S(x)=\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
where $L(x)$ is the set of scoring parent labels retained as criterion-compatible by the Compatibility Auditor. $D_3(x)$ indicates whether at least one benchmark gold parent appears in the genuine ranked Top-3, $R(x)$ indicates whether at least one benchmark gold parent is retained in the criterion-compatible set, and $S(x)$ indicates whether the committed primary diagnosis matches at least one benchmark gold parent.

$D_3$ and $S$ are computed over all 1,000 internal evaluation cases. $R$ is computed only for the 915 checker-eligible cases whose gold-label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} parent represented by a corresponding Criterion Checker. The resulting rates therefore use different denominators and should not be interpreted as consecutive stages of a single case-level funnel.

These indicators provide complementary views of the recorded outputs for the same case. They ask whether a benchmark gold parent was ranked, retained as criterion-compatible, and selected as the committed primary diagnosis; they do not imply that every finalization policy executes these operations in that chronological order.

\section{Statistical Analysis}

The case is the unit of analysis, and all statistical tests are two-sided with a significance level of $\alpha=0.05$. When confidence intervals are reported for a single binary proportion, 95\% Wilson intervals are used. Paired comparisons require identical case IDs and endpoint definitions. For binary correctness outcomes, the effect estimate is the paired difference in correctness proportions, and the exact McNemar test is computed from the two discordant counts: cases correct only under the first configuration and cases correct only under the second. Main paired comparisons report the effect estimate and its confidence interval; detailed discordant counts and $p$-values are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

Confidence intervals for paired accuracy differences and finalization difference-in-differences contrasts are estimated by resampling case IDs with replacement using 10,000 paired bootstrap replicates and seed 42. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles define the reported 95\% confidence interval. Reported $p$-values are interpreted together with effect direction, confidence intervals, and discordant counts rather than as standalone evidence.

Holm correction is applied when multiple prespecified tests belong to the same comparison family defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}; unrelated or isolated comparisons are not pooled into an artificial family. A non-significant result indicates insufficient evidence of a difference and is not interpreted as evidence of equivalence. When compared configurations also differ in context length, number of model calls, output contract, or other execution settings, the paired result is interpreted as a comparison of complete configurations rather than as the isolated effect of one component.

\section{Benchmark Positioning and Retrieval Comparisons}

This section defines the first evaluation block shown in Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-sequence}. The same-split system comparison addresses RQ1, while the matched retrieval comparison addresses RQ3. Both analyses use the parent-label projection and evaluation measures defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}.

\paragraph{Same-split benchmark comparison (RQ1).}
The main held-out comparison includes Majority, TF--IDF + LR, the validation-selected Single configuration, and the validation-selected HiED--DA configuration. All methods are evaluated on the same fixed 1,000-case internal set using the same parent-label projection and scoring implementation. Top-1 Accuracy is the primary predictive endpoint. Genuine Top-3 and multilabel measures are reported only when the corresponding output view is available.

The Majority baseline always predicts the most frequent scoring parent in the training source. TF--IDF + LR is trained on the 13,000 training-source cases using character-boundary 1--2-grams, at most 10,000 features, \texttt{min\_df=2}, \texttt{max\_df=0.95}, and sublinear term frequency. Twelve one-vs-rest logistic-regression classifiers use $C=1.0$, the L-BFGS solver, balanced class weights, and a 2,000-iteration limit; their probabilities define the ranking and thresholded multilabel prediction. Single uses one diagnostic LLM call and produces a committed primary diagnosis together with an optional set of comorbid diagnoses. These comorbid outputs are not elicited as an ordered differential, so a genuine Top-3 view is not assigned.

The comparison is matched at the level of evaluation cases, parent-label projection, and scoring code, but it is not a component-controlled ablation. The systems differ in training procedure, retrieval exposure, number of model calls, and output contract. RQ1 therefore compares complete diagnostic configurations rather than estimating the isolated effect of the multi-agent architecture.

\paragraph{Matched retrieval comparison (RQ3).}
Retrieval is evaluated separately within the Single and HiED--DA architectures under three conditions: no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval. Within each architecture, the model, prompt family, configured diagnostic scope, decoding settings, and output contract remain fixed.

The public-validation split is used to select the primary retrieval configuration separately for Single and HiED--DA according to the predefined promotion rule. The selected configurations are fixed before evaluation on the internal held-out set. Public-validation results are therefore used for configuration selection and are not interpreted as independent test estimates.

Global Top-5 and parent-balanced retrieval differ not only in label balance but also in the number, composition, ordering, and total token length of the retrieved examples. Their comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than the isolated causal effect of parent balancing.

\section{Stage-Wise Failure Analysis}

This section defines the stage-wise analysis used to answer RQ2 and corresponds to the second evaluation block in Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-sequence}. The primary analysis uses the validation-selected parent-balanced HiED--DA trace on the fixed 1,000-case internal held-out set because this run contains the complete ranked, criterion-checking, compatibility, and finalization outputs.

The analysis uses the three indicators defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}. $D_3$ records whether at least one benchmark gold parent appears in the genuine ranked Top-3, $R$ records whether at least one benchmark gold parent is retained in the criterion-compatible set, and $S$ records whether the committed primary diagnosis matches at least one benchmark gold parent. $D_3$ and $S$ are evaluated on all 1,000 internal cases, whereas $R$ is defined only for the 915 checker-eligible cases.

The joint $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis is therefore restricted to the checker-eligible subset. Of particular interest is the ranked-and-retained nonselection profile, defined by $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$: at least one benchmark gold parent is present in the genuine Top-3 and retained as criterion-compatible, but another diagnosis is selected as the committed primary. These indicators do not form a strictly nested runtime funnel because the Criterion Checker evaluates the full configured scope, not only the ranked Top-3.

A supplementary oracle analysis examines a broader candidate window formed from ranked and criterion-compatible diagnoses. Because the oracle uses the benchmark gold label to identify potentially recoverable cases and assumes that corrected cases can be changed without harming initially correct predictions, it is interpreted only as an optimistic, non-deployable upper bound and is not used as the primary answer to RQ2. The exact candidate filter, clustering threshold, and sensitivity settings are specified in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Primary-Selection Interventions}

This section defines the primary-selection experiments used to answer RQ4 and corresponds to the third evaluation block in Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-sequence}. Direct-Answer (DA) is the reference policy, and committed Top-1 agreement is the primary endpoint. The experiments test whether alternative uses of the available diagnostic outputs improve the committed primary diagnosis; they do not assume that any intervention is necessarily better than DA.

\paragraph{Evaluated intervention families.}
The evaluated methods represent three approaches to primary re-selection. First, \emph{recorded-output methods} reuse an existing HiED trace without additional LLM inference. NtS selects from the criterion-compatible set using the deterministic policy defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}, whereas deterministic fusion combines recorded candidate rank, compatibility status, and criterion-support strength before deciding whether to replace the DA primary.

Second, \emph{pairwise and debate methods} introduce new inference to compare plausible alternatives more directly. The pairwise configurations ask the model to choose between closely supported candidates. The independent configuration produces a separate committed prediction, whereas the forced-override configuration promotes a valid in-cluster preference when the predefined eligibility conditions are satisfied. Two-view debate generates competing diagnostic arguments and uses a judge call to select the final primary diagnosis.

Third, \emph{sampling-based methods} test whether repeated Diagnostician outputs yield a more stable decision. Self-consistency (SC) selects the diagnosis receiving the most primary votes across repeated samples. Confidence-informed self-consistency (CISC) uses the same samples but weights each vote by the confidence stated in its output.

These families test whether criterion-derived signals, direct comparison among candidates, or repeated sampling can reduce the observed primary-selection gap.

\paragraph{Comparison design.}
DA and NtS are compared on the same 1,000 cases under three retrieval settings: none, global Top-5, and parent-balanced. Paired differences estimate the effect of substituting NtS for DA, and bootstrap difference-in-differences contrasts test whether that effect varies by retrieval. Shared upstream artifacts permit relatively direct attribution to finalization.

Additional internal intervention results use the validation-selected parent-balanced DA configuration as the reported reference. Paired effects and confidence intervals are reported when case-level paired records are available. Methods that introduce new inference or sampling may also differ from DA in prompt structure, context length, number of model calls, decoding behavior, or aggregation procedure. Their results are therefore interpreted as comparisons between complete configurations rather than as isolated effects of pairwise comparison, debate, or sampling.

Although DA may retain an optional comorbid diagnosis whereas NtS emits one diagnosis, committed Top-1 remains directly comparable. Set-level metrics are not interpreted as isolated effects of primary re-selection because they also reflect output cardinality. Exact eligibility rules, thresholds, inference budgets, sampling settings, and supporting paired statistics are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Robustness and Transfer Analyses}

This section defines the analyses used to answer RQ5 and corresponds to the fourth evaluation block in Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-sequence}. The analyses examine whether the main in-domain findings remain observable across model sizes, a second synthetic corpus, changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and cross-corpus selectors. They evaluate persistence under the tested conditions rather than establishing general robustness or clinical transferability.

\paragraph{Model-scale sensitivity.}
Within-family scale sensitivity is evaluated using Qwen3-4B, 8B, 14B, and 32B on the same 1,000 internal cases. The prompt family, configured diagnostic scope, retrieval policy, decoding contract, and evaluation endpoints are held constant across sizes. Top-1, genuine Top-3, structured-output issues, and abstention events are recorded. This comparison tests whether the candidate-coverage and primary-selection pattern depends strongly on one Qwen3 model size; it does not establish robustness across unrelated model families.

\paragraph{External synthetic evaluation.}
MDD-5k is used to test whether the main in-domain patterns remain observable under a second synthetic data-generation process and different label distributions. The primary external analyses use the full 925-case evaluation set and the 878-case subset for which at least one benchmark gold diagnosis is represented in HiED's main configured output space. A separate 490-case F32-versus-F41 subset is treated as a secondary binary task rather than as the main external evaluation.

The common external DA trace supports multi-way performance, genuine Top-3 coverage, the Top-3--Top-1 gap, and same-trace primary-selection comparisons, but not criterion retention. Without retention, the external test of the full $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis is necessarily partial.

\paragraph{Diagnostic-scope expansion.}
A configuration-only scope experiment expands the diagnostic profile from fourteen to 33 configured categories by adding category-specific checker definitions without changing the model weights. The added categories are not enabled in the primary external evaluation. This experiment separates output-space representability from behavioral recovery: it asks whether making a benchmark diagnosis available to the system is sufficient for that diagnosis to become the committed primary output.

\paragraph{Cross-corpus selector transfer.}
Cross-corpus analyses test whether corpus-derived lexical signals remain useful outside the corpus from which they were obtained. TF--IDF classifiers are trained on one corpus and evaluated both within that corpus and on the other corpus. This analysis measures lexical selector portability rather than the clinical validity of the representation.

\paragraph{Error-pattern analysis.}
Remaining benchmark disagreements are examined through first-listed-label confusion patterns, frequent gold-to-predicted diagnosis pairs, and the stage-wise case profiles defined by $D_3$, $R$, and $S$. The confusion matrix is descriptive and uses one first-listed projected gold label per case; it is not the main multilabel endpoint. Criterion-state and audit-trace analyses further characterize recorded differences among candidate-detection, criterion-verification, and ranked-and-retained nonselection cases without assigning clinical causes to those disagreements.

\section{Complementary Clinical Evaluation Plan}
\label{sec:psychiatrist-case-review}
\label{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan}

The clinical evaluation comprises two separate ongoing studies with different objectives. The first is a pilot blinded psychiatrist review of selected LingxiDiag benchmark disagreements. The second is a real-world validation study using psychiatric data collected at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital (CGMH). Their datasets, sampling procedures, reference standards, and outcomes will be analyzed separately and will not be pooled into one agreement estimate.

\subsection{Pilot Psychiatrist Review on LingxiDiag}
\label{sec:lingxidiag-pilot-review}

The LingxiDiag pilot is an independent blinded dual psychiatrist review designed to characterize primary-selection disagreements and test the feasibility of the annotation procedure. It is not a real-patient clinical validation study. A fixed sample of twenty cases will be selected by simple random sampling without replacement from the 272 checker-eligible internal cases satisfying $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$. At least one benchmark gold parent is therefore ranked and retained as criterion-compatible, but another diagnosis is committed as primary. The sampling seed and selected case identifiers will be frozen before assessment; cases will not be chosen according to preliminary judgments or perceived clinical interest.

Two psychiatrists with different levels of clinical experience will review all twenty cases independently. Each reviewer will receive a separately randomized case order and remain blinded to the other reviewer's responses and to the computational stratum from which the cases were sampled.

The review uses staged disclosure. In the first stage, reviewers receive only the synthetic transcript and configured diagnostic scope. Benchmark labels, system outputs, the $D_3$--$R$--$S$ profile, and the other reviewer's judgments remain concealed. Reviewers record whether the transcript supports a unique primary diagnosis, a preferred primary diagnosis, plausible alternatives, and missing discriminative information. These judgments are locked before further disclosure.

In the second stage, reviewers inspect the ranked differential, criterion states, checker-generated supporting text, criterion-compatible diagnoses, and committed primary diagnosis while benchmark labels remain concealed. They assess the plausibility of the system outputs, consistency of the criterion-level trace with the transcript, and usefulness of the audit artifacts. These judgments are also locked.

In the final stage, benchmark labels are disclosed. Reviewers classify each disagreement as a likely primary-selection error, insufficient transcript information, multiple plausible primary diagnoses, benchmark-label ambiguity, criterion-verification error, diagnostic-scope limitation, or unresolved disagreement. Original independent judgments will be retained, and disagreements will be handled through a prespecified consensus or adjudication procedure after both reviews are complete.

The analysis will report transcript-sufficiency judgments, preferred diagnoses, disagreement categories, audit-trace usefulness, exact reviewer agreement, and an exploratory chance-corrected agreement coefficient. Because the sample contains twenty synthetic cases from one computational failure population, the findings will be interpreted as pilot expert characterization rather than a population-level estimate of clinical diagnostic accuracy.

\subsection{Ongoing CGMH Real-World Validation}
\label{sec:cgmh-validation}

A separate real-world validation study is ongoing using psychiatric data collected at CGMH. Unlike the LingxiDiag pilot, the CGMH cohort will follow prespecified clinical eligibility and inclusion procedures and will not be selected according to whether HiED agrees with an existing diagnosis. This design supports estimation of overall agreement and disagreement patterns on the eligible cohort rather than characterization of a preselected failure stratum.

HiED predictions will be compared separately with the recorded clinical diagnosis and with independent transcript-only assessments by two psychiatrists. The reviewers will assess the available transcript evidence independently under a blinded procedure, with disagreements handled through prespecified consensus or adjudication. Agreement with the recorded clinical diagnosis and agreement with transcript-only psychiatrist consensus will remain separate endpoints because the two references may reflect different information conditions.

Planned outcomes include committed-primary agreement, Top-3 coverage when defined, diagnostic-scope coverage, class-specific performance, system--physician agreement, inter-rater agreement, and the distribution of clinically reviewed disagreement categories. CGMH results will be reported separately from the LingxiDiag pilot. Data collection is ongoing, and no incomplete CGMH results are included in the substantive conclusions of this thesis.

Together, these designs distinguish benchmark positioning, stage-wise localization, intervention evidence, transfer, pilot expert characterization, and real-world agreement validation without treating them as equivalent claims.

Global Top-5 and parent-balanced retrieval also differ in the number, composition, ordering, and total token length of the retrieved examples. Their observed differences therefore reflect complete retrieval configurations and cannot be attributed solely to parent balancing.

\paragraph{Supplementary oracle analysis.}

A supplementary gold-informed oracle estimates an optimistic upper bound on primary-selection headroom. Criterion-supported candidates with a met-ratio of at least 0.5 are ordered by met-ratio. Adjacent candidates whose met-ratio difference is below $T=0.1$ are grouped into a close supported cluster. An initially incorrect case is counted as oracle-eligible when at least one benchmark gold parent appears in any such cluster.

Under this definition, 349 of the 1,000 cases are initially incorrect but contain a benchmark gold parent in a close supported cluster. If all 349 cases were corrected while all 518 initially correct cases remained unchanged, committed Top-1 agreement would reach the optimistic upper bound

\[
\frac{518+349}{1000}=0.867.
\]

This is not the performance of an implemented selector. The oracle uses the benchmark gold label to identify the preferred candidate, assumes that every eligible disagreement can be corrected, and assumes that no initially correct case is harmed. It is therefore a non-deployable, no-harm correction upper bound rather than an estimate of expected recoverable performance. The broader oracle candidate window also differs from the genuine Top-3 used to define the 284-case candidate-to-commitment gap. Full operational details and threshold sensitivity results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

The cross-corpus analysis asks whether lexical label signals learned from one synthetic corpus remain predictive on another. These analyses address different limitations and are therefore interpreted separately.

A system cannot select a diagnosis that it is not configured to emit.

\chapter{Stage-Wise Failure Analysis}
\label{ch:error}

This chapter extends the stage-wise localization reported in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} established where benchmark disagreements occur across candidate detection, criterion retention, and committed primary selection. The present chapter characterizes the cases within those recorded locations by examining candidate ranks, diagnostic confusion directions, criterion-state patterns, diagnostic-set errors, scope limitations, and selected audit traces.

The primary analysis uses the validation-selected parent-balanced HiED--DA trace on the fixed 1,000-case internal test set. Candidate-detection and diagnostic-set analyses use all 1,000 cases, whereas analyses involving gold-label retention use the 915 checker-eligible cases. External diagnostic-scope findings use separately defined MDD-5k populations.

Throughout this chapter, a \emph{failure} denotes disagreement with the projected benchmark gold labels at a recorded system stage. It does not establish a psychiatrist-confirmed diagnostic error or identify its clinical cause. The stages are also not a strict runtime funnel because criterion verification evaluates the full configured diagnostic scope rather than only the ranked Top-3.

\section{Failures before Primary Selection}

The joint $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis in Chapter~\ref{ch:results} established the overall locations of benchmark disagreement. This section further characterizes the cases in which disagreement is already present before committed primary selection. It examines candidate-detection failures and criterion-verification failures separately because they use different operational definitions and denominators.

\paragraph{Candidate-detection failures.}

A candidate-detection failure occurs when no benchmark gold parent appears in the genuine ranked Top-3, or $D_3=0$. This condition occurs in 198 of the 1,000 internal test cases. Because 85 of these cases are scored as \emph{Others} and do not have a corresponding gold-parent checker, the criterion-retention analysis is restricted to the remaining 113 checker-eligible cases.

Among these 113 cases, the full-scope Criterion Checker retains a benchmark gold parent in 79 cases even though that parent is absent from the Diagnostician's Top-3. The remaining 34 cases neither rank nor retain a benchmark gold parent. This pattern reflects the non-nested design of HiED: the Diagnostician produces a limited ranked differential, whereas criterion verification evaluates the complete configured diagnostic scope.

The 79 retained-but-not-ranked cases indicate that information associated with the benchmark diagnosis may still appear in the checker outputs even when candidate generation omits that diagnosis from the leading differential. However, retention outside the Top-3 does not by itself provide a deployable correction, because the committed DA primary must be selected from the Diagnostician's ranked candidates.

Across all 198 candidate-detection failures, the committed primary diagnosis is concentrated in F32 and F41, with 109 and 56 cases, respectively. This concentration describes the prediction destinations associated with candidate omission, but it does not establish why the benchmark diagnosis was omitted or whether the benchmark label was clinically preferable.

\paragraph{Criterion-verification failures.}

A criterion-verification failure occurs when a benchmark gold parent appears in the genuine Top-3 but is not retained in the criterion-compatible set, or $D_3=1$ and $R=0$. Twenty-four of the 915 checker-eligible cases satisfy this condition.

The committed primary diagnosis nevertheless matches a benchmark gold parent in twelve of these cases. These cases show that non-retention does not necessarily imply an incorrect committed primary under DA, because DA preserves the Diagnostician's emitted decision rather than replacing it with the criterion-compatible set. The remaining twelve cases have both gold-label non-retention and an incorrect committed primary; these cases form the criterion-verification failure group used in the mutually exclusive comparisons in Section~\ref{sec:ch8-criterion-evidence}.

Across the 36 gold-label-associated checker outputs in these 24 cases, 286 individual criterion states are recorded. Of these states, 17.1\% are \texttt{met}, 23.4\% are \texttt{not\_met}, and 59.4\% are \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}. These percentages describe diagnosis-by-criterion judgments rather than cases. The high unresolved share indicates that gold-label non-retention is frequently associated with unresolved criterion inputs under the model-generated checker trace. It does not establish that the relevant clinical information was truly absent from the transcript or that the checker states were clinically correct.

Together, these two failure groups show that disagreement can arise before primary selection in different ways. Candidate detection may omit a benchmark diagnosis from the leading differential even when full-scope checking later retains it, whereas criterion verification may remove a ranked benchmark diagnosis because the operational compatibility rule is not satisfied. These cases are therefore distinct from the ranked-and-retained nonselection cases examined in the following section.

\section{Diagnostic-Set and Diagnostic-Scope Failures}

The stage-wise analyses above focus on whether the committed primary diagnosis agrees with at least one benchmark gold parent. Two additional failure types must be considered separately: errors in constructing the complete diagnostic set and errors caused by an insufficient configured diagnostic scope. Neither is equivalent to primary-selection failure.

\paragraph{Diagnostic-set failures.}

A diagnostic-set failure occurs when the committed primary diagnosis is benchmark-consistent but the complete emitted diagnosis set does not exactly match the benchmark gold-label set. The internal HiED--DA run contains 518 cases with a correct committed primary diagnosis, but only 409 cases with exact predicted-set agreement. Consequently, 109 cases have a correct primary diagnosis but an incorrect complete diagnostic set.

Among these 109 cases, 57 contain at least one additional predicted label that is absent from the benchmark gold-label set, 54 omit at least one benchmark gold label, and two contain both an additional prediction and an omitted gold label. These categories overlap in the two cases with both error types and therefore should not be interpreted as mutually exclusive totals.

This distinction shows that selecting a benchmark-consistent primary diagnosis does not guarantee correct representation of comorbidity or secondary diagnoses. Primary selection and diagnostic-set construction are separate decisions and require different evaluation views.

The distinction is also important when comparing Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select. DA may emit a committed primary diagnosis together with one optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits only one re-selected primary diagnosis. Their committed Top-1 values can therefore be compared directly, but differences in Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 also reflect output cardinality and diagnostic-set construction. These set-level measures cannot be interpreted as isolated evidence about primary re-selection.

\paragraph{Diagnostic-scope failures.}

A diagnostic-scope failure occurs when none of the benchmark gold diagnoses is represented in the system's configured output space. Unlike candidate-detection or primary-selection failures, the system cannot recover such a case through better ranking or finalization because the relevant diagnosis is unavailable for emission.

The full 925-case MDD-5k evaluation contains 47 out-of-scope cases. These cases remain part of the full external performance estimate because configured diagnostic coverage is a property of the evaluated system rather than a condition that can be removed after prediction.

Chapter~\ref{ch:external} reports a separate configuration-only scope-expansion experiment. The diagnostic profile is expanded from fourteen to 33 configured categories and from eleven to 30 scoring parents, making 58 sampled benchmark diagnoses newly representable. However, none of these newly representable diagnoses becomes the committed Top-1 prediction.

The 0/58 result distinguishes representability from behavioral recovery. Adding a diagnosis to the configured profile is necessary for the system to emit it, but availability alone does not ensure that the Diagnostician will rank it highly or that the finalization policy will select it. The experiment does not show that diagnostic-scope expansion is generally ineffective, and it does not provide a paired estimate of whether predictions for previously in-scope cases remain unchanged.

These failure types describe different limitations. Diagnostic-set failure follows a benchmark-consistent primary but an incorrect emitted set; diagnostic-scope failure precedes ranking because the relevant diagnosis is unavailable. Both remain distinct from the ranked-and-retained failures characterized in Section~\ref{sec:ch8-primary-selection}.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

The results support a stage-wise interpretation of the evaluated HiED system. HiED is not the strongest same-split predictor, but its recorded ranked, criterion, compatibility, and finalization outputs reveal where benchmark disagreement remains after a plausible diagnosis has entered the candidate set. Across the internal analyses, candidate generation and criterion verification frequently preserve a benchmark gold parent, whereas a substantial remaining group ranks and retains that parent without committing it as primary.

This pattern is reinforced, but not fully replicated, by the external synthetic evaluation. The MDD-5k results reproduce the candidate-to-commitment gap and the absence of a reliable gain from the evaluated selection strategies, but they do not contain the matched criterion-retention trace required for the complete internal stage-wise analysis.

The resulting claim is therefore bounded. The thesis identifies primary selection as the largest remaining recorded bottleneck in the evaluated HiED configurations under synthetic transcript-only benchmarks. It does not establish that every benchmark disagreement is a clinically verified selection error, that transcript-only diagnosis is intrinsically underdetermined, or that the pattern generalizes to real patients.

\section{Why Candidate Detection and Criterion Verification Do Not Resolve Primacy}

The stage-wise results show that candidate detection and criterion verification are necessary components of differential diagnosis, but neither component alone resolves which diagnosis should be committed as primary.

\paragraph{Candidate detection.}

Candidate generation defines the set of diagnoses available for later selection. A selector cannot choose a diagnosis that is absent from the ranked differential, so the candidate-detection failures characterized in Chapter~\ref{ch:error} remain a direct target for improvement. Retrieval provides some evidence that this stage can be improved: in the Single architecture, both evaluated retrieval configurations increase committed Top-1 Accuracy relative to no retrieval. The corresponding effects are less stable in the complete HiED pipeline.

However, improved candidate coverage does not guarantee correct primary selection. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:selection-bottleneck}, a benchmark gold parent appears in the genuine Top-3 substantially more often than it is selected as the committed primary diagnosis. In the directly observed candidate-to-commitment gap, the benchmark diagnosis is already available among the leading candidates, but another diagnosis is selected.

The strict primary-selection subset provides an even clearer example. Figure~\ref{fig:ch8-reference-rank} shows that the nearest benchmark gold parent is usually ranked second rather than appearing near the bottom of the differential. Candidate availability is therefore not the only remaining limitation. The system must also compare close alternatives and determine which one best explains the overall presentation.

The strong same-split TF--IDF baseline further limits a candidate-only explanation. The synthetic transcripts contain predictive signals associated with the benchmark labels, yet the evaluated HiED configuration does not fully convert those signals into committed-primary agreement. This does not establish that every transcript contains clinically sufficient evidence or that the lexical classifier is clinically superior. Its performance may partly reflect corpus-specific wording, class priors, or synthetic generation artifacts.

\paragraph{Criterion verification.}

Criterion verification addresses a different question from primary selection. For each configured diagnosis, it asks whether the transcript satisfies the study-specific compatibility rule. It does not directly compare whether one compatible diagnosis explains the presentation better than another.

This distinction is important in psychiatric differential diagnosis because the same reported feature may support several disorders. Sleep disturbance, fatigue, poor concentration, low mood, worry, avoidance, and functional impairment are not uniquely diagnostic. Several categories may therefore satisfy their configured compatibility rules for the same transcript.

The internal results demonstrate this high-retention, low-selectivity pattern. A benchmark gold parent is retained in 0.937 of checker-eligible cases, but the median criterion-compatible set still contains several diagnostic categories. Criterion verification therefore behaves as a broad evidence filter rather than a unique primary-diagnosis selector.

The group-level criterion analyses reinforce this interpretation. Figure~\ref{fig:ch8-criterion-composition} and Table~\ref{tab:ch8-criterion-ratios} show that the aggregate criterion-state composition of strict primary-selection failures is similar to that of cases with a correct committed primary. Both groups have comparable proportions of \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, and \texttt{insufficient\_evidence} states. Compatible-set size is also similar between the two groups.

These broad checker-derived quantities therefore do not clearly indicate which retained diagnosis should be treated as primary. They record support, contradiction, unresolved evidence, and rule satisfaction, but they do not encode the comparative relations required for primacy. Such relations may include temporal order, illness course, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, explanatory dependence, and whether one diagnosis better accounts for symptoms also associated with another.

Candidate detection and criterion verification should therefore not be considered unsuccessful components. Candidate ranking makes plausible alternatives observable, while criterion verification creates an inspectable record of diagnosis-specific support and uncertainty. Their limitation is narrower: they do not by themselves perform the comparative decision required to select one primary diagnosis from several plausible and criterion-compatible alternatives.

\section{Primary Selection as the Largest Recorded Bottleneck}
\label{sec:discussion-primary-selection}

The internal evidence identifies primary selection as the largest remaining recorded bottleneck in the evaluated HiED configuration. This conclusion is based on the strict primary-selection condition $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$: at least one benchmark gold parent is present in the genuine Top-3 and retained in the criterion-compatible set, but another diagnosis is committed as primary.

This condition occurs in 272 of the 915 checker-eligible cases. It is larger than the mutually exclusive candidate-detection and criterion-verification failure groups characterized in Chapter~\ref{ch:error}. The comparison therefore indicates that the largest identifiable group of remaining benchmark disagreements occurs after a benchmark diagnosis has already been made available by both ranking and criterion verification.

The rank analysis further narrows the interpretation. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ch8-reference-rank}, the nearest benchmark gold parent is ranked second in most strict primary-selection failures and third in the remaining cases. The disagreement is therefore usually not between the committed diagnosis and a remote or weakly ranked alternative. Instead, it often occurs between diagnoses that the system has already placed near the top of the differential.

Table~\ref{tab:ch8-selection-pairs} also shows that these disagreements are not evenly distributed across diagnostic categories. The dominant pattern is F41 as the nearest benchmark gold parent and F32 as the selected primary. The reverse F32-to-F41 direction is much less frequent. This asymmetry suggests that the evaluated system has a systematic tendency to commit F32 when distinguishing depressive and anxiety-related presentations under the benchmark conditions.

The recorded criterion outputs do not provide a simple explanation for this tendency. Strict primary-selection failures and correctly selected cases have similar criterion-compatible set sizes and closely overlapping aggregate criterion-state distributions. A larger number of \texttt{met} criteria, a smaller unresolved share, or a narrower compatible set therefore does not reliably identify the diagnosis that should be committed as primary.

These findings point to a distinction between compatibility and comparative primacy. A diagnosis may satisfy its configured criteria without being the best primary explanation of the complete presentation. Selecting a primary diagnosis requires comparison among plausible alternatives, including their temporal relationship, illness course, syndrome dominance, exclusionary conditions, causal dependence, and ability to explain overlapping symptoms.

The term \emph{bottleneck} remains bounded to the recorded evaluation views. Direct-Answer commits its primary diagnosis before the Criterion Checker produces the audit trace, and the $D_3$, $R$, and $S$ indicators do not form a strictly causal runtime sequence. The analysis localizes benchmark disagreement in the recorded outputs; it does not reveal an internal causal process within the language model.

The 272 cases should also not be interpreted as uniformly incorrect or recoverable clinical decisions. Some may represent genuine primary-selection errors, while others may involve insufficient transcript information, multiple plausible diagnoses, benchmark-label ambiguity, or incorrect model-generated criterion states. The pending 20-case LingxiDiag pilot review will provide exploratory expert characterization of this selected population, not a population-level clinical error estimate.

Primary selection is therefore the largest \emph{recorded} bottleneck under the evaluated synthetic benchmark conditions. The result supports further study of comparative diagnostic decision-making, but it does not establish that every disagreement can be corrected by replacing the finalization rule or that the benchmark gold label is clinically preferable in every case.

\section{Why the Evaluated Selection Strategies Did Not Reliably Close the Gap}
\label{sec:discussion-selection-strategies}

The candidate-to-commitment gap identifies cases in which a benchmark gold parent is already available among plausible alternatives but is not selected as the committed primary diagnosis. This gap represents potential decision headroom, but it does not imply that the available ranking and criterion signals contain a simple rule that can reliably recover the benchmark label.

\paragraph{Criterion-based re-selection.}

Nominate-then-Select provides the clearest test of whether the recorded criterion-compatible set can replace the Diagnostician's emitted primary decision. DA and NtS reuse the same upstream ranking and criterion-checking outputs, and finalization introduces no additional LLM call. Their committed Top-1 difference can therefore be attributed relatively directly to the alternative finalization rule.

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:nts-factorial}, NtS reduces Top-1 Accuracy under all three internal retrieval conditions. The similar reductions across retrieval settings indicate that the observed penalty is not explained only by one retrieval configuration. The criterion-compatible set retains benchmark gold parents at a high rate, but it also retains several alternatives for many cases. Selecting the highest-ranked compatible diagnosis therefore changes correct DA decisions as well as incorrect ones.

This result illustrates why high retention is not equivalent to effective re-selection. Compatibility rules determine whether each diagnosis has sufficient support under its own operational criteria. They do not determine whether that diagnosis is a better primary explanation than another compatible diagnosis.

\paragraph{Fusion of recorded signals.}

Deterministic fusion attempts to combine candidate rank, compatibility status, and criterion-support strength. These signals are individually interpretable, but they remain incomplete representations of comparative diagnosis. Rank reflects the Diagnostician's initial preference, compatibility reflects diagnosis-specific rule satisfaction, and support ratios summarize criterion counts. None explicitly represents temporal precedence, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, or explanatory relationships among diagnoses.

The internal deterministic-fusion point estimate is slightly below DA, although the paired case-level record required for an internal confidence interval was not retained. The external paired analysis also shows a reduction relative to DA. The evidence therefore does not support the hypothesis that a fixed weighted combination of the recorded signals is sufficient to resolve primary selection.

\paragraph{Pairwise comparison and debate.}

Pairwise and debate methods introduce additional inference intended to compare plausible alternatives more directly. However, these methods are not isolated changes to the finalization rule. They differ from DA in prompt structure, context, number of LLM calls, and decision procedure and must therefore be interpreted as complete configurations.

Forced pairwise override reduces internal Top-1, whereas independent pairwise comparison produces only a small positive point estimate whose confidence interval includes zero. Two-view debate performs below DA internally and externally. These results provide no clear evidence that additional comparison calls reliably improve committed diagnosis under the evaluated settings.

One possible explanation is that additional reasoning over the same fixed transcript does not necessarily provide new discriminating evidence. When onset, duration, episode history, substance exposure, causal direction, or exclusionary information is absent or ambiguous, another model call can only reinterpret the existing record. It may reinforce an existing preference, introduce a new inconsistency, or produce a more fluent justification without resolving the underlying evidentiary uncertainty.

\paragraph{Sampling-based aggregation.}

Self-consistency and confidence-informed self-consistency test whether repeated Diagnostician outputs yield a more stable committed decision. The external point estimates are slightly higher than DA, but their confidence intervals include zero. The retained internal records are descriptive and do not support the same level of paired inference.

Repeated sampling measures the stability of model preference rather than the clinical adequacy of that preference. A diagnosis that is repeatedly selected may reflect a stable decision rule, a strong corpus prior, or a systematic bias. Majority voting cannot correct a preference that is consistently directed toward the wrong benchmark label, and confidence weighting cannot help when stated confidence is not reliably aligned with comparative correctness.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}

Taken together, the evaluated strategies do not reliably convert candidate coverage into better committed-primary agreement. Methods that reuse recorded outputs are limited by the broad and non-comparative nature of the available signals. Methods that introduce additional inference remain constrained by the same transcript and may add variability or reinforce existing preferences without acquiring missing evidence.

The negative results should not be interpreted as evidence that criterion-based selection, pairwise comparison, debate, fusion, or self-consistency are generally ineffective. They apply to the specific prompts, thresholds, inference budgets, aggregation rules, models, and synthetic datasets evaluated in this thesis.

The findings instead clarify what a more effective selector may require. It must represent comparative relations among diagnoses rather than relying only on independent support scores. Relevant relations may include temporal order, syndrome dominance, exclusionary evidence, causal dependence, comorbidity structure, and whether one diagnosis explains symptoms that would otherwise be attributed to another. When the transcript does not contain the required discriminating information, the appropriate action may be to request targeted follow-up information or abstain rather than force a different primary diagnosis.

The observed primary-selection gap therefore remains an analytical finding rather than a demonstrated opportunity for automatic correction. It shows that candidate availability and criterion compatibility leave unresolved comparative decisions, but the evaluated strategies do not provide a reliable mechanism for resolving them.

\section[Diagnostic-Set Construction, Diagnostic Scope, and External Transfer]{\raggedright Diagnostic-Set Construction, Diagnostic Scope, and External Transfer}
\label{sec:discussion-set-scope-transfer}

Primary selection is only one component of diagnostic output. The results also identify distinct limitations in constructing the complete diagnosis set, defining the available diagnostic scope, and transferring predictive signals across synthetic corpora. These limitations occur at different stages and should not be interpreted as additional instances of the same primary-selection bottleneck.

\paragraph{Diagnostic-set construction.}

Committed Top-1 agreement evaluates whether the selected primary diagnosis matches at least one benchmark gold parent. It does not require the complete emitted diagnosis set to match the complete benchmark gold-label set.

Chapter~\ref{ch:error} identifies 109 internal cases in which the committed primary diagnosis is benchmark-consistent but the complete emitted set is incorrect. Some cases include an additional predicted diagnosis that is absent from the benchmark set, some omit a benchmark gold diagnosis, and a small number contain both error types.

This distinction is important because primary diagnosis and comorbidity construction are separate decisions. A system may identify an acceptable primary diagnosis while over-predicting a secondary disorder or failing to emit a benchmark comorbidity. Conversely, a change that improves set agreement may do so by changing output cardinality rather than improving primary selection.

The DA--NtS comparison illustrates this measurement boundary. DA may produce a committed primary diagnosis and an optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS emits only one committed diagnosis. Their Top-1 values directly compare primary selection, but Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 also reflect the number and composition of emitted labels. Set-level improvements should therefore not be interpreted as isolated evidence of better primary-diagnosis selection.

Future systems may require a separate decision policy for diagnostic-set construction. Such a policy would need to distinguish primary disorders, true comorbidities, differential alternatives, and symptoms better explained by another diagnosis. Simply retaining every criterion-compatible diagnosis would likely produce broad and clinically unhelpful output sets.

\paragraph{Diagnostic scope.}

Configured diagnostic scope determines whether a diagnosis can be ranked, checked, or emitted at all. A diagnosis that is absent from the configured profile cannot be recovered through better retrieval, candidate ranking, criterion verification, or finalization.

The full MDD-5k evaluation contains 47 cases for which none of the benchmark gold diagnoses is represented in the main configured output space. These cases appropriately remain part of the full external estimate because diagnostic coverage is a property of the evaluated system rather than a post hoc exclusion criterion.

The scope-expansion experiment reported in Table~\ref{tab:scope-expansion-results} separates representability from behavioral recovery. Expanding the profile from fourteen to 33 configured categories makes the benchmark diagnoses of 58 sampled cases newly available, but none becomes the committed Top-1 diagnosis.

This result shows that ontology or profile expansion alone is insufficient under the evaluated configuration. Adding a diagnosis to the prompt and defining a corresponding Criterion Checker makes the category available, but it does not teach the Diagnostician when that category should be ranked above familiar alternatives. Behavioral adaptation may also require representative examples, prompt revision, comparative diagnostic knowledge, or model training that includes the newly introduced categories.

The 0/58 result is therefore not evidence that diagnostic-scope expansion is generally ineffective. It shows only that configuration-level representability does not automatically produce ranking or selection recovery. The experiment also does not establish whether predictions for previously in-scope cases remain preserved after expansion.

\paragraph{External synthetic transfer.}

The MDD-5k evaluation tests whether the main internal patterns remain visible under a second synthetic data-generation process with different label frequencies and transcript characteristics. Figure~\ref{fig:internal-external-gap} shows that genuine Top-3 coverage remains substantially higher than committed Top-1 agreement on both corpora. The external full set contains 259 cases in which a benchmark gold parent is ranked in the genuine Top-3 but another diagnosis is committed as primary.

This result is consistent with transfer of the candidate-to-commitment gap. The external primary-selection comparisons in Table~\ref{tab:mdd-finalization-results} also reproduce the absence of a reliable improvement from the evaluated re-selection strategies. Recorded-output re-selection and debate reduce Top-1, while sampling-based methods yield only small and statistically uncertain positive point estimates.

However, the external evidence does not reproduce the complete internal stage-wise pathway. The common MDD-5k trace does not provide a matched criterion-retention view, so the external analysis cannot reconstruct $R$, the ranked-and-retained nonselection subset, or the joint $D_3$--$R$--$S$ profiles. The external results therefore support persistence of the ranking-to-selection gap, but not full replication of the internal primary-selection localization.

The restricted F32/F41 analysis should also remain separate from the multi-way task. Its substantially higher performance reflects a narrower binary distinction and does not establish general external diagnostic performance.

Cross-corpus lexical results provide an additional limitation. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:lexical-transfer-rewrite}, the TF--IDF classifier performs substantially better when trained and evaluated within the same corpus than when transferred between LingxiDiag and MDD-5k. Strong same-corpus lexical performance therefore appears partly dependent on corpus-specific wording, synthetic generation patterns, or label priors rather than a portable diagnostic representation.

The LingxiDiag within-corpus value reported in the transfer experiment belongs to a separate experimental lineage and should not be confused with the main same-split TF--IDF baseline. The former is used to study transfer, whereas the latter establishes the predictive position of HiED on the fixed internal evaluation set.

Taken together, these findings identify three distinct system boundaries. Diagnostic-set construction determines which additional diagnoses are emitted after the primary decision. Diagnostic scope determines which diagnoses are available to the system at all. External transfer determines whether learned preferences and corpus-derived signals remain useful under another data-generation process. None of these limitations can be reduced to primary selection alone, although each can influence the final committed output.

The evidence remains restricted to synthetic Chinese transcripts. Persistence across two synthetic corpora does not establish transfer to real patients, Taiwanese clinical language, hospital workflows, or clinical outcomes.

\section[Clinical Interpretation and Implications for Auditable Diagnostic Systems]{\raggedright Clinical Interpretation and Implications for Auditable Diagnostic Systems}
\label{sec:discussion-clinical-implications}

The computational results identify where the evaluated system disagrees with projected benchmark gold labels, but they cannot determine the clinical cause of each disagreement. The same recorded profile may reflect an incorrect system decision, incomplete transcript evidence, multiple clinically plausible diagnoses, an ambiguous benchmark label, or an inaccurate model-generated criterion state. Clinical interpretation and system-level failure localization should therefore remain distinct.

\paragraph{Clinical interpretation boundary.}

The strict ranked-and-retained nonselection subset provides the clearest computational evidence of disagreement at committed primary selection. In these cases, a benchmark gold parent is present in the ranked differential and retained as criterion-compatible, yet another diagnosis is committed as primary. This definition identifies the recorded location of disagreement, but it does not establish that the committed diagnosis is clinically incorrect or that the benchmark label is uniquely preferable.

The LingxiDiag pilot review described in Section~\ref{sec:lingxidiag-pilot-review} is designed to examine this boundary. Reviewers first assess the transcript without access to the benchmark label or system outputs, then inspect the recorded ranking and criterion artifacts, and only afterward view the benchmark reference. This staged procedure separates transcript-based clinical judgment from reactions to the system or benchmark.

Because the pilot is ongoing, preliminary judgments and partial agreement estimates are excluded from the present thesis. The computational findings are therefore not described as psychiatrist-confirmed diagnostic errors. Once completed, the pilot will report psychiatrist-assigned interpretations of the twenty sampled cases, including primary-selection error, insufficient information, multiple plausible diagnoses, benchmark ambiguity, criterion-verification problems, or unresolved disagreement. It remains an expert review of synthetic transcripts and is distinct from the ongoing CGMH real-world validation.

\paragraph{Auditability does not imply decision correctness.}

HiED retains a ranked differential, criterion states, checker-generated supporting text, criterion-compatible diagnoses, compatibility-rule traces, and finalization source. These artifacts make the system's recorded outputs inspectable and allow disagreements to be localized across candidate detection, criterion verification, and committed primary selection.

This traceability is useful even when predictive performance is not superior. A reviewer can determine whether a diagnosis was omitted from the leading differential, rejected by an operational compatibility rule, retained but not selected, or unavailable in the configured output space. The trace can also reveal which diagnostic criteria were recorded as supported, contradicted, or unresolved.

However, audit artifacts do not reveal the language model's hidden reasoning, validate the criterion judgments, or establish that the final decision is clinically correct. A plausible explanation can accompany an incorrect prediction, and a diagnosis may satisfy its own configured criteria without being the most appropriate primary diagnosis. Auditability should therefore be treated as an analytical and review capability rather than as evidence of clinical reliability.

\paragraph{Implications for comparative primary selection.}

The results suggest that future primary-selection methods require explicitly comparative evidence. The evaluated compatibility rules examine diagnoses independently, while simple support counts, ranking scores, confidence values, and repeated model preferences do not consistently determine which compatible diagnosis should be primary.

A stronger selector may need to represent relations among diagnoses and evidence, including temporal order, illness course, episode structure, syndrome dominance, exclusionary causes, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity. For example, the selector should distinguish whether anxiety symptoms form an independent primary syndrome, occur mainly within a depressive episode, or remain insufficiently characterized because duration and onset are unresolved.

Structured diagnostic knowledge may help encode these relations, but adding a knowledge graph or another scoring module would not itself guarantee improvement. Such a system would require explicit rules for comparing competing explanations, handling missing evidence, and deciding when the available record does not support a unique primary diagnosis. These directions remain future work rather than evaluated contributions of the present thesis.

\paragraph{Interaction, abstention, and missing evidence.}

The evaluated system operates on a fixed transcript and cannot obtain additional information during inference. Additional model calls, debate, or repeated sampling therefore continue to reason over the same evidence. When key distinctions are absent, further inference may change the model's preference without resolving the underlying uncertainty.

In an interactive setting, a future system could identify the missing fact that best separates the leading candidates and request targeted follow-up information. Relevant questions may concern symptom duration, temporal sequence, prior episodes, substance exposure, functional impairment, exclusionary medical causes, or the relationship among mood, anxiety, trauma, and sleep symptoms.

When interaction is unavailable, the safer output may be an explicit statement that the primary diagnosis cannot be determined from the available transcript. Abstention or uncertainty-aware referral may be more appropriate than forcing a unique answer from several similarly supported candidates. This possibility is not evaluated in the present experiments and should be tested directly in future work.

\paragraph{Deployment and Taiwanese-context boundary.}

Taiwan is the intended deployment context of the proposed framework, not a population in which clinical effectiveness has already been demonstrated. The system is motivated by Traditional Chinese interaction, local model serving, and the need for clinician-facing audit artifacts. All completed quantitative results in this thesis are obtained from synthetic Chinese transcript corpora; real-world validation at CGMH is ongoing and no incomplete results are included.

The thesis therefore does not yet report completed evidence for real Taiwanese patients, locally specific language use, diagnostic practices, hospital information systems, clinician workload, patient outcomes, or prospective safety. The planned CGMH endpoints distinguish recorded clinical diagnoses from transcript-only psychiatrist consensus, but neither agreement analysis is available in the present conclusions.

Consequently, HiED should not be interpreted as an autonomous diagnostic tool or as a clinically validated decision-support system. Its demonstrated contribution is a research framework that makes candidate ranking, criterion verification, and primary selection separately observable under controlled benchmark conditions.

Taken together, the findings support a design principle for auditable diagnostic AI: systems should expose candidate alternatives, diagnosis-specific evidence, unresolved information, compatibility decisions, and finalization sources, while preserving a clear distinction between inspectability and correctness. The next step is not merely to add another selector, but to evaluate comparative diagnostic reasoning, targeted evidence acquisition, abstention, and clinician review using real clinical data and prespecified safety outcomes.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

This chapter defines five boundaries on the reported claims: benchmark and measurement validity, dataset generalizability, experimental-comparison validity, statistical and reproducibility limits, and clinical deployment. They do not invalidate the benchmark results, but specify how far those results can be interpreted.

\section{Benchmark and Measurement Limitations}

The scoring target is agreement with dataset-provided labels after the parent-level projection defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}, not an independently adjudicated clinical diagnosis. Cases may have several projected gold parents, and Top-1 is correct when the committed diagnosis matches any of them. Parent projection enables a common scoring space but removes subtype distinctions; the first-listed parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses such as the confusion matrix.

The metrics evaluate different output views. Top-1 scores the committed primary, genuine Top-3 scores the ranked differential, retention scores the criterion-compatible set, and Exact Match and label-level F1 score the emitted diagnosis set. These measures are not interchangeable, and set-level comparisons between DA and NtS also reflect their different output cardinalities.

The $D_3$, $R$, and $S$ indicators are likewise analytical views rather than a strictly causal runtime funnel: DA commits before checking, and the Checker evaluates the full configured scope rather than only the Top-3. Top-1 and Top-3 use all 1,000 cases, whereas retention uses 915 checker-eligible cases. Table~\ref{tab:gap-counts} therefore separates the 284-case Top-3--Top-1 gap, the 272 checker-eligible ranked-and-retained nonselection cases, and the 349 gold-informed oracle cases. The oracle's 0.867 estimate assumes gold-guided correction with no harm to initially correct cases and is an optimistic upper bound, not an implemented selector.

The strong same-split TF--IDF result shows that this synthetic corpus contains predictive signal, limiting explanations based only on missing transcript information. Its cross-corpus degradation suggests dependence on corpus-specific wording, generation patterns, or label priors. It is therefore a benchmark control, not evidence of clinically superior or portable lexical diagnosis. Overall, these measures localize recorded disagreement but do not establish causal model reasoning, a uniquely correct clinical primary, or automatic recoverability.

\section{Dataset and Generalizability Limitations}

LingxiDiagBench and MDD-5k are synthetic Chinese psychiatric dialogues. They support controlled, reproducible evaluation but may contain repeated templates, simplified ambiguity, limited comorbidity, clearer cues, and less conversational or longitudinal complexity than real consultations. Persistence across both corpora may therefore reflect properties shared by synthetic text rather than clinical generality.

Because the official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable, the main internal estimate uses a fixed 1,000-case held-out subset from the training-source corpus (Table~\ref{tab:data-roles-rewrite}). Case IDs were excluded from training and retrieval, but the split still shares source templates, language, label priors, and annotation procedures; no complete semantic near-duplicate audit was performed. The public-validation split was repeatedly used for prompts, rules, sensitivity analyses, and retrieval selection, so it provides development evidence rather than an independent estimate. The HiED validation--test retrieval reversal illustrates the resulting configuration uncertainty.

MDD-5k adds a second synthetic generation process, different label frequencies, longer transcripts, and a partially different scope. It reproduces the candidate-to-commitment gap (Figure~\ref{fig:internal-external-gap}) but is not a clinical cohort. Its common trace lacks matched criterion retention, so the full $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis cannot be reconstructed. External results also combine corpus shift with output-scope differences and possible loss of middle turns under deterministic prompt truncation; the frequency and diagnostic effect of truncation were not separately estimated.

Cross-corpus TF--IDF performance is much lower than within-corpus performance (Table~\ref{tab:lexical-transfer-rewrite}), limiting portability claims for this representation without showing that all clinically meaningful language is non-transferable. Overall, the study demonstrates same-source behavior and partial persistence under a second synthetic distribution, not generalization to independent clinical data or institutions.

\section{Model and Experimental-Comparison Limitations}
\label{sec:model-comparison-limitations}

The same-split benchmark compares complete predictive configurations under a common label projection and scorer, not isolated architecture components. TF--IDF, Single, and HiED differ in training, retrieval, prompts, calls, intermediate stages, and output contracts, so Single--HiED differences are package effects. Published LingxiDiagBench rows differ further in inference and scoring details and serve only as descriptive positioning.

Retrieval is more controlled because no retrieval, global Top-5, and parent-balanced retrieval are compared within each architecture. Even so, global and parent-balanced configurations differ in example count, composition, ordering, similarity distribution, and prompt length, not only balancing. Single and HiED also select their primary retrieval settings separately on validation; their main comparison is therefore between selected systems rather than a retrieval-matched architecture ablation.

DA and NtS provide the cleanest finalization comparison because they reuse the same ranking and checker artifacts and NtS adds no LLM call. The result nevertheless concerns one deterministic policy, and set-level metrics also reflect that DA may emit an optional comorbid diagnosis whereas NtS emits one label. Deterministic fusion similarly evaluates one fixed combination of recorded rank, compatibility, and support signals rather than fusion in general.

Pairwise, debate, and sampling methods change prompts, calls, decoding, aggregation, or compute and are complete configurations. Debate uses additional calls and a 24,576-token context rather than DA's 8,192-token budget; the conservative gate changes no cases. Scale sensitivity remains within Qwen3, while serving, recovery, and truncation may affect execution. Scope expansion also changes prompts, candidates, checkers, and rules without training. These results establish behavior under declared contracts, not causal rankings of method families, serving stacks, or scope-expansion strategies.

\section{Statistical and Reproducibility Limitations}
\label{sec:statistical-reproducibility-limitations}

The confidence intervals and exact tests quantify uncertainty for the observed benchmark cases under fixed outputs and configurations. They do not cover benchmark validity, prompt or checkpoint choice, synthetic-generator variation, or a new population. Rare labels and small subsets, especially the twelve strict criterion-verification failures, remain unstable. An interval containing zero indicates insufficient evidence of a difference, not equivalence; an interval excluding zero does not identify the causal component when configurations differ in several ways. Holm adjustment applies within the prespecified families in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}, not across every exploratory result.

Evidence also differs in prespecification and record completeness. The same-split comparison, validation-based retrieval selection, internal $D_3$--$R$--$S$ analysis, and paired DA--NtS comparison form the primary evidence; the oracle, scope expansion, lexical transfer, and additional interventions are supporting analyses. Internal SC/CISC lacks complete case-level traces, and deterministic fusion lacks the paired record needed for an interval. Their summaries cannot reconstruct case-level gains and losses and therefore carry less inferential and audit weight.

Reproduction requires exact checkpoints, quantization, tokenizer and serving versions, prompts, diagnostic profile, checker rules, retrieval index, truncation and recovery behavior, case splits, predictions, label projection, output-view definitions, denominators, and statistical procedures. Greedy decoding reduces but does not remove environment dependence. A numerically similar score under another Top-3 construction or eligible population is not the same endpoint. Computational and analytical reproducibility also remain distinct from clinical validity: a perfectly reproduced benchmark result may still rely on an invalid label or checker state.

\section{Clinical Validity and Deployment Boundaries}
\label{sec:clinical-deployment-boundaries}

The computational profiles localize disagreement among configured scope, ranking, compatibility, and commitment, but they cannot determine whether the system, transcript, or benchmark reference is clinically responsible. Benchmark labels have not been adjudicated as unique primaries, and checker states and supporting text are model-generated rather than clinician-validated evidence. The LingxiDiag pilot review and CGMH real-world validation described in Section~\ref{sec:clinical-evaluation-plan} are ongoing, so no preliminary judgments or incomplete agreement estimates are used. The reported computational groups remain benchmark-disagreement profiles, not psychiatrist-confirmed errors.

The evaluation protocol assigns \texttt{Others} when none of a case's benchmark gold diagnoses can be represented in the configured scoring space. HiED itself does not emit an \texttt{Others} diagnosis and does not implement a validated unknown- or out-of-scope detector. It also lacks a clinically validated abstention policy, and its confidence fields are not calibrated to correctness or risk. Scope expansion shows that making a label available does not ensure its use. A clinical system would need to distinguish uncertain in-scope cases from out-of-scope presentations and request evidence, abstain, or refer when the record is insufficient.

Taiwan is the intended application context, not a validated population. Although real-patient data collection is ongoing at CGMH, this thesis includes no completed CGMH results and therefore provides no present evidence for spontaneous or mixed Mandarin--Taiwanese clinical speech, local coding practices, hospital integration, clinician workflow, subgroup fairness, treatment effects, or patient-safety outcomes. It also does not report automation bias, review burden, latency, or cost. Clinical use would require ethics oversight, privacy and access controls, human supervision, incident procedures, monitoring, subgroup surveillance, and revalidation.

Auditability and reproducibility do not establish clinical validity: an inspectable, repeatable trace may still contain an invalid label, checker judgment, or decision. No result establishes clinician equivalence or safe autonomous use. HiED should remain an offline research and review prototype until independent adjudication, prospective workflow and safety evaluation, and governance are complete.

\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis introduced HiED, an auditable multi-stage framework that preserves ranked candidates, diagnosis-specific criterion outputs, deterministic compatibility decisions, and committed primary selection as separately inspectable artifacts. Its contribution is a system and evaluation framework for locating benchmark disagreement, not a state-of-the-art accuracy result.

TF--IDF + logistic regression was the strongest same-split predictor, while the selected Single and HiED--DA configurations produced similar committed-primary performance. HiED nevertheless exposed a substantial gap between candidate availability and final commitment: the largest strict recorded disagreement subset ranked and retained a benchmark gold parent but selected another diagnosis as primary. This supports primary selection as the largest recorded bottleneck under the evaluated benchmark contract.

Retrieval helped Single more clearly than HiED, whose validation-selected ordering reversed on the held-out set. None of the tested deterministic, pairwise, debate, or sampling-based selectors provided a clear attributable gain. The candidate-to-commitment pattern remained visible across Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k, although the external trace lacked matched criterion retention. These completed results remain synthetic benchmark evidence and establish neither automatic recoverability nor clinical validity; the LingxiDiag pilot and CGMH validation are ongoing and excluded from the quantitative conclusions.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}

The five answers below are restricted to the evaluated synthetic benchmarks, declared output contracts, and recorded system artifacts.

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=1.5cm,labelwidth=1.1cm,itemsep=0pt,parsep=0pt]

\item[RQ1] \textbf{How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?}

HiED did not outperform the strongest same-split baseline: TF--IDF + logistic regression had the highest committed Top-1, while selected Single and HiED--DA were nearly identical. HiED's distinct contribution is the genuine ranking, criterion, compatibility, and finalization trace that makes recorded stages separately evaluable. The lexical baseline confirms strong internal signal, but its cross-corpus degradation limits claims of clinical superiority or transfer. HiED is therefore audit-oriented rather than accuracy-leading on this benchmark.

\item[RQ2] \textbf{How often is a benchmark diagnosis present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?}

A benchmark gold parent appeared in the genuine Top-3 more often than it was committed as primary. Among 915 checker-eligible cases, 272 satisfied $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$: a gold parent was ranked and retained, but another diagnosis was selected. This largest strict recorded subset usually placed the nearest gold second and was dominated by F41-to-F32 disagreement. It supports primary selection as the largest \emph{recorded} bottleneck, but neither establishes a causal model process nor proves that all 272 cases are clinically incorrect or recoverable.

\item[RQ3] \textbf{How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?}

Retrieval benefited Single more clearly than HiED: global Top-5 was selected on validation, and both retrieval variants improved held-out Top-1 over no retrieval without a supported global-versus-parent difference. HiED validation endpoints were split, so the predefined rule retained parent-balanced retrieval, but global Top-5 had higher held-out estimates on every reported endpoint. This validation--test reversal did not alter the prespecified trace. Because the configurations also differ in example count, composition, order, and prompt length, the evidence establishes neither a stable HiED winner nor an isolated benefit of parent balancing.

\item[RQ4] \textbf{Do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve over Direct-Answer?}

No evaluated intervention provided a clear and attributable improvement over DA. NtS was the cleanest finalization comparison because it reused the upstream trace, yet it reduced Top-1 under all three retrieval conditions. Fusion did not improve DA; forced pairwise override and debate reduced internal Top-1; and independent pairwise and external sampling produced only small positive estimates with intervals crossing zero. These findings apply to the tested rules, prompts, and budgets, not to the method families in general.

\newpage
\item[RQ5] \textbf{To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and what error patterns characterize the remaining failures?}

The candidate-to-commitment pattern remained visible across Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k, although missing external retention prevented a full $D_3$--$R$--$S$ replication. Scope expansion made 58 sampled diagnoses representable but recovered none as Top-1; TF--IDF transfer degraded across corpora; and internal disagreements concentrated in a few directions. These findings show persistence only within the tested model family, synthetic corpora, scope, and transfer settings, not robustness across unrelated models or clinical populations.

\end{description}

\section{Future Work}

\paragraph{LingxiDiag pilot review.}

The 20-case review in Section~\ref{sec:lingxidiag-pilot-review} should be completed before clinical interpretations are added to the candidate-to-commitment gap. The report should identify the frozen random sample and seed, reviewer characteristics, blinding and consensus procedures, independent and consensus judgments, agreement, audit-trace usefulness, unresolved cases, and disagreement categories. It remains pilot expert characterization of synthetic cases from one failure population.

\paragraph{Ongoing CGMH clinical validation.}

The CGMH study in Section~\ref{sec:cgmh-validation} should be completed and reported separately. It should document eligibility and inclusion flow, scope coverage, system outputs, recorded diagnoses, independent transcript-only ratings and consensus, agreement endpoints, class-specific results, and uncertainty. The two reference standards and pilot sample must not be pooled; workflow, subgroup, safety, privacy, and governance evidence remains necessary before any clinical-use claim.

\paragraph{Comparative selection and technical validation.}

Future selectors should compare diagnoses through temporal order, illness course, syndrome dominance, exclusions, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity rather than only rank or support counts. Tests should use fixed upstream artifacts and declared or matched compute. Missing discriminative evidence should trigger targeted questions, calibrated abstention, or referral. Technical studies should also test unrelated model families with complete case-level artifacts and external traces supporting full $D_3$--$R$--$S$ reconstruction.

HiED exposes the distinction between plausible ranking and commitment. The next steps are to complete the two clinical studies without conflating their claims, validate comparative evidence, support evidence acquisition or abstention, and test the system reproducibly in real Taiwanese clinical settings.
