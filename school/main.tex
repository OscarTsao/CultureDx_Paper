\chapter*{中文摘要}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{中文摘要}
初診精神科晤談逐字稿中的鑑別診斷，本質上是一種在證據不完整條件下進行的比較式決策。多個疾病診斷可能同時與同一份逐字稿相容，但最終通常只能有一個被判定為主診斷。然而，用以區分病程、發病時序、排除病因與共病關係等關鍵鑑別資訊，未必會在單次初診晤談中被完整引出。大型語言模型雖然能流暢地閱讀並理解這類逐字稿，近期也已有大量研究嘗試將其應用於精神科診斷任務，但語言理解的流暢性並不等同於可靠的鑑別診斷能力。

既有評測呈現出一致的落差：模型在粗粒度分類任務上往往表現良好，但一旦進入細粒度的疾病層級鑑別診斷，準確率便會大幅下降。這個落差促使本研究提出一套可稽核的系統架構，用以明確定位錯誤發生於診斷流程中的哪一個階段。模型可能生成一個合理且受到診斷準則支持的候選疾病，卻仍未能將其正確選為主診斷。候選診斷生成、依準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇三者之間的差異，通常會被單一準確率指標所掩蓋，使研究者難以判斷系統究竟在哪個環節失敗。

為了解決這個問題，本研究提出 HiED——一套面向中文精神科初診對話逐字稿的鑑別診斷框架，將候選診斷偵測、ICD-10 逐準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇維持為可分別觀察的階段，而非將整個流程簡化為單一分類事件。同時，系統會在每次預測時輸出逐準則的稽核軌跡，使診斷推論過程能夠被檢視與分析。本研究中的系統並非宣稱已完成臨床驗證的自主診斷工具，而是定位為一個研究工具，用以分析在逐字稿條件下，精神科診斷流程究竟在哪一個階段發生失敗。

因此，本論文有兩項主要貢獻：其一，提出一套可稽核的失敗定位框架，使上述各診斷階段得以分別觀察；其二，提供可重現的證據指出，在僅使用初診逐字稿進行的精神科大型語言模型診斷任務中，主診斷選擇本身構成一個核心瓶頸。換言之，在本研究之合成逐字稿設定下，系統的主要限制不僅在於能否生成合理的候選診斷，而更在於能否在多個皆具一定準則支持的候選之間，進行有效比較並判定最適合作為主診斷者。最後需說明，本文題目所稱之臺灣脈絡，指涉系統之設計動機與預期部署場域（繁體中文互動、本地端推論、面向臨床人員之稽核），而非已完成之臺灣真實病人臨床驗證；本文所有量化結果均來自合成中文逐字稿語料，真實病人之臨床驗證為後續工作。

\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial-consultation psychiatric transcript is a comparative decision under incomplete evidence. Multiple disorders may be compatible with the same transcript, yet only one can typically be judged the primary diagnosis. Key discriminating information---illness course, onset timing, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity relationships---may not be fully elicited within a single initial consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, and recent research has applied them to psychiatric diagnostic tasks, but linguistic fluency does not equate to reliable differential diagnostic ability.

Existing evaluations show a consistent gap: models perform well on coarse-grained classification, but accuracy drops sharply on fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis. This gap motivates an auditable architecture that locates the stage at which a diagnostic error occurs. A model may generate a plausible disorder supported by diagnostic criteria, yet fail to select it as the primary diagnosis. The distinction among candidate-diagnosis generation, criterion-based verification, and primary-diagnosis selection is usually obscured by a single accuracy metric.

To address this problem, we propose HiED, a differential-diagnosis framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts that keeps candidate-diagnosis detection, ICD-10 criterion-by-criterion verification, and primary-diagnosis selection as separately observable stages, rather than a single classification event. The system outputs a criterion-level audit trace with every prediction, allowing the diagnostic inference to be examined. It is not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic tool, but a research instrument for analyzing where transcript-based psychiatric diagnosis fails. The Taiwanese context in the title denotes the intended deployment setting; real-patient Taiwanese validation remains future work.

This thesis therefore makes two main contributions: an auditable failure-localization framework that keeps these stages separately observable, and reproducible evidence that primary-diagnosis selection constitutes a core bottleneck in psychiatric large-language-model diagnosis using only initial-consultation transcripts. Under this study's synthetic transcript-only setting, the system's primary limitation lies not merely in whether it can generate plausible candidate diagnoses, but more critically in whether it can compare among multiple criterion-supported candidates and determine which is best suited as the primary diagnosis.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric outpatient care in Taiwan is widely accessible, but this access also creates a heavy clinical workload. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit different levels of medical facilities without referral, and large medical centers may receive more than ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in one day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A first psychiatric visit must cover history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and an initial diagnostic judgment within limited time. The resulting note or transcript often contains incomplete information about onset, duration, course, medical or substance-related causes, and previous treatment.

The evidence may also be indirect. In Chinese-speaking settings, patients often describe distress through insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, or gastrointestinal symptoms before using explicit mood or anxiety terms~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. Taiwanese consultations may also mix Mandarin, Taiwanese expressions, and local clinical wording. The same condition can therefore be described in several ways within one visit.

Differential diagnosis remains difficult because common psychiatric disorders share many symptoms. Depression, anxiety, stress-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, somatic symptom disorders, and sleep disorders may all involve insomnia, fatigue, poor concentration, and impaired daily function. ICD-10 provides requirements for symptoms, duration, impairment, course, and exclusions~\citep{who2019icd10}, but several disorders may still fit the same transcript. Evidence for both depression and anxiety, for example, may reflect comorbidity or overlapping symptoms from one main condition~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}. Even structured diagnostic studies report limited agreement for some common disorders~\citep{regier2013dsm5}.

These features make first-visit psychiatric diagnosis a problem of comparative bookkeeping under incomplete evidence. The clinician must document a best-supported working diagnosis, keep plausible alternatives visible, and identify which missing information should be collected at follow-up. Time pressure can increase the risk of anchoring or premature closure when several disorders share the same symptoms~\citep{croskerry2003cognitive}.
\section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

Large language models and medical multi-agent systems are attractive for psychiatric transcript analysis because they can process free-form dialogue, summarize symptoms, retrieve relevant examples, assign specialized roles, and return structured outputs~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. However, these capabilities do not by themselves ensure reliable differential diagnosis from a fixed transcript. A fluent answer may still fail because the model never proposes a reference label, because its criterion audit does not support that diagnosis, or because it proposes the diagnosis but commits another one. HiED studies this narrower technical problem; it does not solve short consultation time, missing history, or clinical uncertainty.\ct{Does our model deal with all these problems?}

We study this question through three diagnostic stages. \emph{Candidate detection} asks whether a reference parent label appears in the ranked Top-3. \emph{Criterion verification} evaluates configured criteria and records whether a reference parent is retained in the criterion-compatible set. \emph{Primary selection} asks which candidate is selected as the primary diagnosis; its observed output is the \emph{committed primary diagnosis}. These measures locate disagreements among output views and do not imply that every policy executes a fixed chronological funnel.

Throughout this thesis, \emph{primary selection} denotes the scientific task of choosing one primary diagnosis among plausible candidates. \emph{Finalization} denotes the system-level process that converts the recorded ranking, checker states, and compatibility outputs into the final output; Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select are finalization policies. \emph{Commitment} is reserved for the resulting output state, as in \emph{committed primary diagnosis} or \emph{committed Top-1 agreement}, rather than used as the name of a separate diagnostic stage.

We use the term \emph{primary-selection bottleneck} for the operational pattern in which a reference label is present among leading candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but the system selects a different committed primary diagnosis. The bottleneck is therefore a benchmark-local pattern showing that candidate coverage or criterion compatibility can exceed committed Top-1 agreement; it is not a claim about the true clinical primary diagnosis. It does not show that every error passes both earlier checks, that the corpus identifies a unique clinical primary diagnosis, or that every transcript contains enough evidence for one.\ct{what problem?}

This distinction matters because different errors need different solutions. Missing candidates call for better generation or retrieval. A candidate that fails criterion checking points to the checker, the rules, or missing evidence. A candidate that remains available but is not selected points to the final comparison among plausible diagnoses. The detailed metric definitions are given in \Cref{sec:reference-conventions}.

\section{Overview of HiED and Study Scope}

To make these failure modes observable, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid multi-agent system for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis from initial-consultation transcripts. The intended context is Taiwanese outpatient care, but all quantitative results in this thesis use synthetic Chinese transcript datasets; no Taiwanese patient results are reported.

HiED has five main parts. It formats the dialogue while preserving speaker turns, optionally retrieves similar examples, asks an LLM diagnostician for a ranked differential and a primary diagnosis, checks each configured diagnostic category against operationalized ICD-10 criteria, and applies deterministic rules to form a criterion-compatible set. Each criterion is marked as \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}.

The Direct-Answer policy keeps the diagnostician's primary diagnosis and uses outputs from the Criterion Checker and Compatibility Auditor as audit artifacts. The alternative Nominate-then-Select policy uses the same upstream outputs but allows the criterion-compatible set to determine a different committed diagnosis. Their comparison tests whether criterion compatibility is more useful as an audit or as a selector. Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} describes the architecture in detail.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions.

First, it presents an auditable hybrid architecture that combines LLM diagnosis, explicit ICD-10 criterion checking, and deterministic rules. The system records criterion states and supporting evidence instead of relying only on a free-form rationale. The trace can be inspected and replayed, but it does not prove that the LLM's hidden reasoning is faithful or that the checker judgments are clinically correct~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}.

Second, it presents a stage-wise failure-localization and empirical evaluation framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection, quantifies the primary-selection bottleneck, and tests whether criterion-based finalization and several primary-selection methods close the observed gap. The absence of improvement under the evaluated selection interventions narrows several simple explanations under the tested settings, but it does not show that better selectors or additional clinical information can never help.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

Psychiatric LLM studies cover several different tasks, including examination questions, short vignettes, diagnostic reasoning, and comparison with clinicians~\citep{qiu2023largeai,li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. These studies show that LLMs can produce plausible clinical answers, but performance depends on the input, label space, and reference standard. Detecting depression from text or audio is also different from choosing one primary diagnosis among several overlapping disorders~\citep{li2025addreview}.

Chinese psychiatric datasets make disorder-level evaluation possible. LingxiDiagBench includes static diagnosis and dynamic consultation over the synthetic LingxiDiag-16K corpus and reports a clear drop from broad depression--anxiety classification to fine-grained diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. MDD-5k provides a second synthetic Chinese diagnostic-dialogue resource based on anonymized cases~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. These datasets support reproducible experiments, but they do not replace real-patient validation.

Existing systems also differ in whether they can ask questions. MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, and DSM5AgentFlow study inquiry or interactive diagnostic workflows~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels focus more on diagnosis from information already available in records or transcripts~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. Interactive systems can obtain missing evidence, while fixed-input systems isolate how well a model uses the same evidence. This thesis studies the fixed-input setting.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning}

Retrieval-augmented generation conditions a model on external records selected for the current query rather than relying only on information encoded in model parameters~\citep{lewis2020rag}. In-context-learning studies similarly show that semantically related demonstrations can be retrieved to construct a query-specific prompt and that performance depends on which examples are selected~\citep{liu2022incontext}. In transcript-based diagnosis, retrieved cases can therefore provide prompt-side examples of symptom descriptions and diagnostic labels. They are demonstrations rather than patient evidence and should not directly determine the committed diagnosis.

Similarity retrieval can expose useful corpus-local regularities, but it can also reproduce the frequency and wording patterns of the indexed data. Ordinary nearest-neighbor retrieval may repeatedly select examples from common labels, while a parent-balanced policy changes the label composition of the prompt. It may also change the number, ordering, and total token length of the retrieved examples. A comparison between global and parent-balanced retrieval consequently evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating balancing as a single causal factor.

These considerations motivate the retrieval experiments in this thesis. No retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval are compared using the same training-set retrieval index, evaluation cases, label projection, and scoring implementation. The comparison is repeated in both the Single and HiED pipelines so that retrieval behavior is not inferred from only one architecture.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

Medical multi-agent systems assign different roles to LLMs and combine their outputs through consultation, debate, or adaptive collaboration~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. AMIE and related work also show the value of broad differential diagnosis, realistic consultation, and expert review~\citep{mcduff2025amie,tu2025conversational}. These systems demonstrate that structured interaction can help, but adding agents does not guarantee a better final answer. Agents may repeat the same model bias, and free-form discussion can make it difficult to identify which component changed the decision.

For this thesis, the important issue is controlled attribution. If ranking, criterion checking, debate, and finalization all change at once, an accuracy difference cannot show which change helped. HiED therefore compares two policies that reuse the same ranked candidates and checker outputs: Direct-Answer keeps the diagnostician's decision, whereas Nominate-then-Select uses criterion compatibility to choose the primary.

\section{Auditability and Criterion Grounding}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

A fluent explanation is not proof of faithful reasoning. Model-generated chains can sound coherent while not reflecting the factors that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. In healthcare, explanation quality, interpretability, and clinical validity therefore need separate evaluation~\citep{rudin2019stop,huang2024xiai}.

Diagnostic-chain and symptom-based methods make outputs easier to inspect~\citep{chen2025cod,lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. However, a generated reasoning chain is still another model output. This thesis uses the narrower term \emph{auditability}: an external record that links transcript evidence, criterion states, rule results, and the final output. The record can be checked and replayed, but it does not reveal the model's hidden reasoning or turn checker outputs into clinical ground truth.

Constraint-logic systems are the closest related approach because they translate diagnostic criteria into explicit rules~\citep{kim2025clp}. HiED keeps the learned diagnostician separate from the criterion and rule layer. Under Direct-Answer, the structured layer audits the decision; under Nominate-then-Select, it is tested as a selector. Among the reviewed studies, we did not identify an evaluation that uses this separation to compare reference-label retention with primary selection on the same Chinese psychiatric transcripts.

\section{Structured Diagnostic Knowledge and Primary Selection}

Knowledge-based diagnosis methods represent relations among symptoms, disorders, and missing information. MedKGI supports information-guided inquiry, and DKEC uses structured domain knowledge for label prediction~\citep{wang2025medkgi,ge2024dkec}. Such relations may help when disorders share symptoms but differ in onset, duration, exclusions, or causal direction.

HiED does not implement a full knowledge-graph selector. Instead, this literature motivates future work if simple criterion counts and local re-ranking are not enough. A stronger selector may need to compare which diagnosis best explains the full presentation, not only how many criteria each diagnosis satisfies.

\noindent\textit{Note:} ``Not reported'' indicates that the output or evaluation was not described in the reviewed publication; it does not establish that the underlying implementation lacks the capability.

The comparison shows that prior systems address complementary parts of the diagnostic process. Interactive systems can collect missing information, whereas fixed-input systems permit controlled evaluation under identical evidence. Some methods expose candidate hypotheses or criterion-related outputs, but the reviewed studies do not jointly evaluate candidate detection, criterion verification with reference-label retention, and primary selection on the same fixed cases. HiED is designed to make these three output views separately measurable rather than to claim superiority across all clinical or conversational capabilities.

\section{Research Gap and Research Questions}

Prior work shows that LLMs can diagnose psychiatric cases, retrieve examples, use diagnostic criteria, and work in multi-agent systems. Five gaps remain. First, conventional text classifiers, single LLMs, retrieval-augmented LLMs, and structured systems are not always compared under the same split and endpoint. Second, final accuracy does not show whether a reference label was absent from the candidate list or present but not selected as the committed primary. Third, global nearest-neighbor retrieval and parent-balanced retrieval require matched comparison across architectures. Fourth, criterion-based finalization and later primary-selection methods must be evaluated separately from candidate generation and retrieval. Fifth, it remains unclear whether the observed Top-3--Top-1 selection gap persists across model sizes, external corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope. The precise gap addressed here is that HiED retains candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection as separately observable stages evaluated on the same cases.

This thesis therefore uses five research questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?
\item[RQ2] How often is a reference label present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?
\item[RQ3] How do no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval compare in matched Single and HiED pipelines?
\item[RQ4] Do criterion-based re-selection or the tested primary-selection methods improve over Direct-Answer?
\item[RQ5] To what extent do the main findings persist across model sizes, external synthetic corpora, and changes in the configured diagnostic scope, and what error patterns characterize the remaining failures?
\end{description}

RQ1 and RQ3 use matched comparisons on the same fixed internal test set, with common label projection and evaluation code. Clinical effectiveness, transcript-only human performance, and criterion-level clinical validity remain future work.

\section{Transcript Input and Configured Diagnostic Scope}

HiED processes one Chinese psychiatric consultation transcript at a time. The input is an ordered sequence of turns, each marked as \texttt{Clinician} or \texttt{Patient}; speaker labels and turn order are preserved in the prompt. Transcripts longer than the available prompt budget are deterministically truncated by retaining complete turns from the beginning and end, with an explicit marker for the omitted middle. The system does not summarize or invent missing text. This is a fixed-input study: the system cannot ask the patient a follow-up question during inference.\ct{Is this about input and transcript format?}

ICD-10 is the diagnostic coding system used to name the disorders in this study~\citep{who2019icd10}. The system does not search all ICD-10 diagnoses. Before a run begins, it loads one fixed \emph{configured diagnostic profile} that is used for every patient. The main diagnostic profile contains fourteen configured diagnostic categories. F41 and F43 include subtype-level categories, so the fourteen categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels for scoring. Chapter~\ref{ch:data} defines how mixed in-scope and out-of-scope reference codes are projected for evaluation.

Three terms are used throughout the architecture. A \emph{configured diagnostic category} is an ICD-10 code that the diagnostician may rank and for which the system defines a corresponding criterion checker. The \emph{criterion-checker set} is the collection of those fourteen checker definitions; it is not a learned memory bank. Each criterion receives one of three model-generated evidence states: \texttt{met} when the transcript supports it, \texttt{not\_met} when the transcript contains evidence against it, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the transcript supports neither conclusion. The rules are study-specific operationalizations informed by ICD-10 descriptions, not a reproduction of the full official manual and not independently clinician-validated ground truth.

\section{Global Top-5 and Parent-Balanced Case Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

The Similar-Case Retriever adds relevant training examples to the diagnostician prompt. It follows the retrieval-augmented generation pattern~\citep{lewis2020rag}: BGE-M3 is the multilingual text-embedding model used to convert the query transcript and training transcripts into normalized dense vectors~\citep{chen2024bgem3}. FAISS is the vector similarity-search library and index used for retrieval, not a standalone data store~\citep{douze2024faiss}. FAISS inner-product search over normalized vectors corresponds to cosine-similarity ranking. The study evaluates no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval; the final retrieval setting is selected using the public validation split.

The retrieval classes are fixed by the configured diagnostic profile and are identical for every patient.\ct{Are the candidate classes pre-determined, or patient-specific?} Only the example selected within each class depends on the current transcript. Subtypes that share a parent code, such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2, share one retrieval class. Global Top-5 always returns the five nearest eligible index entries. Parent-balanced retrieval attempts to return at most one high-similarity example for each of the eleven scoring parents and can therefore supply up to eleven examples. This policy is intended to reduce domination by frequent classes, but it also changes example count, label composition, ordering, prompt length, and possibly the available transcript budget. Retrieved cases are included only in the Diagnostician prompt; they do not enter checker prompts or directly choose the final output.

The three conditions use the same training-set retrieval index and embedding model. Their comparison therefore evaluates complete retrieval configurations rather than isolating parent balancing as a single causal factor.

\section{Ranked Differential Generation}

The primary diagnostician configuration uses Qwen3-32B with 4-bit Activation-aware Weight Quantization (AWQ). Its input contains the transcript, the configured diagnostic profile, and optional retrieved examples. Its prompt requests a ranked candidate list containing five ordered diagnoses from that profile, a short reason for each, one primary field chosen from the first three, and at most one separate comorbid field. In clinical prose this is a ranked differential diagnosis; as a formal artifact it is the ranked candidate list. The primary field and the ranked list are stored separately. In the running example, F41.1 can therefore remain in the Top-3 even though F32 is emitted as primary.

The main diagnostician configuration is a system specification, not an ablation. It uses greedy decoding and does not request a calibrated probability or an abstention decision. Confidence-based sampling, calibration, and selective-output approaches are evaluated only as additional selection analyses or future-work directions.\ct{Is this an ablation study? If so, why in Appendix?}

\section{Criterion-Level Evidence Checking}

Each of the fourteen configured diagnostic categories has a corresponding criterion checker based on the study's operational rule set.\ct{Is this ICD 10?} The rules are linked to ICD-10 disorder descriptions~\citep{who2019icd10}, but several thresholds are simplified study rules. Appendix~\ref{app:configured-profile} lists the diagnostic codes and configured thresholds so readers can distinguish the study implementation from official clinical guidance.

For each criterion, the checker returns \texttt{met} when the transcript supports the criterion, \texttt{not\_met} when the transcript contains evidence against it, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the available transcript does not resolve it.\ct{What 3 states?} It may also return a short transcript span linked to the judgment. Each configured diagnostic category is checked as an independent task using the transcript and its category-specific operational criteria. Retrieved examples do not enter checker prompts, and checkers do not communicate with one another. All fourteen configured categories are checked for every case, yielding one category-level audit record for each.

Missing information is not negative evidence. A criterion is \texttt{not\_met} only when the transcript contains evidence against it; otherwise unresolved evidence remains \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}.

\section{Deterministic Diagnostic Compatibility Aggregation}

The Compatibility Auditor is a deterministic rule-based module. For each configured diagnostic category, it reads the Criterion Checker states, applies the predefined compatibility rule, adds the diagnosis to the criterion-compatible set when that rule is satisfied, and retains the rule application in the audit trace. A rule may require all criteria, a minimum number of core and total criteria, a symptom count, or particular criterion groups. The same checker trace always produces the same compatible set. Compatibility with a configured study rule is not primacy and does not mean that the diagnosis is clinically correct or should be primary. Several diagnoses may be designated compatible in the same case.

Checker states are model-generated and have not been validated as clinician ground truth. They create an evidence-linked record that can be inspected and replayed, but they do not prove that the criterion judgments or evidence spans are correct.

\section{Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select Finalization}

HiED defines two finalization policies over the same upstream ranked candidates, criterion states, and criterion-compatible set. Direct-Answer (DA) is the default policy and preserves the diagnostician's emitted decision. Nominate-then-Select (NtS) tests whether criterion-derived compatibility can improve the committed primary diagnosis and emits one selected diagnosis.

Under Direct-Answer (DA), the diagnostician's primary field becomes the committed diagnosis; the first ranked diagnosis is used only when that field is missing. An optional comorbidity is retained when it differs from the primary. Outputs from the Criterion Checker and Compatibility Auditor do not replace the DA committed diagnosis.

Nominate-then-Select (NtS) uses criterion compatibility to choose the committed diagnosis. For configured category $d$, its criterion-support ratio is
\[
\operatorname{met\_ratio}(d)=\min\!\left(1,\frac{\text{number of \texttt{met} criteria for }d}{\text{required criterion count for }d}\right).
\]
The denominator is the required count stored in the configured rule, not the total number of listed criteria. NtS first selects the earliest criterion-compatible diagnosis in the five-diagnosis ranking. If none of those five is criterion-compatible, it selects another criterion-compatible diagnosis by descending met ratio, with ties resolved by the predefined configured-category order. If no diagnosis is criterion-compatible, it falls back to the first ranked diagnosis.

NtS emits only the re-selected primary. DA and NtS therefore reuse the same ranked candidates and checker traces but differ in both the primary-selection rule and secondary-label emission. The primary analysis consequently uses Top-1 Accuracy. Exact Match and classwise F1 for NtS also reflect its single-label output contract and are not interpreted as isolated effects of primary re-selection. Additional primary-selection methods are defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental} and do not alter the core architecture described here.

\section{Recorded Audit Outputs}

The audit artifacts include the predicted diagnosis set, ranked differential, criterion states, short evidence spans, criterion-compatible diagnoses, finalization source, and execution failures. A reviewer can therefore challenge the candidate ranking, an individual criterion judgment, a study rule, or the final selection separately.

This record follows the definition of auditability in Section~\ref{sec:auditability-definition}. It makes the system easier to inspect, but it does not expose the LLM's hidden reasoning, prove clinical correctness, or replace real-patient validation.

\section{Model and Inference Configuration}

The main system uses Qwen3-32B with 4-bit Activation-aware Weight Quantization (AWQ). AWQ reduces model-memory requirements and allows the 32-billion-parameter backbone to be served locally without changing the prompt, diagnostic scope, or finalization logic. No main-system component fine-tunes model weights. vLLM is the inference and serving backend for loading the model and generating outputs; it is not a diagnostic component and does not alter prompts, criteria, rules, or finalization. The system uses greedy decoding, an 8,192-token context window, a 1,536-token output cap, structured JSON output, guided decoding where supported, schema validation, and checker-output recovery for duplicate or invalid criterion items.

Runs are checked for case count, valid outputs, parse failures, ranked-output availability, and candidate validity. A checker-output recovery occurs when an LLM response can be parsed but contains duplicate or invalid criterion items. The parser removes those items and retains the valid criterion records. This is a structured-output schema-adherence warning rather than a hard parse failure.

\section{Configured Output Space and Reference-Label Projection}
\label{sec:reference-conventions}

The main system uses fourteen configured ICD-10 diagnostic categories, listed in Table~\ref{tab:configured-profile-summary}. For evaluation, subtype-level categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels. Let $G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x)$ be the raw benchmark codes for case $x$, let $\pi$ map subtype codes such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2 to their scoring parent, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of eleven configured scoring parents. The in-scope reference set is
\[
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\{\pi(c):c\in G_{\mathrm{raw}}(x),\ \pi(c)\in\mathcal{C}\}.
\]
The scored reference-label set is then
\[
G(x)=
\begin{cases}
G_{\mathrm{in}}(x), & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)\neq\varnothing,\\
\{\mathrm{Others}\}, & G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)=\varnothing.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus, a mixed case such as F90 with F41.1 is scored as $\{\mathrm{F41}\}$ rather than $\{\mathrm{Others},\mathrm{F41}\}$. A case is in scope when $G_{\mathrm{in}}(x)$ is nonempty and out of scope otherwise. Because the main system has neither an \emph{Others} output nor a trained unknown detector, it cannot correctly represent an out-of-scope case.

Benchmark-provided diagnosis codes are referred to as \emph{reference labels}. In this thesis, the term denotes the dataset annotations used for scoring and does not imply an independently reviewed clinical standard. For case $x$, $G(x)$ is the deduplicated reference-label set after parent-level projection. LingxiDiag cases may contain more than one reference parent label, and neither dataset provides a psychiatrist-reviewed unique clinical primary diagnosis. The first-listed reference parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses such as the confusion matrix.

In the running example from Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}, F41.1 is scored as F41. If the reference-label set contains F41 and the system emits F32 while ranking F41.1 second, Top-1 is wrong but Top-3 is correct.

\section{Prediction Views and Metrics}

The evaluator keeps three outputs separate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item The \emph{primary view}, $p_1(x)$, is the final primary diagnosis.
\item The \emph{ranked view}, $R_3(x)$, contains the first three distinct parent labels in the method's genuine ranked output. For HiED, it comes from the recorded Diagnostician ranking and is independent of any later DA or NtS finalization decision.
\item The \emph{multilabel view}, $M(x)$, contains the primary and any emitted comorbid parent.
\end{enumerate}

TF--IDF + logistic regression obtains its Top-3 from ranked class scores. HiED obtains it from the Diagnostician ranking. A Single system without a genuine ranked output is not assigned an artificial Top-3.

Because a case may contain more than one reference label, Top-$k$ Accuracy counts a case as correct when at least one reference label appears among the first $k$ predictions. Exact Match, also called subset accuracy, requires the complete predicted label set to equal the complete reference-label set. These are benchmark-agreement measures, not independent measures of the patient's true clinical diagnosis.

\]

Macro-F1 is the unweighted mean of the twelve one-vs-rest label-level F1 scores obtained by binarizing $G(x)$ and $M(x)$ over the fixed scoring classes. Weighted-F1 uses the same label-level F1 scores but weights each class by its reference-label support.

First-listed-label agreement compares the committed diagnosis only with the first-listed projected reference parent. It is used for the confusion matrix but not for the main result. It may differ from any-reference Top-1 Accuracy when a case contains more than one reference parent label.

\section{Failure-Localization Indicators}

For each case, the thesis records three indicators:
\[
D_3(x)=\mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
R(x)=\mathbb{1}[L(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
S(x)=\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
where $L(x)$ is the criterion-compatible parent set. $D_3$ and $S$ use all cases. Reference-label retention, $R$, is calculated only for cases whose reference-label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} parent represented by a corresponding Criterion Checker. This checker-eligible subset contains 915 of the 1,000 test cases, whereas Top-1 and Top-3 use all 1,000 cases. The rates therefore have different denominators and should not be interpreted as consecutive stages of one case-level funnel.

These indicators ask three simple questions: Was a reference label in the genuine ranked Top-3? Was it retained as compatible under the configured rules? Was it selected as primary? They are analysis views of one recorded output, not a claim that every finalization policy executes the steps in that order.

\section{Statistical Analysis}

The case is the unit of analysis, and the two-sided significance level is $\alpha=0.05$. Single-system binary proportions are summarized with 95\% Wilson confidence intervals. Pairwise comparisons require identical case IDs and endpoint definitions. For binary correctness outcomes, the point estimate is the paired difference in correctness proportions, and the exact McNemar test is computed from the two discordant counts: cases correct only under the first system and cases correct only under the second. These counts are reported with the corresponding $p$-value so that the direction and magnitude of the paired difference remain visible.

For paired accuracy differences and finalization interaction contrasts, case IDs are resampled with replacement using 10,000 paired bootstrap replicates and seed 42. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles form the reported 95\% confidence interval. Reported $p$-values are interpreted together with discordant counts, confidence intervals, and effect direction rather than as standalone evidence.

Holm correction is applied separately within the comparison families defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}; unrelated research questions are not pooled into one family. A non-significant result indicates insufficient evidence of a difference and is not treated as proof of equivalence. Paired tests require identical cases and endpoint definitions. When systems also differ in context budget, number of model calls, or other execution settings, the paired result is interpreted as a comparison between complete configurations rather than as an isolated component effect.

\section{Predictive Performance Comparisons}

RQ1 asks how HiED compares with conventional and LLM-based systems under a common split, parent-label mapping, and scoring implementation. Top-1 Accuracy is the main measure for predictive comparison; other metrics characterize ranked and multilabel outputs when the corresponding output views are available. This comparison changes the complete prediction system while keeping the internal evaluation cases, label projection, and scoring implementation fixed. It positions HiED against conventional and single-model baselines but does not isolate one architectural component. Configuration selection and held-out evaluation are kept separate.

\subsection{Public-Validation Configuration Selection}

The public validation split is used to select retrieval separately for Single and HiED--DA. The predefined promotion rule considers Top-1, genuine Top-3 when available, Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1. The selected configuration for each architecture is locked before held-out test evaluation. Validation results are configuration-selection evidence, not an independent test estimate.

\subsection{Held-Out Internal-Test Evaluation}

The held-out comparison includes Single without retrieval, the validation-selected Single configuration, HiED--DA, and HiED--NtS. These rows are complete system configurations rather than cumulative component ablations: retrieval, architecture, ranked-output contract, finalization, and emitted-set cardinality may differ. The held-out split is not used to revise the selected policies.

\subsection{Same-Split Conventional Baselines}

The Majority baseline predicts the most frequent training-corpus parent. TF--IDF + LR is trained on the 13,000 training cases using character $n$-gram features and twelve one-vs-rest logistic-regression classifiers. Class probabilities define its Top-3; labels with probability at least 0.5 form the multilabel output, with the highest-probability class retained when no label reaches the threshold. Single uses one diagnostic LLM call and emits a primary with an optional secondary diagnosis; because it does not emit a genuine ordered differential, Top-3 is undefined. Exact lexical hyperparameters are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

All systems use the same held-out cases, order, parent projection, and scoring code.

\section{Stage-Wise Failure Localization}

RQ2 asks whether benchmark disagreement occurs during candidate detection, criterion verification, or primary selection. The main analysis is the joint distribution of candidate detection, reference-label retention after criterion verification, and primary selection. This analysis uses one recorded HiED--DA output trace and compares the three views on the same eligible cases. It uses the indicators defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}.

\subsection{Candidate Detection}

Candidate detection is represented by $D_3(x)=\mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing]$, where $R_3(x)$ is the genuine ranked Top-3 and $G(x)$ is the reference-label set. It measures whether candidate generation places at least one reference parent among the leading diagnoses; it does not evaluate which diagnosis is selected as primary.

\subsection{Criterion Verification and Reference-Label Retention}

Retention is represented by $R(x)=\mathbb{1}[L(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing]$, where $L(x)$ is the criterion-compatible parent set. It is defined only for checker-eligible cases and therefore has a different denominator from the all-case $D_3$ and $S$ rates. The criterion-compatible set is generated by model-based checking and study-specific rules; it is not a clinician-confirmed diagnosis. Compatible-set size and criterion-state distribution are reported to characterize selectivity as well as retention.

\subsection{Primary Selection}

Primary selection is represented by $S(x)=\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)]$, where $p_1(x)$ is the committed primary diagnosis. Under DA, the committed primary is drawn from the genuine ranking. Cases with $D_3=1$ and $S=0$ therefore identify a reference parent that is ranked but not selected as the committed primary; the definition does not imply clinical recoverability.

\subsection{Detection--Verification--Selection Joint Analysis}

The case-level $2\times2\times2$ table is restricted to the checker-eligible subset for which $D_3$, $R$, and $S$ are all defined. It reports $P(R\mid D_3)$, $P(S\mid R)$, and $P(S\mid D_3,R)$. The strict operational primary-selection subset is $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$: a reference parent is detected and retained after verification but is not selected as the committed primary. The indicators are not strictly nested because criterion checking covers the configured diagnostic scope rather than only the genuine Top-3.

\subsection{Oracle-Based Supporting Analysis}

The oracle considers diagnoses in a broader window formed from the ranking and criterion-compatible set. Diagnoses meeting the support threshold are ordered by met ratio, and adjacent diagnoses within the prespecified ratio difference are linked into clusters. A misclassified case is identified when any cluster contains a reference parent. The analysis uses the reference label, does not require the DA primary and reference to occur in the same cluster, and assumes that identified errors can be corrected without harming correct cases. It is therefore an optimistic upper bound rather than a deployable selector.

\section{Retrieval Configuration Comparisons}

RQ3 compares complete retrieval configurations separately within Single and HiED--DA. Retrieval is varied within each architecture while the model, prompt family, diagnostic scope, decoding settings, and output contract remain fixed. Retrieval configurations are compared primarily by paired Top-1 differences within each architecture, with other native output metrics reported descriptively.

\subsection{Retrieval Conditions}

No retrieval supplies no demonstrations. Global Top-5 inserts the five most similar training cases without parent control. Parent-balanced retrieval attempts to select at most one high-similarity example per configured scoring parent. Retrieved examples are provided only to the Diagnostician.

\subsection{Within-Architecture Comparisons}

The Single comparison family contains no retrieval, global Top-5, and parent-balanced retrieval under a fixed Single prompt, model, label space, decoding setup, and output contract. The HiED--DA family uses the same three retrieval conditions while holding the Diagnostician, Criterion Checkers, compatibility rules, DA finalization, label space, and scoring implementation fixed. Paired tests require identical case IDs and endpoint definitions within each family.

\subsection{Interpretation of Retrieval Contrasts}

Validation results determine the selected retrieval policy for each architecture before the held-out comparison. Test-set retrieval ablations characterize generalization of those choices but do not retrospectively change them. Global and parent-balanced retrieval differ in example count, label composition, ordering, and prompt length as well as balancing policy. Their contrast is therefore interpreted as a comparison of complete retrieval configurations, not an isolated estimate of parent balancing.

\section{Evaluation of Primary-Selection Strategies}

RQ4 first evaluates a controlled change between finalization policies and then evaluates additional primary-selection methods under explicitly weaker attribution contracts. Methods that reuse recorded DA outputs are separated from methods that require new inference; only the former permit relatively direct attribution to the final selection rule. Committed Top-1 agreement is the main measure for comparing primary-selection strategies. Finalization is treated as an implementation mechanism for primary selection, not as a separate diagnostic capability.

\subsection{Controlled Comparison of Finalization Policies}

DA preserves the Diagnostician's emitted primary and optional secondary diagnosis. NtS reuses the recorded ranked candidates, checker states, and criterion-compatible set, performs no additional LLM inference, re-selects one primary, and emits only that diagnosis. The primary contrast is
\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{NtS-DA}}=\mathrm{Top\text{-}1}_{\mathrm{NtS}}-\mathrm{Top\text{-}1}_{\mathrm{DA}}.
\]
Because NtS emits one label, its Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 also reflect output cardinality and are not used to isolate primary re-selection.

\subsection{Retrieval-by-Finalization Interaction}

DA and NtS are compared within each of the three retrieval conditions on identical ordered cases. Exact McNemar tests and paired-bootstrap intervals evaluate each within-condition difference. Difference-in-differences bootstrap contrasts test whether the finalization effect varies across retrieval configurations. Similar point estimates are not treated as evidence of equivalence.

\subsection{Additional Primary-Selection Methods}

Additional methods are grouped by execution contract. Common-output interventions, including deterministic fusion, reuse recorded DA artifacts and permit selector-level attribution within the stated rule. Complete new-inference configurations, including independent pairwise comparison, forced pairwise application, and debate, may change upstream outputs, calls, or context budget and are interpreted only as full-configuration comparisons. Sampling-based aggregation and learned selectors are supporting analyses when their case-level records or execution contracts do not match the primary controlled evidence. Detailed prompts, thresholds, sampling schedules, and tie-breaking rules are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:supporting}.

\section{Robustness and External Synthetic Transfer}

RQ5 examines whether the main benchmark patterns depend on model scale or corpus, how configured scope constrains performance, and which stage-wise error patterns characterize the remaining cases. These analyses change model scale, evaluation corpus, diagnostic scope, or selector training corpus and are interpreted as robustness or transfer evidence rather than component ablations. Because they address distinct questions, each uses the endpoint declared in its subsection rather than a pooled composite measure.

\subsection{Model-Scale Sensitivity}

Qwen3-4B, 8B, 14B, and 32B are evaluated on the same internal cases using a common prompt family, diagnostic scope, retrieval policy, decoding contract, and endpoint. Run-integrity, parsing, and abstention events are recorded separately. This analysis evaluates within-family scale sensitivity and does not establish robustness across unrelated model families.

\subsection{External Predictive Performance}

MDD-5k is evaluated using the checker-covered full set, the active-profile in-scope subset, and a restricted F32/F41 subset. Full-set Top-1 is the primary external endpoint. In-scope multi-way metrics and restricted binary metrics are secondary views with distinct label-space interpretations. MDD-5k is an external synthetic corpus, not a clinical validation cohort.

\subsection{External Top-3--Top-1 Selection Gap}

The common external DA trace supplies genuine Top-3 and committed Top-1 outputs on the same ordered cases. Their case-level difference identifies reference labels that are ranked but not selected as the committed primary. A matched external criterion-retention trace is unavailable, so the external analysis does not estimate $R$ or reproduce the full internal detection--verification--selection analysis.

\subsection{Diagnostic Scope Expansion}

The scope-expansion analysis changes the configured diagnostic profile without updating model weights. It evaluates cases whose reference parent becomes representable and defines recovery as committing that newly representable parent as Top-1. The experiment distinguishes output-space representability from behavioral recovery; it does not test learned clinical adaptation.

\subsection{Cross-Corpus Selector Transfer}

Cross-corpus analyses include TF--IDF classification, standalone BGE-M3 case-neighbor label voting, and score fusion where the required records are available. Same-split predictive baselines, within-corpus fitting, and cross-corpus transfer are reported separately because they answer different questions.

\section{Psychiatrist Case-Review Study}
\label{sec:psychiatrist-case-review}

At the time of this manuscript revision, the psychiatrist case review was ongoing. This section reports the prespecified case-sampling strategy, review procedure, outcome measures, and analysis plan. The main review outcome is the proportion of reviewed ranked-and-retained nonselection cases for which psychiatrist consensus prefers a benchmark reference diagnosis as the primary diagnosis. Preliminary or partially completed review results are not used in the quantitative conclusions of Chapters~6 and~7. The final review results will be reported in Chapter~8 after all planned case assessments and agreement analyses are complete.

\subsection{Case Sampling}

The primary review population is sampled from checker-eligible internal cases satisfying $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$. In this operational pattern, a benchmark reference diagnosis is present in the genuine ranked Top-3 and retained after criterion verification but is not selected as the committed primary diagnosis. Cases are selected without using preliminary reviewer judgments. Any secondary comparison strata are sampled and reported separately so that they do not alter the denominator of the primary outcome.

The completed report will state the planned and completed sample sizes, sampling fractions, exclusions, replacement rules, number of participating psychiatrists, reviewer qualifications and clinical experience, language competence, and prior exposure to the benchmark or system. These characteristics are not inferred from partial review data.

\subsection{Transcript-Only Review}

Reviewers first receive only the transcript and the diagnostic scope available to the system. The benchmark reference labels, system outputs, $D_3$--$R$--$S$ pattern, and other reviewers' judgments remain concealed. For each case, reviewers record a preferred primary diagnosis, clinically plausible alternatives, whether the transcript supports a unique primary diagnosis, whether additional clinical information is required, and the information needed to resolve the differential. Transcript-only ratings are locked before system outputs are shown.

\subsection{Review of System Outputs}

After the transcript-only ratings are locked, reviewers inspect the system's ranked differential, criterion states, linked evidence spans, criterion-compatible candidates, and committed primary diagnosis while the benchmark reference remains concealed. They assess whether the system-selected diagnosis remains clinically plausible and whether the audit trace accurately identifies supporting, contradictory, and unresolved information. Audit-trace usefulness is evaluated separately from agreement with the committed diagnosis.

After these ratings are locked, the benchmark reference is revealed for a final review of the model--reference disagreement. Reviewers classify whether the case is most consistent with a primary-selection error, insufficient transcript information, multiple clinically plausible diagnoses, ambiguity in the benchmark reference label, criterion-verification error, or diagnostic-scope limitation. Categories may overlap, and unresolved cases remain explicitly unresolved.

\subsection{Outcome Measures and Agreement Analysis}

The primary outcome is the \emph{clinician-confirmed primary-selection error rate}: the proportion of reviewed ranked-and-retained nonselection cases for which the prespecified reviewer-consensus rule identifies a benchmark reference diagnosis as the preferred primary diagnosis. In compact form,
\[
\widehat{\pi}_{\mathrm{clinical}}
=
\frac{n_{\mathrm{reference\ preferred}}}
{n_{D_3=1,\,R=1,\,S=0\ \mathrm{reviewed}}}.
\]
The term \emph{clinician-confirmed} refers only to reviewer consensus under this transcript-review contract; it does not convert benchmark labels into clinical ground truth or constitute clinical validation.

Secondary outcomes include reference-diagnosis plausibility within the differential, unique-primary determinability, transcript-information sufficiency, required additional information, plausibility of the committed diagnosis, presence of multiple plausible diagnoses, benchmark-reference ambiguity, disagreement classification, and audit-trace usefulness. Inter-rater agreement is reported separately for preferred-primary selection, transcript sufficiency, and disagreement classification. The reviewer-consensus rule, panel composition, raw agreement, and prespecified chance-corrected agreement statistic will be reported before any substantive interpretation of the completed outcomes.

The similarity of the three point estimates does not itself establish the absence of a retrieval-by-finalization interaction. The prespecified interaction contrasts are small: global minus balanced is $-0.001$ (95\% CI [$-0.013$, $0.010$]), none minus balanced is $0.000$ (95\% CI [$-0.012$, $0.012$]), and global minus none is $-0.001$ (95\% CI [$-0.011$, $0.009$]). \subsection{Primary versus Set-Level Effects}

DA may emit a secondary diagnosis, whereas NtS emits one diagnosis. NtS Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 therefore reflect both primary re-selection and output cardinality. Their differences cannot be interpreted as evidence of better primary selection or probabilistic calibration. A separate checker-sensitivity analysis leaves DA Top-1 unchanged across the five full-size settings, as expected because DA does not allow the downstream audit to overwrite the Diagnostician's primary.

\section{Chapter Summary}

The in-domain evidence supports five conclusions. First, TF--IDF + logistic regression is the strongest same-split predictor, so HiED is not an accuracy-leading system on this benchmark. Second, retrieval improves Single relative to no retrieval, whereas the marginal HiED Top-1 contrasts lack stable statistical support. Third, candidate detection and reference-label retention exceed primary-selection accuracy, with 272 cases in which a reference label is ranked and retained but not selected. Fourth, NtS lowers Top-1 under all three retrieval conditions, and no additional method provides a clear and attributable improvement. Fifth, the Top-3--Top-1 selection gap remains observable across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes, while disagreements concentrate in a small number of asymmetric diagnostic pairs. Chapter~\ref{ch:external} examines which parts of this pattern and intervention behavior remain observable under external synthetic distribution shift.

This chapter analyzes disagreements between model outputs and benchmark reference labels. The primary analysis uses the parent-balanced HiED--DA trace on the fixed 1,000-case internal test set because complete ranking, checker, compatibility, and finalization artifacts are available for that run. Candidate-detection and diagnostic-set analyses use all 1,000 cases. Analyses involving reference-label retention use the 915 checker-eligible cases, and external diagnostic-scope findings use separately identified MDD-5k populations.

The term \emph{failure} denotes an operational benchmark disagreement at a recorded system stage. It does not establish a psychiatrist-confirmed diagnostic error or its clinical cause. The stages are also not a strict runtime funnel because criterion verification covers all configured diagnostic categories rather than only the genuine Top-3. Denominators and operational conditions are therefore stated in each section.

\section{Candidate-Detection Failures}

A candidate-detection failure occurs when no benchmark reference parent appears in the genuine Top-3, or $D_3=0$. This condition occurs in 198 of the 1,000 internal cases. Eighty-five of these cases use the \emph{Others} reference and are not eligible for reference-label retention analysis, leaving 113 checker-eligible candidate-detection failures.

The full-scope checker retains a reference parent in 79 of these 113 cases despite its absence from the Diagnostician's Top-3; the remaining 34 cases neither rank nor retain a reference parent. This non-nested profile shows that candidate generation and criterion verification expose different recorded signals. Across all 198 candidate-detection failures, the committed primary is concentrated in F32 (109 cases) and F41 (56 cases), but these benchmark counts do not determine whether the omitted reference was clinically preferable.

\section{Criterion-Verification Failures}

A criterion-verification failure occurs when a reference parent appears in the genuine Top-3 but is not retained in the criterion-compatible set, or $D_3=1$ and $R=0$. Twenty-four of the 915 checker-eligible cases meet this condition. The committed primary nevertheless matches a reference parent in twelve cases; the other twelve have an incorrect committed primary and form the criterion-verification failure group used in the mutually exclusive comparison in Section~\ref{sec:ch8-criterion-evidence}.

Across the 36 reference-associated checker outputs in these 24 cases, 286 criterion states are recorded: 17.1\% are \texttt{met}, 23.4\% are \texttt{not\_met}, and 59.4\% are \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}. These are criterion states rather than case counts. The high unresolved share describes the model-generated rule inputs associated with non-retention, but it does not establish that the clinical information was absent or that the checker judgment was correct.

\section{Primary-Selection Failures}

The strict primary-selection failure condition is $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$: a reference parent is ranked and retained after criterion verification but another diagnosis is selected as the committed primary. This condition occurs in 272 of the 915 checker-eligible cases. It is narrower than the 284-case Top-3--Top-1 gap reported over all internal cases.

\section{Diagnostic-Set Errors}

Diagnostic-set errors are separated from primary-selection failures. The internal run has 518 cases with a correct committed primary and 409 cases with exact predicted-set agreement. Consequently, 109 cases have a correct primary but an incorrect full diagnostic set. Fifty-seven contain at least one extra predicted label, 54 omit at least one reference label, and two exhibit both conditions.

This distinction is also required when interpreting finalization policies. DA may emit a primary and an optional secondary diagnosis, whereas NtS emits one diagnosis. Differences in Exact Match, Macro-F1, or Weighted-F1 between these policies therefore reflect both primary re-selection and output cardinality rather than an isolated change in primary-selection quality.

\section{Diagnostic-Scope Failures}

A diagnostic-scope failure occurs when the benchmark reference diagnosis is unavailable in the configured output space. The full 925-case MDD-5k evaluation contains 47 such cases. They remain part of the full external performance estimate because a system cannot select a diagnosis that it is not configured to emit.

The separate configuration-expansion experiment increases the profile from fourteen to 33 configured categories and makes 58 sampled reference diagnoses representable. None becomes the committed Top-1 diagnosis (0/58), as reported in Table~\ref{tab:scope-expansion-results}. The result distinguishes successful output-space expansion from behavioral recovery; it does not show that label-space expansion is ineffective in general.

\section{Chapter Summary}

The stage-wise analysis identifies distinct benchmark failure locations. Candidate detection fails in 198 internal cases, while full-scope checking still retains a reference parent in 79 of the 113 checker-eligible cases in this group. Twenty-four ranked references are not retained after verification, and twelve of those cases also have an incorrect primary. The largest strict subset contains 272 ranked-and-retained cases in which another diagnosis is selected, with F41-to-F32 disagreements predominating. Criterion-state composition in this subset closely resembles that of correctly selected cases, indicating that aggregate checker-state ratios alone do not resolve primacy. Diagnostic-set mismatches and diagnostic-scope failures remain separate problems, and clinical causes await completion of the psychiatrist case review.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

\section{Summary of Principal Findings}

The internal results first establish predictive position: TF--IDF + logistic regression is the strongest same-split predictor, while HiED provides ranked differentials, criterion-level audit artifacts, and stage-wise observability rather than benchmark accuracy leadership. Within HiED, genuine Top-3 reference coverage reaches 0.802 and committed Top-1 agreement reaches 0.518. A reference parent is retained in 857 of 915 checker-eligible cases, and 272 cases rank and retain a reference parent without selecting it as the committed primary.

The controlled finalization-policy comparison does not convert this gap into a gain. NtS decreases Top-1 by 2.8--2.9 percentage points across all three internal retrieval conditions, and no evaluated additional primary-selection configuration provides a clear and attributable improvement over DA. On MDD-5k, Top-3 also remains above Top-1, but the missing external criterion-retention trace prevents a complete external detection--verification--selection analysis.

These findings support a benchmark-local primary-selection bottleneck in the evaluated system. They do not establish that every disagreement is a clinically verified selection error, that transcript-only diagnosis is intrinsically underdetermined, or that the pattern generalizes to patients. The ongoing psychiatrist case review is not used in the substantive conclusions until its prespecified assessments are complete.

\section{Candidate Detection Is Necessary but Not Sufficient}

Candidate generation remains necessary because a selector cannot choose a diagnosis that is absent from the ranked differential. The 198 internal candidate-detection failures therefore identify a direct target for improved ranking. Retrieval supports this stage in the Single architecture: both retrieval configurations improve Single relative to no retrieval. However, the corresponding Top-1 changes are smaller and not statistically supported in the full HiED pipeline.

The 284-case Top-3--Top-1 difference shows why candidate coverage is insufficient. In these cases, a reference parent has already entered the genuine ranked differential but is not selected as primary. The strong TF--IDF baseline reinforces this limitation of a candidate-only explanation: the same synthetic transcripts contain predictive benchmark signal that the evaluated HiED configuration does not fully use. At the same time, corpus-specific lexical cues may contribute to TF--IDF performance, so this result does not establish that every case contains clinically sufficient information.

\section{Criterion Verification Does Not Determine the Primary Diagnosis}

Criterion verification asks whether each configured diagnosis is compatible with the transcript under study-specific rules. It does not compare which compatible diagnosis best explains the presentation. Common features such as sleep disturbance, fatigue, low mood, worry, avoidance, and reduced concentration can therefore support several categories simultaneously. Compatibility is not comparative diagnosis.

This distinction explains the high-retention, low-selectivity pattern. A reference parent is retained in 0.937 of checker-eligible cases, while the median criterion-compatible set contains six of fourteen configured categories. The criterion-state composition of strict primary-selection failures is also close to that of correctly selected cases. Criterion verification remains useful for exposing support, contradiction, unresolved evidence, and rule application, but neither compatible-set size nor aggregate state ratios determine primacy.

\section{Primary Selection Remains the Main Bottleneck}

The strict computational evidence consists of 272 cases satisfying $D_3=1$, $R=1$, and $S=0$. The nearest reference parent is second in 171 cases and third in 101, and F41-to-F32 is the dominant nearest-reference-to-selected pair. These cases have passed both the ranked-candidate and criterion-retention conditions used in the analysis, leaving primary selection as the recorded location of disagreement.

This is a system-level benchmark claim rather than a clinical judgment. Some cases may reflect an incorrect primary-selection decision; others may contain insufficient transcript information, multiple plausible diagnoses, an ambiguous reference label, or an erroneous checker state. The ongoing psychiatrist case review is designed to estimate this composition. Until it is complete, the 272 cases should not be described as uniformly recoverable or clinically erroneous.

\section{Why the Evaluated Selection Strategies Did Not Close the Gap}

The evaluated strategies mostly recombine existing signals. NtS promotes diagnoses from the criterion-compatible artifacts, while deterministic fusion combines rank, compatibility, and criterion support. Pairwise comparison and debate add new inference but still operate over the same fixed transcript, and self-consistency measures repeated model preference rather than acquiring new discriminating evidence.

The results suggest that support counts and repeated preferences do not encode the comparative relations required for primacy. Relevant distinctions may include onset, temporal course, syndrome dominance, exclusions, causal direction, and whether one diagnosis better explains symptoms assigned to another. NtS and deterministic fusion reduce performance, forced pairwise override and debate are lower than DA, and the small sampling-based point estimates lack statistical support. These findings apply to the tested configurations; they do not establish that every pairwise, debate, fusion, or sampling method must fail.

\section{Primary-Diagnosis and Diagnostic-Set Errors Are \texorpdfstring{\mbox{Distinct}}{Distinct}}

Primary selection and multilabel set construction use different output contracts. A system may select a benchmark-consistent primary while adding an unsupported secondary diagnosis or omitting a reference comorbidity. This occurs in 109 internal cases with correct primaries but non-exact diagnostic sets.

The distinction is essential for DA--NtS interpretation. DA may emit an optional secondary diagnosis, whereas NtS emits a single diagnosis. A change in Exact Match or label-level F1 can therefore arise from output cardinality even when primary-selection accuracy decreases. Set-level agreement should not be described as calibration, and it cannot substitute for the committed Top-1 comparison.

\section{Diagnostic Scope Expansion Does Not Ensure Diagnostic Recovery}

Configured scope determines whether a diagnosis can be emitted at all. The 47 out-of-scope MDD-5k cases therefore represent a distinct limitation from selecting among available labels. Expanding the profile successfully increases representability, but none of the 58 newly representable reference parents becomes the committed primary.

Availability in the output space is thus necessary but insufficient for behavioral adaptation. A broader profile may also require diagnostic examples, criterion definitions, comparative training, prompt adaptation, or a selector that has learned when the new categories should displace existing ones. The 0/58 result concerns the tested configuration-only expansion and is not evidence against ontology expansion as a general method.

\section{External Synthetic Transfer and Corpus-Specific Signals}

On the common 925-case MDD-5k trace, genuine Top-3 reaches 0.8508 while committed Top-1 reaches 0.5708, leaving 259 ranked-but-not-selected cases. NtS and deterministic fusion again decrease Top-1, while sampling-based aggregation yields small positive but unsupported point estimates. These findings are consistent with transfer of the ranking-to-selection gap and the limitations of the evaluated selection strategies.

The external evidence is incomplete. MDD-5k lacks a criterion-retention trace matched to the internal analysis, so it cannot reproduce $R$, $P(R\mid D_3)$, or $P(S\mid D_3,R)$. The restricted F32/F41 task is easier than the full multi-way task, and both corpora are synthetic. Cross-corpus TF--IDF and case-neighbor results also degrade substantially relative to within-corpus evaluation, indicating that fitted lexical and similarity signals are corpus dependent. The external results are therefore consistent with, but do not fully confirm, the internal stage-wise interpretation.

\section{Clinical Interpretation of Benchmark Disagreements}

The computational strata cannot determine whether the system, the transcript, or the benchmark reference is clinically responsible for a disagreement. The psychiatrist case-review study addresses this boundary by separating transcript-only primary choice, reference plausibility, information sufficiency, multiple plausible diagnoses, and review of recorded system outputs.

At the time of this manuscript revision, the study is ongoing. Partial judgments and agreement estimates are excluded to avoid selective reporting. Once complete, the review can estimate what proportion of ranked-and-retained nonselection cases are judged to be primary-selection errors and what proportion instead reflect insufficient evidence or reference ambiguity. It remains an expert review of synthetic transcripts rather than clinical validation.

\section{Implications for Auditable Psychiatric Diagnostic \texorpdfstring{\mbox{Systems}}{Systems}}

Audit and selection should remain conceptually separate. HiED records ranked candidates, criterion states, linked evidence, criterion-compatible diagnoses, and finalization source. These artifacts make disagreements inspectable, but they neither reveal hidden reasoning nor establish clinical correctness. The demonstrated contribution is analytical traceability.

Future selectors require comparative evidence rather than another independent compatibility score. Structured-knowledge systems such as MedKGI and DKEC motivate relations among symptoms, diagnoses, temporal course, exclusions, and missing information~\citep{wang2025medkgi,ge2024dkec}. A diagnostic graph could represent support, contradiction, causal direction, explanatory dependence, and comorbidity, then compare which candidate explains the overall presentation with fewer unresolved conflicts. This direction remains unevaluated.

Fixed transcripts also impose an evidence boundary. Interactive systems such as MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, and DSM5AgentFlow can ask additional questions~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}, whereas fixed-record systems such as MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels operate on available records~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. When duration, course, substance use, exclusion, or episode history is unresolved, a future system should identify the discriminating fact, request targeted follow-up information when interaction is possible, or abstain.

Finally, Taiwan is the intended setting rather than a validated population. All quantitative evidence comes from synthetic Chinese corpora. The thesis does not evaluate real Taiwanese patients, local language use, hospital workflow, clinician behavior, or patient outcomes. HiED should therefore remain a research framework for auditable analysis rather than an autonomous diagnostic tool.

\section{Chapter Summary}

Candidate detection is necessary but does not explain the large Top-3--Top-1 gap. Criterion verification retains reference labels at a high rate but evaluates compatibility rather than comparative primacy. The largest strict computational subset is consequently located at primary selection, yet its clinical composition remains unresolved until psychiatrist review is complete. The tested finalization and selection strategies do not supply a clear improvement, and diagnostic-set construction, configured scope, and corpus-specific transfer remain separate limitations. These findings support audit-oriented system design with explicit comparative evidence and bounded claims, not clinical deployment.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

\section{Construct Validity and Measurement Limitations}

The evaluation target is agreement with benchmark reference labels after parent-level projection. It is not an independent judgment of the patient's true diagnosis. Parent projection makes two corpora easier to compare but removes subtype detail. Top-1 Accuracy with a reference-label set is also less strict than matching only the first listed diagnosis. Top-1 Accuracy, Top-3 Accuracy, Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 therefore measure different parts of benchmark performance; none alone measures clinical correctness or safety.

The selection bottleneck is an observed difference among output views, not a proven causal process inside the model. Under Direct-Answer, the primary diagnosis is chosen before the checker creates the audit trace. Top-1 Accuracy and Top-3 Accuracy use all 1,000 test cases, whereas reference-label retention uses 915 checker-eligible cases. These measures help locate errors but should not be read as one nested funnel. The oracle upper-bound recovery analysis is likewise an upper bound, not a deployable selector.

\paragraph{Lexical baseline and construct validity.} The strong internal TF--IDF result may reflect real transcript signal, source-corpus lexical regularities, synthetic-generator artifacts, or a mixture of all three. It narrows the interpretation of the LLM selection bottleneck but does not establish that a lexical classifier is clinically superior or transferable. Conversely, because this stronger same-split baseline exists, HiED errors alone cannot establish that primary diagnosis is intrinsically unrecoverable from a transcript.

\section{Internal Validity and Statistical Limitations}

The official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable, so the main in-domain result uses a 1,000-case held-out split from the original training-source corpus. The cases were excluded from retrieval and development, but the split still shares templates and source distribution with the training data. The thesis does not report a full template-level or embedding near-duplicate audit, and foundation-model exposure to the public corpus cannot be ruled out.

The public validation split is used repeatedly for prompt, rule, sensitivity, and retrieval-configuration selection. Its results are configuration-selection evidence rather than an independent test estimate. The separate internal split reduces direct selection bias in the main estimate, but repeated use of one public split may still favor choices adapted to its corpus-specific patterns.

Not every comparison isolates one component. Single versus HiED changes several modules and measures a package effect. Common-upstream finalization methods hold recorded DA outputs fixed more closely, whereas independent pairwise reasoning, debate, self-consistency, and case-neighbor methods change calls, sampling, context, or input signals. Their results compare the tested configurations rather than establish a causal ranking of method families.

The debate comparison also differs in inference budget. The primary Direct-Answer pipeline uses an 8,192-token context window, whereas the complete advocate--judge debate requires 24,576 tokens and additional model calls. Its result therefore evaluates the full debate configuration rather than the isolated effect of debate under a matched context or compute budget.

Several analyses are exploratory or use small samples. Rare classes have wide uncertainty, and a non-significant difference does not prove equivalence. Holm correction is applied within the predefined families in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental}, not across every analysis in the thesis. Oracle, lexical, BGE-M3, scope, and selection-intervention analyses therefore remain supporting or exploratory evidence even when an isolated result is favorable.

NtS is derived deterministically from the recorded DA ranking, criterion states, and criterion-compatible set without additional LLM calls. This isolates the specified primary-selection rule more closely than a newly generated pipeline run, but NtS emits only one label. Its Top-1 supports a paired primary-selection analysis, while Exact Match and classwise F1 conflate re-selection with output cardinality. The comparison remains an offline benchmark analysis and does not establish prospective behavior or clinical benefit.

Complete internal case-level sampling traces were not retained for SC and CISC. Those results provide weaker auditability than methods with case-level predictions and are treated as descriptive supporting evidence rather than paired selection-intervention evidence.

The primary analysis system also depends on one model family, prompt set, quantized checkpoint, serving stack, context window, and decoding setup. The Qwen3 size analysis does not establish that the same findings hold for unrelated models or runtimes.

\section{External Validity and Clinical Generalizability}

Both quantitative corpora are synthetic. They support controlled and reproducible experiments, but may contain generator-specific language, simplified ambiguity, limited comorbidity, and less noise than real practice. MDD-5k provides a useful distribution shift, but it is still synthetic and does not provide the same verification analysis as the internal set.

An ongoing psychiatrist case-review study uses transcript-only review under the configured diagnostic scope before revealing system outputs or benchmark references. Because the planned assessments and agreement analyses are incomplete, no partial comparator estimate is used in this manuscript. The present version therefore cannot determine whether a unique primary is recoverable by psychiatrists from each transcript or whether some model--reference-label disagreements reflect task ambiguity.

The benchmark reference labels and model-generated criterion states have not yet received a completed independent psychiatrist review. The computational analysis therefore identifies failure locations rather than clinically verified diagnostic errors, and it provides no completed evidence that clinician inspection is improved by the recorded audit trace. Final reporting of the ongoing review must include reviewer selection, panel size, blinding adherence, retrospective-review bias, agreement, unresolved cases, and the applicable institutional ethics determination.

The intended setting is Taiwanese outpatient psychiatry, yet the study does not evaluate real Taiwanese patients, Traditional-Chinese clinical speech, mixed Mandarin--Taiwanese language, local coding practice, or hospital workflow. The configured diagnostic scope also maps unsupported diagnoses to \texttt{Others}. Expanding the list of legal outputs does not by itself teach the model when to use new labels, as shown by the scope-expansion analysis.

No result in this thesis establishes clinical effectiveness, clinician equivalence, treatment benefit, or safe autonomous use. There is no tested clinical abstention or unknown-diagnosis detector, calibrated confidence, subgroup fairness analysis, workflow study, or measurement of automation bias. Runtime cost, per-case token use, and latency were not evaluated as a formal workflow endpoint. Real-patient validation and operational evaluation must therefore precede any deployment claim.

\section{Reproducibility, Governance, and Deployment Boundaries}

Computational reproducibility requires the datasets, exact checkpoint and quantization, retrieval index, configuration, serving environment, structured-decoding implementation, and evaluation code. Greedy decoding reduces sampling variation, but exact identity may still depend on model revision, serving-library version, hardware, and schema recovery. Analytical reproducibility additionally requires case-level predictions, label projection, endpoint definitions, and the declared paired-comparison families. Complete internal SC/CISC traces are a known exception.

Neither form of reproducibility establishes clinical validity. An exactly reproduced criterion state can still be clinically wrong, and a reproduced benchmark gain does not establish workflow value, safety, or patient benefit.

Future work with patient data will require institutional approval, privacy controls, access logging, monitoring, incident procedures, and periodic revalidation. Until these requirements and the clinical studies above are completed, HiED should remain a research and review prototype.

\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis introduced HiED, a hybrid multi-agent framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, deterministic compatibility aggregation, and primary selection while recording the resulting committed primary diagnosis.

The same-split lexical baseline achieved higher Top-1 performance than HiED on the internal benchmark. HiED nevertheless showed substantially higher ranked candidate coverage than committed Top-1 performance, and criterion checking frequently retained a reference diagnosis without determining which compatible diagnosis should be primary. Criterion-derived Nominate-then-Select finalization and the tested pairwise, debate, fusion, and sampling-based selection-intervention configurations did not provide a clear and reproducible improvement in primary selection; supporting case-neighbor analyses likewise transferred poorly. Retrieval improved the Single system, but the results did not support an advantage for parent-balanced over global retrieval or a statistically supported retrieval gain for HiED. The Top-3--Top-1 selection gap also remained visible on the external synthetic corpus.

HiED's contribution is therefore not state-of-the-art benchmark accuracy, but an auditable architecture and evaluation framework that localizes disagreement across candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection. The results are limited to synthetic benchmark data and do not establish clinical validity or autonomous diagnostic safety.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}

This section summarizes how the final experiments answer the five research questions introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:related}. Each answer is limited to benchmark performance on synthetic Chinese psychiatric transcripts and should not be interpreted as evidence of clinical effectiveness.

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] HiED did not outperform the strongest same-split baseline under the common evaluation contract. The TF--IDF and logistic-regression classifier achieved higher Top-1 Accuracy, and the paired difference remained significant after correction for multiple comparisons. HiED instead provided ranked differential diagnoses, criterion-level evidence records, and explicit finalization artifacts. Its value therefore lies in decomposing the diagnostic process rather than maximizing benchmark accuracy alone.

\item[RQ2] The largest observed discrepancy lies at primary selection after candidate detection and criterion verification. Reference diagnoses were frequently present in the genuine ranked output and retained under the configured compatibility rules, yet another diagnosis was selected as the committed primary. This supports a primary-selection bottleneck in HiED under the benchmark protocol, but it does not establish the clinically correct primary diagnosis or a universal causal bottleneck in transcript-based diagnosis.

\item[RQ3] Retrieval improved the Single system relative to no retrieval, but global and parent-balanced retrieval did not differ significantly. HiED showed no statistically supported retrieval gain across the tested strategies. Because the strategies also differed in the number and composition of examples supplied to the model, the results do not establish an independent benefit of parent balancing.

\item[RQ4] NtS reduced Top-1 Accuracy relative to Direct-Answer across all three retrieval conditions, and the complete forced pairwise-override configuration produced a substantial decrease. The tested selection rules did not improve primary selection under their respective evaluation contracts. Because NtS emits one diagnosis, its set-level metrics also reflect output cardinality.

\item[RQ5] The Top-3--Top-1 selection gap remained visible across the evaluated Qwen3 sizes and on MDD-5k, so the pattern was not confined to one model size or synthetic corpus. Expanding the configured diagnostic scope made additional diagnoses representable but recovered none of the tested newly representable cases. Benchmark disagreements also concentrated in a small number of diagnostic pairs, especially depression--anxiety confusions. These findings support robustness only within the tested synthetic datasets, Qwen3 family, and configured analyses; they do not establish cross-model or clinical generality.
\end{description}

\section{Future Work}

\subsection{Completion and Reporting of the Psychiatrist Case-Review Study}

The ongoing psychiatrist case-review study described in Section~\ref{sec:psychiatrist-case-review} should be completed before its findings are incorporated into the substantive interpretation of the selection gap. No preliminary outcome or partial agreement estimate is used in the present conclusions. After all prespecified reviews are complete, Chapter~8 should report reviewer characteristics, the clinician-confirmed primary-selection error rate, secondary outcomes, agreement estimates, disagreement categories, and unresolved cases. The study remains an expert review of synthetic transcripts rather than clinical validation or a prospective workflow evaluation.

\subsection{Comparative Selection with Structured Diagnostic Knowledge}

The current criterion layer evaluates each diagnosis independently and therefore represents compatibility more directly than comparative primacy. A stronger selector should explicitly model relations among diagnoses, symptoms, temporal course, exclusion conditions, causal explanations, and comorbidities. One possible direction is a structured diagnostic knowledge graph in which nodes represent diagnoses, symptoms, episodes, and clinical findings, while edges represent temporal order, support, contradiction, exclusion, and explanatory dependence. The selector could then compare which candidate explains the overall presentation with the fewest unresolved conflicts, rather than selecting the diagnosis with the largest number of independently satisfied criteria. This approach should be evaluated against the current criterion-count and finalization rules under the same candidate rankings and case sets.

\subsection{Targeted Evidence Acquisition and Abstention}

Some cases may remain ambiguous because the transcript does not establish duration, onset, previous episodes, substance use, medical causes, or the relationship between concurrent syndromes. In these cases, applying another ranking rule may not be sufficient. Future systems should identify the unresolved fact that would best distinguish the leading candidates, ask a targeted follow-up question when interaction is possible, or abstain from committing to one diagnosis when the available evidence is insufficient. Conversational diagnostic systems show that LLMs can collect history~\citep{tu2025conversational}; the value of each question should be evaluated by whether it reduces diagnostic ambiguity rather than only by conversational fluency.

\subsection{Prospective Clinical Evaluation in Taiwan}

After the target labels, criterion audit, and final system configuration have been validated, HiED should be evaluated prospectively on de-identified Taiwanese psychiatric consultation transcripts. The inclusion criteria, primary and secondary outcomes, system configuration, subgroup analyses, and statistical plan should be specified before outcome analysis and publicly registered when appropriate. The evaluation should distinguish the recorded clinical diagnosis, transcript-only psychiatrist review, and fuller clinical assessment. Subsequent studies should examine workflow value, subgroup performance, automation bias, privacy, monitoring, and patient safety before any deployment claim is made.

\bigskip
\noindent A system may rank a benchmark-supported diagnosis as plausible without selecting it as the committed primary. HiED makes this distinction observable by separating candidate detection, criterion verification, and primary selection. The next step is not simply to add more agents, but to validate the evidence representation, model comparative diagnostic relations more explicitly, and evaluate the resulting system on real Taiwanese clinical data.

\section{Oracle-Based Upper-Bound Analysis}

The broad close-cluster oracle identifies 349 misclassified internal cases whose reference parent occurs in any supported cluster. It does not require the committed DA primary and the reference parent to occur in the same cluster, uses the reference label to identify eligible cases, and assumes every identified error can be corrected without harming a correct prediction. The resulting 0.867 upper bound is therefore deliberately optimistic and is not presented as a main-text performance level or a deployable local-selection estimate.

\section{Evidentiary Boundary for Sampling-Based Selection Analyses}

Internal self-consistency and confidence-informed self-consistency results are retained only as descriptive supporting analyses because complete case-level samples, seed schedules, and aggregation records are unavailable. They are not included in the paired selection-intervention table and do not support case-level claims comparable to methods with retained predictions. External sampling runs use their separately specified execution contracts and are interpreted as complete configurations rather than isolated selector changes.
