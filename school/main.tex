初診精神科晤談逐字稿中的鑑別診斷，本質上是一種在證據不完整條件下進行的比較式決策。多個疾病診斷可能同時與同一份逐字稿相容，但最終通常只能有一個被判定為主診斷。然而，用以區分病程、發病時序、排除病因與共病關係等關鍵鑑別資訊，未必會在單次初診晤談中被完整引出。大型語言模型雖然能流暢地閱讀並理解這類逐字稿，近期也已有大量研究嘗試將其應用於精神科診斷任務，但語言理解的流暢性並不等同於可靠的鑑別診斷能力。

既有評測呈現出一致的落差：模型在粗粒度分類任務上往往表現良好，但一旦進入細粒度的疾病層級鑑別診斷，準確率便會大幅下降。這個落差促使本研究提出一套可稽核的系統架構，用以明確定位錯誤發生於診斷流程中的哪一個階段。模型可能生成一個合理且受到診斷準則支持的候選疾病，卻仍未能將其正確選為主診斷。候選診斷生成、依準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇三者之間的差異，通常會被單一準確率指標所掩蓋，使研究者難以判斷系統究竟在哪個環節失敗。

為了解決這個問題，本研究提出 HiED——一套面向中文精神科初診對話逐字稿的鑑別診斷框架，將候選診斷偵測、ICD-10 逐準則驗證，以及主診斷選擇維持為可分別觀察的階段，而非將整個流程簡化為單一分類事件。同時，系統會在每次預測時輸出逐準則的稽核軌跡，使診斷推論過程能夠被檢視與分析。本研究中的系統並非宣稱已完成臨床驗證的自主診斷工具，而是定位為一個研究工具，用以分析在逐字稿條件下，精神科診斷流程究竟在哪一個階段發生失敗。

因此，本論文有兩項主要貢獻：其一，提出一套可稽核的失敗定位框架，使上述各診斷階段得以分別觀察；其二，提供可重現的證據指出，在僅使用初診逐字稿進行的精神科大型語言模型診斷任務中，主診斷選擇本身構成一個核心瓶頸。換言之，在本研究之合成逐字稿設定下，系統的主要限制不僅在於能否生成合理的候選診斷，而更在於能否在多個皆具一定準則支持的候選之間，進行有效比較並判定最適合作為主診斷者。最後需說明，本文題目所稱之臺灣脈絡，指涉系統之設計動機與預期部署場域（繁體中文互動、本地端推論、面向臨床人員之稽核），而非已完成之臺灣真實病人臨床驗證；本文所有量化結果均來自合成中文逐字稿語料，真實病人之臨床驗證為後續工作。

\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Differential diagnosis from an initial-consultation psychiatric transcript is a comparative decision under incomplete evidence. Multiple disorders may be compatible with the same transcript, yet only one can typically be judged the primary diagnosis. Key discriminating information---illness course, onset timing, exclusionary causes, and comorbidity relationships---may not be fully elicited within a single initial consultation. Large language models can read such transcripts fluently, and recent research has applied them to psychiatric diagnostic tasks, but linguistic fluency does not equate to reliable differential diagnostic ability.

Existing evaluations show a consistent gap: models perform well on coarse-grained classification, but accuracy drops sharply on fine-grained, disorder-level differential diagnosis. This gap motivates an auditable architecture that locates the stage at which a diagnostic error occurs. A model may generate a plausible disorder supported by diagnostic criteria, yet fail to select it as the primary diagnosis. The distinction among candidate-diagnosis generation, criterion-based verification, and primary-diagnosis selection is usually obscured by a single accuracy metric.

To address this problem, we propose HiED, a differential-diagnosis framework for Chinese psychiatric initial-consultation transcripts that keeps candidate-diagnosis detection, ICD-10 criterion-by-criterion verification, and primary-diagnosis selection as separately observable stages, rather than a single classification event. The system outputs a criterion-level audit trace with every prediction, allowing the diagnostic inference to be examined. It is not a clinically validated autonomous diagnostic tool, but a research instrument for analyzing where transcript-based psychiatric diagnosis fails. The Taiwanese context in the title denotes the intended deployment setting; real-patient Taiwanese validation remains future work.

This thesis therefore makes two main contributions: an auditable failure-localization framework that keeps these stages separately observable, and reproducible evidence that primary-diagnosis selection constitutes a core bottleneck in psychiatric large-language-model diagnosis using only initial-consultation transcripts. Under this study's synthetic transcript-only setting, the system's primary limitation lies not merely in whether it can generate plausible candidate diagnoses, but more critically in whether it can compare among multiple criterion-supported candidates and determine which is best suited as the primary diagnosis.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, multi-agent systems, psychiatric differential diagnosis, clinical decision support, ICD-10, auditable AI

\section{Background and Motivation}

Psychiatric outpatient care in Taiwan is widely accessible, but this access also creates a heavy clinical workload. Under National Health Insurance, patients may visit different levels of medical facilities without referral, and large medical centers may receive more than ten thousand outpatient visits across departments in one day~\citep{lin2020outpatient}. A first psychiatric visit must cover history taking, mental-status observation, risk screening, and an initial diagnostic judgment within limited time. The resulting note or transcript often contains incomplete information about onset, duration, course, medical or substance-related causes, and previous treatment.

The evidence may also be indirect. In Chinese-speaking settings, patients often describe distress through insomnia, fatigue, dizziness, chest tightness, or gastrointestinal symptoms before using explicit mood or anxiety terms~\citep{kleinman1982neurasthenia,ryder2008somatic}. Taiwanese consultations may also mix Mandarin, Taiwanese expressions, and local clinical wording. The same condition can therefore be described in several ways within one visit.

Differential diagnosis remains difficult because common psychiatric disorders share many symptoms. Depression, anxiety, stress-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, somatic symptom disorders, and sleep disorders may all involve insomnia, fatigue, poor concentration, and impaired daily function. ICD-10 provides requirements for symptoms, duration, impairment, course, and exclusions~\citep{who2019icd10}, but several disorders may still fit the same transcript. Evidence for both depression and anxiety, for example, may reflect comorbidity or overlapping symptoms from one main condition~\citep{brown2001comorbidity}. Even structured diagnostic studies report limited agreement for some common disorders~\citep{regier2013dsm5}.

These features make first-visit psychiatric diagnosis a problem of comparative bookkeeping under incomplete evidence. The clinician must document a best-supported working diagnosis, keep plausible alternatives visible, and identify which missing information should be collected at follow-up. \section{Problem Definition and Challenges}
\label{sec:problem-definition}

Large language models and medical multi-agent systems are attractive for psychiatric transcript analysis because they can process free-form dialogue, summarize symptoms, retrieve relevant examples, assign specialized roles, and return structured outputs~\citep{li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025,tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. However, these capabilities do not by themselves ensure reliable differential diagnosis from a fixed transcript. A fluent answer may still fail because the model never proposes a gold label, because its criterion audit does not support that diagnosis, or because it proposes the diagnosis but commits another one. HiED studies this narrower technical problem; it does not solve short consultation time, missing history, or clinical uncertainty.\ct{Does our model deal with all these problems?}

We study this question with three measures. \emph{Candidate detection} asks whether a gold parent label appears in the ranked Top-3. \emph{Criterion verification} asks whether a gold parent appears in the rule-confirmed set when that diagnosis has a corresponding checker. \emph{Committed selection} asks whether the committed diagnosis belongs to the gold parent set. These measures are used to locate disagreements among output views; they do not form a fixed chronological funnel for every policy.

We use the term \emph{primary-selection bottleneck} for the operational pattern in which a gold label is present among leading candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but the system commits a different diagnosis. The bottleneck is therefore a benchmark-local pattern showing that candidate ranking or criterion compatibility can exceed final commitment; it is not a claim about the true clinical primary diagnosis. It does not show that every error passes both earlier checks, that the corpus identifies a unique clinical primary diagnosis, or that every transcript contains enough evidence for one.\ct{what problem?}

This distinction matters because different errors need different solutions. Missing candidates call for better generation or retrieval. A candidate that fails criterion checking points to the checker, the rules, or missing evidence. A candidate that remains available but is not selected points to the final comparison among plausible diagnoses. The detailed metric definitions are given in \Cref{sec:gold-conventions}.

\section{Overview of HiED and Study Scope}

To make these failure modes observable, this thesis introduces HiED, a hybrid multi-agent system for Chinese psychiatric differential diagnosis from initial-consultation transcripts. The intended context is Taiwanese outpatient care, but all quantitative results in this thesis use synthetic Chinese transcript datasets; no Taiwanese patient results are reported.

HiED has five main parts. It formats the dialogue while preserving speaker turns, retrieves class-balanced examples, asks an LLM diagnostician for a ranked differential and a primary diagnosis, checks each active diagnosis against operationalized ICD-10 criteria, and applies deterministic rules to form a logic-confirmed set. Each criterion is marked as \texttt{met}, \texttt{not\_met}, or \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}.

The selected Direct-Answer policy keeps the diagnostician's primary diagnosis and uses outputs from the Criterion Checker and Compatibility Auditor as audit artifacts. The alternative Nominate-then-Select policy uses the same upstream outputs but allows the logic-confirmed set to determine a different committed diagnosis. Their comparison tests whether criterion compatibility is more useful as an audit or as a selector. Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture} describes the architecture in detail.

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes two main contributions.

First, it presents an auditable hybrid architecture that combines LLM diagnosis, explicit ICD-10 criterion checking, and deterministic rules. The system records criterion states and supporting evidence instead of relying only on a free-form rationale. The trace can be inspected and replayed, but it does not prove that the LLM's hidden reasoning is faithful or that the checker judgments are clinically correct~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}.

Second, it presents a stage-wise failure-localization and empirical evaluation framework that separates candidate detection, criterion verification, and committed selection, quantifies the primary-selection bottleneck, and tests whether criterion-based finalization and several repair methods close the observed gap. The negative repair results narrow several simple explanations under the tested settings, but they do not show that better selectors or additional clinical information can never help.

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related}

\section{Psychiatric LLM Diagnosis}

Psychiatric LLM studies cover several different tasks, including examination questions, short vignettes, diagnostic reasoning, and comparison with clinicians~\citep{qiu2023largeai,li2024taiwan,raballo2025,sarma2025}. These studies show that LLMs can produce plausible clinical answers, but performance depends on the input, label space, and reference standard. Detecting depression from text or audio is also different from choosing one primary diagnosis among several overlapping disorders~\citep{li2025addreview}.

Chinese psychiatric datasets make disorder-level evaluation possible. LingxiDiagBench includes static diagnosis and dynamic consultation over the synthetic LingxiDiag-16K corpus and reports a clear drop from broad depression--anxiety classification to fine-grained diagnosis~\citep{xu2026lingxidiagbench}. MDD-5k provides a second synthetic Chinese diagnostic-dialogue resource based on anonymized cases~\citep{yin2024mdd5k}. These datasets support reproducible experiments, but they do not replace real-patient validation.

Existing systems also differ in whether they can ask questions. MIND, WiseMind/ProAI, MAGI, and DSM5AgentFlow study inquiry or interactive diagnostic workflows~\citep{li2026mind,wu2026wisemind,bi2025magi,ozgun2025dsm5agentflow}. MentalSeek-Dx and MoodAngels focus more on diagnosis from information already available in records or transcripts~\citep{sun2026mentalseek,xiao2025moodangels}. Interactive systems can obtain missing evidence, while fixed-input systems isolate how well a model uses the same evidence. This thesis studies the fixed-input setting.

\section{Medical Multi-Agent Reasoning}

Medical multi-agent systems assign different roles to LLMs and combine their outputs through consultation, debate, or adaptive collaboration~\citep{tang2024medagents,kim2024mdagents}. AMIE and related work also show the value of broad differential diagnosis, realistic consultation, and expert review~\citep{mcduff2025amie,tu2025conversational}. These systems demonstrate that structured interaction can help, but adding agents does not guarantee a better final answer. Agents may repeat the same model bias, and free-form discussion can make it difficult to identify which component changed the decision.

For this thesis, the important issue is controlled attribution. If ranking, criterion checking, debate, and finalization all change at once, an accuracy difference cannot show which change helped. HiED therefore compares two policies that reuse the same ranked candidates and checker outputs: Direct-Answer keeps the diagnostician's decision, whereas Nominate-then-Select uses criterion compatibility to choose the primary.

\section{Auditability and Criterion Grounding}
\label{sec:auditability-definition}

A fluent explanation is not proof of faithful reasoning. Model-generated chains can sound coherent while not reflecting the factors that produced the answer~\citep{turpin2023unfaithful}. In healthcare, explanation quality, interpretability, and clinical validity therefore need separate evaluation~\citep{rudin2019stop,huang2024xiai}.

Diagnostic-chain and symptom-based methods make outputs easier to inspect~\citep{chen2025cod,lee2026interpretable,lyu2026depllm}. However, a generated reasoning chain is still another model output. This thesis uses the narrower term \emph{auditability}: an external record that links transcript evidence, criterion states, rule results, and the final output. The record can be checked and replayed, but it does not reveal the model's hidden reasoning or turn checker outputs into clinical ground truth.

Constraint-logic systems are the closest related approach because they translate diagnostic criteria into explicit rules~\citep{kim2025clp}. HiED keeps the learned diagnostician separate from the criterion and rule layer. Under Direct-Answer, the structured layer audits the decision; under Nominate-then-Select, it is tested as a selector. Prior work has not used this separation to compare gold-label retention with primary selection on Chinese psychiatric transcripts.

\section{Structured Diagnostic Knowledge and Primary Selection}

Knowledge-based diagnosis methods represent relations among symptoms, disorders, and missing information. MedKGI supports information-guided inquiry, and DKEC uses structured domain knowledge for label prediction~\citep{wang2025medkgi,ge2024dkec}. Such relations may help when disorders share symptoms but differ in onset, duration, exclusions, or causal direction.

HiED does not implement a full knowledge-graph selector. Instead, this literature motivates future work if simple criterion counts and local re-ranking are not enough. A stronger selector may need to compare which diagnosis best explains the full presentation, not only how many criteria each diagnosis satisfies.

The comparison shows that prior systems address complementary parts of the diagnostic process. Interactive systems can collect missing information, whereas fixed-input systems permit controlled evaluation under identical evidence. Some methods expose candidate hypotheses or criterion-related outputs, but the reviewed studies do not jointly evaluate candidate detection, criterion-level retention, and final primary commitment on the same fixed cases. HiED is designed to make these three output views separately measurable rather than to claim superiority across all clinical or conversational capabilities.

\section{Research Gap and Research Questions}

Prior work shows that LLMs can diagnose psychiatric cases, retrieve examples, use diagnostic criteria, and work in multi-agent systems. Four gaps remain. First, conventional text classifiers, single LLMs, retrieval-augmented LLMs, and structured systems are not always compared under the same split and endpoint. Second, final accuracy does not show whether a gold label was absent from the candidate list or present but not committed. Third, ordinary nearest-neighbor retrieval and class-balanced retrieval require matched comparison. Fourth, criterion-based finalization and later repair methods must be separated from broader robustness and transfer analyses. The precise gap addressed here is that HiED retains ranked candidate generation, criterion compatibility, and final commitment as separate outputs evaluated on the same cases.

This thesis therefore uses five research questions:

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] How does HiED compare with conventional and single-model baselines under the same data split, label mapping, and primary evaluation endpoint?
\item[RQ2] How often is a gold label present among ranked candidates or criterion-supported alternatives but not selected as the committed diagnosis?
\item[RQ3] Does class-balanced retrieval improve over no retrieval and ordinary global nearest-neighbor RAG in matched Single and HiED pipelines?
\item[RQ4] Do criterion-based re-selection or the tested selection-repair methods improve over Direct-Answer?

\chapter{HiED System Architecture}
\label{ch:architecture}

\section{Transcript Input and Configured Diagnostic Scope}

HiED processes one Chinese psychiatric consultation transcript at a time. The input is an ordered sequence of turns, each marked as \texttt{Clinician} or \texttt{Patient}; speaker labels and turn order are preserved in the prompt. Transcripts longer than the available prompt budget are deterministically truncated by retaining complete turns from the beginning and end, with an explicit marker for the omitted middle. The system does not summarize or invent missing text. This is a fixed-input study: the system cannot ask the patient a follow-up question during inference.\ct{Is this about input and transcript format?}

ICD-10 is the diagnostic coding system used to name the disorders in this study~\citep{who2019icd10}. The system does not search all ICD-10 diagnoses. Before a run begins, it loads one fixed \emph{configured diagnostic profile} that is used for every patient. The canonical diagnostic profile contains fourteen configured diagnostic categories. F41 and F43 include subtype-level categories, so the fourteen categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels for scoring. A raw gold label outside those parents is evaluated as \emph{Others}.

Three terms are used throughout the architecture. A \emph{configured diagnostic category} is an ICD-10 code that the diagnostician may rank and for which the system defines a corresponding criterion checker. The \emph{criterion-checker set} is the collection of those fourteen checker definitions; it is not a learned memory bank. Each criterion receives one of three model-generated evidence states: \texttt{met} when the transcript supports it, \texttt{not\_met} when the transcript contains evidence against it, and \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} when the transcript supports neither conclusion. The rules are study-specific operationalizations informed by ICD-10 descriptions, not a reproduction of the full official manual and not independently clinician-validated ground truth.

\section{Global Top-5 and Parent-Balanced Case Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

The Similar-Case Retriever adds relevant training examples to the diagnostician prompt. It follows the retrieval-augmented generation pattern~\citep{lewis2020rag}: BGE-M3 is the multilingual text-embedding model used to convert the query transcript and training transcripts into normalized dense vectors~\citep{chen2024bgem3}. FAISS is the vector similarity-search library and index used for retrieval, not a standalone data store~\citep{douze2024faiss}. FAISS inner-product search over normalized vectors corresponds to cosine-similarity ranking. The canonical configuration uses class-balanced retrieval: instead of returning only the globally nearest cases, it attempts to return at most one high-similarity example for each of the eleven parent labels in the configured output space. This reduces domination by frequent classes such as depression and anxiety.

The retrieval classes are fixed by the configured diagnostic profile and are identical for every patient.\ct{Are the candidate classes pre-determined, or patient-specific?} Only the example selected within each class depends on the current transcript. Subtypes that share a parent code, such as F41.0, F41.1, and F41.2, share one retrieval class. Global Top-5 retrieval and class-balanced retrieval use the same FAISS index but different selection policies. Retrieved cases are included only in the Diagnostician prompt; they do not enter checker prompts or directly choose the final output.

The evaluation includes three distinct retrieval conditions: no retrieval, ordinary global Top-5 nearest-neighbor retrieval, and class-balanced retrieval. They use the same retrieval index and embedding model, while differing in whether and how retrieved examples are selected.

\section{Ranked Differential Generation}

The canonical diagnostician uses Qwen3-32B with 4-bit Activation-aware Weight Quantization (AWQ). Its input contains the transcript, the configured diagnostic profile, and optional retrieved examples. Its prompt requests a ranked candidate list containing five ordered diagnoses from that profile, a short reason for each, one primary field chosen from the first three, and at most one separate comorbid field. In clinical prose this is a ranked differential diagnosis; as a formal artifact it is the ranked candidate list. The primary field and the ranked list are stored separately. In the running example, F41.1 can therefore remain in the Top-3 even though F32 is emitted as primary.

The reference diagnostician is a system specification, not an ablation. It uses greedy decoding and does not request a calibrated probability or an abstention decision. Confidence-based sampling, calibration, and selective-output ideas are evaluated only as later repair or future-work directions.\ct{Is this an ablation study? If so, why in Appendix?}

\section{Criterion-Level Evidence Checking}

Each of the fourteen configured diagnostic categories has a corresponding criterion checker based on the study's operational rule set.\ct{Is this ICD 10?} The rules are linked to ICD-10 disorder descriptions~\citep{who2019icd10}, but several thresholds are simplified study rules. Appendix~\ref{app:active-profile} lists the diagnostic codes and configured thresholds so readers can distinguish the study implementation from official clinical guidance.

Every checker assigns the three states defined at the start of this chapter.\ct{What 3 states?} It may also return a short transcript span linked to the judgment. The five ranked diagnoses are checked first, followed by the remaining configured diagnostic categories, so every case receives an audit over the same fourteen-category profile.

Missing information is not negative evidence. A criterion is \texttt{not\_met} only when the transcript contains evidence against it; otherwise unresolved evidence remains \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence}.

\section{Deterministic Diagnostic Compatibility Aggregation}

The Compatibility Auditor is a deterministic rule-based module. For each configured diagnostic category, it reads the Criterion Checker states, applies the predefined confirmation rule, adds the diagnosis to the rule-confirmed set when that rule is satisfied, and retains the rule application in the audit trace. A rule may require all criteria, a minimum number of core and total criteria, a symptom count, or particular criterion groups. The same checker trace always produces the same confirmed set. Confirmation means compatibility with the configured study rule; it is not primacy and does not mean that the diagnosis is clinically correct or should be primary. Several diagnoses may be confirmed in the same case.

Checker states are model-generated and have not been validated as clinician ground truth. They create an evidence-linked record that can be inspected and replayed, but they do not prove that the criterion judgments or evidence spans are correct.

\section{Direct-Answer and Nominate-then-Select Finalization}

HiED defines two finalization policies over the same upstream ranked candidates, criterion states, and logic-confirmed set. Direct-Answer (DA) is the reference policy and preserves the diagnostician's emitted decision. Nominate-then-Select (NtS) tests whether criterion-derived compatibility can improve the committed primary diagnosis. Two NtS output contracts separate primary re-selection from single-label emission.

Under Direct-Answer (DA), the diagnostician's primary field becomes the committed diagnosis; the first ranked diagnosis is used only when that field is missing. An optional comorbidity is retained when it differs from the primary. Outputs from the Criterion Checker and Compatibility Auditor do not replace the DA committed diagnosis.

Nominate-then-Select (NtS) uses criterion compatibility to choose the committed diagnosis. It selects the first confirmed diagnosis in the five-diagnosis ranking. If none is confirmed, it selects another rule-confirmed diagnosis with the highest met ratio. If no diagnosis is confirmed, it falls back to the first ranked diagnosis.

\emph{NtS-single} emits only the re-selected primary, so it changes both the primary-selection rule and the cardinality of the emitted diagnostic set. The controlled \emph{NtS-primary} contract applies the same primary-selection rule while preserving the DA comorbidity after parent projection, except when it duplicates the newly selected primary. NtS-primary is an analytic configuration that isolates primary re-selection from secondary-label suppression. NtS-primary and NtS-single consequently have identical committed primaries and Top-1 values, while their set-level metrics can differ.

DA and NtS-single reuse the same ranked candidates and checker traces, but differ in both the primary-selection rule and secondary-label emission: DA preserves an optional comorbid diagnosis, whereas NtS-single emits only the re-selected primary. The NtS-primary controlled replay preserves the DA secondary output when isolating primary re-selection. Pairwise comparison and debate are selection-repair methods rather than finalization policies of the reference system.

\section{Recorded Audit Outputs}

The audit artifacts include the predicted diagnosis set, ranked differential, criterion states, short evidence spans, confirmed diagnoses, finalization source, and run failures. A reviewer can therefore challenge the candidate ranking, an individual criterion judgment, a study rule, or the final selection separately.

This record follows the definition of auditability in Section~\ref{sec:auditability-definition}. It makes the system easier to inspect, but it does not expose the LLM's hidden reasoning, prove clinical correctness, or replace real-patient validation.

\section{Model and Inference Configuration}

The canonical checkpoint uses 4-bit AWQ to reduce model-memory requirements and allow the 32-billion-parameter backbone to be served locally without changing the prompt, diagnostic scope, or finalization logic. No canonical system component fine-tunes model weights. vLLM is the inference and serving backend for loading the model and generating outputs; it is not a diagnostic component and does not alter prompts, criteria, rules, or finalization. The system uses greedy decoding, an 8,192-token context window, a 1,536-token output cap, structured JSON output, guided decoding where supported, schema validation, and checker-output recovery for duplicate or invalid criterion items.

Runs are checked for case count, valid outputs, parse failures, ranked-output availability, and candidate validity. A checker-output recovery occurs when an LLM response can be parsed but contains duplicate or invalid criterion items. The parser removes those items and retains the valid criterion records. This is a structured-output schema-adherence warning rather than a hard parse failure.

\section{Configured Output Space and Evaluation Label Projection}
\label{sec:gold-conventions}

The canonical system uses fourteen configured ICD-10 diagnostic categories, listed in Table~\ref{tab:configured-profile-summary}. For evaluation, subtype-level categories are projected to eleven broader parent labels. For example, panic disorder (F41.0), generalized anxiety disorder (F41.1), and mixed anxiety and depressive disorder (F41.2) are evaluated under the F41 parent label. A case is considered in scope when at least one gold diagnosis maps to this configured parent-label set. A case is considered out of scope when none of its gold diagnoses maps to the set. Out-of-scope gold labels are recorded as \emph{Others}. Because the canonical system has neither an \emph{Others} output nor a trained unknown detector, it cannot correctly represent these cases.

Following standard machine-learning evaluation terminology, benchmark-provided diagnosis codes are referred to as \emph{gold labels}. In this thesis, the term denotes the dataset annotations used for scoring and does not imply an independently adjudicated clinical gold standard. For case $x$, $G(x)$ is the deduplicated gold-label set after parent-level projection. LingxiDiag cases may contain more than one gold parent label, and neither dataset provides an independently adjudicated unique clinical primary diagnosis. The first-listed gold parent is used only for explicitly single-label descriptive analyses such as the confusion matrix.

In the running example from Chapter~\ref{ch:architecture}, F41.1 is scored as F41. If the gold-label set contains F41 and the system emits F32 while ranking F41.1 second, Top-1 is wrong but Top-3 is correct.

\section{Prediction Views and Metrics}

The evaluator keeps three outputs separate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item The \emph{primary view}, $p_1(x)$, is the final primary diagnosis.
\item The \emph{ranked view}, $R_3(x)$, contains the primary followed by the remaining deduplicated diagnostician ranking, limited to three parent labels.
\item The \emph{multilabel view}, $M(x)$, contains the primary and any emitted comorbid parent.
\end{enumerate}

Because a case may contain more than one gold label, Top-$k$ Accuracy counts a case as correct when at least one gold label appears among the first $k$ predictions. Exact Match, also called subset accuracy, requires the complete predicted label set to equal the complete gold-label set. These are benchmark-agreement measures, not independent measures of the patient's true clinical diagnosis.

Top-3 and committed Top-1 are computed over all 1,000 internal test cases. Gold-label retention in the logic-confirmed set is computed over the 915 cases with at least one non-\emph{Others} gold parent covered by an active Criterion Checker.

\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}1\ Accuracy}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
\[
\mathrm{Top\text{-}3\ Accuracy}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
\mathrm{Exact}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_x \mathbb{1}[M(x)=G(x)].
\]

Macro-F1 gives each of the twelve scored classes equal weight. Weighted-F1 weights classes by their reference frequency. A system without a real ranked output is not assigned an artificial Top-3 from a fixed candidate list.

A legacy single-label metric compares the committed diagnosis only with the first-listed gold parent. It is used for the confusion matrix but not for the main result. This is why the canonical run can have first-listed agreement 0.509 and Top-1 Accuracy 0.518 without contradiction.

For each case, the thesis records three indicators:
\[
D_3(x)=\mathbb{1}[R_3(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
R(x)=\mathbb{1}[L(x)\cap G(x)\neq\varnothing],
\]
\[
S(x)=\mathbb{1}[p_1(x)\in G(x)],
\]
where $L(x)$ is the logic-confirmed parent set. $D_3$ and $S$ use all cases. Gold-label retention, $R$, is calculated only for cases whose gold-label set contains at least one non-\emph{Others} parent represented by an active Criterion Checker. This checker-eligible subset contains 915 of the 1,000 test cases, whereas Top-1 and Top-3 use all 1,000 cases. The rates therefore have different denominators and should not be interpreted as consecutive stages of one case-level funnel.

These indicators ask three simple questions: Was a gold label in the ranked candidate list? Was it supported by the criterion audit? Was it selected as primary? They are analysis views of one recorded output, not a claim that every finalization policy executes the steps in that order.

\section{Statistical Analysis}

The case is the unit of analysis. Single-system binary proportions are summarized with 95\% Wilson confidence intervals. Pairwise comparisons are restricted to systems evaluated on the same case IDs with the same endpoint definition. For binary correctness outcomes, the exact McNemar test is computed from the two discordant counts: cases correct only under the first system and cases correct only under the second. These counts are reported with the corresponding $p$-value so that the direction and magnitude of the paired difference remain visible.

Reported $p$-values are interpreted together with the discordant counts and effect direction rather than as standalone evidence. The matched analysis reports both raw exact McNemar $p$-values and Holm-adjusted values within three comparison families: baseline comparisons, Single retrieval variants, and HiED retrieval variants. A non-significant result indicates insufficient evidence of a difference and is not treated as proof of equivalence. Comparisons that do not share the same cases, endpoint, or execution contract are treated as descriptive rather than as paired statistical tests.

It produces ranked parent-label scores and a predicted label set, but no criterion-level audit.

\paragraph{Single-model baselines.} Single uses one LLM diagnostic call with the same configured label space and emits a primary diagnosis plus an optional comorbidity. It does not invoke HiED's Criterion Checkers, deterministic logic, or formal ranked-differential contract. Single + global RAG keeps this diagnostic contract but prepends the five globally nearest training cases. Single + balanced RAG instead attempts to retrieve at most one high-similarity example per configured parent class.

\paragraph{HiED comparison.} HiED--DA uses the ranked Diagnostician, the full Criterion Checker set, deterministic compatibility rules, and Direct-Answer finalization. Its output includes a ranked Top-5, a committed primary, an optional comorbidity, and the audit trace. The RQ1 comparison is therefore a matched benchmark comparison of complete system packages, not a one-component ablation between Single and HiED.

The final comparison uses the same 1,000 case IDs, case order, label mapping, and evaluation implementation. Majority and TF--IDF + logistic regression provide conventional references, while the additional HiED retrieval variants belong to RQ3.

\section{Failure Localization Across Ranking, Verification, and Commitment (RQ2)}

RQ2 uses the $D_3$--$R$--$S$ measures defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}. Because the Direct-Answer committed diagnosis occupies the first position of the recorded ranking, every Top-1-correct case is also Top-3 correct. Therefore, when both metrics are computed on the same 1,000 cases, the Top-3--Top-1 difference counts cases in which at least one gold parent appears within the Top-3 but the committed diagnosis is not a gold parent.

Criterion retention cannot be added as a third marginal stage in the same 1,000-case funnel because it is defined only for the 915 checker-eligible cases. The case-level analysis therefore constructs a $2\times2\times2$ joint table on those 915 cases, where $D_3$ indicates that a gold parent appears in the Top-3, $R$ indicates that a gold parent appears in the logic-confirmed set, and $S$ indicates that the committed diagnosis is a gold parent. The joint table supports quantities such as $P(R\mid D_3)$, $P(S\mid R)$, and $P(S\mid D_3,R)$ without mixing denominators. Confirmed-set size and criterion-state counts additionally show whether the audit rejects the gold label or keeps many diagnoses at the same time.

A separate gold-in-close-supported-cluster analysis estimates a broader oracle opportunity beyond the recorded Top-3. For each case, diagnoses considered by either the Diagnostician or the deterministic rule layer are retained when their criterion met ratio is at least 0.50 and are sorted by met ratio. Two adjacent diagnoses are linked when their met-ratio difference is smaller than 0.10; consecutive links form a close cluster. The scan covers the full retained candidate list rather than only the Top-3. A misclassified DA case is counted as oracle-recoverable when at least one gold parent appears in any close cluster. This definition does not require the deployed primary and the gold parent to occupy the same cluster, so it is deliberately broader and more optimistic than a selector choosing only among alternatives close to the current primary. Because it also uses the gold label and assumes every counted case can be corrected without harming a currently correct prediction, the resulting 349 cases form an opportunity upper bound rather than a deployable method or an accuracy estimate.

\paragraph{Pairwise comparison.} The pairwise gate retains candidates with a criterion met ratio of at least 0.50 and identifies adjacent alternatives separated by less than 0.10 after sorting by support. The independent run regenerates the upstream pipeline before invoking the pairwise comparison, so its effect includes generation variation. The controlled replay fixes the DA ranking, checker trace, and logic result and applies only the downstream pairwise preference. Forced override adopts that preference whenever available, even when the original DA decision would otherwise be retained, and serves as a safety stress test rather than a proposed deployment policy.

\paragraph{Two-view debate.} Debate is a ranking reconciliation rather than a pro-versus-con argument about one diagnosis. The candidate set is the union of the Diagnostician Top-5 and the logic-confirmed diagnoses. Advocate-D receives the ranking, rationale, and retrieved-case gist. Advocate-C receives the same candidates and criterion-evidence table, but not the Diagnostician rationale or retrieved cases. Each produces a ranked Top-3 for at most two rounds. Agreement on the first candidate ends the debate; otherwise a neutral judge selects from the shared candidate set. This is an independently generated pipeline run, and the debate contract emits one primary rather than preserving the DA secondary label. Because the advocate and judge prompts combine the transcript summary, candidate set, and intermediate arguments, the debate protocol requires a larger context window than the reference Direct-Answer pipeline. The final debate run therefore uses a 24,576-token context window, compared with 8,192 tokens for the reference system.

\paragraph{Deterministic fusion.} The fusion sweep evaluates fixed, zero-LLM rules over the recorded DA rank, logic-confirmation status, and criterion met ratio. The selected rule was chosen on development data before held-out evaluation. It keeps DA unless the DA primary is unconfirmed and a confirmed Top-3 alternative exceeds its met ratio by at least 0.20. The held-out test therefore evaluates one validation-selected rule rather than selecting among the 293 candidates on the test set.

\paragraph{Self-consistency and CISC.} Self-consistency samples the Diagnostician repeatedly and selects the primary by vote. Confidence-informed self-consistency uses the same samples but weights the vote by the Diagnostician's verbalized primary confidence rather than model logits. The retained run-level protocol uses $K=3$ and $K=5$ samples at temperature 0.7 with fixed seed schedules. Criterion Checkers, deterministic logic, and finalization are not rerun, so these are primary-commitment probes rather than uncertainty estimates for the full HiED pipeline. The internal SC/CISC rows are retained only as summary-level evidence; the fully auditable sampling manifest is available for the external MDD-5k variant matrix.

For all repairs, parent-projected Top-1 Accuracy is the primary outcome. Top-3 Accuracy, Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 are secondary. Replay methods also report wrong-to-correct and correct-to-wrong flips.

\section{Model-Size Robustness and External Evaluation Design (RQ5)}

RQ5 uses several distinct analyses:

\begin{itemize}
\item Qwen3 models at 4B, 8B, 14B, and 32B test size sensitivity within one model family.
\item Retrieval and similarity studies separate prompt-side retrieval from output-level reranking.
\item MDD-5k tests transfer to another synthetic corpus.
\item A broader diagnostic profile tests whether adding output classes improves scope coverage.
\item Confusion pairs and aggregate audit patterns describe where errors are concentrated.
\end{itemize}

These analyses answer different questions. Model-size stability does not prove cross-family robustness. Scope expansion shows that a diagnosis can be added to the output space, not that the model has learned when to select it.

\chapter{Error Analysis and Audit-Based Failure Localization}
\label{ch:error}

This chapter analyzes disagreements between model outputs and benchmark gold labels. As defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:data}, these gold labels are dataset annotations used for evaluation rather than independently adjudicated clinical diagnoses. The analysis combines aggregate confusion counts, criterion-state summaries, ranking-to-commitment disagreements, and scope-related failures. These categories are not mutually exclusive: one case may involve both missing evidence and an incorrect final commitment. The purpose is to localize where the recorded outputs disagree, not to assign a single clinically adjudicated cause to every error.

\section{Error Distribution Across Diagnostic Labels}

The largest confusion is F41$\rightarrow$F32 (178 cases), compared with only 24 in the reverse direction. Several other errors also end at F32, including F39, \emph{Others}, F51, and F98 cases. This suggests a frequent depression-label attractor when symptoms overlap or the transcript does not clearly establish duration, onset, autonomic symptoms, or the main source of impairment. The counts describe disagreement between model outputs and benchmark gold labels in a synthetic corpus; they do not adjudicate which diagnosis is clinically correct in every case.

\section{Missing and Unresolved Diagnostic Evidence}

Missing information is also common in the audit trace. An \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} state may be appropriate when a transcript never establishes prior episodes, substance use, medical causes, or whether a second syndrome is independent. Changing thresholds can retain or reject more candidates, but it cannot create a missing fact. This supports testing targeted evidence acquisition rather than relying only on new weights or rules.

\section{Gold Labels Ranked but Not Committed}

The 284 candidate-present, wrong-commitment cases are the clearest selection errors: a gold parent is already in Top-3, but another diagnosis is chosen. Criterion verification offers related evidence, but not on the same denominator: it retains a gold parent in 857 of 915 eligible cases and confirms a median of six diagnostic categories. The audit therefore often preserves the answer without resolving primacy.

Two further patterns concern the output contract. NtS-single changes the committed primary and removes secondary labels, whereas NtS-primary changes only the primary. Likewise, broader scope makes more diagnoses legal to emit, yet the retargeting sample recovers none of the 58 newly available gold parents. Output cardinality, scope, and primary selection must therefore be evaluated separately.

\section{Output Cardinality and Diagnostic-Scope Errors}

NtS-single emits only the re-selected primary. Its apparently more conservative set behavior therefore combines two interventions: changing the primary and deleting the DA secondary output. The NtS-primary analysis separates these effects by preserving the same re-selected primary while retaining the DA comorbidity set; it has exactly the same Top-1 as NtS-single but lower Exact Match and slightly higher classwise F1.

The error archetype is consequently not that verification chooses a better diagnostic set. Primary re-selection remains harmful, while single-label emission can improve Exact Match when the benchmark gold set is itself a singleton and can harm cases in which a secondary diagnosis is appropriate. Top-1, Exact Match, classwise F1, and output cardinality must therefore be reported as distinct operating characteristics. A deliberately conservative one-label display may still be a usable interface choice, but it should be justified as an output policy rather than as evidence that verification resolved diagnostic primacy.

\section{Using the Audit Trace for Analysis and Clinical Review}

In offline research, gold labels allow the analyst to ask whether the diagnosis was in scope, in Top-3, retained by verification, and finally selected. The same trace also shows how many alternatives were confirmed and which facts remained unresolved. This supports failure localization, but it uses the gold label and is unavailable during live inference.

For a new case without a gold label, a clinician can instead review the evidence for the final primary, the leading alternatives, unresolved discriminative criteria, useful follow-up questions, and the source of finalization. This can expose ambiguity and unsupported claims, but it cannot determine the correct diagnosis automatically.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

The main finding is that one accuracy score hides three different questions: whether the gold label appears in the ranked candidate list, whether the criterion layer finds it compatible with the transcript, and whether the system selects it as the primary diagnosis. On the test set, HiED--DA reaches 0.802 Top-3 Accuracy and 0.518 Top-1 Accuracy. Gold-label retention in the confirmed set is 857 of 915 checker-eligible cases, but the median confirmed set contains six configured diagnostic categories. These values use different denominators and do not form a strict sequence. Together, they show that finding a plausible diagnosis is easier than choosing one final diagnosis.

The external MDD-5k results support the same point more narrowly. In the matched 925-case run, Top-3 Accuracy is 0.8508 and Top-1 Accuracy is 0.5708. This shows that the ranking-to-selection gap remains under a different synthetic data distribution. However, the external analysis does not include the same verification measure and therefore does not reproduce the full internal analysis.

\section{What the Lexical Baseline Changes}

The matched TF--IDF result prevents the selection bottleneck from being read as proof of intrinsic transcript underdetermination. A supervised lexical model reaches 0.632 Top-1 on the same internal synthetic split, substantially above the canonical HiED--DA result. The benchmark therefore contains lexical signal that the evaluated LLM pipeline does not fully exploit. The selection bottleneck remains a valid description of where HiED and the matched LLM configurations fail, but it is a system-level empirical finding rather than a universal ceiling on transcript-only prediction.

The lexical result has its own boundary. Synthetic corpora may contain generator-specific wording and label cues, and the thesis's cross-corpus selector results already caution that fitted lexical signals need not transfer. A transcript-only human ceiling and external real-patient evaluation remain necessary to distinguish model limitation, corpus shortcut, and genuine missing-information limits.

\section{Primary Finding: Compatibility Does Not Resolve Primacy}

The criterion checker evaluates each diagnosis separately. It asks whether the transcript supports that diagnosis, not whether it is better than the other supported diagnoses. This distinction matters because common symptoms such as poor sleep, fatigue, low mood, worry, avoidance, and reduced concentration can support several disorders. Choosing a primary diagnosis often depends on comparative facts such as onset, duration, previous episodes, exclusionary causes, and whether one disorder explains the other symptoms.

This explains why broad criterion confirmation does not lead directly to a reliable final choice. NtS demonstrates the compatibility--primacy distinction causally. Across no retrieval, global Top-5 retrieval, and parent-balanced retrieval, applying the criterion-derived primary rule to the same upstream outputs reduces Top-1 by 2.8--2.9 percentage points. The NtS-single policy also changes output cardinality, so its Exact Match behavior cannot be credited to improved primary re-selection. The current criterion track is useful as a high-recall audit, but the tested rule is not a better primary selector.

The near-identical negative point estimates across the three retrieval conditions weaken the hypothesis that the original balanced-only NtS result was a retrieval-specific artifact. Because every interaction confidence interval includes zero, however, this is evidence of no detected moderation rather than proof that the retrieval conditions are equivalent.

The stage-wise analysis still changes how errors are understood. Missing the gold label in the ranked candidate list points to candidate generation or retrieval. Finding it in the ranked candidate list but not in the confirmed set points to evidence interpretation or missing information. Finding it in both views but selecting another diagnosis points more directly to comparative selection. These cases require different remedies and cannot be separated by endpoint accuracy alone.

\section{Why the Tested Repair Methods Did Not Improve Selection}

The tested repair methods cover several simple explanations for the gap. Pairwise reasoning and debate add more comparison. Deterministic fusion applies fixed rules to existing signals. Self-consistency tests whether greedy decoding hides a stable majority choice. Similarity-based reranking tests whether case-neighbor labels can reorder the candidates.

None of these methods gives a clear and reproducible improvement over Direct-Answer under the tested protocols. Pairwise reasoning does not change correctness on MDD-5k, the validation-selected fusion rule reduces performance, the self-consistency gains are small and not statistically significant, and the context-extended debate run performs worse. The experiments use different procedures, so they are not one matched component ablation. Their shared result is more limited: the remaining gap is not fixed by a simple threshold, majority vote, local override, or extra reasoning round.

This is not evidence that the broader method families cannot work. It shows only that the tested versions do not combine the available candidate and criterion signals well enough. Future selectors may need to compare candidates through explicit temporal relations, exclusion conflicts, and evidence that favors one diagnosis over another.

\section{Model Error Versus Missing Diagnostic Information}

The present experiments cannot fully separate two causes of error. First, the transcript may contain enough information, but the model may use it poorly. Second, the transcript may omit the information needed for a unique primary diagnosis. The 284 cases in which the gold label is in the Top-3 but is not selected show that useful candidate generation has already occurred in many errors. Broad confirmed sets and frequent \texttt{insufficient\_\allowbreak{}evidence} states also suggest that some cases lack clear discriminating facts, but they do not prove that missing information caused each error. The stronger same-split TF--IDF result provides an important counterexample to a universal missing-information explanation: at least some label signal is recoverable from these transcripts, although synthetic lexical shortcuts may contribute.

A transcript-only human study is needed to separate these causes. Psychiatrists should receive the same transcript and label space as the model. If clinicians also disagree or judge the evidence insufficient, the task or gold label may be the main limit. If clinicians consistently resolve cases that HiED misses, the main problem is more likely the method.

\section{Relation to Prior Psychiatric and Multi-Agent Systems}

Compared with interactive psychiatric agents, HiED deliberately studies fixed-input diagnosis: every method receives the same transcript and cannot ask follow-up questions. Compared with free-form multi-agent debate, HiED separates role-specific generation, criterion checking, deterministic compatibility auditing, and final commitment so that these outputs can be inspected independently. More agents therefore do not automatically imply better commitment. The tested debate and repair methods show that additional reasoning steps can add cost or change execution contracts without improving Top-1 Accuracy. Auditability can still have value without a Top-1 gain because it identifies candidates, unresolved evidence, and finalization sources for human review. The same ranking-to-commitment issue also appears in broader candidate-ranking problems: retrieving or naming a plausible item is easier than choosing the final item under incomplete evidence.

\section{Implications for System Design and Clinical Use}

HiED's audit trace is useful because it records the ranked candidates, criterion states, supporting text, confirmed diagnoses, and finalization source. This makes disagreements easier to inspect. It does not reveal the model's hidden reasoning or prove that the checker is clinically correct. The audit supports review; it does not create trust by itself.

The results also suggest that some cases require new evidence rather than another ranking rule. When several diagnoses remain plausible because duration, course, substance use, exclusion, or episode history is missing, the system should identify the missing fact, ask a focused follow-up question, or abstain. Comparative selection is useful when the evidence is sufficient; evidence collection is needed when it is not.

Finally, the Taiwanese setting is the intended use context, not the population validated here. All quantitative evidence comes from synthetic Chinese corpora. The thesis does not test real Taiwanese patients, local language use, hospital workflow, clinician behavior, or patient outcomes. At this stage, HiED is best viewed as a research system and a prototype for clinician review, not an autonomous diagnostic tool.

\chapter{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{ch:limitations}

\section{Construct Validity and Measurement Limitations}

The evaluation target is agreement with benchmark gold labels after parent-level projection. It is not an independent judgment of the patient's true diagnosis. Parent projection makes two corpora easier to compare but removes subtype detail. Top-1 Accuracy with a gold-label set is also less strict than matching only the first listed diagnosis. Top-1 Accuracy, Top-3 Accuracy, Exact Match, Macro-F1, and Weighted-F1 therefore measure different parts of benchmark performance; none alone measures clinical correctness or safety.

The selection bottleneck is an observed difference among output views, not a proven causal process inside the model. Under Direct-Answer, the primary diagnosis is chosen before the checker creates the audit trace. Top-1 Accuracy and Top-3 Accuracy use all 1,000 test cases, whereas gold-label retention uses 915 checker-eligible cases. These measures help locate errors but should not be read as one nested funnel. The oracle upper-bound recovery analysis is likewise an upper bound, not a deployable selector.

\paragraph{Lexical baseline and construct validity.} The strong internal TF--IDF result may reflect real transcript signal, source-corpus lexical regularities, synthetic-generator artifacts, or a mixture of all three. It narrows the interpretation of the LLM selection bottleneck but does not establish that a lexical classifier is clinically superior or transferable. Conversely, because this stronger same-split baseline exists, HiED errors alone cannot establish that primary diagnosis is intrinsically unrecoverable from a transcript.

\section{Internal Validity and Statistical Limitations}

The official LingxiDiagBench test split was unavailable, so the main in-domain result uses a 1,000-case held-out split from the original training-source corpus. The cases were excluded from retrieval and development, but the split still shares the generator, templates, and source distribution of the training data. The thesis does not report a full template-level or embedding near-duplicate audit, and foundation-model exposure to the public corpus cannot be ruled out. Because LingxiDiagBench generation and the evaluator both involve the Qwen3 family, generator--evaluator family dependence is also possible.

Not every comparison isolates one component. Single versus Single + balanced RAG mainly tests retrieval. Single + balanced RAG versus HiED--DA changes several parts of the system and measures a package effect. Direct-Answer versus Nominate-then-Select is more tightly matched, while pairwise, debate, fusion, self-consistency, and similarity-based reranking use different execution procedures. Their results support conclusions about the tested implementations, not a general ranking of method families.

The debate comparison also differs in inference budget. The reference Direct-Answer pipeline uses an 8,192-token context window, whereas the complete advocate--judge debate requires 24,576 tokens and additional model calls. Its result therefore evaluates the full debate configuration rather than the isolated effect of debate under a matched context or compute budget.

Several analyses are exploratory or use small samples. Rare classes have wide uncertainty, and a non-significant difference does not prove that two methods are equivalent. Multiple repair attempts also increase the chance of isolated favorable results. The thesis reduces this risk through predefined evaluation contracts, paired tests, and reporting negative results, but exploratory findings remain exploratory.

The balanced NtS-single row is a descriptive parity anchor for the NtS-primary replay because its upstream checker and logic-confirmed traces are not exact matches, even though its parent-level primary agrees for all 1,000 cases. Checker states remain algorithmic outputs rather than clinician labels, and an offline replayed change is not a deployable correction merely because benchmark labels are available for evaluation.

The canonical system also depends on one model family, prompt set, quantized checkpoint, serving stack, context window, and decoding setup. The Qwen3 size analysis does not establish that the same findings hold for unrelated models or runtimes.

\section{External Validity and Clinical Generalizability}

Both quantitative corpora are synthetic. They support controlled and reproducible experiments, but may contain generator-specific language, simplified ambiguity, limited comorbidity, and less noise than real practice. MDD-5k provides a useful distribution shift, but it is still synthetic and does not provide the same verification analysis as the internal set.

The intended setting is Taiwanese outpatient psychiatry, yet the study does not evaluate real Taiwanese patients, Traditional-Chinese clinical speech, mixed Mandarin--Taiwanese language, local coding practice, or hospital workflow. The fixed diagnostic scope also maps unsupported diagnoses to \texttt{Others}. Expanding the list of legal outputs does not by itself teach the model when to use the new labels, as shown by the retargeting experiment.

No result in this thesis establishes clinical effectiveness, clinician equivalence, treatment benefit, or safe autonomous use. There is no tested clinical abstention or unknown-diagnosis detector, calibrated confidence, subgroup fairness analysis, workflow study, or measurement of automation bias. Runtime cost, per-case token use, and latency were not evaluated as a formal workflow endpoint. Real-patient validation and operational evaluation must therefore precede any deployment claim.

\section{Reproducibility, Governance, and Deployment Boundaries}

The code, configurations, metric definitions, and retained result files support computational checking. Exact replay still requires the external datasets, the same model weights and serving environment, and reconstruction of the retrieval index. Reproducing a number is also not the same as validating the clinical meaning of the metric.

Future work with patient data will require institutional approval, privacy controls, access logging, monitoring, incident procedures, and periodic revalidation. Until these requirements and the clinical studies above are completed, HiED should remain a research and review prototype.

\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}

This thesis introduced HiED, a hybrid multi-agent framework that separates ranked differential generation, criterion-level evidence checking, deterministic compatibility aggregation, and primary-diagnosis commitment.

The matched lexical baseline achieved higher Top-1 performance than HiED on the internal benchmark. HiED nevertheless showed substantially higher ranked candidate coverage than committed Top-1 performance, and criterion checking frequently retained a gold diagnosis without determining which compatible diagnosis should be primary. Criterion-derived Nominate-then-Select finalization and the tested pairwise, debate, fusion, self-consistency, and similarity-based repair methods did not provide a clear and reproducible improvement in primary selection. Retrieval improved the Single system, but the results did not support an advantage for parent-balanced over global retrieval or a statistically supported retrieval gain for HiED. The ranking-to-commitment gap also remained visible on the external synthetic corpus.

HiED's contribution is therefore not state-of-the-art benchmark accuracy, but an auditable architecture and evaluation framework that localizes disagreement across candidate generation, evidence verification, and final commitment. The results are limited to synthetic benchmark data and do not establish clinical validity or autonomous diagnostic safety.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}

This section summarizes how the final experiments answer the five research questions introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:related}. Each answer is limited to benchmark performance on synthetic Chinese psychiatric transcripts and should not be interpreted as evidence of clinical effectiveness.

\begin{description}[style=nextline,leftmargin=3cm,labelwidth=2.5cm]
\item[RQ1] HiED did not outperform the strongest matched baseline on the internal benchmark. The TF--IDF and logistic-regression classifier achieved higher Top-1 performance, and the paired difference remained significant after correction for multiple comparisons. HiED instead provided ranked differential diagnoses, criterion-level evidence records, and explicit finalization artifacts. Its value therefore lies in decomposing the diagnostic process rather than maximizing benchmark accuracy alone.

\item[RQ2] The main failure occurs between retaining a compatible diagnosis and committing to it as primary. Gold diagnoses were frequently present in the ranked differential and retained by criterion verification, yet another diagnosis was selected. This supports a ranking-to-commitment bottleneck under the tested benchmark protocol, while the model-generated criterion judgments remain unvalidated clinically.

\item[RQ3] Retrieval improved the Single system relative to no retrieval, but global and parent-balanced retrieval did not differ significantly. HiED showed no statistically supported retrieval gain across the tested strategies. Because the retrieval strategies also differed in the number and composition of examples supplied to the model, the results do not establish an independent benefit of class balancing.

\item[RQ4] Criterion-derived NtS primary selection reduced Top-1 performance relative to Direct-Answer across the tested retrieval conditions. Preserving or removing the Direct-Answer secondary label changed set-level metrics but not the NtS primary diagnosis. The tested pairwise, debate, fusion, self-consistency, and similarity-based methods also did not provide a clear and repeatable improvement.

\item[RQ5] The ranking-to-commitment gap remained visible across the evaluated Qwen3 model sizes and on MDD-5k. However, all quantitative datasets were synthetic, and the model-family analysis did not include unrelated backbone families. The findings therefore support robustness within the tested experimental scope, not general clinical or cross-model validity.
\end{description}

\section{Future Work}

\subsection{Clinician Validation of Diagnostic Labels and Criterion Audits}

Future work should first validate both the benchmark target and the criterion-level audit. Psychiatrists should review the same transcripts and diagnostic scope provided to the system, indicate whether one primary diagnosis is adequately supported, record acceptable alternatives, and identify information that is missing for differential diagnosis. A separate annotation study should evaluate the Criterion Checker's evidence states and linked transcript spans. These analyses would help distinguish errors caused by the model, incomplete transcripts, ambiguous dataset labels, or incorrect criterion judgments.

\subsection{Comparative Selection with Structured Diagnostic Knowledge}

The current criterion layer evaluates each diagnosis independently and therefore represents compatibility more directly than comparative primacy. A stronger selector should explicitly model relations among diagnoses, symptoms, temporal course, exclusion conditions, causal explanations, and comorbidities. One possible direction is a structured diagnostic knowledge graph in which nodes represent diagnoses, symptoms, episodes, and clinical findings, while edges represent temporal order, support, contradiction, exclusion, and explanatory dependence. The selector could then compare which candidate explains the overall presentation with the fewest unresolved conflicts, rather than selecting the diagnosis with the largest number of independently satisfied criteria. This approach should be evaluated against the current criterion-count and finalization rules under the same candidate rankings and case sets.

\subsection{Targeted Evidence Acquisition and Abstention}

Some cases may remain ambiguous because the transcript does not establish duration, onset, previous episodes, substance use, medical causes, or the relationship between concurrent syndromes. In these cases, applying another ranking rule may not be sufficient. Future systems should identify the unresolved fact that would best distinguish the leading candidates, ask a targeted follow-up question when interaction is possible, or abstain from committing to one diagnosis when the available evidence is insufficient. Conversational diagnostic systems show that LLMs can collect history~\citep{tu2025conversational}; the value of each question should be evaluated by whether it reduces diagnostic ambiguity rather than only by conversational fluency.

\subsection{Prospective Clinical Evaluation in Taiwan}

After the target labels, criterion audit, and final system configuration have been validated, HiED should be evaluated prospectively on de-identified Taiwanese psychiatric consultation transcripts. The inclusion criteria, primary and secondary outcomes, system configuration, subgroup analyses, and statistical plan should be specified before outcome analysis and publicly registered when appropriate. The evaluation should distinguish the recorded clinical diagnosis, transcript-only clinician review, and fuller clinical adjudication. Subsequent studies should examine workflow value, subgroup performance, automation bias, privacy, monitoring, and patient safety before any deployment claim is made.

\bigskip
\noindent A psychiatric language model may recognize the correct diagnosis as plausible without selecting it as primary. HiED makes this distinction observable by separating ranking, criterion verification, and commitment. The next step is not simply to add more agents, but to validate the evidence representation, model comparative diagnostic relations more explicitly, and evaluate the resulting system on real Taiwanese clinical data.
